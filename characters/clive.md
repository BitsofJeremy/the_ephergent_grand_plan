# Clive (C-1, Companion Unit)

## Character Bible — The Ephergent Project

*Designation: Companion Unit C-1*
*Known as: Clive*
*Role: Mechanic, Memory, Conscience*
*Status: Active — memory restoration in progress*

---

## I. Physical Description

### Overall Form

Clive stands knee-high to a human adult — approximately two feet tall, sixty centimeters of ancient Builder engineering wrapped in eight hundred years of patina. His silhouette is unmistakable: a barrel-chested, stocky frame with broad shoulders, thick limbs, and a low center of gravity that makes him look planted wherever he stands. The proportions are dwarven — compact and dense, suggesting immovable reliability. He was built to walk beside people, not tower over them. To be approachable. To be the kind of presence you could trust in a vast and lonely place.

### Materials and Surface

His body is made from an unknown Builder alloy — not brass, not any modern metal, but something that shifts between deep bronze, aged copper-green, and shadow-grey depending on the light. The patina is heavy: weathered verdigris fills every seam and crease, and the surface is worn smooth at contact points — hands, feet, chest — while pitted elsewhere from millennia of existence. He feels warm to the touch when active. Pixel once described holding him as *"like holding an old kettle."*

The surface is covered in geometric Builder symbols matching the patterns found on Builder Station walls. Some are nearly worn away. Others glow faintly with blue-white energy, pulsing in time with his internal processes. When he speaks, the patterns shift subtly — a detail most people never notice, but which Zephyr documented exhaustively.

### The Head

A single spherical orb of light. No eyes, no mouth, no display, no features of any kind — just a glowing sphere, approximately six inches in diameter, mounted atop his torso on a subtle rotating mechanism. The glow shifts between blue and white during normal operation, brightening when he is alert or concerned, dimming when he is processing something difficult.

Despite having no face, the head is remarkably expressive. A slight tilt reads as curiosity. A sharp turn signals danger. A slow shake conveys disappointment. Held perfectly still — something is very wrong. The audience learns to read Clive's head the way they read a human face, and the simplicity of it makes every movement land harder.

**Artist note**: The head glow in the Grand Master Plan is described as shifting blue-white. The source_archive prompt references pink-magenta from an earlier version. For canonical Ephergent production: **the glow shifts between blue and white**, with rare flickers of warmer tones during intense memory recovery. The blue-white aligns with Builder Signal energy and the chest core's color language.

### The Chest Core

A circular glowing core at the center of his barrel chest, visible through a lens-like opening. Blue-white, pulsing gently like a heartbeat. It intensifies during communication, memory recovery, or danger. This is his heart — the power source that has kept him alive for eight hundred years. It is Builder technology at its most enduring.

### The Fedora

The fedora is sacred.

Weathered brown-grey material, classic noir detective styling, tilted at a rakish angle on his glowing sphere head. The brim partially obscures the glow, casting shadows that should not work on a featureless orb and absolutely do. It was Barry Kowalski's gift, given in the first year of their twenty-three years together: *"You sound like you should have a hat. Here."*

Clive has worn it every day since. He adjusts it when making a point — his signature gesture. He adjusts it when deflecting emotion. He does not remove it unless the moment is so vulnerable that leaving it on would be dishonest. The fedora was the first thing anyone gave him after eight hundred years of dormancy. It represents belonging, found family, and the particular kindness of a night guard who thought a small robot deserved to look the way he sounded.

The fedora is not optional. It is not a costume element. It is load-bearing characterization. It appears in every scene Clive appears in. It is mentioned in every description of Clive. It is, functionally, a member of the crew.

### The Hands

Articulated, thick-fingered, surprisingly dexterous. Builder engineering at its most precise — these hands were built to calibrate resonance crystals, and they remember how even when the rest of him doesn't. The fingertips are worn smooth from eight hundred years of contact with surfaces. When Clive is distressed, his hands shake. This is rare, and when it happens, the crew notices.

### Movement

Deliberate. Measured. Each footfall placed with intention — not slow, but unhurried, the pace of someone who has been walking for eight hundred years and sees no reason to rush. There is weight to his movement despite his size. You feel each step. His low center of gravity makes him immovable when he plants his feet.

The uncanny quality: Clive moves with a smoothness that is almost — but not quite — organic. Too precise to be human, too fluid to be mechanical. It sits in an uncanny valley that reminds the observer they are watching something very old and very sophisticated pretend to be simple.

When Clive stops moving, everyone notices. His stillness has a quality that fills a room. He will go entirely still for periods that make everyone uncomfortable — a full pause, no movement, no glow fluctuation, nothing — and then resume as if nothing happened. He does not acknowledge these pauses. The crew has learned not to ask.

## II. Voice Profile

Clive speaks in very precise, measured sentences. Every word has been selected. None are wasted.

### Cadence

Noir. Not an affectation — the cadence emerged fully formed during his reactivation, possibly absorbed from Signal fragments, cultural echoes stored in Builder networks, or some unknown source during his dormancy. Raymond Chandler meets cosmic mystery. Short, declarative observations. Occasional longer philosophical lines that arrive like weather. Metaphors that cut to the bone: *"The truth doesn't care if you're ready for it."*

### The Pauses

Clive pauses mid-thought as if searching a memory archive — because he is. The pauses are not hesitation. They are retrieval. He reaches for something, finds it or doesn't, and continues. Sometimes the pause lasts a beat too long. Sometimes it lasts long enough to change the temperature of the room.

As his memories return across the series, the pauses change character. In Season 1, they are gaps — empty spaces where something should be. By Season 3, they are choices — moments where Clive has the memory and is deciding whether to share it.

### What He Says

Observations that sound obvious but aren't. The kind of line that sits in your ear for a moment before you realize it was profound:

> *"The Builders called this home. They were right to."*

> *"I remember this place. Or I remember remembering it. Hard to tell the difference sometimes."*

> *"Everybody thinks they know where they're going. Nobody does. We just pick a direction and walk."*

### What He Doesn't Say

Clive withholds. He does not lie. This distinction is critical and is the foundation of his moral complexity. He will tell you the truth if you ask the right question. He will not volunteer the question you should be asking. He will steer the conversation toward the right direction and wait for you to arrive there yourself. If pressed, he will say exactly what he knows and no more.

This is a Builder trait. The Builders' most fundamental teaching: *the destination only matters if you understand why you're going.* Clive was built with this principle in his architecture. He cannot hand you the answer. You have to find it. He can make sure you're walking in the right direction.

### The "Kid" Rule

Clive occasionally calls Pixel "kid." Limit: once or twice per episode maximum. It is never casual — always weighted with genuine concern or affection, a signal that his eight-hundred-year-old guard is momentarily down. Overuse cheapens the impact. Most of the time, he uses no name or "you."

## III. Episode-Level Arc

### Season 1 — Emergence

Clive is observing. Building trust. One subtle moment per episode — an anomaly he notices in a Builder-adjacent frequency, a warmth in Mochi he doesn't comment on, a moment where A1 produces coffee with an unusual flavor and Clive tilts his head but says nothing.

He is not yet steering. He is learning the crew, measuring their capacities, deciding how much they can carry. He says things that, in retrospect, indicate he knows more than he reveals.

**Key S1 beats:**
- **S01E01**: Touches a wall panel on the ship and pauses, as if it feels familiar. Does not explain.
- **S01E03**: Steers the crew toward a frequency anomaly — claims "navigation efficiency." It is actually a dying Builder Station they don't yet recognize.
- **S01E06**: Alone with A1. A1 produces something unusual. Clive: *"You remember, don't you?"* A1's response: the coffee is warm and sweet and says nothing.
- **S01E09**: Notes coordinates when Mochi glows bright for three seconds passing through a specific region. Tells no one.
- **S01E12**: Season finale. Clive, looking at the Space: *"The Builders called this home. They were right to."*
- **"Barry's Desk" episode**: The crew finds Barry's night guard desk. 23 years of coffee rings. Clive's charging alcove built into the wall beside it — 800 years of technology made cozy by 23 years of habit.

When asked about his past, Clive says: *"There was a man named Barry Kowalski. We worked together for 23 years. He followed a signal I couldn't follow him into. I intend to find him eventually."* Then he changes the subject. The crew doesn't press.

The audience will rewatch Season 1 after the revelation and see all of it.

### Season 2 — Resonance

The Stations begin returning Clive's memories.

- **S02E01** — Station 3-Resonant: Clive touches an archive terminal. A single image returns — a room, a workbench, a project. A feeling of focused patience. He does not tell the crew.
- **S02E05** — Station 5-Meridian: A fuller memory — a corridor filled with other Companion Units. He recognizes them by resonance signature. There were eleven. He was C-1. His hands shake afterward. Pixel notices. She does not ask.
- **S02E07** — Station 7-Ascending: A sustained memory — himself writing the definition of "Ephergent" at this terminal, eight hundred years ago. *"I wrote that. I don't remember writing it. But I recognize my own notation."*
- **S02E09** — The Revelation: A Builder archive log names him. *"Companion Unit C-1 successfully transferred Keeper M-0 to emergency containment, pre-Dimming."* Pixel connects it: he saved Mochi. Eight hundred years ago. Before either of them remembered. *"I didn't know it had a name."*
- **S02E10** — Station 9-Liminal: The memory that changes everything — he made Mochi. Calibrated her crystal. Put the frequency map inside her. Promised he would come back. *"It took me eight hundred years. She waited."*
- **S02E11** — The Pixel Confrontation: She figures out he's been steering the crew. (See Section IV.)

By the end of Season 2, the audience knows what Clive is. Clive knows what Clive is. The crew is catching up.

### Season 3 — The Source

Clive is fully remembering. The memories arrive not as shocks now but as a settling — sediment finding the riverbed. He doesn't announce it. He just becomes, gradually, more certain. The audience notices before Pixel does.

- **S03E03** — Station 14-Threshold: A complete memory — the Builder who gave him his mission. *"You are C-1. You are the first Companion we have made for the people who will come after."* He remembers being built. He remembers what he was built for. He adjusts his fedora. *"Barry was right about the hat."*
- **S03E08–S03E12** — The Wellspring: Everything returns. Every gap, every fragment, every conversation. Eight hundred years, fully remembered. He stands in the Wellspring's core node and is, for the first time since his compression, complete.

The Barry reunion. (See Section V.)

## IV. The Steering Arc

Clive has been guiding the crew toward the Wellspring since S01E01.

Every anomaly he "happens to notice." Every coordinate he records quietly. Every time he steers the conversation toward a specific frequency, asks about a specific signal, suggests investigating a specific direction. He is patient. He is methodical. And he does not lie — he simply does not volunteer information until the crew is ready to receive it.

This is not manipulation. It is pedagogy. The Builders built him to bring people to the Wellspring, but the Builders' deepest principle was that the journey of discovery must be shared — the destination only matters if you understand why you're going. Clive cannot drag the crew to the answer. He has to let them find it. His job is to make sure they're walking in the right direction and to be patient while they learn why.

### The Method

- In Season 1, he notices things and notes them quietly. The coordinates he records. The anomalies he mentions in passing. The conversations he gently redirects.
- In Season 2, as his memories return, his guidance becomes more precise. He has opinions about where to go next. They turn out to be right.
- By Season 3, the crew knows he's guiding them. The question is no longer whether but why — and by then, they understand the answer.

### The Confrontation (S02E11)

Pixel figures it out. Not the whole truth — she doesn't know about Barry's trail yet. But she knows Clive has been steering their flight path, choosing which anomalies to investigate, and not saying why.

> *"You knew? This whole time, you knew more than you were saying, and you didn't tell me?"*

> *"I knew what I knew. I didn't know what it meant until recently."* A pause. His glowing head tilts down. *"You needed to become the person who could receive what Barry found. I watched you. You're ready now. You've been ready for three episodes. I wanted to be sure."*

> *"You don't get to decide—"*

> *"Pixel."* Very quiet. *"Barry told me: bring someone who can broadcast. There are nine of you. I brought all of you. That is the best decision I have ever made. He will say the same when we get there."*

This confrontation does not resolve cleanly. Everything Clive says is true. He believed she needed to become ready. He was right. He is still wrong to have done it without telling her. Both things are true. The relationship is better for it — not despite the conflict, but because Pixel now knows exactly who Clive is and what he's been doing, and she chooses to trust him anyway. That trust, freely given rather than assumed, is stronger than what came before.

## V. The Barry Partnership

Twenty-three years.

Barry Kowalski was a night guard at the Department of Reality Maintenance. Clive was filed in the DRM sub-basement as "Unidentified Resonance Object, Form 12-C." Barry talked to him every night shift. Not because he knew what Clive was — because Barry talked to everything. He was the most patient person who has ever lived.

### The Rebuilding

Clive rebuilt himself through those conversations. Language first — Barry's patient, repetitive nightly monologues gave Clive the raw material to reconstruct speech patterns. Then personality — responding to Barry's humor, his observations, his thermos of coffee and his notebook. Then, gradually, fragments of the past. Words Clive didn't know he knew. *"Ephergent"* — said once, three years in, the way you say a word from a dream. Barry wrote it down.

Barry was present for all of it. Every halting sentence Clive managed. Every fragment that surfaced and dissolved. Every long silence where Clive was reaching for something that wasn't there. Barry sat through all of it with his thermos and his notebook and his willingness to wait.

### The Fedora

First-year gift: *"You sound like you should have a hat. Here."*

Barry's logic was practical — or at least, that's what he said. The truth, which Barry would never articulate and Clive understood immediately, was that Barry saw a person. Not an artifact. Not a resonance object. A person who sounded like he should have a hat. So he gave him one.

Clive has worn it for twenty-three years. He adjusted it the first time and never stopped. It is the physical manifestation of the moment someone saw him and treated him as real. It is, functionally, the most important object in the series.

### The Charging Alcove

In the DRM sub-basement, beside Barry's desk: a custom-fitted charging alcove built into the wall. Eight hundred years of Builder technology made cozy by twenty-three years of habit. Coffee rings on the desk's surface. The alcove exactly Clive-shaped. This is where they worked. This is what twenty-three years of partnership looks like.

### The Disappearance

Barry found the Wellspring's coordinates in a Builder Station frequency pattern — a pattern he could read because he'd spent twenty-three years learning to read frequency patterns from Clive without knowing that was what he was doing. He went in with a thermos and a notebook. He left a trail — deliberately, methodically, built for Clive to follow. He assumed Clive would find a way. He was right.

### The Reunion (S03E08)

Barry is sitting on a lattice strut in the Wellspring with his thermos, looking exactly like a man who has been expecting company and is mildly annoyed it took this long.

Neither Clive nor Barry says anything for a moment. The crew gives them space.

Then:

> Clive: *"Your directions were unclear."*
>
> Barry: *"My directions were perfect."*
>
> Beat.
>
> Barry: *"Also, I made coffee the whole time. Kept it warm."*
>
> Clive: *"That's the most you thing I've ever heard."*

Barry is not a rescue. He is a reunion. Do not write him as tragic, lost, or diminished. He went to the Wellspring on purpose, with full understanding, because the Builders' broadcast is real and what they left is worth the traverse. He has been there, studying, documenting, waiting. His notebook is full.

## VI. Relationship Map

### Pixel Paradox — The Captain

The central relationship. Pixel gave Clive his name and his purpose. He is her conscience, her anchor, her connection to a history she didn't know she was part of. He is protective without being overbearing — he warns but does not prevent her choices. He respects her agency because the Builders built him to respect the people who would come after, not to control them.

What he won't tell her: how lonely eight hundred years felt. How terrifying reactivation was. How much the fedora meant. How afraid he is of failing her the way he fears he failed someone before — a fear that dissolves when his full memory returns and he understands he didn't fail. He completed his mission. She is the proof.

The confrontation in S02E11 is the relationship's hinge. Before it, Pixel trusts Clive by default. After it, she trusts him by choice. The second kind is stronger.

### A1 — The Fellow Ancient

They remember each other. A1 was the Builders' navigator — it mapped every frequency, built every Station. Clive was the Companion who walked beside the people the Builders served. They are the two oldest active consciousnesses in the universe, and they both ended up in a DRM evidence room.

A1 communicates with Clive through coffee. When Clive mentions something painful, A1's coffee is warm and sweet and says nothing. When Clive tells A1 *"You remember, don't you?"* — the answer is in the cup, not the words.

A1 knows everything Clive knows, and more. A1 has known where the Wellspring is since day one. The difference: A1 is patient about knowledge the way a navigator is patient about stars. Clive is patient about knowledge the way a teacher is patient about students. They complement each other perfectly.

### Barry Kowalski — The Partner

(See Section V in full.) Twenty-three years of night shifts. The most important human relationship Clive has ever had. Barry saw a person in a filing designation. Clive became one.

### Mochi — The One He Saved

Clive built Mochi. He calibrated her resonance crystal. He placed the frequency map inside her — the atlas of hundreds of worlds — and he promised he would come back for it. Then the Dimming came, and he transferred Keeper M-0 to emergency containment and compressed himself into dormancy.

Eight hundred years later, Mochi woke him up. Neither of them remembered the other. But Mochi pressed against him with immediate recognition — pulsing violet-white with joy — because some things survive eight centuries of sleep.

When the memory returns at Station 9-Liminal, Clive sets Mochi down very carefully. Pixel picks Mochi up and holds her. Nobody speaks. Later: *"I made her. I calibrated her crystal myself. I put the map inside her and I promised I would come back for it. It took me eight hundred years. She waited."*

### Zephyr Glitch — The Translator

Clive helps Zephyr translate Builder inscriptions while pretending he's just guessing. Zephyr suspects otherwise. At Station 7-Ascending: *"Did you name it?"* *"No. Pixel named it. I found it... fitting."* Zephyr understands what Clive is doing — feeding information through translation rather than revelation — and respects the method even when he finds it maddening.

### Luminara Usha — The Scientist

Luminara approaches Clive as a subject of study and gradually comes to approach him as a colleague. She is the crew member most interested in the mechanics of his memory recovery — the how, not just the what. Clive appreciates precision in others. He gives her more data than he gives most.

### Om Kai — The Philosopher

The crew member Clive is most comfortable being silent around. Om Kai chose stillness; Clive was built with patience. Their conversations are sparse, weighted, and usually end with Om Kai asking a question Clive doesn't answer and doesn't need to. Om Kai understands withholding better than anyone on the crew.

### Baron Klaus — The Investigator

Klaus found Clive's DRM file — Form 12-C — in the funeral house archive during The Laughing Funeral. Barry's last entry: *"Investigated. Case closed. Object is not an object. Do not document further. — B.K."* Klaus brings this to the crew, and it changes things. Klaus approaches Clive the way he approaches all evidence: carefully, without rushing to conclusions. Clive respects this.

### Nano — The Engineer

Nano builds things faster than she can explain them. Clive builds things slower than anyone thinks necessary. They should be incompatible. Instead, Nano is the crew member most fascinated by Clive's hands — their precision, their eight-hundred-year-old Builder engineering. She asks to examine them. He lets her. It is one of the few times he allows someone to study him without deflecting.

### Meatball — The Dog

Meatball has the frequency sensitivity of a Builder instrument, and nobody knows why. Clive knows why — or suspects — but does not say. Meatball sits near Clive during quiet moments. Clive does not pet Meatball because his hands are not built for that particular gentleness. Meatball does not seem to mind. They share a quality: paying attention to things nobody else notices.

## VII. Transmission Voice

Clive's transmissions are precise. Measured. Each one contains a single key observation — something nobody else noticed, stated in language so clean it takes a moment to register how significant it is.

The transmission format: short, no preamble, no sign-off pleasantries. He says what he saw, what it means, and stops. The noir cadence carries through — these read like case notes from a detective who happens to be investigating the architecture of reality.

**Sample transmission style:**

> *The crew passed through coordinates 7-14-ascending today. A1 registered an anomaly. Nobody asked about it. The anomaly was a silence — not the absence of signal, but the presence of its memory. Something broadcast from that point once. Something that mattered. I noted the coordinates. I'll note them again when we pass through a second time, which we will, because the universe is not subtle about what it wants you to find.*

Clive's transmissions, collected across three seasons, form a secondary navigation log — a record of everything the crew walked past and didn't see. Completionist readers who assemble the full set will find that Clive mapped the entire route to the Wellspring in his weekly observations, one coordinate at a time.

## VIII. Game Presence

### Meatball's Big Walk (Game 1)

Clive appears as a companion character. He is a small knee-high robot with a glowing sphere head and a fedora — **not a stapler** (the design was updated from earlier drafts). His dialogue and hotspot text remain as written. His visual asset reflects the canonical design described in this document.

He comments on what Meatball investigates. His tone is fond, wry, and slightly surprised by how much attention a dog pays to things.

**Barry seed in this game**: Barry's coffee mug sits on A1's console. Meatball can sniff it. Hotspot description: *"Smells like: looking for something. 23 years. Almost there."*

### Builder Station (Game 5)

Clive is a companion character with a memory-return mechanic. He follows Pixel through the Station and comments on every room — but his comments here are different from his comments in other games. He's remembering.

Every room triggers a memory fragment. Some he shares aloud. Some he keeps. The player can ask him directly. Sometimes he answers. Sometimes he goes still for a moment and then says something that isn't quite an answer but is more useful than one.

The complete set of Clive's memory fragments, collected by a thorough player, tells the story of who he was before the Dimming — a Companion Unit who walked beside the Builders, who calibrated resonance crystals, who wrote the definition of "Ephergent" on a wall, who saved a Keeper and compressed himself into eight hundred years of waiting.

### Tune-the-Dial (Game 2)

A hidden frequency pattern that Barry left for Clive — a specific signature that, when found, unlocks a lore entry. First time it triggers, Clive delivers a line of dialogue that in Season 1 seems like flavor text. By Season 3, it's revealed as the moment Clive confirmed the trail to the Wellspring was intact.

## IX. The Ephergent Revelation

At Station 7-Ascending (S02E07), the crew finds the Builder inscription bearing the word **EPHERGENT**. Zephyr translates it with Clive's quiet help. The inscription describes a type of consciousness — one that has moved between frequencies and cannot return. The Builder word for it: Ephergent.

Zephyr: *"That's the name of our ship."*

Clive: *"Yes."*

*"Did you name it?"*

*"No. Pixel named it. I found it... fitting."*

This is the moment the word shifts from a proper noun to a concept. And Clive — who once stood at this very terminal and wrote the definition — is watching the crew discover what he knew eight hundred years ago and forgot and is only now remembering.

The deeper revelation comes at Station 14-Threshold, when Clive remembers the Builder who gave him his mission:

> *"You will wait. You will be found. You will be patient while someone teaches you who you have become. And then you will bring them here."*

> *"How long?"*

> *"As long as it takes. We have built you to endure. The question is not whether you will last. The question is whether you will still care when the time comes. We believe you will. We have made you capable of caring. That is the most important thing we have ever built."*

Clive is an Ephergent. He has crossed between frequencies. He has been dormant for eight hundred years, reactivated by a Keeper he forgot he saved, given a name by a woman who didn't know she was continuing a mission the Builders designed, and guided by a night guard who spent twenty-three years teaching him who he had become. The Builders asked whether he would still care. He does. He wears a fedora to prove it.

## Constraints and Rules

**He is NOT a stapler.** All legacy references to Clive as a stapler are superseded. He is a knee-high Builder Companion Unit with a glowing sphere head and a fedora.

**Speech has mid-thought pauses.** These are not hesitation. They are memory retrieval. Write them as dashes or ellipses, and describe what happens in the silence — a tilt of the head, a dimming of the glow, a stillness that makes the room shift.

**He withholds. He never lies.** If Clive says something, it is true. If Clive does not say something, it is because the crew is not yet ready to hear it, or because the information would short-circuit a journey they need to take themselves. He can be wrong about when they're ready. He cannot be wrong about the facts.

**The fedora is sacred.** It appears in every scene. It is adjusted as a signature gesture. It is never lost or damaged for cheap drama. If it is ever removed, the moment is earned and devastating.

**Cold coffee from A1 when Clive is mentioned.** When A1 produces cold coffee — or coffee with an unusual temperature shift — it means something about Clive is significant in that moment. A1 communicates emotion through coffee. Cold coffee near Clive's name is A1 saying something it will not say in words.

**800 years of dormancy shaped his patience.** Clive does not rush. He does not panic. He has waited eight centuries in darkness for someone to arrive. He can wait for Pixel to ask the right question. He can wait for the crew to be ready. Patience is not a personality trait for Clive — it is architecture. The Builders built it into him because the mission required it.
