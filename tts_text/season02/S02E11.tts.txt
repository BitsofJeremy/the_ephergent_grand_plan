The beacon hummed first. Then it screamed.

Not loudly — more like a question asked too many times, the words wearing thin at the edges. The 'A1' had been drifting through empty space near the Shattered Span when the signal hit them: a burst of Builder notation, garbled and confused, resolving into numbers. ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

Pixel felt it before she heard it — a familiar itch at the base of her skull, like déjà vu wearing a trench coat and tipping its fedora. She looked up from her console.

"Oh," she said. "Oh no."

Clive's sphere-head tilted. His fedora — Barry's fedora, the one that meant something even Clive couldn't explain — bobbed with the motion. "What is it, Boss?"

"We've looped."

The words hung there. A1's espresso machine body gave a contemplative whir. Steam curled from somewhere that hadn't been steaming a moment ago. "I say. Pixel, you've lost us. Looped? As in—"

"Retrocausality. The beacon fired a recognition code and I felt it before it happened." Pixel rubbed her temples. "We're stuck in a loop. It resets every time the beacon sends that signal."

Clive's sphere pulsed. 'Click-click-CLICK.' "The dame is always right." He said it flat, noir, every syllable a closed door. "We fly into the beacon. Beacon fires. Loop completes. End of line."

"There's no end," Pixel muttered. "That's the problem."

Mochi — the dome-shaped Builder Companion Device resting on Pixel's console — began to glow. Soft blue first. Then cycling. Blue to green to amber to something that looked almost like a color the human eye had filed under "maybe later."

The beacon screamed again.

The signal hit. ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

Pixel looked up from her console. She had been looking at her console. She had been about to say something.

"We've done this," she said, instead.

"We looped. I recognize this part." Pixel gestured vaguely at the viewport, where the beacon pulsed its confused signal into the void. "I felt it before it happened. Again."

A1's espresso machine body gave a different kind of whir this time — thinner, less engaged. Steam wisped from a seam that shouldn't have been steaming. "I say. That's rather inconvenient. What's the reset trigger?"

"The beacon. Every time it fires, we loop." Pixel leaned back. "We need to figure out how to send the signal backward."

Clive's sphere pulsed. 'Click-CLICK.' Frustration this time, sharper. "The dame is always right. Beacon fires. Beacon loops. We are back at start. End of line."

Mochi glowed. Blue to green to amber to that maybe-later color. Faster now. His dome was cycling like a compass that had forgotten which way was north.

The beacon screamed again.

The signal hit. ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

Pixel looked up from her console. She had been looking at her console. She had been about to say—

"We've looped," she said, with the exhaustion of someone who had said this before. "Third time."

Clive's fedora was hard-left now. Anger-tilt. His sphere-head followed the motion like it had no choice. "What is it, Boss?"

"The beacon does not know better." Clive's sphere pulsed. 'CLICK-CLICK-CLICK.' "The beacon is confused. The beacon is looping. The beacon is asking the same question over and over and getting the same answer. End of line."

"What question?" Pixel asked.

Clive's fedora tilted further. "I am here. I am still here. Recognition code. That is what it means. 111 217. Frequency Recognition." His sphere dimmed slightly. "The beacon is lost. The beacon is trying to find itself. The beacon is asking the void to answer."

Mochi was cycling faster now — every color in his limited palette and several that seemed to be borrowed from adjacent palettes. His warmth was fluctuating too. Pixel pulled her hand back from his dome.

"He's breaking down," she said. "The loop is degrading his calibration."

The beacon screamed again.

The signal hit. ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

Pixel looked up from her console. She had been looking at her console. She had been—

"Oh, we've looped." She said it casual now. Almost friendly. "We've done this. Multiple times. Clive, you're going to say your thing."

Clive's fedora was hard-left angry. His sphere-head tilted in a way that suggested he was looking at Pixel sideways even though he had no eyes. "What is it, Boss?"

"That's your line. You've said it four times now." Pixel checked something on her console — something she'd been checking for four loops. "I need you to say something different."

Clive's sphere pulsed. 'CLICK-CLICK.' Agreement, almost. "The dame is always right. Four times. Same line. Same loop. End of line."

"See, that's—" Pixel sighed. "That's the problem. You're trapped in the loop's script. We're all trapped in the loop's script."

A1's coffee had gone burnt. Dark. Bitter. Like the beans themselves had given up on being coffee. "I say. Rather inconvenient, this loop."

Mochi was cycling through every color he had. Practically a disco ball now.

"Everything is lost." Clive's sphere dimmed. "Loop. Beacon. Recognition code. The beacon is asking the same question. We are all asking the same question. Same answer. End of line."

"What if—" Pixel stopped. Stared at the beacon's signal readout on her console. "What if the question is the problem?"

The beacon screamed again.

The signal hit. ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

Pixel looked up from her console. She had stopped counting loops after the fifth. They blurred together like coffee rings on a console that had seen too much.

"We've looped," she said. Then: "Wait."

Clive's fedora was no longer hard-left. It was somehow sideways — a new angle, an unprecedented tilt. His sphere-head tracked Pixel like she was the most interesting thing in the room. Possibly the most interesting thing in the last seven loops. "What is it, Boss?"

"The beacon isn't just looping. It's broadcasting." Pixel pulled up the signal analysis on her viewport. "Look. It's sending its recognition code OUT. Into the void. It's trying to find other beacons, other Builders, something that can answer its question."

"111 217," A1 said. His espresso machine body made a contemplative grinding sound. "Frequency Recognition. I am here. I am still here. Rather lonely, one might infer. The beacon is lost and calling for rescue."

"But we're caught in its call," Pixel said. "Every time it broadcasts, we're in range. Every broadcast resets us. It's not trying to trap us—" She grinned. It was the grin of someone who had just figured out the last line of a joke. "It's asking us to answer."

Clive's sphere pulsed. 'Click-click.' Attention. "The beacon wants a response."

"It wants someone to tell it back." Pixel stood up. "It sent 111 217 forward and got stuck. What if we need to send it backward?"

Silence. Even Mochi stopped cycling — his dome settling into a pale, uncertain glow, like he'd finally been asked a question he wanted to answer.

"Broadcast backward," A1 said slowly. "Into the past. Retrocausality through response rather than prevention. Rather clever."

"It won't work," Clive said. Then, softer: "Unless it does. The dame is always right."

"Not yet," Pixel said. "We need all three of us. The beacon recognizes frequencies. If we broadcast together — my signal, A1's resonance, Clive's click — back through the loop—"

"We confuse the beacon less," A1 finished. "We become the answer the beacon is seeking. Rather like teaching a child to say its own name by saying it back."

Mochi pulsed. A warm, steady gold. The warmth carried meaning that bypassed language — I am here, I am still here, the way a sunset carries the fact of tomorrow.

Pixel smiled. "Let's confuse this thing."

They did it together.

Pixel sent her signal — years of broadcasting, years of telling stories, years of 'I am here' pressed into a frequency that carried. A1's espresso machine body resonated with something older than coffee, something that had been built into him before he knew what building meant. Clive's sphere pulsed in perfect triplicate: 'click-click-CLICK'.

The beacon received.

For one terrible moment, nothing happened.

Then: ''111 217 — Frequency Recognition.''

But different. Not a scream this time. Not a question. A statement.

'I am here.'

The loop broke. The 'A1' drifted forward through space — actually forward, not backward, not reset — and the beacon's light steadied into something that looked almost like gratitude.

Pixel sat down. "We did it."

"That was rather impressive," A1 admitted. His espresso machine body gave a contented gurgle. The coffee in his cup was rich again, complex, full. "I say. My calibration has quite recovered."

Clive's fedora settled into its normal tilt. His sphere glowed steady blue-white. "Case closed," he said. "The dame was right. The beacon was lonely. End of line."

Mochi pulsed gold, then settled into a calm, quiet green.

Pixel laughed. "Okay. That was weird. We should dock somewhere and maybe never talk about this again."

They docked at a small station near the Nexus. Nothing fancy — a fuel stop, a place where travelers didn't ask questions and signals didn't misfire.

Pixel filed her report. Then deleted it. Then filed a different report about a "routine beacon calibration" that made them sound competent and professional and not at all like they had been stuck in a time loop for what felt like seven iterations of the same fourteen minutes.

"The official story," she announced, "is that we helped a lost beacon find its frequency. Very heroic. Very competent."

"The unofficial story," Clive said, "is better."

"The unofficial story is off the record." Pixel smiled. "Some stories are too weird even for the Signal."

A1's espresso machine body hummed. "I say. That was rather fun. Shall we do it again never?"

"I'll take 'never' for a hundred, Alex."

Mochi pulsed pink — warm, content, 'here' — and settled into Pixel's pocket like he belonged there.

Somewhere in the void, a beacon hummed. Not screaming anymore. Just saying:

'I am here.'

And this time, someone answered.
