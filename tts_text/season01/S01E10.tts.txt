Bureaucracies need paperwork. Even interdimensional ones.

The Ephergent had navigated into a frequency that was nothing but filing cabinets. Endless rows of them, stretching toward a horizon that kept recomposing itself every time someone blinked. Metal drawers labeled in six languages, none of which contained anything that looked like an exit. The air tasted like toner and existential exhaustion.

Clive's sphere-head pulsed a slow blue-white as he surveyed the landscape. "Civilization's gotta have rules, Boss. Problem is when the rules are the whole point."

"Transportation Authorization Forms available here." The vending machine materialized from between two cabinets like it had been waiting since the last ice age. Big red letters. Dull chrome body. A slot that looked like it could swallow whole documents. "Emotional Processing Tokens accepted. Four tokens per form."

Pixel checked her pockets. Mochi pulsed a soft, questioning warmth from her jacket.

"I've got... grief tokens from the Verdanya job," Pixel said. "And maybe some existential dread from the Clockwork Unraveling?"

"Those are vendor-specific. This machine requires Emotional Processing Tokens. Standard issue." The machine's display flickered. "Current balance: zero."

Arc's voice came through the ship's comm. British. Concerned. "Pixel, I've been analyzing this frequency's structural composition. It's a bureaucratic attractor state. Forms generate other forms. The machine you're addressing is likely a processing hub for cross-frequency transit authorization."

"So we're stuck here?"

"Not necessarily stuck. Merely administratively encumbered."

Pixel stared at the machine. Transportation Authorization Forms. Four tokens. Sixty forms a dozen.

The Grabovoi codes came from Barry's notes — and Pixel knew one. The code for unexpected money: 520 741 8. Spaced properly, Barry had written. The spaces are the architecture. The numbers are just the payload.

Pixel closed her eyes. Concentrated. Whispered the code.

Five. Two. Zero. Seven. Four. One. Eight.

The machine gave a sound like a camera shutter having a stroke. Sparks showered from its currency slot.

Something was dropping. Metal, clinking, overwhelming.

Pixel opened her eyes.

Tokens. Everywhere. Stacked in neat columns. Piled in leaning towers. Two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven Emotional Processing Tokens, each one stamped with a tiny serial number and a government seal from a department that hadn't existed since the Second Structural Collapse.

Arc's voice sharpened. "Pixel. Your token count appears to be... excessive."

The machine shuddered. Its display went red.

OVERFLOW WARNING. EMOTIONAL PROCESSING CAPACITY EXCEEDED. AUDITOR DISPATCHED.

"Auditor?" Pixel said.

The air tore open. Literally. Like reality had a paper jam. A figure stepped through — tall, grey, carrying a stamp that looked heavy enough to invalidate a planet. It wore spectacles and the expression of someone who'd been processing forms since before the Builders left.

"UNEXPECTED ASSET DECLARATION," it said. "REQUIRED: FORM 27B / QUANTITY: 2,847 / PENALTY: PROCESSING FEE."

Clive tipped his fedora. "Look, Boss. The machine will require therapy after that."

Mochi pulsed warm against Pixel's chest — sympathetic, confused, slightly amused.

"I'm not paying a processing fee on tokens I didn't even ask for!"

The auditor raised its stamp. " APPEAL PROCESS AVAILABLE. FILL FORM 27B."

"I need Form 27B to get the form to appeal the fee?"

"NEXT WINDOW: 4-6 BUSINESS HOURS."

Clive's sphere pulsed. Click-click-click. "Kid. You whisper the code right, it gives you money. You whisper it wrong?" He gestured at the tower of tokens. "You get the money's evil twin. That's what bureaucracy is — the mirror version of what you asked for."

Arc's coffee came through the comm — bitter, worried. "Pixel, Clive's metaphor is distressingly accurate. You've essentially declared a small fortune in emotional currency to a system that runs on emotional currency. It's like announcing you're carrying contraband to a customs official."

The auditor stamped something. A token exploded.

"SIR," Pixel said to the auditor, "I'm trying to get transportation authorization."

"FORM 27B."

"I DON'T HAVE FORM 27B."

"AVAILABLE FROM VENDING UNIT." The auditor gestured at the machine, which was still sparking. "PURCHASE WITH EMOTIONAL PROCESSING TOKENS."

"I HAVE TOKENS. TWO THOUSAND OF THEM."

"PLEASE DEPOSIT."

The machine's slot opened. Waiting.

Pixel looked at Clive. He looked at her.

"Four tokens per form," Clive said. "You got almost three grand in tokens. That's a lot of forms, Boss."

The auditor loomed.

Pixel fed the machine one token. Then another. Then another.

Form after form printed. Transportation Authorization Forms. Each one approved in advance. Each one signed by someone who'd never existed.

By token five hundred, the auditor had relaxed. By token a thousand, it had left. By token two thousand, the machine had started humming a sound that almost resembled contentment.

When Pixel finished, she had transportation authorization for the next twelve frequencies. Stamps on every page. Signatures in triplicate. A note from the department of "Yes, Actually."

Clive watched the last form print. "Most beings request assistance. You declare a fortune and receive bureaucracy in return."

Arc's coffee thinned. Exhausted. "The Spaces, Pixel. You must respect the spaces."

Mochi pulsed warm. Almost laughing.

Pixel gathered her stack of approved forms, shoved two thousand unused tokens into her pocket, and walked toward the frequency's exit — which, now that the paperwork was in order, had materialized exactly where it should be.

Behind her, the vending machine settled into a satisfied hum.

Somewhere in the Space, bureaucracy had won a small victory.

But the Ephergent had its transit forms.

And that was enough.
