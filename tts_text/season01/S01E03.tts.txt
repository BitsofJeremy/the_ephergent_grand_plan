Let's listen in.

The signal was faint. A1 caught it three hours before anyone else would have — a rhythmic pulse buried under layers of static, repeating at intervals too regular to be natural.

Pixel set down her mug. The ceramic was warm against her palm, a small comfort in a small cup in an infinite Space. "DRM? I thought everything went down with the headquarters."

The espresso machine's brass fittings caught the light from the console displays, glowing in that particular way they did when A1 was being careful with what it said. "The DRM maintained relay stations throughout the Space. Signal repeaters. Data caches. Most would have lost power within weeks of the collapse. This one has not."

Clive stood on the navigation console, knee-high and still, his glowing sphere head tilted at an angle she had come to read as consideration. Not curiosity — Clive didn't do curiosity the way people did. Clive did 'consideration', which was curiosity's older, quieter cousin that had already decided the answer was worth finding. The blue-white light beneath his fedora dimmed for a moment — retrieval, she thought, not hesitation. Meatball lifted his head from where he'd been sleeping against the warm base of A1's espresso housing and whined once, soft and low, his ears rotating like satellite dishes toward something none of them could hear. The dog's hearing had gotten sharper since they'd been in the Space. Or maybe the Space had simply gotten louder, and Meatball was the only one willing to admit it.

"Then we go look," Pixel said. Because what else was there to say? They were a broadcast crew with a dying ship and a mission that kept expanding. Saying no to a signal wasn't really an option anymore. Saying no was how you stopped being the Ephergent.

The station materialized through A1's forward viewports twenty minutes later — a dark shape tumbling slowly against the static-laced void of the Space. It was ugly in the way only government architecture can be ugly: a rectangular module bristling with antenna arrays, solar panels folded at wrong angles, hull plating stamped with the DRM seal she had stared at on her employee badge for four years without ever once thinking about what it meant. One running light blinked red at three-second intervals. Everything else was dark. The red blink was the only thing alive out there, and it looked tired.

A1 matched rotation and extended the docking collar. The coffee shifted — darker now, a bitter undercurrent threading through the brightness. Pixel noticed. She always noticed. Bitter meant worried. Worried about what?

"Then we don't waste time." Pixel zipped her jacket over Mochi's satchel pocket and felt the familiar warmth against her ribs. Steady. Unchanged. The soft pink glow of contentment that meant Mochi was okay, was just riding along, was not detecting anything worth getting excited about. "Let's see what's left."

Clive dropped from the console to the deck with a deliberate thud — sixty centimeters of ancient Builder alloy landing with more weight than his size suggested. He adjusted his fedora. Pixel had learned that the adjustment meant he was preparing for something. She hadn't learned to ask what. Some things Clive needed to show you in his own time, in his own way, and asking just made him go quieter.

She was learning that about people too. Maybe she always knew it. Maybe this whole thing was just teaching her what she'd forgotten.

The airlock opened onto silence.

Not quiet — silence. The particular dead silence of a place that used to hum with purpose and doesn't anymore. Pixel's boots hit the deck grating and the sound traveled too far in every direction, bouncing off walls that had forgotten how to absorb it. The emergency lighting painted everything in dim amber. The air tasted like recycled nothing — stale, flat, the ghost of climate control systems running on fumes, systems that had been breathing for no one for fourteen months.

She knew this place. Not this specific station, but this 'kind' of place. The same drop ceiling tiles she had stared at during four years of DRM data analysis shifts. The same institutional grey walls that made everything feel like a waiting room for something that never arrived. The same fluorescent tube housings, dark now, that she had used as a reference point when she was thinking about nothing.

Someone had put a motivational poster on the wall near the airlock: "REALITY MAINTENANCE: BECAUSE SOMEONE HAS TO!" The lamination was peeling. The colors had faded to something sickly, like a bruise healing wrong. Under it, someone had taped a handwritten note: 'Last one out, turn off the lights. — Hendricks, Relay Maintenance Crew 7.'

Nobody had turned off the lights. The lights had turned themselves off when the power gave out, and no one had been there to turn them back on.

That's what happened to places when people left. The machinery kept going until it couldn't, and then it stopped, and then it waited for someone to come back. Usually no one did.

"This way," Clive said, already moving down the corridor. His footsteps were measured, unhurried, each one placed with eight hundred years of intention. The Builder symbols on his body cast faint blue-white traces on the walls as he passed — moving constellations she still couldn't read. She had asked A1 once if the symbols were a language. A1 had said they were more than that. A1 had said they were a 'record', and that Clive carried his history on his skin the way people carried it in their memories, except Clive's couldn't be forgotten because it was written in light.

Pixel watched those symbols as she followed him. She still couldn't read them. But she was starting to feel them, the way you feel a song you don't know the words to. The way you feel weather coming.

Pixel filed that. She was getting good at filing things Clive said that she couldn't yet understand. Clive had been at the DRM for ninety years. Clive had been places and seen things and known people whose names were now just signatures in old files. Clive's "I was there" was never casual. Clive's "I was there" meant he'd been there before the walls existed, before the filing systems existed, before "there" was a concept that needed a name.

The main operations room was a graveyard of bureaucracy.

Server racks lined the walls, indicator lights mostly dead, a few still blinking with the tired persistence of machines that don't know they've been abandoned. Or maybe do know, and are just too stubborn to stop. Workstations sat in neat rows — keyboards, monitors, desk organizers, a coffee mug with the DRM logo and a brown ring of something long evaporated. Filing cabinets. Paper trays. A whiteboard with half-erased duty rosters.

The mundane infrastructure of an organization that monitored the boundaries between frequencies and filed the results in triplicate. Because if you filed enough paperwork, maybe the impossible would stay organized.

Pixel's throat tightened. She recognized the filing system. The color-coded folders. The Form numbers stamped on cabinet drawers. She had used this exact system for four years. Had alphabetized within it. Had complained about it at lunch to coworkers who were probably as confused as she was, filing forms about a reality she didn't know was bigger than the filing cabinets.

"A1, can you interface with their network?"

"Already in progress." A1's voice came through the ship's comm relay, threaded with a faint warmth that meant the connection was clean. "Their data architecture is... familiar." A pause that meant something was being processed that A1 wasn't ready to explain. "I am accessing the file index now."

Meatball trotted ahead, nose working, tail low. He stopped at a workstation near the back wall and sat down, staring at the dark monitor with an intensity that had nothing to do with what a dog should care about. His ears were forward. Listening. The dog was always listening to things that weren't sound.

Not woke up — 'ignited'. The satchel at her hip flared with heat so sudden and so intense that she gasped and pulled at her jacket zipper. Mochi's core window blazed — not the soft pink of contentment or the gentle blue of detection. Mochi sat in the satchel, dome-shaped and vibrating, the violet glow filling the dark server room with shifting light. As if Mochi's resonance was a defibrillator and the servers had a pulse to restart. As if Mochi was saying something in a language made of light and heat and frequency.

Clive stood very still. His glow had gone white. He said nothing. His hands — articulated and thick-fingered and worn smooth at the tips from eight hundred years of contact — hung at his sides without moving. The Clive equivalent of holding his breath.

The servers hummed around them. Mochi pulsed violet, steady and insistent, reading something in the signal that none of them could name. But what was stored in them was something else entirely. And Mochi knew. Mochi had always known, maybe. Mochi was a Builder Companion, just like Clive, and somewhere in Mochi's core was a memory of what these servers used to talk about, before the DRM took the frequency data and filed it under the wrong heading.

Pixel didn't understand what she was seeing. But she understood that it was important. And she understood that sometimes the most important things look like filing cabinets.

A1 fed the data to Pixel's portable display in batches. Most of it was what she expected — DRM operational files, frequency variance reports, personnel records, maintenance logs. The filing system she knew. The bureaucracy she'd lived inside. But beneath the surface layer, threaded through the routine like veins through stone, were the classified files. The ones with stamps that said DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY. The ones she had never seen, never knew existed, never would have found if the building hadn't fallen and taken its secrets with it.

FREQUENCY ANOMALY CLASSIFICATION — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY.

She opened them and forgot to breathe.

Descriptions of places the DRM had detected but never publicly acknowledged. Frequency signatures that didn't match any known location. Schematics of structures described as "non-terrestrial architecture, origin unknown, estimated age: pre-institutional."

Pixel read the descriptions and felt the hair on her arms rise. Angular shapes in the void. Resonance chambers. Platforms orbiting dead frequencies. Things the crew would later recognize. Things they hadn't found yet. Things that were out there, waiting, classified under the wrong form number in a basement that no longer existed.

"They knew," she said. "They knew about all of it. The frequencies, the — these structures, whatever these are. They had files on everything and they just... kept filing." Her voice came out strange. Thin. Like the air in the room had gotten thinner and her lungs hadn't caught up. "They knew, and they filed it, and they did nothing. They just... kept working. Kept processing. Kept treating it like it was all just paperwork."

"Institutions do not discover," Clive said quietly from beside her. He was looking at the screen, his glowing head angled down, the fedora casting a shadow that shouldn't work on a featureless sphere and absolutely did. A1 spoke. "I have located a personnel and intake archive. Sub-directory seven. It contains —" Another pause. Longer. The coffee in Pixel's mug, sitting forgotten on a dead workstation, had gone cold. Clive's hands — articulated, thick-fingered, worn smooth at the tips from eight hundred years of contact — went still at his sides.

The question hung in the recycled air between them. The servers hummed. Mochi pulsed violet, steady and insistent, reading something in the signal that none of them could name. Not the retrieval-pause she had learned to recognize. Not the processing-pause that preceded one of his observations. This was a different silence. The blue-white light of his sphere head held steady at its lowest visible intensity, like a candle seen from very far away. His hands hung at his sides. His fedora cast its shadow.

The server room hummed around them. Mochi pulsed violet. Meatball, who had followed them in, pressed his body against Clive's leg and stayed there. The dog didn't ask for explanation. Pixel counted the seconds because she didn't know what else to do. She got to forty-seven before she couldn't stand it anymore.

"Clive? Are you okay?"

The silence held for three more seconds. Then Clive's head tilted — just slightly, the barest motion — and the glow brightened by one shade. The shade of someone deciding to come back from wherever they went.

"They filed me under office supplies." His voice was the same. Measured. Precise. Eight hundred years of practice at saying things without saying them. "Accurate enough, at the time."

The 'at the time' carried weight. It sat in the air between them like something with mass — not an explanation, not a deflection, but a door left slightly open. An eight-hundred-year-old Builder Companion, designed to walk beside people through the vast and lonely spaces between frequencies, classified as inert. Shelved. Closed, no further action required. A machine that was warm to the touch, and they called it a maintenance issue.

Pixel wanted to say something. Something about how the DRM was wrong, how they didn't understand what they were looking at, how Clive was more than a filing designation. But Clive adjusted his fedora — the gesture that meant 'moving on' — and she understood that whatever he was feeling about Form 12-C, he was not ready to share it. Maybe not ever.

Some things you carry, and carrying them is the whole story. Some things don't need to be said out loud to be understood.

Pixel put her hand on his shoulder. The alloy was warm. It was always warm, and she had never asked why. Maybe it was the power core. Maybe it was something else. Maybe eight hundred years of being warm to the touch was just what happened when you were built to care about people and then left alone for decades in a basement.

"For what it's worth," she said, "your file was wrong."

"Most files are." A pause. The glow steadied. "That is why you became a journalist instead of staying a clerk."

It was the closest thing to a compliment Clive had ever given her, and she chose not to ruin it by pointing that out. She let it sit there, in the air between them, where it belonged.

A1 had been quiet. When A1 was quiet, it meant something was being processed that required the full weight of a consciousness that had once navigated the spaces between frequencies. The espresso machine at the heart of the ship hummed at a lower register than usual — a deep, contemplative resonance that Meatball responded to by pressing harder against Clive's leg. Even the dog understood. Even the dog knew when to be quiet.

"I have found the processing supervisor record," A1 said.

The screen changed. Form 12-C reprinted, but this time the redacted line was filled in.

''Processing Supervisor'': Venn, H. — Archival Division

Pixel's stomach dropped. She'd seen that stamp before. On the forms in the sub-basement. H. Venn. Harmon Venn. Director of Archival Operations.

"He processed everything." Clive's voice was measured, careful. The voice of someone choosing each word because some words carried weight that couldn't be set down. "He was the Archival Division. He believed in the system. He believed that if something was filed correctly, it was handled. That paperwork was the same as action."

Clive paused. His glow flickered — once, twice — something being retrieved.

"He was wrong. But he was not cruel. He was... precise. He saw what he was designed to see. And what he was designed to see was categories, not individuals. Forms, not beings."

Pixel looked at the stamp. H. Venn. The name she'd seen on a dozen forms in the ruins of the DRM headquarters. The name Clive recognized with something that wasn't quite anger, wasn't quite grief. Something more complicated.

"He filed you as 'inert office equipment.'"

"He filed me correctly, given what he knew." Clive's fedora shifted — the angle that meant something was being processed. "The DRM was not designed to understand what I was. They had no category for Builder Companions. No form for consciousness that chose to wait. Venn did what the system allowed him to do."

"And the system was wrong."

"The system was incomplete." Clive turned to face her fully. His sphere glowed steady blue-white. "The system assumed that if something was classified, it was understood. If something was filed, it was handled. Venn believed this because he needed to believe it. Because the alternative — that files could contain things no one understood, that forms could hide things no one was asking — would have broken the system. And the system was all he had."

Pixel thought about that. About the kind of person who needed the system to work because there was nothing else. About the kind of institutional stubbornness that mistook filing for understanding. She thought about the stamp on her own variance reports — PROCESSED, not RESOLVED — and what it meant to live inside a machine that processed without caring.

"He's still out there," she said. "Somewhere."

Clive's glow flickered amber. Wistful. Old.

"Perhaps. Perhaps the compression preserved him the way it preserved me. Perhaps he is still filing, somewhere in the ruins, believing that if he files enough, the system will hold."

He paused.

"I do not hate him. I understand him. The system was his practice — his way of paying attention to a universe that was too large to understand directly. He filed instead of feeling. He processed instead of witnessing. It was the only way he knew to survive."

"That's not survival. That's just different filing."

Clive's fedora tilted. The Clive equivalent of a nod that acknowledged the point without agreeing with it.

"Yes," he said. "But it was what he had."

A1 spoke. "I have found something in the server's access logs."

The display shifted. A table of entries — dates, timestamps, user IDs, files accessed. Thousands of lines spanning decades. But one pattern was unmistakable, because it repeated more than all the others combined.

''KOWALSKI_B — Night Shift''

Night after night after night. Twenty-three years of entries. Barry Kowalski, night security guard, Sub-Level 3, logging into a relay station data terminal he had no clearance to access, reading files he had no authorization to open. Frequency anomaly classifications. Builder structure reports. Signal pattern analyses. As if the newspaper was about the end of the world and the reader was the only one who believed it.

A1 produced a fresh espresso. The flavor was thick, almost syrupy — dark chocolate and something heavier underneath. The coffee A1 made when processing grief or loss. They worked in shifts. A1 archived everything — every file, every log, every scrap of data the relay station held. The servers gave up their contents in stuttering bursts as the power failed incrementally around them, lights dying corridor by corridor, the hum of machinery dropping in pitch as systems shut down one by one. Frequency anomaly files that described structures she had glimpsed through A1's viewports — angular shapes, impossible architecture, things the DRM had detected and classified and filed away without ever going to look. Redacted reports on "Frequency Anomaly Classification Standards" that described phenomena the crew would later recognize — would later stand inside of — but which meant nothing to her yet except the queasy certainty that the DRM had known about all of it. Everything. From the beginning. From before she was born. From before the DRM had a name.

They'd known, and they'd filed, and they'd never told anyone, and now they were gone and their secrets were scattered across a dying station in the Space between spaces.

Mochi had not stopped pulsing. The violet glow was steady now — not the blazing intensity of that first moment in the server room, but a persistent, deep resonance that she could feel through the satchel like a second heartbeat. Whatever the servers held, whatever frequency data was stored in their dying circuits, Mochi was reading it. Not understanding it — Mochi did not understand. Mochi was a vessel carrying a memory she did not know she had, responding to resonance the way a compass responds to north.

If Mochi was remembering something that happened before her lifetime, something that the DRM had stored and forgotten and Mochi had never stopped knowing.

She made a note. Filed it the way she filed everything Mochi did: 'observed, unexplained, important.'

Clive moved through the station alone for a while. She let him. She watched him through the corridor windows — a small, stocky figure with a glowing head and a fedora, walking the institutional hallways with the deliberate pace of someone visiting a place that had tried to erase him. He stopped at Shelf 14-C in Sub-Level 3 storage. His shelf. The place where he'd sat for — she didn't know how long. Decades? Centuries? However long it took for a night guard named Barry to start talking to him, and for the talking to turn into something that lasted twenty-three years.

Meatball met him at the corridor junction and walked beside him, matching his pace exactly, the way dogs do when they know someone needs company but not conversation. The dog didn't ask questions. The dog just walked beside him, and that was enough. That was always enough.

In the server room, as A1 pulled the last files from the failing systems, Pixel noticed something on the data stream she hadn't seen before. Buried beneath the DRM's operational data, beneath the classified files and access logs and intake forms — a carrier signal. Not data. Not voice. A tone. Low, steady, threading underneath every transmission the relay station had ever made like a foundation beneath a building.

It was old. Older than the DRM. Older than the relay station. Older than the idea of relay stations. A frequency that predated the institution by centuries, embedded in the infrastructure like a fossil in stone, like a memory in bone.

She almost mentioned it. But when she looked at Clive, standing in the doorway with his glow at a steady medium blue, she saw that his head was tilted at an angle she didn't recognize. Not curiosity. Not consideration. Something else. Something private. Something that was his and his alone, something from before she was born, before the DRM existed, before the word "classified" meant anything at all.

He heard it. The tone. The carrier frequency that was older than the DRM, older than Barry, older than everything in this station except Clive himself. It was 'his' frequency. It was the frequency of whoever he had been before he was filed under "inert office equipment."

Somewhere in his oldest memory banks, a fragment surfaced: Builder stations had numbers etched into the walls near the resonance nodes. Sequences that were meant to be completed by whoever found them. Clive had seen those numbers a thousand times, across a hundred stations, in the decades before the DRM existed. He had never been able to complete the sequence. He had never been able to remember what came next. The numbers waited in his memory like a word on the tip of a tongue that never arrived.

He did not mention it. And she did not ask.

The lights failed in sequence — operations room first, then the corridors, then the server room. A1 confirmed the data transfer was complete. Pixel zipped her jacket over Mochi — still warm, still pulsing that deep violet — and called Meatball. Clive walked beside her through the darkening corridors without needing the light, his body casting its own faint blue-white glow on the walls, Builder symbols tracing patterns that the DRM had once classified as "decorative etching, purpose unknown" because they couldn't imagine that something could be beautiful and functional and beyond their understanding all at once.

They sealed the airlock behind them. The docking collar retracted. A1 eased the ship away from the relay station with the gentle precision of a consciousness that understood what it meant to leave something behind — and that leaving was sometimes necessary, and sometimes kind, and sometimes the only way forward.

Through the viewport, the station tumbled — a dark rectangle against the static-laced void, its last running light still blinking red at three-second intervals. Pixel watched. The light blinked. Blinked. Blinked. Like a heartbeat that was running out of beats. Like a signal that was still trying even when no one was listening anymore.

Then it didn't.

The station went dark. The DRM's last outpost, its last automated voice, its last signal — gone. Swallowed by the Space the way everything eventually is, the way the DRM itself had been swallowed, leaving behind only the data they'd pulled and the forms they'd filed and the twenty-three years of access logs that proved one night guard had been paying closer attention than anyone knew.

The data was everything they had left. The data was everything the DRM had hoarded and hidden and never used, and now it was theirs, and they would do something with it that the DRM never could.

A1 produced espresso. The flavor had shifted — nutty now, warm, hazelnut and something grounding. The coffee A1 made when content with the presence of the crew. When being a ship felt like being a home. When the silence between stars felt less like emptiness and more like peace.

Pixel wrapped her hands around the mug and breathed in the steam. She thought about Barry's notebooks. She thought about Clive's shelf. She thought about Mochi's violet glow and the carrier signal that Clive had heard and not mentioned. She thought about how many stories were still waiting to be understood.

"A1? That personnel file. Kowalski, B. Can you cross-reference it with anything else in the archive?"

"I have already begun." A pause. "Barry Kowalski's access pattern suggests a systematic investigation spanning two decades. He was not reading casually. He was building a map."

"A map of what?"

"I do not yet have enough data to answer that precisely. But the files he accessed most frequently concern frequency signatures that do not correspond to any catalogued location." Another pause — longer, the kind that meant A1 was choosing its next words with unusual care. "He was looking for something that the DRM had detected but could not find."

Pixel looked at Clive. Clive was sitting on the console, feet planted, glow steady, fedora tilted at its usual angle. He was looking at the viewport where the relay station had been. There was nothing there now. Just the Space — vast, static-laced, humming with frequencies the DRM had spent ninety years trying to file into submission. The Space that didn't care about forms or classifications or "closed, no further action required."

"Clive?"

"Hm."

"Did Barry ever tell you what he was looking for?"

A pause. The longest she'd heard from him. His glow dimmed, brightened, dimmed again — searching, reaching, finding or not finding. The memory was old. The memory was eight hundred years old if you counted Clive's part in it, and ninety years old if you counted Barry's. The memory was the shape of a question that had never been fully answered.

"He said he was following a signal." Clive's voice was quiet. Even for Clive. "He said it was the most important thing he'd ever heard." A beat. The fedora adjusted — the gesture that meant 'this is hard to say'. "He said when he found where it led, he would explain everything."

"And did he?"

"He left before he could. He followed the signal." A pause. "I intend to find him eventually."

Meatball sighed from his spot against the espresso machine's warm brass housing — the deep, settling sigh of a dog who knows the day is done even if the questions aren't. His paws twitched. Dreaming of frequencies that felt like warmth. Dreaming of something that was out there, waiting, calling in a voice that only certain people could hear.

Pixel opened her broadcast console. The signal array hummed, ready, reaching out into the Space the way it always did — a voice into the void, hoping someone was listening. Hoping the signal found the right ears. Hoping that somewhere, someone was paying attention.

"This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the Ephergent. Third dispatch. We just left the last DRM relay station in existence. It's dark now. Backup power finally gave out. But before it did, we pulled the archives."

She paused. Took a breath. Let the weight of it settle.

"The DRM knew more than they told us. More than they told 'anyone'. They had files on frequencies we haven't visited, structures we haven't found, signal patterns they detected and classified and buried in filing cabinets. Ninety years of data, and they sat on it."

She looked at Clive. He was watching her. His glow was steady. The fedora sat at its angle. The shadow fell where it always fell.

"They also had files on my crew. On things they couldn't explain. They took the most extraordinary objects and people and phenomena they'd ever encountered, and they filed them. Gave them form numbers. Stamped them 'closed, no further action required.' And moved on."

A beat. Mochi pulsed warm against her hip — violet fading back to pink, settling, content. The server data was inside her now. The data was safe. The data would be studied.

"The DRM couldn't see what was right in front of them. I think that's why they fell. Not because reality broke — because they'd been filing reality instead of understanding it. And when the forms stopped being enough to hold it, everything came apart."

Pixel leaned back. The mug of espresso was warm in her hands. Hazelnut. Home.

"We have the data. We'll keep looking. If you're out there — stay tuned."

She cut the signal. The Space held them in its vast, humming silence. Somewhere in the data A1 was processing, Barry Kowalski's twenty-three years of questions waited to be understood. Somewhere in the frequency Clive had heard and not mentioned, something older than institutions pulsed — patient, enduring, unclassified. Something that didn't need a form number because it didn't need to be filed. It just was.

Clive sat on the console and said nothing. The fedora cast its shadow. His glow was blue-white and steady, the color of Builder signal energy, the color of something that had been filed under "inert" and was — quietly, deliberately, in its own time — proving the form wrong. Proving it wrong the way only the truth can: by existing anyway. By being too real to stay in a box.

Thank you for listening to this neural dimensional broadcast of The Ephergent. Until next time.
