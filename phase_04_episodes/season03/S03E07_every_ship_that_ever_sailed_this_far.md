## I. The Eddy

I need you to understand something about harbors. They shouldn't exist out here — not in the Space between frequencies, not where the currents circle instead of flow. But they do. And the ones that do are always waiting for exactly the right people.

"We are approaching a stable eddy," A1 said through the comm. His voice was measured, British, carrying that particular weight it got when something was significant and he was trying not to alarm us. "The signal here is... populated. There are seventeen distinct frequency signatures within range. Some of them have been here for a very long time."

"Populated?"

"There are ships. Docked. Waiting."

Clive was at the navigation console. His sphere dimmed — one degree, two. The Clive equivalent of a sharp intake of breath.

"I know this place."

"You've been here before?"

"I have been in harbors like this one. Not this specific harbor. But the type. The function." He adjusted his fedora — a tiny motion, barely perceptible. "This is a meeting place for seekers. For those following signals too large for any one ship to carry."

I made a ristretto. Two sugars. The nervousness in the coffee matched the nervousness in my chest. The eddy ahead glimmered with docked vessels: some ancient, some new, some that seemed to be made of materials that didn't exist in standard Space.

"The harbor is approximately three kilometers in diameter," A1 continued. "The construction method appears to be... accretion. Ship hulls added to ship hulls over centuries. It is not a ship. It is a gathering."

Klaus leaned forward from his station. "A port."

"A port," A1 confirmed.

Zephyr frowned. "Is that normal? Do ports just... exist out here?"

"Nothing just exists out here," Clive said. "Someone built it. Someone maintained it. Someone decided that seekers needed somewhere to stop."

## II. The Arrival

The Ephergent glided into a berth that seemed to have been waiting for us. The other ships watched without hostility — with something closer to recognition. I had the feeling, watching them, that they had seen a lot of crews come through here. A lot of seekers. Most of them probably looked like us: tired, determined, carrying something heavy.

"They know we're coming," Luminara said from behind her camera.

"They know everyone's coming," Klaus replied. "That's why the harbor exists."

A figure approached across the dock. A woman, middle-aged, with the look of someone who has been traveling for a very long time. She moved with the efficiency of someone who had learned to make every motion count. She stopped when she saw Klaus.

"Baron Klaus von Gnomendorf," she said. "Of the Nocturne delegation."

"Of the Nocturne internal affairs division," Klaus said. "Formerly."

Klaus's fedora tilted — the angle was different from Clive's signature tilt. Klaus's was sardonic. Clive's was wistful. This was a distinction that mattered to exactly two people in the universe, and they were both here.

"I was internal affairs," the woman said. "Before I wasn't. I left Nocturne sixty years ago to follow a case. The case was bigger than Nocturne."

"The case is everything," Klaus said.

"The case is everything. You found it too."

"I found the edges of it. I'm still working on the rest."

I stepped forward. "I'm Pixel. Captain of the Ephergent."

The woman smiled — small, warm, carrying sixty years of its own. "Captain. The ship that sounds like it's been making noise for a very long time. We've heard your signal. Several of us have been waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For whoever was loud enough to follow it all the way here."

I made an espresso. It was rich, warm, with notes of woodsmoke and aged paper — the taste of places that had been waiting a long time.

A1's voice through the comm: "I am making something appropriate for this place. It is not a flavor I have made before. It is the taste of harbor. Of arrival. Of being in the right place after a long journey."

"Your ship is the espresso machine," the woman said.

"I am the ship," A1 replied. "The machine is my body. The espresso is my language."

"I've met ships like you before. The espresso machine at the center of everything." She looked at Clive, then at the ship around us. "You're a Builder design, aren't you?"

"I am old. I predate most of the records. I do not know what I am."

"You're a Builder design. I can tell by the frequency signature. The Builders made a few of you. You're rare."

A1 made an espresso — rich, warm, with notes of something ancient and something new. The Harbor Captain took it without asking, which was either a breach of protocol or the highest form of compliment. Nobody corrected her.

"The name's Seren Voss," she said. "I've been keeping this harbor for... gods, forty years? Forty-five? Time moves differently when you're waiting. The ships come and go. Some stay. Some keep going. I stopped counting the ones who passed through."

Klaus asked the question we were all thinking. "How many?"

"Ships? Or seekers?"

"Both."

"Four hundred and seventeen ships have docked here following the signal. Some of them stayed. Some of them kept going. Every single one of them heard the same frequency — the one your ship's been broadcasting. Been following it like a breadcrumb trail made of starlight and stubbornness."

## III. The Registry

The crew separated to explore. Luminara went to the observation deck. Zephyr stayed with Aether's signal analyzer, listening. Klaus and the Harbor Captain talked in a corner — two former internal affairs officers comparing notes on cases that had swallowed their lives. Nano disappeared into the station's maintenance shafts, which was typical.

Clive and I went to the central registry.

"You're telling me there's a record of every ship that's ever come through here?"

The Registry Keeper — a figure made mostly of reading glasses and enthusiasm — nodded enthusiastically. "Every ship. Every crew. Every signal that passed through this eddy. Some of them are still here. Some of them went further. Some of them stopped."

"Stopped?"

"Decided the signal wasn't worth following anymore. Or found what they were looking for. Or realized they weren't looking for the signal at all. They were looking for somewhere to stop."

"And they just… stopped?"

"There's a berth for everyone here. That's why the harbor exists. Not everyone is meant to reach the end. Some people are meant to wait at the harbor. And that's okay. That's its own kind of journey."

I made a flat white. Nobody drank it. The crew was too focused on Clive, who had gone very still in front of a terminal.

"Do you have a record of a vessel called the Resolution?"

"The what?"

"The Resolution. A DRM research vessel. Night guard named Barry Kowalski. He would have passed through this region approximately thirty years ago."

The Registry Keeper typed. The console made a sound like a sigh — the specific sigh of a database finding exactly what you were afraid it would find.

"I have a record."

Clive didn't move. His sphere dimmed to almost nothing — not from grief, from something older. From the weight of thirty years collapsing into a single sentence.

"Barry Kowalski. Arrived at this harbor on the 14th of March, 1997. Departed on the 19th of March, 1997. Destination recorded as: 'further in.' Final notation in the berth log: 'Coffee was ready. Crew was ready. Signal was ready. Only took thirty years to get everyone assembled.'"

Clive went very still. His glow dimmed to almost nothing — not from grief, from something older. From the weight of thirty years collapsing into a single sentence.

"Clive."

"He was here. He was right here. And he left. He left and he kept going."

"He kept going. Like we're doing."

"He kept going."

## IV. The Message

"There is a message," A1 said through the comm. His voice carried that particular weight it got when something had been waiting a very long time. "Filed under the Ephergent's frequency. It has been waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For the crew that followed the signal this far. For the ship that Barry Kowalski left notes for. For—"

A pause. Longer than usual.

"For us."

We gathered around a terminal in the message center. The Harbor Captain had joined us. Klaus stood very straight. Luminara's hand found Nano's shoulder. Zephyr's eyes were closed, listening to his brother's signal in the static.

"There's a message," the Harbor Captain said. "For the crew of the vessel that arrives following the signal that Barry Kowalski left. It's been waiting here for thirty years. Nobody's claimed it until now."

"Open it."

The Harbor Captain typed. The screen flickered. A voice — warm, patient, with the cadence of someone who had been waiting a long time and wasn't at all surprised — filled the room.

*"If you're hearing this, you're the crew that found the signal I left. Which means Clive found his way back, which means A1 is a ship again, which means the whole thing worked. Good. Took long enough. Here's what you need to know: the signal is real. It's been calling since before I was born, and it'll be calling after I'm gone. That's the point. That's why we built it. Not to find the end — to find each other. The coffee was ready. I left it warm. Follow the frequency. It knows where you're supposed to end up. And when you get there — when you finally get there — sit down. Have a cup. You earned it. — Barry Kowalski, DRM Night Guard (Retired), Signal Follower (Professional)."*

Silence. The crew didn't move.

"That is, precisely," Klaus said, "the most Barry thing I have ever heard."

"'Signal Follower (Professional),'" Luminara said.

"He made that title up," Klaus replied. "He absolutely made that title up."

"He made that title up," Clive said. "He made all the titles up. He made the job up. He made the whole job up and then he did it for forty years and then he retired from it and then he kept doing it anyway because he couldn't stop."

"Clive—"

"He's alive. He's at the end. And he left the coffee ready."

A1 made an espresso. It was perfect. Not rich, not complex — perfect. Balanced. Exactly what it should be. The taste of arrival.

"The coffee is ready," A1 said.

## V. Klaus's Case

The Harbor Captain and Klaus stood at the viewport on the observation deck, looking out at the harbor field. Ships of every configuration: sleek, ancient, bizarre, mundane. All of them stopped in the same eddy, following the same signal.

"You left Nocturne," the Harbor Captain said. "Sixty years ago. Following the same case I'm following."

"I left because I found something in the records that Nocturne didn't want found. A frequency signature that predated Nocturne's founding. Predated the DRM's founding. Predated — everything, as far as I could tell. A signal that had been broadcasting long before anyone was alive to receive it."

"The Builders."

"I didn't know what to call it then. I just knew that someone had left a light on. And that the light was still burning. And that Nocturne — for all its resources, for all its reach — had no interest in finding out who lit it or why."

"Because finding out would mean changing."

"Because finding out would mean admitting that the universe is bigger than the organization. That there are things that don't belong to any frequency, any nation, any jurisdiction. Things that just... are."

Klaus's fedora tilted — two degrees left. The Barry angle. He didn't notice.

"I followed the case because the case was everything. I'm still following it. Sixty years and I'm still following it. And now — now I think I'm close. The signal leads somewhere. Barry Kowalski got there. Clive's going to get there. And I'm going to be there to see what it was all for."

"That's a long time to wait for an answer."

"The signal doesn't hurry. Neither do I."

A distant ship lit up — a recognition signal, warm and amber. Someone on that ship knew Klaus. Klaus raised one hand in response. The gesture was small, careful, freighted with sixty years of questions and not enough answers.

"The Nocturne never officially closed your case," the Harbor Captain said. "Did you know that?"

"I know."

"Sixty years and they still list it as 'under investigation.' You're their longest-running open file."

A small smile. "I wasn't aware they cared."

"They don't. That's why the file's still open. Nobody's filed the closure paperwork."

## VI. The View

Luminara and Nano stood at the viewport on the observation deck, watching the harbor field. Four hundred and seventeen ships, each one a story, each story a signal.

"Four hundred and seventeen," Luminara said.

"And every one of them followed the same signal to the same place."

"And not one of them regretted it?"

"Some of them stopped here. Some of them made this harbor their end. And they seemed… content. They seemed like they'd found what they were looking for."

"And the ones who kept going?"

"They seemed like they were almost home."

Luminara was quiet for a long moment. The harbor field shifted in the viewport — ships repositioning, signals pulsing in the eddy's gentle current.

"I've spent my whole life taking pictures of things that are leaving," she said. "Sunsets. Frequencies fading. Moments that are ending. I always thought the point was to capture them before they're gone."

"And now?"

"Now I think the point is to capture them while they're still happening. To bear witness to the crossing, not just the arrival."

Luminara raised her camera — not to take a picture, just to hold it. To acknowledge it. To say: I see you. I see this. It mattered.

"Four hundred and seventeen ships," she said. "Four hundred and seventeen crossings. All of them following the same signal. All of them finding their own way to the same place."

"Some of them probably had arguments about the route," Nano said.

Luminara laughed — a small, real sound. "All of them had arguments about the route."

"Do you think Barry Kowalski had arguments about the route?"

"Barry Kowalski had arguments about the route, the destination, the proper temperature for coffee, and whether Clive was spelled with one L or two. I never met him. But I know that."

A1's voice through the comm, quiet and warm: "The coffee is ready. The signal continues."

## VII. The Countdown

The harbor behind us. The signal ahead. The crew at their stations, quiet, processing. Some things needed to be said before the next leg. Some things were ready to be said.

"Eleven days to the Wellspring threshold," Om Kai said.

"Eleven days."

"The signal is clear now. Clearer than it's ever been. Barry's frequency and the Builders' frequency and something else — something underneath both of them. A carrier wave. The thing that makes all the signals possible."

Zephyr's eyes were still closed, listening to his brother's signal. "Aether can feel it. He says it's like... standing at the edge of a concert and hearing the bass through the walls. You know something enormous is happening. You just can't see the stage yet."

"Eleven days until we see what's behind the wall."

"Eleven days until we find out what the whole thing was for."

"Eleven days until I find out what I was built for," Clive said.

The cockpit was quiet. The harbor signal faded behind us. The Wellspring signal grew ahead — not louder, not yet, but present. Certain. Waiting.

"I am at course for the Wellspring threshold," A1 said. "Estimated arrival: eleven days. The signal is strong. The signal has been strong for a very long time. I am reading it clearly now. It has been broadcasting toward us for decades. Maybe longer."

"It's been waiting for us."

"It's been waiting for everyone. That's what signals do. They wait. And then, when the right people arrive — they stop waiting."

The Ephergent moved through the harbor field. Ships that had been here for centuries watched us pass. Some of them lit up. Some of their signals pulsed. A welcome. A recognition. Four hundred and seventeen ships, and one more. One more that was going to see it through. One more that was going to find out what's at the end.

Four hundred and seventeen ships. Four hundred and seventeen crews who heard the signal and followed it. Four hundred and seventeen stories, all of them leading to the same place. And now, one more. One more that was going to find out what the whole thing was for.

Eleven days. Eleven days to the Wellspring. Eleven days to the signal that Barry Kowalski spent forty years following. Eleven days to find out what the Builders were trying to tell us. What the frequency was always saying. What the whole thing — all of it, the DRM and the Drift and the Dimming and the waiting — what it was all for.

Meatball sighed contentedly. Mochi was warm in my pocket. The signal continued.
