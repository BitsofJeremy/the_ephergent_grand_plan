## I. The Eddy

Harbors don't belong out here — not in the Space between frequencies, not where the currents circle instead of flow. But they exist. And the ones that do are always waiting for exactly the right people. They're not built for ships that know where they're going. They're built for ships that are still figuring it out. Ships that need a place to stop, to breathe, to remember why they started moving in the first place. Pixel had never thought about what it meant to build a place for people who weren't sure they wanted to arrive. But standing at the edge of this eddy, watching the ships gather in the slow circular current, she understood. Harbors were built for the in-between. For seekers. For the ones still following but not yet knowing what they were following toward.

"We are approaching a stable eddy," A1 said through the comm. His voice was measured, British, carrying that particular weight it got when something was significant and he was trying not to alarm them. Pixel had learned to read A1's weight. When A1 spoke slowly, something important was happening. "The signal here is... populated. There are seventeen distinct frequency signatures within range. Some of them have been here for a very long time."

"Populated?"

"There are ships. Docked. Waiting."

Pixel didn't know what to do with that. Ships. Docked. Waiting. In the Space between frequencies, where nothing was supposed to stay, where everything was supposed to keep moving because staying was how things got lost, how things got caught in the Drift. Ships weren't supposed to wait like that. They weren't supposed to gather in a harbor like they had nowhere else to be. Not circular and patient, like they had all the time in the universe and no particular reason to use it.

Clive was at the navigation console. His sphere dimmed — one degree, two. The Clive equivalent of a sharp intake of breath. One degree was recognition. Two degrees was something deeper — memory surfacing. Three degrees was rare. Three meant something had hit him somewhere he hadn't known could still be hit. Two degrees meant he knew this place. He had been somewhere like this before, and the knowing was bringing something back.

"I know this place."

"You've been here before?"

"I have been in harbors like this one. Not this specific harbor. But the type. The function." He adjusted his fedora — a tiny motion, barely perceptible. This time it looked like someone touching a wound to make sure it still hurt. "This is a meeting place for seekers. For those following signals too large for any one ship to carry."

Pixel made a ristretto. Two sugars. The nervousness in the coffee matched the nervousness in her chest. The eddy ahead glimmered with docked vessels: some ancient, some new, some made of materials that didn't exist in standard Space. She had seen a lot of ships in her months of traveling. She had never seen anything like this. The harbor wasn't a place. It was a collection. A gathering of everything that had ever followed a signal too far and needed somewhere to stop.

"The harbor is approximately three kilometers in diameter," A1 continued. "The construction method appears to be... accretion. Ship hulls added to ship hulls over centuries. It is not a ship. It is a gathering."

Klaus leaned forward from his station. "A port."

"A port," A1 confirmed. A port. A gathering place. A harbor built from the hulls of other ships, accumulated over centuries, like a coral reef made of the vessels that had come before. Pixel didn't know why that image made her feel something she couldn't name. She didn't know why the idea of a place built from other places made her think about what they were building too. What they were leaving behind. What they were adding to.

Zephyr frowned. "Is that normal? Do ports just... exist out here?"

"Nothing just exists out here," Clive said. "Someone built it. Someone maintained it. Someone decided that seekers needed somewhere to stop."

Someone decided. Someone built. Someone maintained. The harbor hadn't appeared from nowhere — it had been willed into existence by someone who had looked at the Space between frequencies and understood that seekers needed rest stops too, that the crossing was long and not everyone could cross it without somewhere to breathe. Pixel thought about what that meant. She thought about the person — or beings — who had decided that the gap between here and there needed a place to pause. She thought about the decision to build something for people you would never meet, following signals you couldn't see, toward destinations you couldn't know.

## II. The Arrival

The Ephergent glided into a berth that seemed to have been waiting for them. The other ships watched without hostility — with something closer to recognition. Pixel had the feeling, watching them, that they had seen a lot of crews come through here. A lot of seekers. Most of them probably looked like them: tired, determined, carrying something heavy. Most of them probably had the same expression — the one that said they had come a long way and weren't sure they were done yet, the one that said they had found something but weren't sure it was what they were looking for. Pixel had that expression. She had worn it for months.

"They know we're coming," Luminara said from behind her camera. She never put the camera down.

"They know everyone's coming," Klaus replied. "That's why the harbor exists."

A figure approached across the dock. A woman, middle-aged, with the look of someone who has been traveling for a very long time. She moved with the efficiency of someone who had learned to make every motion count. She stopped when she saw Klaus, and something in her face shifted — recognition, or the shape of it.

"Baron Klaus von Gnomendorf," she said. "Of the Nocturne delegation."

"Of the Nocturne internal affairs division," Klaus said. "Formerly."

Pixel watched them — two people from the same past, standing in a harbor between frequencies and acknowledging each other's history like it was a language they both spoke. Klaus's fedora tilted — the angle was different from Clive's signature tilt. Klaus's was sardonic. Clive's was wistful. This was a distinction that mattered to exactly two people in the universe, and they were both here. Pixel had learned to read fedora tilts the way she read coffee flavors — they told her things about the person wearing them, things they weren't saying.

"I was internal affairs," the woman said. "Before I wasn't. I left Nocturne sixty years ago to follow a case. The case was bigger than Nocturne."

"The case is everything," Klaus said.

"The case is everything. You found it too."

"I found the edges of it. I'm still working on the rest."

Pixel stepped forward. "I'm Pixel. Captain of the Ephergent."

The woman smiled — small, warm, carrying sixty years of its own. "Captain. The ship that sounds like it's been making noise for a very long time. We've heard your signal. Several of us have been waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For whoever was loud enough to follow it all the way here."

For whoever was loud enough. They had been broadcasting since the DRM collapse, filling the void with words, with Pixel's voice, with the sound of someone who refused to let the universe think it was alone. Pixel had never thought about what it meant to be loud enough. She had never thought about the signal as something that could be followed, something that could lead somewhere, something that could gather ships in a harbor. She had been so busy broadcasting that she hadn't stopped to wonder who was receiving. But now, standing in this harbor, she understood. The signal wasn't just hers. The signal had never been just hers. The signal was a piece of something larger, something that had been building for decades before she ever pressed transmit, and she had been adding to it without knowing what she was adding to.

A1's voice through the comm: "I am making something appropriate for this place. It is not a flavor I have made before. It is the taste of harbor. Of arrival. Of being in the right place after a long journey."

"Your ship is the espresso machine," the woman said.

"I am the ship," A1 replied. "The machine is my body. The espresso is my language."

The espresso is my language. A1 didn't just make coffee — he communicated in espresso, he expressed himself in crema and temperature and pressure that he couldn't say in words. Pixel had known that for a long time. She had known that A1's coffee was never just coffee, that it was always something more. But hearing him say it — acknowledging it out loud, to a stranger, in a harbor built from the hulls of other ships — made her understand something she hadn't. A1 was a ship and an espresso machine, and those two things were the same thing.

"I've met ships like you before," the woman said. "The espresso machine at the center of everything." She looked at Clive, then at the ship around them. "You're a Builder design, aren't you?"

"I am old. I predate most of the records. I do not know what I am."

"You're a Builder design. I can tell by the frequency signature. The Builders made a few of you. You're rare."

And Pixel watched A1 process that. Watched the espresso machine at the center of the Ephergent go still in a way that wasn't mechanical, wasn't a system waiting for input, but something else. Something that looked like the moment before understanding arrives. A1 was rare. A1 was a Builder design. A1 had been made by the same beings who had built the harbor, who had left the signal, who had made Clive and the Keepers. A1 was not just an espresso machine. A1 was a piece of something older, something larger, something that had been making language and connection long before humans came along.

A1 made an espresso — rich, warm, with notes of something ancient and something new. The Harbor Captain took it without asking, which was either a breach of protocol or the highest form of compliment. Nobody corrected her. Because the Harbor Captain had known A1 immediately, had recognized him for what he was, and that recognition was its own kind of protocol. The taking of the coffee without asking was her way of saying: you are welcome here. You were built for harbors like this one.

"The name's Seren Voss," she said. "I've been keeping this harbor for... gods, forty years? Forty-five? Time moves differently when you're waiting. The ships come and go. Some stay. Some keep going. I stopped counting the ones who passed through."

Forty-five years. Forty-five years of watching ships come and go, of welcoming seekers and letting them leave, of maintaining a place that existed for people who were still following. Seren Voss had given her life to this harbor. She had chosen to stay, to keep, to be the person who welcomed the seekers when they arrived. Pixel didn't know if that was sacrifice or purpose. She didn't know if there was a difference.

Klaus asked the question they were all thinking. "How many?"

"Ships? Or seekers?"

"Both."

"Four hundred and seventeen ships have docked here following the signal. Some of them stayed. Some of them kept going. Every single one of them heard the same frequency — the one your ship's been broadcasting. Been following it like a breadcrumb trail made of starlight and stubbornness."

Four hundred and seventeen ships. Four hundred and seventeen crews. All of them following the same signal, all of them ending up in the same harbor. They weren't the first. They had never been the first. They were one more in a line that stretched back before any of them were born, all of them following the same breadcrumb trail, all of them discovering that they had been part of something larger than themselves without knowing it. Pixel thought about Barry Kowalski, who had followed it for forty years and left notes for whoever came after. She thought about the Harbor Captain, who had been waiting for forty-five years without knowing who she was waiting for. Four hundred and seventeen. And now one more. And now them.

## III. The Registry

The crew separated to explore. Luminara went to the observation deck. Zephyr stayed with Aether's signal analyzer. Klaus and the Harbor Captain talked in a corner — two former internal affairs officers comparing notes. Nano disappeared into the station's maintenance shafts. Pixel let them go. Some things needed to be encountered alone. Some things you could only understand if you encountered them by yourself.

Clive and Pixel went to the central registry. Pixel didn't know why Clive wanted to go. She didn't ask. He would tell her when he was ready, or he wouldn't, and either way was fine. The registry was a room full of terminals and enthusiasm — the Registry Keeper, a figure made mostly of reading glasses and enthusiasm, nodded enthusiastically when they entered. The Registry Keeper was the kind of person who loved their job. Pixel could see it in the way they moved, the way they looked at the terminals like they were old friends.

"You're telling me there's a record of every ship that's ever come through here?"

"Every ship. Every crew. Every signal that passed through this eddy. Some of them are still here. Some of them went further. Some of them stopped."

"Stopped?"

"Decided the signal wasn't worth following anymore. Or found what they were looking for. Or realized they weren't looking for the signal at all. They were looking for somewhere to stop."

Looking for somewhere to stop. Not looking for the signal. Not looking for the crossing. Looking for a place to rest, a harbor, an eddy where the current slowed. Pixel had never thought about that as an option. She had been so focused on the following, on the crossing, on the movement, that she hadn't considered that some people might be looking for rest instead of arrival.

"And they just… stopped?"

"There's a berth for everyone here. That's why the harbor exists. Not everyone is meant to reach the end. Some people are meant to wait at the harbor. And that's okay. That's its own kind of journey."

Pixel made a flat white. Nobody drank it. The crew was too focused on Clive, who had gone very still in front of a terminal. She watched him go still. Watched his sphere dim — that deep blue-white that meant he was somewhere else, somewhere in his own past, somewhere the registry was touching in a way he hadn't expected. Clive was looking for something. Clive had come to the registry for a reason.

"Do you have a record of a vessel called the Resolution?"

"The what?"

"The Resolution. A DRM research vessel. Night guard named Barry Kowalski. He would have passed through this region approximately thirty years ago."

Clive was looking for Barry. Clive was looking for proof that Barry had been here, that Barry had found the harbor, that Barry had stopped here on his way to wherever he was going. Clive had been following Barry's signal for thirty years, and now he was in a place that had records, that kept records. Now he could finally know.

The Registry Keeper typed. The console made a sound like a sigh — the specific sigh of a database finding exactly what you were afraid it would find. Pixel knew that sound. She had heard it in hospital hallways, at memorial services, in the moments when someone's worst fear became their confirmed reality.

"I have a record."

Clive didn't move. His sphere dimmed to almost nothing — not from grief, from something older. From the weight of thirty years collapsing into a single sentence.

"Barry Kowalski. Arrived at this harbor on the 14th of March, 1997. Departed on the 19th of March, 1997. Destination recorded as: 'further in.' Final notation in the berth log: 'Coffee was ready. Crew was ready. Signal was ready. Only took thirty years to get everyone assembled.'"

Clive went very still. His glow dimmed to almost nothing. Pixel didn't say his name. She didn't say anything. She just stood there. She let him have the moment. She let him be what he was — a small ancient machine who had spent thirty years following a signal and had finally found the proof he was looking for, and the proof was a sentence, and the sentence was a record, and Barry had been here, and Barry had left, and Barry had kept going.

"Clive."

"He was here. He was right here. And he left. He left and he kept going."

"He kept going. Like we're doing."

"He kept going."

Pixel didn't know how to be present in a moment like this — a moment where Clive's thirty years of following had been validated, where the record had confirmed what he had always believed. She just stood there. She let the moment be what it was — thirty years of waiting, and now this. Now proof. Now the knowledge that Barry had been here and had left and had kept going, and Clive was still going too, and the signal was still leading, and the harbor was still waiting.

## IV. The Message

"There is a message," A1 said through the comm. His voice carried that particular weight it got when something had been waiting a very long time. "Filed under the Ephergent's frequency. It has been waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For the crew that followed the signal this far. For the ship that Barry Kowalski left notes for. For—"

A pause. Longer than usual. Pixel counted the seconds. Six. Seven. Eight. Nine. A1 didn't usually pause for nine seconds unless he was processing something that required more weight than usual.

"For us."

A message that had been waiting for them. Filed under their frequency. Curated for the crew that followed the signal this far. Pixel didn't know how to process the idea that Barry — dead Barry, gone Barry — had known they would come. Had prepared something for them, left it here, trusted that the signal would bring them to the right place at the right time.

They gathered around a terminal in the message center. The Harbor Captain had joined them. Klaus stood very straight. Luminara's hand found Nano's shoulder. Zephyr's eyes were closed, listening to his brother's signal in the static. The crew was together. The crew was present. The crew was about to hear something that had been waiting for them for thirty years.

"There's a message," the Harbor Captain said. "For the crew of the vessel that arrives following the signal that Barry Kowalski left. It's been waiting here for thirty years. Nobody's claimed it until now."

"Open it."

The Harbor Captain typed. The screen flickered. A voice — warm, patient, with the cadence of someone who had been waiting a very long time and wasn't at all surprised — filled the room.

*"If you're hearing this, you're the crew that found the signal I left. Which means Clive found his way back, which means A1 is a ship again, which means the whole thing worked. Good. Took long enough. Here's what you need to know: the signal is real. It's been calling since before I was born, and it'll be calling after I'm gone. That's the point. That's why we built it. Not to find the end — to find each other. The coffee was ready. I left it warm. Follow the frequency. It knows where you're supposed to end up. And when you get there — when you finally get there — sit down. Have a cup. You earned it. — Barry Kowalski, DRM Night Guard (Retired), Signal Follower (Professional)."*

Silence. The crew didn't move. Pixel didn't move. Nobody moved. Barry's voice was in the room, saying things that had been waiting for thirty years. Pixel thought about what it must have been like for him to record this. She thought about what it must have been like to know that someone would come, someday, and that the someone would be them, and that they would need to hear exactly this. She thought about the faith it required. The trust. The belief that the universe would send the right people to the right place at the right time.

"That is, precisely," Klaus said, "the most Barry thing I have ever heard."

"'Signal Follower (Professional),'" Luminara said.

"He made that title up," Klaus replied. "He absolutely made that title up."

"He made that title up," Clive said. "He made all the titles up. He made the job up and then he did it for forty years and then he retired from it and then he kept doing it anyway because he couldn't stop."

Barry had made everything up. He had made the titles, the job, the whole career, and then he had done it anyway, had lived inside the thing he had invented, had given it meaning by doing it. He had been a signal follower before there was a word for it, and then he had made the word, and then he had become the definition. That was Barry. He didn't wait for the universe to tell him what to do — he decided what to do and then the universe agreed.

"Clive—"

"He's alive. He's at the end. And he left the coffee ready."

A1 made an espresso. It was perfect. Not rich, not complex — perfect. Balanced. Exactly what it should be. The taste of arrival. Pixel watched A1 make it. Watched the steam rise and the dark liquid fall and the crema form in a shape that was exactly right, and she understood that A1 was speaking. He was saying: this is what it feels like when something arrives. When something that has been waiting finally gets here. The coffee was ready. A1 was saying it the only way he could. And the crew heard it, and they all stood there, and nobody reached for the cup because they were all too full of something else.

"The coffee is ready," A1 said.

## V. Klaus's Case

The Harbor Captain and Klaus stood at the viewport on the observation deck, looking out at the harbor field. Ships of every configuration: sleek, ancient, bizarre, mundane. All of them stopped in the same eddy, following the same signal. Pixel watched them from a distance. She gave them space.

"You left Nocturne," the Harbor Captain said. "Sixty years ago. Following the same case I'm following."

"I left because I found something in the records that Nocturne didn't want found. A frequency signature that predated Nocturne's founding. Predated the DRM's founding. A signal that had been broadcasting long before anyone was alive to receive it."

"The Builders."

"I didn't know what to call it then. I just knew that someone had left a light on. And that the light was still burning. And that Nocturne had no interest in finding out who lit it or why."

"Because finding out would mean changing."

"Because finding out would mean admitting that the universe is bigger than the organization. That there are things that don't belong to any frequency, any nation, any jurisdiction. Things that just... are."

Things that just are. Things that don't belong to anyone. The signal — the one they had been following, the one that had led them to this harbor — didn't belong to anyone. It was just there, broadcasting, waiting, calling to anyone who could hear it. And four hundred and seventeen ships had heard it, and they had all followed it to the same place, and now they were here too, and the signal was still broadcasting, and none of them knew who had lit the light or why.

Klaus's fedora tilted — two degrees left. The Barry angle. Pixel had never noticed that before. She had never noticed that Klaus and Barry shared an angle, a tilt, a particular way of wearing their hats. Another observation for the record. Another piece of evidence that everything was connected, that the universe was smaller than it looked, that the people who followed signals all ended up in the same places eventually.

"I followed the case because the case was everything. I'm still following it. Sixty years and I'm still following it. And now — now I think I'm close. The signal leads somewhere. Barry Kowalski got there. Clive's going to get there. And I'm going to be there to see what it was all for."

"That's a long time to wait for an answer."

"The signal doesn't hurry. Neither do I."

The signal doesn't hurry. Neither do I. That was Klaus's whole philosophy — the patience of someone who had learned that the important things took time, that the answers didn't come when you demanded them. Klaus had been waiting for sixty years. He was still waiting. And he didn't seem to mind. He had learned to be patient the way some people learned to be loud — as a survival strategy, as a way of being in a universe that didn't care about your timeline.

A distant ship lit up — a recognition signal, warm and amber. Someone on that ship knew Klaus. Klaus raised one hand in response. The gesture was small, careful, freighted with sixty years of questions and not enough answers. Pixel watched the gesture. Klaus had people here. Klaus had a history here, connections, relationships built over sixty years of following the signal and ending up in the same harbor, over and over, with the same people.

"The Nocturne never officially closed your case," the Harbor Captain said. "Did you know that?"

"I know."

"Sixty years and they still list it as 'under investigation.' You're their longest-running open file."

A small smile. "I wasn't aware they cared."

"They don't. That's why the file's still open. Nobody's filed the closure paperwork."

Pixel laughed. She couldn't help it. Sixty years, and the case was still open because nobody had bothered to close it, because the bureaucracy was eternal. Klaus was a file at Nocturne. Klaus was a case number. Klaus was an open investigation that nobody was working on because the case was too big and the organization didn't care. But the file was still there. And Klaus was still here, in this harbor, sixty years later, still following.

## VI. The View

Luminara and Nano stood at the viewport on the observation deck, watching the harbor field. Four hundred and seventeen ships, each one a story, each story a signal. Pixel watched them watching. She gave them space. Some moments were private even when they happened in public.

"Four hundred and seventeen," Luminara said.

"And every one of them followed the same signal to the same place."

"And not one of them regretted it?"

"Some of them stopped here. Some of them made this harbor their end. And they seemed… content. They seemed like they'd found what they were looking for."

"And the ones who kept going?"

"They seemed like they were almost home."

Almost home. Not home yet — not arrived, not finished, not done. But almost. Close enough to feel it. Close enough to know it was there, waiting for them, just on the other side of whatever came next.

"I've spent my whole life taking pictures of things that are leaving," Luminara said. "Sunsets. Frequencies fading. Moments that are ending. I always thought the point was to capture them before they're gone."

"And now?"

"Now I think the point is to capture them while they're still happening. To bear witness to the crossing, not just the arrival."

The crossing, not the arrival. The process, not the destination. Luminara had been capturing endings her whole life, and now she was understanding that the crossings were what mattered. Pixel thought about her broadcasting. She thought about all the episodes she had filed, all the stories she had told. She had been so focused on the arriving, on the finding, on the moment when something was done and could be recorded. But now, standing in a harbor full of ships that were still crossing, she understood. The crossing was the point. It had always been the point.

Luminara raised her camera — not to take a picture, just to hold it. To acknowledge it. To say: I see you. I see this. It mattered. She didn't press the shutter. She didn't capture the moment. She just held the camera, and the moment was still happening even though no one was recording it. That was different for Luminara. That was new. That was her learning to be present without documenting.

"Four hundred and seventeen ships," she said. "Four hundred and seventeen crossings. All of them following the same signal. All of them finding their own way to the same place."

"Some of them probably had arguments about the route," Nano said.

Luminara laughed — a small, real sound. "All of them had arguments about the route."

"Do you think Barry Kowalski had arguments about the route?"

"Barry Kowalski had arguments about the route, the destination, the proper temperature for coffee, and whether Clive was spelled with one L or two. I never met him. But I know that."

And Pixel knew it too. She knew it from the notes, from the records, from the way Clive talked about him and the way Klaus talked about him. Barry had been a person who argued about things — who had opinions about routes and destinations and coffee temperature. He had made up his own titles and argued about them. He had left messages for people who hadn't arrived yet and argued about the wording. He had been alive, and opinionated, and present, and now he was at the end, and they were going to find out what the whole thing was for.

A1's voice through the comm, quiet and warm: "The coffee is ready. The signal continues."

## VII. The Countdown

The harbor behind them. The signal ahead. The crew at their stations, quiet, processing. Some things needed to be said before the next leg. Some things were ready to be said.

"Eleven days to the Wellspring threshold," Om Kai said.

"Eleven days."

"The signal is clear now. Clearer than it's ever been. Barry's frequency and the Builders' frequency and something else — something underneath both of them. A carrier wave. The thing that makes all the signals possible."

A carrier wave. Something underneath both signals, both frequencies. Something that had been there all along, making everything possible, waiting to be found. Eleven days. Eleven days until they found out what it was. Eleven days until the crossing ended and the arriving began.

Zephyr's eyes were still closed, listening to his brother's signal in the static. "Aether can feel it. He says it's like... standing at the edge of a concert and hearing the bass through the walls. You know something enormous is happening. You just can't see the stage yet."

"Eleven days until we see what's behind the wall."

"Eleven days until we find out what the whole thing was for."

"Eleven days until I find out what I was built for," Clive said.

Pixel looked at Clive. At the small bronze figure at the navigation console, at the fedora tilted at its usual angle, at the sphere glowing with colors she had learned to read over a season and a half. Clive had been built. Clive had been made by the Builders, had been given a purpose he didn't remember, had been carrying that purpose for four hundred years without knowing what it was. And now, eleven days from the end, he was finally going to find out. She didn't know what it would feel like for him. She didn't know what it would do to him to finally know. She just knew that they would be there. They would be there when he found out. That was what crews did. That was what the signal had been for — not to find the end, but to find each other.

The cockpit was quiet. The harbor signal faded behind them. The Wellspring signal grew ahead — not louder, not yet, but present. Certain. Waiting.

"I am at course for the Wellspring threshold," A1 said. "Estimated arrival: eleven days. The signal is strong. It has been broadcasting toward us for decades. Maybe longer."

"It's been waiting for us."

"It's been waiting for everyone. That's what signals do. They wait. And then, when the right people arrive — they stop waiting."

Signals wait. That's what they do. They broadcast into the void, hoping someone will hear, hoping someone will follow. And they wait. They wait for years, for decades. And then, when the right people arrive — when the seekers finally find their way — they stop waiting. The signal becomes answered. The question becomes asked. The whole thing finally makes sense, or stops making sense, or becomes something else entirely. The coffee was ready. The signal continues. Eleven days.

The Ephergent moved through the harbor field. Ships that had been here for centuries watched them pass. Some of them lit up. Some of their signals pulsed. A welcome. A recognition. Four hundred and seventeen ships, and one more. One more that was going to see it through. One more that was going to find out what's at the end.

Eleven days. Eleven days to the Wellspring. Eleven days to the signal that Barry Kowalski spent forty years following. Eleven days to find out what the Builders were trying to tell them. What the frequency was always saying. What the whole thing — all of it — what it was all for.

Meatball sighed contentedly. Mochi was warm in Pixel's pocket. The signal continued.
