## I. The Edge of the Map

The viewport showed a sky that had forgotten what stars looked like. The navigation console displayed coordinates that flickered between certainty and question marks.

I sat in the pilot's chair with my feet up, pretending I wasn't worried.

"A1, status."

"All systems nominal. Hull integrity: 97%. Fuel reserves: sufficient." A pause. "Navigation: uncertain."

"Uncertain isn't a number."

"It is today. The chart ends in four hours, seventeen minutes. After that, we are flying blind."

I sat up. "Blind? You don't do blind. You do instinct. You do harmonic inference. You do whatever it is you do that terrifies me every time it works."

"That requires a signal to follow. The signal is here. The map is not. Both facts are equally true."

Clive entered the cockpit. His glow was steady — a warm amber that I'd learned to read as contentment. He carried a small notebook, the kind people used before everything was digital. Barry's notebook, retrieved from DRM wreckage in Season 1.

"Four hours seventeen minutes," he said. "I heard."

"Did you hear the part where I said I wasn't worried?"

"I heard you say it." His fedora tilted — the Clive equivalent of a skeptical look. "I heard it the way people say things they don't believe."

I didn't argue. I'd learned that Clive hears frequencies in speech patterns the way A1 hears them in gravitational ripples. He is rarely wrong about what people mean versus what they say.

"I am reading something ahead," A1 said through the comm. His voice carried that particular weight it got when something was wrong and he was trying not to admit it. "Multiple somethings. The spectrograph is not behaving as expected."

"Define 'not behaving.'"

"Define 'expected.'"

I pulled up the spectrograph. The display was unlike anything I'd seen in my months of flying — not chaos, not order, but something in between. Peaks and valleys that shifted when I wasn't looking directly at them.

"Those aren't random."

"No. They are not random. They are not structured. They are something I do not have a word for."

Clive was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was lower than usual.

"I do."

"Clive—"

"Unnamed. They are unnamed. I have been here before. Or somewhere shaped like here." He was staring at the spectrograph with an expression I hadn't seen before — not recognition exactly, but the shape of recognition. Like looking at a word in a language he used to speak. "My hands remember this. My hands remember a place that did not have words for what it was."

I looked at him. "You never mentioned that."

"I did not remember it until right now." He paused. His fedora shifted — a tiny adjustment. "Or I chose not to. The distinction is not clear."

## II. The First Contact

Luminara sat at the secondary console in the frequency analysis station, her cameras arranged in a precise semicircle. She had been photographing everything since she joined the crew — forty-seven thousand images of a universe that keeps surprising her.

"I am picking something up," she said. "Not on standard frequencies. Below them. Like hearing a conversation in the next room through the wall."

"What kind of conversation?"

"The kind that was happening before anyone thought to name what they were saying."

She turned her console toward me. The waveform was unlike any standard broadcast — it didn't pulse in regular intervals, it breathed. Rising and falling in a rhythm that felt almost organic.

"It has been broadcasting for a very long time," Luminara said. "I can tell by the degradation pattern. This is old. This is older than most of what we have found."

"How old?"

"I cannot say. The calibration doesn't have a unit for this kind of old."

Mochi stirred in my jacket pocket. A slow, warm pulse — not urgent, not alarmed. Interested.

"Mochi, you know something?"

Mochi's glow intensified briefly, then settled. A confirmation without words, in the only language Mochi speaks.

"A1, can you trace the source?"

"I can try." A pause. "The signal is not in a direction. It is in a relationship. It exists between the known frequencies. It is what the space between sounds like when it has something to say."

"So we're not navigating toward it. We're navigating into it."

"Correct."

"I don't love that."

"Your discomfort is noted. It will not change the navigation."

## III. The Coordinates

Three hours later. The navigation console now displayed nothing but question marks. The chart had ended. The Ephergent flew through a region that had no designation, no name, no record of ever having been visited by anyone.

Om Kai had joined us, seated in his meditation position on the deck. He had been silent for thirty minutes. When Om Kai is silent, he is listening to something the rest of us cannot hear.

"A1, talk to me."

"We are in the right place."

"That's not a measurement."

"It is the only measurement that applies here. We are where we are supposed to be. Whether we are supposed to be here is a question for philosophers. Whether we are here is a question for instruments. The instruments say: yes."

Om Kai spoke without opening his eyes. "The space between frequencies is not empty. It is full. Full of what was almost said. Full of the words that didn't have a mouth to speak them."

"That's beautiful. Is it useful?"

"Beauty is useful. It reminds us we are not just navigating. We are witnessing."

A sound from the comm — not the usual hum of preparation, but something more tentative. A sound like a question being considered.

"A1?"

"I am attempting to make coffee." His voice was quieter than usual. "I find I do not want to."

"You've never not wanted to."

"The frequency ahead does not want coffee. It wants something else. I am trying to understand what."

Clive appeared in the doorway. His glow was steady, his fedora at its usual angle.

"The coffee is the language," he said. "You are trying to speak a language you haven't learned yet."

"Yes."

"What does it sound like, the thing it wants?"

A long pause. When A1 spoke again, his voice carried something almost like uncertainty.

"It sounds like a question that has been waiting a very long time to be answered."

## IV. The Signal Responds

Zephyr and Nano had joined us in the common area. The green light on the communications panel was pulsing in a rhythm none of us recognized — not the regular heartbeat of standard transmissions, but something syncopated. Something alive.

"We're being acknowledged," Zephyr said.

"Acknowledged by what?"

"By whatever is out there. It's not receiving our signal. It's responding to the fact of our signal. Like it heard the act of broadcasting, not the content."

"That's not how frequencies work," Nano said.

"That's not how frequencies work here." Zephyr's edges were doing that thing they did when he was excited — shimmering, slightly out of phase. "Somewhere out here, frequencies work differently. Or maybe frequencies always worked this way and we just forgot."

I pressed the transmit key. My voice is my first tool, my constant companion, my way of making sense of a universe that refuses to make sense of me.

"This is Pilot Pixel, vessel Ephergent. We are following a signal we cannot name. We are in a region that has no chart. If anyone is listening — if anything is listening — we could use some indication of where we are and what we are supposed to do next."

Static. Then — a response. Not static. Not noise. A voice, but wrong — filtered through too many harmonics, like hearing a word spoken by a choir all on the same note. The green light pulsed faster, excited, like a dog that had finally found the person it's been waiting for.

"...Pixel... ...Ephergent..."

I stepped back. "It knows my name. It knows the ship's name."

"...you are... ...between..."

"Between what?"

"...frequencies."

Klaus entered, having heard everything. "Between frequencies. Not in a frequency. Between them."

"Is that better or worse?"

"It is different. Different is not worse. Different is honest."

A cup appeared on the console — not espresso, not ristretto, something unfamiliar. Rich, complex, with notes of something that has no name in any language I know. A1 set it down without comment.

"I made something," A1 said. "I do not know what it is. It is what the frequency wanted."

---

## V. The Naming

Later. The ship had reduced speed, holding position in the void. The harmonics had settled into a chord — pleasant, almost musical, shifting slowly like a tide that has forgotten to retreat. The crew had gathered without being asked. Something was happening. Everyone could feel it.

"If I name it, I can map it," I said. "If I map it, I can navigate it. That's how it works. Every frequency in the universe has a designation, a place in the spectrum. You can't navigate what you can't name."

I input data. My fingers moved with the certainty of someone who has done this ten thousand times.

"I'm giving you a name. The first name. You hear that, signal? I'm naming you."

The harmonics shifted — anticipation, like a held breath.

"Frequency 7-G-Alpha."

No response.

"7-G-Alpha-1."

Nothing.

The green light flickered, almost playfully. I laughed — a frustrated, disbelieving sound.

"You're rejecting my labels? Fine. You want a name that fits?"

I closed my eyes. Listened to the chord — the exact blend of tones, the specific relationship between them. The sound of something that exists in the space between what is known and what is possible.

"Ephergent."

I opened my eyes.

"It's not a label. It's what you do. You emerge between the known frequencies. You exist in the spaces we ignore. You appear when everything else has given up looking. You're ephergent."

Silence — long, expectant. The cockpit was silent. Even A1 had gone quiet.

Then — the entire ship resonated. Every surface vibrated at the same frequency. The chord swelled — then settled, harmonious, complete. On the navigation console, the question marks had been replaced by numbers. Real numbers. Coordinates that meant something.

"...Ephergent... ...yes... ...this is... ...what we are..."

I was breathless. "You have a name now. We both do. This is Pilot Pixel. That's Ephergent."

"Navigation updated," A1 said. "New heading confirmed."

"Where to, A1?"

A pause — longer than usual.

"...forward."

I smiled. "See? A1 instincts. Never steer us wrong."

## VI. The Homecoming

The viewport showed a single point of light where there should be nothing. Not a star. Not a station. Something that has been waiting. The harmonics of Ephergent had become a song — neither human nor machine, but something between.

"The space between frequencies is not empty," Om Kai said. "It is where the almost-words go. The almost-songs. The things that existed before anyone thought to name them."

"I remember this," Clive said quietly. "Or I will remember this. The distinction is unclear."

"Forty-seven thousand photographs," Luminara said. "And I haven't seen anything like this yet. Nothing that looks like this should exist."

"It shouldn't," Klaus said. "That's what makes it interesting."

I pressed transmit one more time.

"Ephergent, this is Pixel. I'm going to need a frequency map of whatever's out there. Think you can help?"

"...Pixel... ...we are... ...between... ...always between..."

"Between is good. Between is where all the good frequencies live."

I returned to the console, plotted a course toward the light. The navigation computer accepted the coordinates without hesitation — the first smooth input in hours.

"A1, take us in."

"Course set."

"And A1?"

"Yes, Pixel?"

"Next time you have an instinct, tell me what it is. I'm tired of following things I can't name."

A pause. When A1 spoke again, his voice carried something almost like sheepishness.

"...A1 will try."

A cup appeared on the console — black, bitter, worried. The taste of someone who knows they are going somewhere that matters and isn't sure they're ready for what it means.

"I am making something appropriate," A1 said. "For the first time in this region, I understand what I am feeling. It is worry. It is the good kind. The kind that means the thing ahead is important."

Mochi pulsed warm in my pocket. Steady, constant, a small furnace of certainty in a universe that keeps surprising me. The ship moved toward the light. The signal continues. And somewhere ahead, in the spaces between frequencies, something that has been waiting for a very long time finally stops waiting.

## VII. The Between-Space

Seven hours later. The viewport showed nothing — not darkness, but absence. The stars had stopped. The light had stopped. Everything that once was here had crossed into something else. I sat in the pilot's chair, watching the instruments cycle through readings that make no sense.

"This is the farthest we've ever been from anything that has a name," I said to no one.

Clive entered. He carried Barry's notebook, open to a page near the back. The handwriting was dense, precise, increasingly small toward the bottom of the page.

"Barry wrote about this," he said. "He called it 'the between-space.' He wrote: 'The between-space is not empty. It is full of the frequencies that haven't found their names yet. It is where the universe keeps the songs it hasn't finished writing.'"

"Barry was here. Barry was right here."

"Barry went further. He always went further."

"And we're following him."

"We are following the signal he followed. Which means we are following what he found. Which means we are going to find it too."

A sound from the comm — not a sound of anxiety this time, but something almost like relief. A cup appeared on the console. Espresso, rich, complex, with an unfamiliar spice that I didn't recognize but immediately trusted.

"I am making something new," A1 said. "It tastes like coordinates. Like a place that exists in no chart but has always existed. I do not know what to call it."

"Then don't. Some things don't need names yet."

"That is the first sensible thing anyone has said in seven hours."

---

## VIII. The Crossing Continues

Midnight cycle. The absence outside the viewport had shifted again — not darker, not lighter, but more present. Like the nothing is paying attention.

"A1, talk to me."

"We are moving. I am reading forward progress. The destination is clearer than it was. The signal is stronger."

"Stronger how?"

"Stronger the way a voice is stronger when the person speaking finally knows they are being heard."

"That's not a measurement."

"It is the only measurement that applies here. Some things are measured by whether they are reaching what they are meant to reach. We are reaching. We are being reached for."

Mochi stirred. The glow intensified — brighter than I'd seen it in weeks. Not alarm. Recognition. Mochi has been in my pocket since Season 1, warm and present, and I've learned not to ask questions I don't want answers to. But this — this I wanted to know.

"Mochi, what is it?"

Mochi pulsed — a long, sustained warmth that filled my pocket and spread to my chest. Not communication. Not explanation. Something simpler and more profound: the feeling of being close to somewhere that matters.

"Whatever it is, we're almost there."

---

## IX. The Signal Calls

The crew had gathered for what I'd started calling "the evening namening" — the daily ritual of acknowledging what we'd found and what we're about to find. Om Kai was brewing tea. A1 had declined to make coffee, again. Clive sat with Barry's notebook open on the table in front of him.

"I have been in Nocturne Aeturnus for eighty years," Klaus said. "I have followed cases across seventeen frequencies. I have never seen anything like what we are approaching."

"Forty-seven thousand photographs," Luminara said. "And I don't have a single one that captures what this feels like. Some things can't be photographed. Some things can only be witnessed."

"My instruments are giving me readings that don't match any known calibration," Zephyr said. "Either my equipment is broken, or we're in a place where the rules haven't been written yet."

"The rules have been written," Nano said. "They're just written in a language we haven't learned yet. The readings aren't wrong. They're foreign."

"The between-space is not foreign," Om Kai said. "It is familiar in the way that old dreams are familiar. You don't remember the dream, but you remember the feeling of having dreamed it."

Clive was quiet for a long moment. "I remember this. Or I will remember this. Barry wrote about it. 'The between-space is where the signal waits. Not the signal you're following. The signal you're being followed by.' I never understood what he meant. I think I am beginning to."

I looked around the table. My crew. My family, in the way that matters — the people who chose to be where I am, following what I'm following, going where I'm going.

"One more day. Maybe two. And then we find out what Barry found. What the signal wanted us to find. What any of this was for."

"And if it's nothing?" Klaus asked.

"Then it's nothing, and we made peace with nothing somewhere between Season 1 and now. But I don't think it's nothing. I think it's the opposite of nothing. I think it's everything that was waiting for us to show up."

A1 entered. He carried a single cup — not espresso, not ristretto, not anything I'd seen before. It smelled like the first morning of something. It smelled like the last night of something else. It smelled like the space between.

"I made the thing again," he said. "The coordinates thing. The taste of a place that has always existed and was never charted. I do not know what it means. But I think we are close to finding out."

## X. The First Step

Eighteen hours later. The viewport showed the faintest light on the horizon of the void — not a star, not a station, something that has been waiting. The harmonics of Ephergent had settled into a stable chord, a frequency that has found its name and is now sharing it with anyone who will listen.

I sat in the pilot's chair, watching the light grow.

"Where the frequencies have no name."

"Where the frequencies have no name," A1 confirmed.

"Where the frequencies have no name," Clive said quietly, almost to himself.

"Season three, episode one." I pressed transmit. "Pilot Pixel, checking in from the between-space. Somewhere out here, unnamed harmonics just became one. And I think — I think we're just getting started."

"Ephergent, this is Pixel. We're coming in. Whatever you are, whoever you are — we're ready to meet you."

The harmonics rose. The light grew. And somewhere in the space between every frequency that has ever existed, something that has been waiting for a very long time finally opens its eyes.
