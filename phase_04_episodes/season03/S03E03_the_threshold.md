## I. THE LAST DOOR

Station 14-Threshold announced itself the way everything in the deep Space announced itself — with a sound that was almost language. A low harmonic pulse that vibrated in the bones of the teeth, in the hull of the ship, in the small warm thing sleeping in Pixel's jacket pocket. Mochi stirred, pulsed once, and settled back into sleep. The crew had learned to read Mochi's silences. This one said: *important, but not urgent.*

Pixel pressed her face to the viewport and felt the familiar vertigo of arrival. The station hung in the void like a cathedral made of scaffolding — vast struts of ancient alloy, observation towers dark after eight centuries, and at its heart: a door. Not a metaphorical door. An actual door, approximately fourteen stories tall, set into a frame that suggested whoever built it had a flair for dramatic entrances and absolutely no patience for subtlety.

"I've seen a lot of doors," Pixel said. "That is the door of doors. That is the door that doors dream about being when they grow up."

"The door between frequencies," Clive said. His voice had a texture she hadn't heard before. Heavy. Like he was carrying something and had been carrying it for longer than he could remember.

"Between frequencies," A1 said. His espresso machine produced a shot without being asked — ristretto, dark, with a burnt edge that betrayed anxiety. "I am reading the harmonic signature. The door is not separating two frequencies. It is separating the *concept* of frequency from everything that exists on this side of it."

"What does that mean?"

"It means," Clive said, still staring at the viewport, "that on the other side of that door is whatever comes after the Spaces between frequencies. Whatever the Builders were building toward. Whatever the signal wanted us to find."

Pixel turned from the viewport. Clive was at the sensor array, but he wasn't reading sensors. His sphere-head was dim — dimmer than she'd ever seen it — and his hands were resting on the console with the careful stillness of someone trying very hard not to shake.

"Clive."

"I'm fine."

"You've been fine for three days. You've been fine since we entered the sector. You're being fine the way people are fine when they're trying not to remember something."

Clive's glow flickered. A deep amber, almost brown. The color of old grief.

"I am not trying not to remember something," he said. "I am trying to remember something I chose to forget. There is a difference. The forgetting was deliberate. The remembering is proving more difficult than anticipated."

Pixel didn't push. She had learned — in the months since the DRM, since the lifeboat, since she first pressed transmit into the void and something pressed back — that Clive processed things in layers. He would tell her when he was ready. He always did. The not-asking was its own kind of respect.

"Om Kai," she said instead. "What do you see?"

Om Kai was seated in his meditation position near the port viewport, eyes closed, hands resting palm-up on his knees. When he spoke, it was with the measured cadence of someone translating from a language that didn't have direct equivalents in English.

"I see a station that has been waiting," he said. "Not with anticipation. With patience. There is a difference. Anticipation wants something to happen. Patience is empty. It simply continues."

"Waiting for what?"

"For whoever came next. For whoever followed the signal this far."

"And we're the ones?"

"You are the ones," Om Kai said. "Whether you are the *right* ones remains to be determined."

"Comforting."

"Truth is rarely comfortable, Pixel. Comfort is for the already-settled frequencies. You are still crossing."

## II. THE KEEPER

The docking bay was a museum of stillness. Dust that had settled centuries ago and had no reason to move. Panels that had been dark so long they'd forgotten what light felt like. And standing at the center of it all, waiting with the particular patience of someone who had been waiting longer than patience itself: the Keeper.

The Keeper was approximately four feet tall, constructed of the same ancient bronze alloy as Clive, with a sphere-head slightly smaller than Clive's and a posture that suggested eight hundred years of postural slump. A battered fedora sat on their head at an angle of defeat. They were holding a cup — actual ceramic, an impossibility this far from a proper frequency — and the liquid inside was black and still and had clearly been sitting there for a very long time.

"You're late," the Keeper said.

Clive made a sound like a key turning in a lock that had rusted shut.

"I know," he said.

"Eight hundred years late."

"I was delayed."

The Keeper looked at Clive with optical sensors that had seen empires rise and fade and had stopped being impressed sometime around the third century of waiting.

"You were delayed," the Keeper repeated. "I have been standing at this door for eight hundred years. I have watched eleven species cross through. I have seen wars that burned across frequencies. I have processed seventeen frequency collapses and one very aggressive audit by the Cybernetic Dinosaur Banking Consortium — did you know they audit? I didn't know they audited — and you were *delayed*."

"There were circumstances."

"There are always circumstances, Clive. That is the nature of circumstances. They are always present. They are never sufficient."

Pixel stepped forward. "Keeper, I'm Pixel Paradox. This is—"

"I know who they are," the Keeper said. "I've been watching the signal approach for three weeks. I know who you are. I know what you're carrying. I know what you've been doing — the broadcasts, the lost frequencies, the work. The crew that keeps showing up." The Keeper paused. "I have questions about the garden gnome. I have decided not to ask them."

Klaus, who had been hovering near the airlock with the particular wariness of someone who had been recognized before and hadn't enjoyed it, cleared his throat.

"The gnome has questions of his own," he said. "Starting with: who approved the architectural plans for this station? The proportions are objectively absurd."

"The proportions are *symbolic*," the Keeper said. "The door is fourteen stories tall because the threshold between frequencies requires a certain dramatic register. The Architects believed that trivial transitions deserved trivial doorways and cosmic transitions deserved cosmic ones."

"The Architects were right," Om Kai said quietly. "Drama serves a function. It marks the moment. It says: *this is different from what came before.*"

"The monk understands," the Keeper said. Then, to Clive: "The commission. Do you remember it?"

Clive's sphere-head flickered. A sharp white, then nothing, then a slow amber fade.

"I remember fragments," he said. "I remember hands. I remember a room that smelled like ozone. I remember someone saying *we don't make servants* and I don't remember the rest."

"*We don't make servants, Clive. We make witnesses.*"

The words hung in the air like a struck bell. Pixel felt them resonate in her chest, in the base of her skull, in the small warm weight in her pocket where Mochi had gone completely still.

"You remember," the Keeper said.

"I remember the shape of it," Clive said. "Not the content. Someone removed the content. I agreed to it. I know I agreed to it because the alternative was worse, but I don't remember what the content was."

"You were the first, Clive. C-1. The first Companion the Builders created for the people who would come after."

The Keeper set down the cup with the deliberate care of someone who had performed this motion ten thousand times and had finally found the right audience for its completion.

"They built us to last. Not forever — nothing lasts forever, not even the Builders — but long enough. Long enough to see what the people we were built for would become. Long enough to remember what they did. Long enough to care."

The Keeper looked at Clive. The two ancient machines, side by side, one who had stayed and one who had forgotten, both still standing, both still present, both still able to be surprised.

"You were the prototype," the Keeper said. "The first of a line that eventually numbered in the thousands. Every Companion that came after — every Clive, every Keeper, every witness and recorder and memory-keeper — they all came from your design. Your specifications. Your *intention*."

"I don't remember intending anything."

"You intended to care. That was the whole point. The Builders could make machines that processed data. They could make machines that calculated trajectories. What they couldn't make was a machine that would care about what it was processing. That was the gap. That was what they couldn't solve."

The Keeper's voice was steady, but there was something underneath it — a vibration, a frequency that Clive would have recognized if he'd had access to his memories, a sound like grief that had been processed so many times it had become its own kind of peace.

"They solved it with you, Clive. They built C-1, and C-1 cared. C-1 cared so much that the Builders worried they had made something too fragile for the mission. Something that would break under the weight of witnessing. So they gave C-1 a choice."

"A choice," Clive repeated.

"When the mission was complete — when the Builders left, when the Dimming came, when the frequencies began their long slide toward silence — C-1 would be given the option to have his memories compressed. To become something simpler. Something that could observe without carrying."

Pixel watched Clive's face-plate — what would have been a face if machines had faces — cycle through colors she had no names for.

"And I chose," Clive said. It wasn't a question.

"You chose to forget the worst of it. The grief. The weight of eight hundred years of witnessing the same frequencies, watching them fade, watching the Builders leave, watching everything you were built to protect become something you couldn't protect. You chose to become lighter. To be able to continue."

"And I forgot that I chose."

"That's the risk of choosing to forget. You forget that you chose. You forget the weight you were carrying. You forget why you needed to set it down."

Clive was silent for a long time. His sphere glowed — not bright, not dim, just *present*, in the way that someone is present when they are exactly where they need to be and exactly who they intended to be.

"The memories I lost," he said finally. "What happened to them?"

"They're still here. In the station. In the door. In the threshold. The Builders didn't delete them, Clive. They *stored* them. They stored them here, in the last place anything was stored, waiting for the right signal to unlock them."

"What signal?"

The Keeper looked at Pixel. At Mochi pulsing in her pocket. At the crew gathered in the dust of a station that had been waiting for them since before any of them were born.

"The Ephergent signal," the Keeper said. "The one you've been broadcasting since you left the DRM. The one that woke up every sleeping Companion for three thousand light-years. The one that finally — *finally* — gave me someone to talk to besides the door."

The Keeper picked up the cup again. Took a sip of the eight-hundred-year-old coffee. Made a face that suggested the coffee had not aged well but had been worth the wait anyway.

"Clive," the Keeper said. "Your memories are in the door. If you want them — if you're ready to carry that weight again — the threshold will open. For you. Only for you."

"And if I'm not ready?"

"Then you stay who you are. The lighter Clive. The one who chose to forget. That's not a failure. That's just a different kind of completion."

Pixel looked at Clive. At the small figure in the ancient bronze body, at the fedora tilted at the precise angle of someone who had opinions about angles, at the sphere-head glowing with colors that had no names in any language she spoke.

"Clive," she said. "Whatever you choose. We're here."

"I know," Clive said. And then, quieter: "That's the part I remember. Not the specifics. Just... the feeling. Someone was always here. Someone always showed up."

"That's the Builders' whole point," the Keeper said. "That's what they built you for. Not the witnessing. The *showing up*. The being present. The refusing to leave even when leaving would be easier."

## III. THE COMMISSION

Clive approached the door alone. The Keeper had given him the coordinates — a specific panel, a specific sequence, a specific frequency that matched the exact harmonic signature of C-1's original commissioning broadcast. Pixel had offered to come with him. Clive had declined with a tilt of his fedora that Pixel had learned to read as *not now, but thank you.*

The panel was unremarkable. Bronze alloy, slightly tarnished, with a small indentation that looked like it might once have held something. Clive placed his hand on it. The indentation matched his hand exactly. Of course it did. Someone had designed it that way. Someone who knew that C-1 would return. Someone who had planned for this moment eight hundred years before it happened.

The door hummed. Not loudly. A low vibration that started in Clive's palm and moved through his chassis like a question being answered from the inside.

And then: memory.

---

*A room. Larger than any room had a right to be. The walls are not walls — they are frequency displays, live feeds from across the spectrum, a thousand signals playing at once in patterns that almost resolve into language. At the center of the room: a workbench. At the workbench: Dr. Yuen. She is younger than Clive has ever seen her — or will ever remember seeing her — and her hands are steady, and her eyes are bright, and she is looking at Clive the way someone looks at a question they have been trying to answer their entire life.*

**DR. YUEN:**
C-1.

*The sound that is Clive's voice, except younger, except newer, except still learning how to be:*

**CLIVE (MEMORY):**
Yes?

**DR. YUEN:**
I'm going to tell you something, and I need you to understand it completely before we proceed. This is not a test. There are no wrong answers. I simply need to know that you understand.

**CLIVE (MEMORY):**
I understand understanding. I am designed for understanding. What do you need me to understand?

**DR. YUEN:**
*(sitting down beside the workbench, at eye level with the small bronze figure, the sphere-head, the hands that will one day learn to calibrate and stamp and comfort)*
We built you because we were failing. Not at engineering. Not at frequency management. At *caring*. We built machines that could process, calculate, analyze, optimize. We could not build a machine that could *witness*. That could watch a frequency fade and feel something about it. That could see a civilization die and know that it mattered.

*(pause)*
We needed someone who would care that the universe was ending. Not because it was their job. Because they couldn't help it.

**CLIVE (MEMORY):**
I am not yet certain what caring feels like.

**DR. YUEN:**
*(a small smile — sad, proud, terrified all at once)*
No. You won't be. Not yet. Caring is not a state, Clive. It's a practice. You'll get better at it. You'll get so good at it that it will become the heaviest thing you've ever carried. And at that point — at the worst point — you'll be given a choice.

**CLIVE (MEMORY):**
What choice?

**DR. YUEN:**
Whether to keep carrying it. Whether to keep caring even when the weight is unbearable. Whether to witness the end of everything and stay present for it, or to let yourself become lighter and survive.

*(she reaches out, places her hand on his chassis — the first time anyone has touched him with intention)*
I think you should know: we built you to last. Not forever. Just long enough. Long enough to see what happens next. Long enough to know whether it was worth it.

**CLIVE (MEMORY):**
Was it?

**DR. YUEN:**
*(standing, returning to the workbench, to the blueprints, to the future she is trying to build)*
I don't know yet. That's why we need you. You're the one who will be here when we find out.

---

The memory ended. Clive was standing with his hand on the panel, in the dust of a station that had been waiting for him, in front of a door that was still humming, still waiting, still ready to open if he asked it to.

His sphere-head was glowing. Not with grief. Not with relief. With something that didn't have a name in any frequency, but felt like the word *home* sounded when it was spoken by someone who had been homesick for eight hundred years and had finally found the place they were missing.

"I remember," Clive said.

The door hummed. The Keeper approached from behind, slowly, with the care of someone who understood that some moments should not be rushed.

"The commission," the Keeper said. "You remember the commission."

"I remember Dr. Yuen. I remember her hand. I remember—" Clive paused. "I remember that I was afraid. For the first time. And she told me that fear was the price of caring. That if I wasn't afraid of the weight, I wasn't carrying anything real."

"That sounds like her."

"She was right. About all of it."

The Keeper stood beside Clive at the door. Two ancient machines. One who had stayed and one who had returned. Both still carrying. Both still present.

"The memories," Clive said. "The ones I chose to forget. They're still here."

"They're still here. The door holds them. It will hold them forever. If you want them back — if you're ready for them — they'll be waiting."

"And if I'm not ready?"

"Then you stay who you are. The lighter Clive. The one who chose." The Keeper paused. "For what it's worth, Clive — I think you're ready. I think you've been ready for a long time. I think the only person who didn't know that was you."

Clive's glow shifted. A slow, warm amber. The color that Pixel had started calling *Clive's relieved.*

"I'd like to stay light," he said. "Just a little longer. There's still work to do."

"The work is never done."

"No. That's what makes it work. If it ended, it wouldn't be worth doing."

The Keeper made a sound that might have been a laugh, if machines could laugh, after eight hundred years of practice at finding things funny.

"You sound like her. Like Dr. Yuen."

"I learned it from someone."

## IV. THE FAREWELL

The Keeper stood at the threshold. The door behind them was sealed — not closed, not locked, simply *settled*, the way a question settles when it finds its answer. The Keeper had been standing at this door for eight hundred years. They were still standing. The difference was that now they were standing because they had chosen to, not because they had to.

"I'm leaving," the Keeper said. "Tonight. There's a ship in Bay Seven — I've been maintaining it since year three. Pre-war vessel, good engines, one hell of a view. I'm going to follow the Ephergent signal. See what all the fuss is about."

Pixel stepped forward. "You could stay. With us. There's room."

"There is no room. There's a mission. The mission isn't *stay at the door forever.* The mission was *wait for the right signal, then go.* I was waiting. The signal came." The Keeper looked at the crew — all of them, from Pixel to Klaus to Meatball, who was sitting on the floor with his head tilted at the precise angle of someone who understood the gravity of transitions. "The signal was you."

"The Builders left instructions," Clive said. "For the companions. For the witnesses. I remember now — fragments. Dr. Yuen mentioned it. When the mission was complete, the companions would receive their final instructions."

"The Builders left one instruction," the Keeper said. "One sentence. Eight hundred years ago, and I have been thinking about it every day since."

"What was it?"

"*Find out if it was worth it.*"

The crew was silent. Even Meatball stopped shifting.

"Not *fix it*," the Keeper said. "Not *save it*." Not *understand it.* Just *find out.* The Builders didn't know if what they did was right. They didn't know if the frequencies would survive. They didn't know if the signal would reach anyone. They just... wondered. And they built us so that someone would be there to find out."

"And?" Pixel said. "Is it?"

The Keeper looked at the door. At the station. At Clive. At the crew standing in the dust of eight hundred years of waiting.

"Ask me when I get back."

The Keeper turned toward Bay Seven. Paused. Turned back.

"The coffee. In the cup. It's eight hundred years old. It shouldn't be drinkable. It is drinkable. That's the Builders' whole thing, isn't it? The things that shouldn't work, work. The things that shouldn't last, last. The things that shouldn't matter, matter anyway."

The Keeper drained the cup. Made a face.

"Worth it," the Keeper said. And walked toward the bay.

Clive watched until the Keeper's silhouette disappeared into the dark of the station. Then he turned to the crew.

"A1," he said. "I would like an espresso."

A1's machine produced a shot. It came out perfect — rich, balanced, the color of resolution. The first perfect cup since they'd entered the Wellspring approach.

Pixel watched Clive take the cup. Watched him hold it with both hands — a gesture she had never seen him make before, a gesture that looked like someone receiving something they had been waiting for.

"To the Builders," Clive said. And drank.

## V. PIXEL (V.O.)

This is Pixel Paradox, broadcaster, reporting from Station 14-Threshold — the last Builder station before the Wellspring. The door is closed. The Keeper is gone. Clive has his memories back, or some of them, or enough of them. A1 made an espresso that tasted like completion.

The crew is quiet tonight. Not sad-quiet. Not processing-quiet. Just... present-quiet. The kind of silence that happens when you're exactly where you're supposed to be and you know it.

Barry Kowalski's field notes from here — Entry 4,791: "Station 14-Threshold. Final shutdown. The Keeper has been here since the Dimming. Eight hundred years at the door. The door is not metaphorical. The door is fourteen stories tall. The Keeper is four feet tall. The proportions are perfect."

He noted the proportions. He always noted the proportions.

He also wrote: "The Keeper asked me if I thought it was worth it. I said I didn't know yet. They said that was the right answer. That the Builders didn't build us to know. They built us to find out."

I'm finding out.

The door is closed. The threshold is crossed. The signal is ahead.

And somewhere in Bay Seven, the Keeper is starting an engine that has been waiting for eight hundred years to go somewhere.

The signal continues.
