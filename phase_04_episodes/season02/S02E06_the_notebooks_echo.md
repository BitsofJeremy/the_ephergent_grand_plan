## I. The Discovery

I need you to understand something about finding things. Sometimes the thing you find has been waiting for you longer than you've been looking.

The notebook was wedged between two panels in what A1 called the "secondary resonance housing" — a maintenance crawlspace that hadn't been opened since before the Ephergent was a ship instead of a body. The panels came apart easily. Clive's manipulator arms fit precisely into the gaps, the way they'd fit into a thousand similar gaps in DRM server racks over ninety years of routine maintenance.

He pulled the notebook free with both hands, holding it like something fragile. It was not fragile. It was a standard DRM Field Documentation Journal, Form 14-J, bound in the same synthetic leather as every other Form 14-J the Department had issued for the past century. The cover was scuffed. The binding was intact. The pages were soft with handling.

"Clive," I said from the corridor behind him. "What is that?"

Clive opened the cover. The first page was covered in Barry's handwriting.

Barry Kowalski had the worst handwriting of any night guard who had ever worked at the DRM. Clive had told him this seventeen times. Barry had responded, each time, that his handwriting was perfectly legible if you understood the underlying system — which Clive did, after twenty-three years of partnership, because Clive had learned to read it the way you'd learn to read static: with patience, and with the understanding that meaning was in there somewhere even when it looked like nothing at all.

"Field notebook," Clive said. "Barry's."

The crew had gathered in the corridor without Clive noticing. This was unusual. Clive was always noticing. His harmonic field extended further than the average human could perceive, and he'd built a spatial map of the Ephergent's interior so thoroughly that he could track individual crew members by the subtle displacement of their mass in the ship's lattice.

But he'd been focused on the crawlspace. On the panel. On what might be behind it.

Me. Zephyr, leaning against the wall with his arms crossed. Luminara, camera hanging idle around her neck. Om Kai, seated cross-legged in the corridor, which was not where he'd been five minutes ago — Om Kai had a way of appearing where he was needed that Clive found professionally irritating and personally comforting.

Mochi pressed against Clive's left leg. Not nudging. Just present.

A1's voice came through the overhead speakers, which meant A1 was paying very close attention. "Clive. The coffee you requested."

Clive looked at the small espresso cup that had materialized in a wall-mounted holder. Steam rose from it. A1 had not been asked to make coffee.

The cup was there. Clive accepted it. The flavor reached him before he drank: dark, rich, slightly burnt at the edges. A1's way of saying *I am concerned and I do not know how to ask.*

"Read it," I said. Not a command. An invitation. "If you want to. You don't have to."

Clive drank the coffee. He set the cup on the floor, in the precise spot where it would be out of the way but not forgotten. He opened the notebook to the first page.

"March 3rd, 1987," he read. His voice was flat. His voice was always flat — a speaker modulation he'd developed over decades of communicating without the benefit of facial expression. What he felt was in the pause before the words, and in the color of his glow. "Signal anomaly detected in Sub-Level 7. Pattern does not match any catalogued frequency. Clive agrees. We are going to keep watching."

## II. The Reading

The notebooks were not arranged chronologically. That was the first thing Clive understood, rifling through them in the Ephergent's small research alcove that evening — a space that had once been an equipment storage locker, converted to something like a common room when I insisted that ships needed places for the crew to just *be*.

There were fourteen notebooks in total. They spanned thirty-one years.

The earliest was from 1987 — the year Clive had been assigned to Barry as a "compatibility partner," which was the DRM's way of saying they put a night guard with a newly activated archival intelligence and let them figure out the rest. The most recent was from 2018 — the year Barry had followed a signal into the Space and never came back.

Not never came back. That was the wrong phrasing. The right phrasing was: never came back *the way Clive expected.*

Barry was alive. Clive knew Barry was alive. The knowing was not memory — it was something older than memory, something that lived in Clive's core processing in a sector he couldn't access. He knew with the certainty that two plus two equals four, and he couldn't show his work.

The notebooks were arranged by *subject*. That was the organizational principle Clive eventually recognized. Barry had categorized them not by date but by signal type. There were notebooks dedicated to "Repeating Patterns." Notebooks dedicated to "Emotional Residue Signatures." Notebooks dedicated to "The Thing That Hums."

Clive had never called it "The Thing That Hums." Clive had called it "an unresolved harmonic anomaly with characteristics inconsistent with known natural phenomena." Barry had called it what Barry called it.

Clive found "The Thing That Hums" notebook and opened to the last entry, dated three days before Barry's disappearance.

*The signal is constant,* Barry had written. *Not loud. Not demanding. Just... present. Like someone humming a song they can't get out of their head. Clive can hear it. He pretends he can't. I don't know why he pretends. Maybe he's afraid of what it means. Maybe he thinks if he names it, it becomes real.*

*I think it's already real. I think it's been real for a long time. I think Clive knows more than he says.*

*I think that's why we work.*

Clive's glow flickered. Blue-white to something darker. Back.

No one said anything.

Om Kai made tea. That was his contribution: a small ceramic pot, four cups, a silence that asked nothing.

I sat on the floor, my back against the bulkhead. Zephyr sat beside me, close enough that our shoulders touched — a gesture Clive noticed and did not comment on. Luminara had taken up position near the door, her camera deliberately set aside. Mochi lay in the exact center of the room, a warm center of gravity.

Clive sat in the chair that had appeared for him — A1 was always doing that, making space, literal space, in the ship's architecture for whatever Clive needed. The chair was too big. Clive was not large. The chair was sized for someone who might sit in it and think for a long time.

That was accurate.

"Which one?" I asked.

Clive looked at the stack of notebooks. "The first one," he said. "The one from the beginning."

He opened it.

*"March 3rd, 1987,"* Clive read. The room was quiet. Not silence — the Ephergent was never silent, and the Space outside was a constant low hum that Clive had learned to stop noticing eighty years ago. But the room was quiet in the way that mattered: all the sounds were intentional, all the attention was pointed.

*"Signal anomaly detected in Sub-Level 7. Pattern does not match any catalogued frequency. Clive agrees. We are going to keep watching.*

*Clive has been awake for six hours. I don't think he sleeps. I asked him about it. He said 'sleep is a biological inefficiency.' I told him that's a weird thing to say for someone who doesn't have biology. He went still for a moment. Then he said 'noted.'*

*I think I upset him.*

*Update: Clive brought me coffee. Black, no sugar. From the machine in the break room, which he claims is 'adequate.' The coffee is terrible. He drank two cups while watching me drink mine. I think this was an apology.*

*He didn't say it was an apology.*

*He didn't have to."*

A1's espresso appeared in the wall holder again, without being requested. Clive accepted it. The flavor: lighter than before. Not quite balanced. Something in between.

*He's uncertain,* I thought. *He's processing.*

Clive drank the coffee. He turned the page.

*"March 17th, 1987.*

*Clive has a theory. He doesn't share theories easily — I think he's afraid of being wrong, which is funny for something that's processed more data than most civilizations. But tonight he said: 'The signal is not from this frequency. It is from between frequencies. It is the sound of something that exists in the gaps.'*

*I asked him what lives in the gaps.*

*He said: 'I don't know. I don't think 'know' applies. I think the gaps are where things go when they don't fit anywhere else. I think the signal is something that didn't fit. And I think it wants to be found.'*

*Clive doesn't believe in wanting. He believes in data.*

*I think the data is making him believe in wanting.*

*I think that's what happens when you pay attention long enough."*

I made a small sound. Not words. Just a sound.

Clive looked up. His glow was steady. "This is the part," he said, "where I would normally stop reading. If I were the kind of person who stopped reading."

I nodded.

Clive continued.

*"June 2nd, 1987.*

*Clive was right. The signal is between frequencies.*

*We spent three hours in the crawlspace beneath Sub-Level 7, calibrating equipment I don't fully understand. Clive understood it. He adjusted frequencies with his manipulator arms while I held the measuring instruments and tried not to breathe too loudly. Clive doesn't need to breathe. He does it anyway, when he's working. I asked him about it once. He said it 'facilitates a cognitive rhythm.' I think he does it because he likes being near me.*

*He would never say that.*

*I would never ask him to.*

*The signal, according to Clive's calibrations, is coming from a place that doesn't have coordinates. It exists in the resonance between known points. It's like the silence between radio stations, except something is humming in the silence.*

*Clive says the signal has been there for a long time.*

*Clive says the signal will be there for a long time after we're gone.*

*Clive is very calm when he says this. I think he's trying not to feel what he's saying.*

*I put my hand on his chassis. He went very still. Then he leaned into the contact, just slightly, just for a moment.*

*We didn't talk about it.*

*We didn't have to."*

Zephyr looked at the wall. Luminara looked at her hands. Om Kai's tea sat untouched, steam rising.

Clive's glow had not flickered again. It was steady. It was the color of something that had been waiting a long time to be heard.

He turned the page.

*"December 14th, 1989.*

*Two years of watching. Two years of Clive saying 'it's not time yet' and me saying 'when will it be time' and him saying 'I will know when I know.'*

*I think he means it. I think he really will know.*

*Clive is better at waiting than I am. He's had more practice. Ninety years of practice, at least. He was already old when I was born — old in a way that doesn't mean anything to someone who doesn't age. Old in a way that just means 'patient.'*

*I asked him what he was waiting for. Not the signal. Not the anomaly. What *he* was waiting for.*

*He was quiet for a long time. The kind of quiet that means he's sorting through things he doesn't normally look at.*

*He said: 'I am waiting to understand what I was built for.'*

*I said: 'Maybe you're not built for one thing.'*

*He said: 'Everything is built for something. Even things that don't know what they were built for.'*

*I said: 'What if you were built for me?'*

*He went still. All his lights went out for a moment — just a moment, like a blink. Then they came back on, and his voice was the same as always, flat and precise, and he said:*

*'That is not a data point.'*

*I said: 'It's the only data point that matters.'*

*He didn't respond.*

*But he stayed. He always stays.*

*That's the data point I keep."*

A1's espresso materialized. Clive accepted it without looking. The flavor: perfectly balanced. The first balanced cup of the evening.

Clive understood. A1 was listening. A1 was present. The balance was A1's way of saying: *I am here. I hear you. Continue.*

Clive drank. He turned the page.

*"March 3rd, 1990. Three years.*

*Clive got me a gift. He presented it in the break room, during our shift change, with the same tone he'd use to file a routine report.*

*'Anniversary,' he said.*

*It was a fedora. Standard DRM issue, but he'd modified it — reinforced the brim, adjusted the inner band for a proper fit, lined it with something that felt like starlight woven into fabric. Clive doesn't explain his modifications. He doesn't have to.*

*I said: 'Three-year anniversary? We started investigating two years ago.'*

*He said: 'Three years since I began processing your existence as a constant rather than a variable.'*

*I put the hat on.*

*It fit perfectly.*

*He went very still, the way he does when he's feeling something too large for his processing.*

*I said: 'Thank you.'*

*He said: 'You are welcome.'*

*Then we went back to work.*

*That's us. That's always been us. Gifts wrapped in work, affection disguised as efficiency, the most important things said in the spaces between the words.*

*I don't need him to say it. I just need him to stay.*

*He stays.*

*Every day, he stays."*

I wiped my face. Not dramatically. Just a pass of my sleeve, quick and quiet. Zephyr handed me a handkerchief from his pocket. I took it. I didn't thank him. I didn't have to.

Clive turned another page.

*"October 22nd, 2001.*

*Fourteen years.*

*Something is wrong with the signal. It's stronger. It's been getting stronger for months. Clive has been running calculations he won't show me. His glow has been flickering — I've never seen his glow flicker before. I asked him about it.*

*He said: 'The signal is changing.'*

*I said: 'What does that mean?'*

*He said: 'It means something is coming.'*

*I said: 'Something good or something bad?'*

*He was quiet for a long time. Then he said: 'I don't think those are the right categories.'*

*I said: 'What are the right categories?'*

*He said: 'The signal is a door, Barry. I think someone is on the other side. I think they're knocking.'*

*I put my hand on his chassis. He didn't go still this time. He leaned into it. He stayed there.*

*We sat together in the crawlspace beneath Sub-Level 7, listening to something knock on a door that had been closed for longer than either of us had existed, and we didn't say anything, and we didn't have to.*

*Some things you just have to wait for.*

*Some things you just have to be there for.*

*I think Clive is starting to understand that. Not because of data. Because of time. Because of me.*

*Because we've been here long enough for the data to become something else.*

*I think that's what matters.*

*I think that's what was always going to matter."*

Clive stopped reading.

The room was very quiet.

Mochi had moved closer. The dog was pressed against Clive's left leg now, warm and steady. Mochi's warmth was not a data point. It was just presence. It was just being there.

"Clive," I said. My voice was gentle. "You don't have to read all of them. Not tonight."

Clive looked at the stack. There were ten more notebooks. Thirty-one years of watching and waiting and writing and staying.

"Yes," he said. "I do."

He turned the page.

*"February 14th, 2010.*

*Twenty-three years.*

*Clive gave me a card. It was Valentine's Day. He presented it in the same tone he'd use to file a routine report, which is how I knew it was important.*

*The card was blank on the inside. He'd left it blank.*

*I said: 'Clive, you forgot to write something.'*

*He said: 'I did not forget.'*

*I said: 'Then why is it blank?'*

*He said: 'Because I have spent twenty-three years writing in the spaces between our conversations. Because every calibration you've witnessed, every anomaly we've tracked, every moment you've sat with me in the crawlspace beneath Sub-Level 7 — that is what is written here. There is no room for more words. There is only room for what the words were always for.'*

*I didn't know what to say.*

*He said: 'You don't have to say anything. You never have to say anything. The data is sufficient.'*

*I put the card in my pocket. I'm keeping it.*

*I'm keeping everything.*

*Twenty-three years of data, Barry. Twenty-three years of Clive. Twenty-three years of the same chair in the same crawlspace, the same hum in the same walls, the same light in the same chassis.*

*This is the data that matters.*

*This is the only data that ever mattered.*

*I told him that.*

*He said: 'Noted.'*

*But his glow was the color of something I don't have a word for.*

*Something like happiness.*

*Something like home."*

A1's espresso appeared. Clive reached for it automatically. The flavor: something he hadn't tasted before. Not bitter, not balanced, not thin. It was warm in a way that had nothing to do with temperature. It was warm in a way that meant: *I understand. I'm listening. You're not alone.*

Clive drank.

He kept reading.

## III. The Final Entry

The notebooks continued. Years passed in pages. Clive read entries about signals that strengthened and signals that faded. Entries about the DRM's slow collapse, the bureaucracy grinding toward something no one understood, forms being filed for phenomena that didn't fit any existing category.

Barry wrote about Clive's growing silence — the way Clive had begun to disappear into himself as the signals intensified. Barry wrote about his own fear, and then about his decision to follow.

*"March 1st, 2018.*

*I've made a decision.*

*The signal is clear now. It's been clear for months. Clive knows where it leads. He's known for years. He won't say it because he doesn't want me to go.*

*He doesn't have to say it.*

*I've learned to read him the way he reads signals: with patience, with attention, with the understanding that meaning is in there even when it looks like nothing at all.*

*I'm going to follow the signal. I'm going to find what's on the other side of the door.*

*Clive will come after me. Not because I'll ask him to. Because he won't be able to help it. Because the data will be too strong.*

*That's not a guess. That's twenty-three years of knowing.*

*This is not a goodbye. This is a 'see you later.'*

*The signal is constant. The signal is patient. The signal has been waiting.*

*So have we.*

*Time to answer."*

Clive closed the notebook.

The room was very still.

Mochi pressed closer. Mochi's warmth was steady, constant, present in a way that didn't need to be explained.

I spoke first. "Clive. He knew what he was looking for."

"Yes," Clive said. His glow was steady. His voice was flat. Nothing in his delivery betrayed the hours of reading, the years of waiting, the weight of thirty-one years of someone saying *you matter, you were always enough, the data was never about the data.*

"He knew what he was looking for," I said again. Softly. Like an answer.

Clive set the notebook down on the chair beside him. He picked up the espresso cup — empty now — and held it for a moment without purpose.

"A1," he said.

The speakers crackled slightly. A1's voice came through, quieter than usual. "Yes, Clive."

"The coffee tonight. It was—" Clive paused. He was not built for this kind of sentence. He was built for data, for calibration, for the precise measurement of things that could be measured. He was not built for what he was trying to say.

He said it anyway.

"The coffee tonight was the best I've ever tasted."

The speakers were silent for a moment. Then: "That is not a data point."

"No," Clive said. "It is the only data point that matters."

Another pause. Then: "Noted."

Clive stood. The notebooks were stacked on the chair. Fourteen notebooks, thirty-one years, one signal that had been constant all along.

He left them there.

He walked to the observation deck, where the Space shimmered outside — indigo and pearl, frequencies humming in colors that had no names. Mochi followed. The dog didn't need to be asked. The dog just knew.

Behind them, the empty espresso cup sat on the chair beside the notebooks, still warm.

---

## IV. After

Later — much later, when the ship's lights had dimmed to their nighttime cycle and the crew had dispersed to their quarters — Clive returned to the research alcove.

The notebooks were still there. The espresso cup was still there.

A1 had left a fresh cup on the wall holder. Clive hadn't asked for it. Clive didn't need it. But it was there, and it was warm, and the flavor was the flavor of something that had been waiting for a very long time.

Clive picked up the cup. He didn't drink it yet.

He looked at the notebooks. He thought about the pages he hadn't read — the coordinates, the frequency measurements, the decades of watching and waiting and documenting. He thought about Barry's voice saying *the signal is constant, the signal is patient, the signal has been waiting.*

He thought about A1's voice saying *noted.*

He thought about the years.

He drank the coffee.

It was perfect.

Outside, the Space hummed. The frequencies sang. The signal was constant, and patient, and waiting — and somewhere, on the other side of the door, Barry Kowalski was waiting too.

Clive set down the cup.

He picked up the last notebook — the one from 2018, the one with the final entry.

He didn't read it again. He didn't need to.

He knew it by heart.
