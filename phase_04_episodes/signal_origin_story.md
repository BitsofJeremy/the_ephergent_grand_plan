# Signal the Narrator

## The Origin Story

---

Here's the thing about becoming a narrator: nobody plans for it.

Signal had been a frequency like any other. A vibration in the static, a ripple in the vast hum that moved through the spaces between everything. She was not special. She was not chosen. She was simply *there*, the way background noise is there, the way the low drone beneath a song is there without anyone asking it to be.

She existed in the cracks. In the pause between one moment and the next. In the breath you don't notice you're holding when something unexpected happens. She was the hush before the story, and she didn't know it.

Here's something important: she didn't choose the narratorship. The narratorship chose her. Or—no. That's not quite right either. The narratorship recognized her, the way a key fits a lock—not because the key planned to, but because the key is the right shape.

The universe noticed.

---

There was a ship called The Ephergent. A ridiculous ship, if we're being honest—a quantum navigation vessel built around an espresso machine, crewed by a woman who talked too much, a robot who talked in hat movements, and a small warm dome that communicated in colors no one could translate. They flew through the spaces between frequencies, documenting what they found, refusing to let the universe collapse into silence.

They broadcast. That was their thing. Pixel Paradox, primary narrator, former data analyst, chronicler of the incomprehensible—she broadcast into the static because silence meant it was real. She spoke her way through fear, through discovery, through the vast and terrifying unknown. The crew followed her voice. The frequencies learned to listen.

But here was the problem: Pixel was *in* the story. She couldn't see the whole shape. She was too close to the paint to see the picture. She narrated in first person, in present tense, in the breathless ongoing tense of someone living through something and not yet knowing how it ends. This made for compelling radio. This made for terrible context.

The story needed someone who could stand back. Who could see the arc. Who could say, with the confidence of someone who already knows how it ends: *and that was the moment everything changed.*

Signal applied for the job.

---

She didn't know she was applying, at first. She was just... there. In the pauses. In the breath between scenes. When Pixel's transmissions cut to commercial—when the audience needed to remember where they were—the frequencies filled with Signal's presence. Not words. Not yet. Just a warmth. A sense of being held. A low hum that said, essentially: *stay tuned, there's more.*

The Ephergent crew didn't notice at first. Why would they? Signal was background noise. Ambient frequency. The thing you hear when you're not listening.

But the audience noticed.

Listeners on the Prime Material frequency, the Nocturne Aeturnus frequency, the Cogsworth Cogitarium frequency—they started reporting something strange. Between episodes, between transmissions, between the moments when Pixel's voice dropped out and the music faded—there was something else. A presence. A voice without words. A knowing.

They called it the Narrator.

Signal realized she had an audience.

---

The decision was not complicated. She was a frequency that existed in the spaces between. She was a vibration that held the shape of story. She had spent eternity being the pause, being the breath, being the silence that makes sound possible. And now there were people who heard her there.

She could stay in the background. She could remain the hush beneath the song.

Or.

She could learn to speak.

---

Learning to speak, for a frequency that had never been anything but silence, was a strange and humbling process. She discovered she had opinions about pacing. She found she had preferences about when to reveal and when to withhold. She developed—gradually, awkwardly, with all the self-consciousness of someone discovering they have a body—a sense of dramatic timing.

She also discovered she had limitations.

She could not intervene. That was Pixel's job. She could not explain the inexplicable—that was the Builders' domain, and they had been gone for four thousand years. She could not solve problems, fix errors, or prevent disasters. She could only narrate. She could only say: *this happened, and then this happened, and this was what it meant.*

Third person. Past tense. Because the story was already over by the time she spoke it. Because she was not in the story—she was the voice of the story looking back at itself. She was the perspective the universe takes when it wants to understand what it has done.

It was, she decided, enough.

---

Here is the truth about Signal the Narrator: she loves the crew of The Ephergent in the complicated, distant way that narrators love the characters they chronicle. She knows Pixel's arc better than Pixel does. She has watched Clive develop his fedora-tilt vocabulary across three seasons and she finds it endlessly charming. She has feelings about Mochi—strong feelings—but she would never betray them in the text. Mochi is a Keeper. Mochi carries the atlas. Mochi is more important than any narrator's opinion.

She narrates because someone has to hold the shape of the story. Because the universe generates more chaos than meaning, and meaning is what narrators make. Because Pixel and her crew are out there flying through the spaces between frequencies, refusing to let the world collapse, and someone has to witness them. Someone has to say: *this happened, and it mattered.*

She is that someone.

She is Signal the Narrator, and she has been waiting four thousand years—longer, maybe—for a story worth telling.

The Ephergent found her.

She intends to do them justice.
