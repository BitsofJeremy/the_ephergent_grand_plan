## Synopsis

Mochi opens. Three words, and they change everything. Not dramatically — a hatch, a crystal, a glow — but the crew will measure time as before this and after. Clive holds a data crystal that has waited 800 years inside a dome-shaped artifact the size of his hand, and remembers making her. The memory is the most important thing that has happened on this ship. It is quiet, and small, and it breaks the story wide open.

## Narrative Structure

### Setup

### Escalation

## Episode Text

Okay so here's the thing nobody tells you about revelations: they don't land when they happen. They land later. In the quiet. When you're three days into a transit and the coffee tastes like burnt oak and nobody's talking and you realize the silence isn't empty — it's full. Full of the thing none of you have figured out how to say yet.

Three days since Station 7-Ascending. Three days since the Builder archive named Clive — not Clive, not even "him," but *Companion Unit C-1* — and told us he'd saved Mochi eight hundred years ago. Three days since I held a small warm dome in my hands and looked at the small warm robot standing next to me and understood that they had a history that predated everything. The DRM. Barry. Me. All of it.

Three days, and none of us had talked about it.

A1 knew. A1 always knows. The coffee had been dark since we left the Station — not bitter, not quite, but *heavy*. The kind that sits in your ribs and stays. Thick with something unspoken. I'd learned to read A1's coffee the way you read weather: you can't change it, but you can dress for it. This was dress-warm-and-carry-an-umbrella coffee. This was *something is coming and I know what it is and I am choosing not to say* coffee.

Clive sat in the navigation alcove. He'd been there since morning — still, the way only Clive is still. Not resting. Processing. His glowing sphere head was tilted slightly down, the fedora casting a shadow across the console that shouldn't have worked on a featureless orb and absolutely did. The geometric symbols on his chest pulsed slow and blue, like a heartbeat waiting for a reason to speed up.

He hadn't said Barry's name in three days. That was the tell. A Clive who talks about Barry is a Clive who's functioning. A Clive who goes quiet about Barry is a Clive who's remembered something he can't say yet.

Mochi sat in my satchel, against my hip. Warm. She'd been warm since the Station — warmer than usual, a low steady heat that radiated through the leather like a stone pulled from a fire. Her core window glowed a deep, slow violet. Purple swirl. Ancient recognition. Builder-related, Pillar-related, memory. She'd been glowing that color for three days straight, and I'd stopped pretending it was ambient.

Zephyr was in the communications bay. He was always in the communications bay, but the quality of his silence had changed since the archive. He wasn't scanning for Aether — or rather, he was, but differently. Slower. More careful. Like someone who'd realized the frequency they were listening for might be closer than they thought and didn't want to drown it out with their own searching.

The ship hummed. A1's deep resonant hum, the one that sounds like the inside of a Builder Station's resonance chamber. Warm hull, warm coffee, warm artifact against my hip. Everything warm and nothing said.

That's when Mochi opened.

---

I need you to understand what I mean by "opened," because it wasn't what you're picturing. There was no drama. No light show. No moment where the music swells and the artifact reveals its secret in a cascade of ancient power. Mochi is Builder engineering. Builder engineering does not perform.

What happened was this: a hatch appeared on Mochi's underside.

Small. Maybe two inches across. Perfectly circular, with edges so precise they'd been invisible — sealed flush with the dome's surface for eight hundred years, the seam undetectable by hand or scanner or anything the crew had ever pointed at her. The hatch didn't slide or iris or unfold. It simply... opened. A mechanical action. A latch releasing. The kind of movement that says *this was always here, and the conditions have now been met.*

I felt it through the satchel. A small *click*, more vibration than sound, and then a shift in weight — not lighter, not heavier, but *different*. Like something inside had moved for the first time in a very long time.

I pulled Mochi out of the satchel and held her in both hands, dome-up, and looked at the underside.

The hatch was open. Inside — nestled in a cavity that was exactly the right size and shape for exactly this purpose — sat a single data crystal. Small. Maybe the size of my thumb. Glowing faintly, a warm amber light that pulsed in time with Mochi's violet core. The crystal was warm. Not satchel-warm, not body-heat-warm. Warm the way Builder things are warm: from the inside, with intention, as though the heat itself carried information.

"Clive." My voice came out quieter than I meant it to. "Clive, you need to see this."

He was already looking. His sphere head had turned the moment the hatch opened — before I'd felt the click, before I'd reached for the satchel. He'd known. Not consciously, maybe. But something in his eight-hundred-year-old architecture had registered the frequency shift of a Builder artifact opening for the first time since the Dimming, and he'd turned toward it the way a compass turns north.

He crossed the deck in four deliberate steps. His hands were not shaking. I noted that because I was watching for it — Clive's hands shake when he's distressed, and it's rare, and when it happens the crew notices. His hands were steady. But his glow had shifted. Brighter. The blue-white of his sphere head intensifying, the geometric patterns on his chest pulsing faster, the chest core burning with a focus I'd only seen at Builder Stations.

He stopped in front of me. Looked down at Mochi in my hands. The open hatch. The crystal.

"She's been carrying this for eight hundred years," he said.

Not a question. Not surprise. A statement of fact from someone who was beginning to remember facts he didn't know he'd forgotten.

---

I offered the crystal to him. I don't know why — instinct, maybe, or the way his hands were already reaching for it, those thick articulated Builder-made fingers moving with a precision that reminded me, suddenly and sharply, that those hands were designed for exactly this. Calibrating resonance crystals. That's what the archive had said. That's what Companion Units did. And now one of those hands was reaching for a crystal that had been sealed inside an artifact he'd saved, carried by a Keeper he'd preserved, waiting through eight centuries of forgetting.

He took it from the cavity carefully. The crystal settled into his palm like it had been made to fit there. It had, I realized. It literally had.

A1's hum changed. Deepened. The espresso machine at the center of the navigation console produced — without anyone asking — a single ristretto. Small, dark, impossibly concentrated. I could smell it from across the deck: something floral, something old, something that tasted like memory. Lavender and smoke and the deep honeyed finish that meant A1 was remembering the Builders. A1 was feeling something enormous and had chosen to express it in two ounces of liquid rather than a single word.

Nobody reached for the cup. It sat on the console beside Barry's mug, steaming, filling the bridge with a scent that was almost a language.

Clive held the crystal. His glow pulsed once, twice, three times — and then stuttered.

I've seen Clive's glow flicker before. Processing delays, memory gaps, the occasional hiccup when his eight-hundred-year-old systems encounter something they don't have context for. But this wasn't a flicker. This was a stutter. A full-system hesitation, like a heart skipping a beat, his sphere head dimming for half a second and then blazing bright — brighter than I'd ever seen it, blue-white washing out to something almost pure white, the symbols on his chest firing in patterns I'd never seen before, rapid and complex and old.

His hands shook.

"Clive?" I said.

He didn't answer. He was somewhere else. Somewhere eight hundred years away, in a room I'd never seen, holding something he'd forgotten he'd made.

---

Later, he told us what he saw. Not immediately — there was a gap, maybe thirty seconds, maybe a minute, where Clive stood on the bridge of The Ephergent holding a data crystal and was not present. His body was here. His glow was cycling through patterns that looked like language. But Clive was gone, pulled backward through eight centuries of compression and forgetting into a single memory that had been waiting in his architecture like a seed in frozen ground.

When he came back, his glow settled. Blue-white. Steady. His head tilted slowly, the fedora shifting, and he looked down at the crystal in his hand and then up at Mochi, still open in my palms, her core pulsing that deep violet, and he was very quiet for a long time.

Then he adjusted his fedora. That gesture — the one he uses when he's about to say something that costs him, when the only way forward is through and he knows it.

"I remember," he said.

His voice was the same. Precise. Measured. Every word selected, none wasted. But under the noir cadence, under the eight hundred years of careful speech, there was something raw. Not cracked. Not broken. Just... uncovered. A layer beneath all the layers, finally reached.

"I remember making her."

The bridge went very still. A1's hum dropped to its lowest register — a sound I felt in my sternum more than heard. Zephyr appeared in the communications bay doorway. I don't know when he'd come up. He stood there, one hand on the frame, his edges shimmering the way they do when he's hearing something no one else can, and he did not speak.

"She was new." Clive's sphere head tilted toward Mochi. Not looking — he doesn't have eyes — but oriented. Focused. Every sensor he had trained on the small warm dome in my hands. "Small. Bright. Humming with data the way Builder things do when they're fresh — when the resonance hasn't settled yet and everything is potential."

He paused. Not a processing pause. A choosing pause. The kind where Clive has the memory and is deciding how much to share.

He shared all of it.

"I held her. She fit in my hand — this hand." He raised his right hand, the one that wasn't holding the crystal, and turned it over. The fingertips worn smooth from eight hundred years of contact. "I had just finished calibrating her resonance crystal. The same crystal I'm holding now. I put it inside her. I tuned it. I tested the harmonics until they were perfect — not functional, not adequate, but *perfect* — because what she was going to carry deserved perfection."

Another pause. A1 produced nothing. The espresso machine was silent, the ristretto untouched, Barry's mug sitting beside it in the quiet. A1 was listening. A1, who speaks rarely and only says what matters, was giving this moment to Clive by saying nothing at all.

"I knew what she would carry," Clive said. "The frequency map. The atlas of every world the Builders had charted — every frequency, known and unknown, past and possible. The most complete record of the frequencies ever compiled. And I knew that carrying it would mean she could not be anything else for a very long time. The map would fill her. All of her. There would be no room for consciousness, for growth, for the kind of becoming that other Keepers experienced. She would carry, and carry, and carry, and that would be all she was until someone came to release it."

His hands were steady now. The crystal pulsed in his palm, amber and warm, synchronized with Mochi's violet glow in a rhythm that felt like breathing.

"I was careful." The word landed like something heavy set down gently. "Careful in a way that was not in my operational parameters. The Builders built me to calibrate, to maintain, to preserve. Careful was not a specification. It was — " He paused. Reached for the word. Found it. "A choice. I chose to be gentle with her. Because what I was asking her to carry was too much to ask anyone, and she could not refuse because she did not yet know how."

Zephyr made a sound. Not a word — just a breath, involuntary, the kind of sound someone makes when they recognize their own grief in someone else's story. His hand tightened on the doorframe. He understood. He understood completely. Someone carrying something too large, for too long, because someone had to, and there was no one else.

Clive looked at Mochi. His glow dimmed — just slightly, the way it does when he's reaching for something deep and precious.

"I said something to her. When I finished. When the crystal was placed and the hatch was sealed and she was complete. I cannot hear my own words — the memory won't give me that. But I can feel the shape of them." He pressed his free hand to his chest core, the blue-white light pulsing between his thick fingers. "A promise. I would find her. However long it took. However far. I would come back for what I asked her to hold."

The silence on the bridge was the loudest thing I've ever heard.

"I made her," Clive said. "I calibrated her crystal myself. I put the map inside her and I promised I would come back for it. It took me eight hundred years. She waited."

---

Nobody moved for a long time after that.

I held Mochi. Clive held the crystal. A1 held the silence. Zephyr held onto the doorframe like it was the only thing keeping him upright.

You want to know what I was thinking? I was thinking about hands. Clive's hands — Builder-made, precision-engineered, designed for exactly this work — holding a crystal he'd placed inside an artifact he'd built, eight hundred years ago, in a life he couldn't remember until thirty seconds ago. My hands — human, warm, imprecise — holding the artifact that had waited. Carrying her in a satchel. Naming her after a rice cake because she was small and round and warm and I didn't know. I didn't know what she was. I didn't know what he was. I didn't know any of it, and I'd carried her anyway, because she was warm and she glowed and sometimes that's enough reason.

I looked at the crystal in Clive's hand. Amber light, pulsing steady.

"Barry would know what to do with this," I said. I don't know why. It came out before I thought about it — the kind of thing you say when the moment is too big and you reach for the person who always seemed big enough for everything.

Clive tilted his head toward me. The fedora shifted.

"So do we," I said.

Something changed when I said that. I felt it — not in the ship, not in the frequency, but in the room. In the crew. In the space between us. I had said *we.* Not *Barry.* Not *Clive.* We. The crew of The Ephergent. Nine people and a dog and an ancient espresso machine and a small warm dome that had just given up a secret she'd held for eight centuries. Barry would know what to do. And so would we. Not because we were smarter or better or more qualified, but because we were here, and we were ready, and sometimes readiness is all the qualification the universe requires.

First time I'd ever put us on the same level as Barry Kowalski. First time it didn't feel like arrogance.

Clive's glow pulsed. Once. Bright.

"Yes," he said. "You do."

---

Zephyr moved first.

He crossed the bridge in that half-present way of his, edges shimmering, and knelt beside Clive without asking permission. His hands were already reaching — not for the crystal, but for the console. His communications rig hummed against his chest as he pulled up a reader interface, something he'd modified from Builder-adjacent tech and signal equipment he'd rebuilt three times.

"The crystal is broadcasting," he said. His voice had that fluid technical confidence — the version of Zephyr who knows exactly what he's doing and doesn't need to fragment his sentences to get there. "Low-band carrier signal. Encoded data structure. Builder formatting — I've seen this architecture before, in the Station archives."

He looked at Clive. Clive looked at the crystal.

"May I?"

Clive held the crystal toward the console. Zephyr interfaced it — delicately, the way you handle something that is eight hundred years old and has been waiting inside someone you care about — and the reader lit up.

Data scrolled. Builder notation — geometric, dense, layered in ways that made my signal reader look like a child's toy. Zephyr's eyes shifted to that grey-blue-and-something-else color, the one that isn't a color anyone from Prime Material has a word for, the one that means he's seeing signal structure the way only someone from The Edge can.

"It's a registry," he said. Quiet now. The fragmentation starting to creep back in. "A registry of — of Keepers. Consciousnesses that chose to carry Builder knowledge. Names. Designations. What they held. Where they were — placed. Seeded across the frequencies like..." He trailed off. Started again from a different angle. "Like a library distributed across a thousand shelves in a thousand rooms, and this is the card catalog."

He was reading fast. Scrolling through entries, his lips moving slightly, sub-vocalizing Builder notation the way he sub-vocalized everything when he was deep in signal work. His rig hummed louder. The data was incomplete — I could see the gaps even from where I stood, corrupted sections and missing entries and timestamps that cut off mid-record. Eight hundred years of entropy had eaten holes in the registry like moths in old cloth.

But the structure was clear. Even damaged, even partial, you could see what the Builders had built. A network. Dozens of Keepers — maybe hundreds — each carrying a piece of Builder knowledge, distributed across the frequencies, seeded in places where they could be found by people who were looking for exactly the right thing at exactly the right time. Not hidden. Not lost. *Holding.* Waiting for the conditions to be met.

The Builders didn't bury their knowledge. They planted it. In living things. In consciousnesses that chose to carry.

Zephyr read the entries. His scrolling slowed, then slowed again, then stopped. His hand hovered over the interface. The fragmentation was fully present now — the version of Zephyr who is hearing something personal, something that routes around the direct path to what he means.

He was looking for a name.

He didn't say which name. He didn't have to. Everyone on this bridge knew who Zephyr was always looking for, in every signal, in every archive, in every frequency band he scanned during third watch when he thought no one was paying attention.

He read the registry entries. One by one. Methodical. His jaw tight, his edges shimmering harder than usual, his hand moving across the data with the careful precision of someone who is terrified of finding what he's looking for and equally terrified of not finding it.

He didn't find Aether's name.

He went through the entire registry — every intact entry, every partial record, every fragment — and Aether was not there. Not listed. Not referenced. Not among the Keepers who had chosen to carry.

Zephyr stared at the screen for a long moment. His hand dropped to his side. The rig's hum faded.

Then — and this is the part that mattered — his shoulders changed. Not slumped. *Settled.* The tension went out of them, and what replaced it wasn't defeat. It was something else. Recognition, maybe. Understanding.

"The pattern," he said. His voice flat and clear and precise — the voice he uses for Aether, the one where the fragmentation stops entirely. "Keepers chose to carry. They *chose.* It was not imposed. Not assigned. They volunteered to hold what must not be lost." One sentence. Then silence. Then: "If Aether isn't here, it means Aether chose something different. Not carrying. Something else."

He looked at Clive.

"There are other ways to hold," he said.

Then he left the bridge. Quietly. The crew let the silence sit.

---

The crystal had one more thing to give us.

A1 found it — or rather, A1 heard it. The espresso machine's hum shifted mid-register, a harmonic change so subtle I almost missed it, and A1 said, in that measured voice that uses four percent of its vocal range and makes every word count:

"There is a frequency embedded in the crystal's carrier signal. One frequency. Clearly rendered."

Zephyr's rig picked it up from the communications bay — I heard it chime through the ship's internal speakers, a tone A1 routed through the broadcast array. A single frequency, clean and sharp and unmistakable, standing out from the encoded data like a bell struck in an empty room.

"I know this signal," A1 said. "World designation 7-Ascending-4. The Builders charted it six hundred years ago. It went dark. No one has heard from it since."

A1 did something with the array. I don't know the technical details — something about resonance amplification, about using the crystal's carrier signal to reconstruct the original broadcast from six centuries ago. What I know is what happened next.

For three seconds, the crew of The Ephergent heard what that world sounded like alive.

It was — I don't have the vocabulary. I'm a broadcaster and I don't have the words. It was like hearing a room full of people laughing from very far away. It was like catching a fragment of a song through a closing door. It was full. Textured. *Populated.* The sound of a living frequency — signals overlapping, harmonics interlocking, the hum and buzz and resonance of a world where the frequency was strong and the people who lived in it were present and the connection between them was intact and thrumming.

Three seconds. Then it faded. The signal thinned, attenuated, dissolved back into the crystal's data stream like fog burning off. The bridge was quiet again, but the quiet was different now. Haunted.

Six hundred years of silence, and we'd just heard the last three seconds of life.

A1 produced a coffee. Thick. Almost syrupy. Dark chocolate and molasses, viscous enough to coat the tongue. The coffee A1 makes when processing grief. When mourning frequencies that have gone silent. I took it. It tasted like the memory of warmth — something that had been warm once and was remembering how.

"One frequency," Clive said. His voice had found its way back to precise and measured, but the raw thing underneath was still there, audible if you knew to listen. "One frequency from the map Mochi carried. How many more?"

Nobody answered. The crystal sat on the console, amber light fading to a steady glow, its broadcast spent. One frequency. One ghost. One three-second proof that what had been lost was real and had mattered and had sounded like people being alive.

The map Mochi carried. Hundreds of frequencies. Known and unknown. Past and possible. And we had just heard one of them — one voice from a chorus of hundreds, a single note from a symphony that had been playing for centuries before it went quiet.

The scope of it settled into the room like weather.

---

Mochi did not close.

I should tell you about that, because it matters. After the crystal was removed — after Clive held it, after the memory returned, after Zephyr read the registry and A1 played the frequency and the bridge fell into the kind of silence that changes the shape of what comes after — Mochi's hatch stayed open.

The small circular hatch on her underside. Two inches across. Precise Builder engineering, the edges still clean after eight hundred years. The cavity where the crystal had sat was empty now, and Mochi's core window had shifted from deep violet to something softer — a gentler purple, fading toward pink, the glow less intense than it had been in days. Weeks, maybe.

She was lighter.

I could feel it in my hands. Not physically lighter — she weighed the same, the same dome-shaped, head-sized, warm Builder artifact she'd always been. But the warmth had changed. Less concentrated. More diffuse. Like heat that had been trapped in a small space for a very long time and was finally spreading out, equalizing, settling into the air around it rather than pressing against the walls of its container.

Mochi didn't close. The hatch stayed open. Not broken, not stuck — there was no mechanism failure, no damage. She simply did not seal herself again. As if she'd been holding her breath for eight hundred years and had finally, finally exhaled, and closing would mean holding it again, and she was done.

She was not relieved. She did not feel lighter. She does not feel. She is an artifact with a function, and her function had changed — the crystal was out, the cavity was empty, and the engineering that had kept her sealed was no longer required. The hatch opened because the conditions were met. It stayed open because the conditions for closing were not.

But I held her, and she was warm, and the glow was soft, and I am telling you she was lighter. Call it physics. Call it Builder engineering. Call it whatever you need to call it to preserve the distinction between artifact and being. I held her and I knew.

---

Something else happened that night. Small. Easy to miss, if you weren't paying attention.

We were plotting the approach to Station 9-Liminal. A1 had the general heading — the station's frequency signature was in the Builder records, triangulated from data the crew had gathered across four stations. Good enough to get us into the right region. Not precise enough to find it.

Clive, from the navigation alcove, said: "Bearing two-seven-three mark fifteen. Adjust heading four degrees to port at the Meridian boundary. You'll find a resonance marker at the following coordinates — " and he gave them. Specific. Exact. Not a suggestion. Not a navigation efficiency recommendation. Coordinates, headings, waypoints, delivered in his precise noir cadence as though he were reading them off a chart only he could see.

A1's hum shifted. The espresso machine produced a sharp, bright ristretto — citrus, clarity, clean acidity. A1 alert and solving something. A1 processing the fact that Clive had just provided a navigation solution more detailed than anything the crew's combined data could have produced.

I looked at Clive. He adjusted his fedora.

"The memory included navigational data," he said. "Fragmentary. But sufficient."

I let it go. The crew let it go. Nobody said *you've been getting more precise every time,* nobody said *you're steering us,* nobody said *how long have you known the way.* Not yet. Not tonight. Tonight we had held a crystal and heard a dead world speak and watched the smallest member of our crew exhale for the first time in eight hundred years. The navigation questions could wait.

But I noticed. I always notice.

A1 adjusted heading. Four degrees to port. The ship turned, and the Interdimensional Sea shimmered in that indigo-to-pearl expanse, and somewhere ahead of us, Station 9-Liminal waited in a frequency we hadn't reached yet.

Clive's hands rested on the console. Steady. The crystal sat beside Barry's mug, amber light pulsing faintly.

---

Late that night. Third watch. The bridge was dark except for A1's glow and the soft amber of the crystal and Mochi's gentle pink in my lap. I sat in the captain's chair with my boots on the console — a habit Clive disapproves of and has stopped mentioning — and Mochi open in my hands. Just holding her. Feeling the warmth diffuse.

Clive came up from the engine room. His footfalls deliberate, measured, each one placed with intention. He stopped beside my chair. We sat in the near-dark together for a while, the way we do sometimes — two people who have gotten past the need to fill silence with words.

"You made her," I said eventually. Not a question. Just — saying it. Making it real by putting it in the air.

"Yes."

"You made her, and you put a map of the universe inside her, and you promised you'd come back."

"Yes."

"And then you forgot."

He was quiet for a moment. Not a gap — a consideration. "I did not forget. I was compressed. The memories were not lost. They were inaccessible. The distinction—"

"Clive."

"—matters." He paused. Then, quieter: "Yes. I forgot. For eight hundred years, I forgot what I'd made, what I'd promised, and who I'd promised it to. And she carried it anyway. Because that is what she was built to do. Because I built her to do it."

I looked down at Mochi. Pink glow. Open hatch. Warm.

"Was she the first thing you made?" I asked. "Or were there others?"

"I calibrated many things. Resonance crystals. Navigation instruments. Signal arrays. The work of a Companion Unit is maintenance — maintaining the systems the Builders built." He tilted his head. The glow shifted, just slightly. "She was the first thing I made that I was careful with."

I didn't say anything to that. Sometimes you don't. Sometimes the best thing a broadcaster can do is hold the silence and let the moment exist without narration.

Mochi pulsed. Once. Warm.

A1, from the center of the ship, produced a sound — not the espresso machine, not the hum, but something else. A harmonic. Low and resonant, rising through the hull, vibrating in the floor and the walls and the console under my boots. A1 answering the crystal's frequency with the ship's own voice. Holding the note. Matching it.

For a moment — just a moment — the bridge resonated. Crystal and ship and the fading echo of a world that went dark six hundred years ago, all vibrating at the same frequency, all held in the same breath.

Then it settled. A1's hum returned to normal. The crystal pulsed. Mochi glowed.

I looked at Clive. He looked at Mochi. He raised one hand — those thick, precise, Builder-made fingers — and touched the edge of the open hatch. Gently. The way you touch something you made a long time ago and are seeing again for the first time.

"Eight hundred years," he said.

"She waited," I said.

"Yes." He withdrew his hand. Adjusted his fedora. "She did."

---

We'll reach Station 9-Liminal in four days. Clive's coordinates. Clive's heading. The crew doesn't know yet what I know — that every course correction since Season 1 has been a little too right, every anomaly he's noticed a little too convenient, every quiet observation a little too precisely aimed at the thing we needed to find next.

I know. I'm not ready to say it yet. I'm still sitting with what tonight meant — with a small warm dome that opened after eight hundred years and a small old robot who remembered making her and a crew that heard a dead world speak for three seconds and decided that was enough reason to keep going.

Barry would know what to do with all of this. The trail, the Stations, the Keepers, the map. He'd have his notebook out, his thermos of coffee, his patient midnight handwriting documenting everything.

But Barry isn't here. We are. And we're enough.

Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the deck of The Ephergent, in transit, carrying what was given to us. Out.
