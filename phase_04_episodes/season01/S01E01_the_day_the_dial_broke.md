
# The Day the Dial Broke

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The DRM headquarters is, by all appearances, the most unremarkable government building in the city. Beige walls. Drop ceiling. The faint smell of recycled air and old carpet. Pixel Paradox sits at her desk in the data analysis wing, electric blue hair in a messy bun, surrounded by manila folders, a cracked monitor running spreadsheets, and a filing cabinet she's never seen the bottom of. Through the windows: a normal city skyline, morning traffic, pigeons on the ledge. The Department of Reality Maintenance — she's never been entirely sure what they're maintaining, but the pay is decent and nobody bothers her. Meatball sleeps under her desk, paws twitching. Clive — a knee-high robot of ancient bronze alloy, his glowing sphere head topped with a battered fedora — stands at his usual post. She could swear he was closer to the window yesterday. He's by the filing cabinet now. She doesn't think about it.

In her jacket pocket, against her hip, something is warm. She notices it for a half-second — warmth she'd attribute to body heat, to the jacket, to anything other than what it actually is. She files it away. Later. There will be time later. There isn't, but her brain hasn't caught up to that yet.

A1's chrome espresso machine body occupies the corner of her cubicle, steam curling from his spout as he brews her third cortado of the morning. He's a smart appliance — office-issued, supposedly just a high-end coffee maker — but sometimes the steam curls in patterns that look almost deliberate. Sometimes a faint blue flicker runs across his surface, like a glitch in the chrome. The coffee is always perfect. *Always.* "The variance report for Sector 12 is done, Pixel. I took the liberty of auto-filling the compliance metrics. You're welcome."

"You're a lifesaver, A1. Literally. That form has seventeen subsections."

"Eighteen. They added one last quarter. 'Subsection R: Ambient Environmental Unease Survey.' I took the liberty of checking 'yes.'"

Pixel snorts. She's never understood what half these forms actually measure. Ambient variance. Environmental compliance. Sector frequency reports. It sounds important and means nothing to her. She files them. That's the job.

Then the hum changes.

It starts low — a vibration in the floor, in the walls, in the fillings of Pixel's teeth. Not like a building hum. Like something *underneath* the building, something vast, waking up. Every fluorescent light in the office flickers simultaneously. Her monitor cracks — not from impact, but from the *inside*, like the screen disagreed with itself. Meatball bolts upright, ears flat, whimpering at something, staring at a point in the air where there's nothing to see. And Clive — her *desk companion* — begins pulsing light from his sphere head in a precise, deliberate rhythm. Pixel stares at it. Her brain, trained by years of idle pattern recognition, reads it before she can stop herself: *DEEP FAULT. RUN.*

She looks at Clive. At a *robot* that just communicated a message. "What the—"

The wall behind her phases. There's no other word for it. One moment it's beige drywall. The next it's glass. Then stone. Then something that looks like coral made of light. Then back to drywall. Then all of them at once. The floor buckles — not cracking, but *rippling*, like the building is a reflection in water and someone just threw a stone into it. Pixel's chair slides sideways. Filing cabinets topple. Forms scatter into rifts that open and close in the air like mouths. Through the chaos, Pixel sees things that her brain rejects outright — geometries that fold in on themselves, spaces that are bigger on the inside, a hallway that leads in two directions simultaneously.

THIS ISN'T POSSIBLE. None of this is possible.

### Escalation

Pixel doesn't think. She grabs A1's espresso machine body — heavier than she remembers, warm with residual brewing heat, and for a split second his chrome surface flashes with blue light brighter than any appliance glitch — and Clive scrambles up onto her shoulder, his small legs finding purchase on her jacket, his sphere head glowing steady against her neck. Meatball is already at the door, barking at her to *move*. They run through corridors that have become nightmares — a hallway that stretches to infinity, then compresses to nothing, then is just a hallway again. DRM employees are screaming, running, some frozen in place staring at walls that have become windows into places that shouldn't exist. A filing cabinet falls into a rift and doesn't come back. The break room is gone — not destroyed, just *absent*, replaced by a space that hurts to look at.

Pixel's mind is a siren of *this can't be happening this can't be happening this can't be happening.*

They reach a section of the building she's never been in. A door she's never seen. Behind it: a bay. An *evacuation* bay. With a vessel. A lifeboat. With a hatch, an engine, an actual *cockpit*. In a building she thought processed regulatory paperwork.

"What the HELL is this?" she breathes. But Meatball is already inside, and the building behind them is folding like origami, and there's no time.

She shoves A1 onto the cramped deck. Clive drops from her shoulder to the console, his feet finding purchase on the metal surface with a soft click. For a moment — just a moment — Clive's glow dims. His sphere head tilts toward the console panel beneath him, and his surface hums against the metal. Pixel sees it: a flicker of something that isn't surprise. Something that isn't recognition either. More like... home. Then Clive straightens, adjusts his fedora, and says nothing. Pixel doesn't have time to ask.

Meatball wedges himself under the single bench seat. The pre-recorded evacuation message begins: *"Your reality is experiencing turbulence. Please secure all personal items and maintain a calm disposition. The DRM thanks you for your service."*

"Turbulence?!" Pixel shouts at nobody. "TURBULENCE?!"

She slams the hatch. The lifeboat's engine — something she has no name for, something that *hums* in a way engines don't — coughs to life. The vessel lurches, shudders, and then there's a sensation like being turned inside out, like every atom in her body is a radio dial being spun to a station that doesn't exist, and they punch through *something* — through the wall of whatever the world was — and into the space beyond.

The Space.

In her pocket, against her hip, Mochi pulses warm. A slow, steady warmth that Pixel attributes to adrenaline, to fear, to the heat of running. She doesn't think about it. Not yet.

The engine hums with a strange resonance — and underneath the hum, something else. An undertone that doesn't match any mechanical frequency. Pixel hears it as a texture, a color in the sound. A1 notices too — his chrome surface flickers, and for a moment his processing registers it and files it away without comment. Something old. Something that predates the engine. Something the lifeboat picked up from its surroundings and carried in, the way a vessel carries the memory of the frequencies that shaped it.

Pixel presses her face to the scratched porthole and her breath stops.

It's an ocean. A vast, impossible ocean of shimmering static, light that moves like water, color that doesn't map to any spectrum she knows. Ribbons of aurora streak through the void — not light exactly, but *something*, something alive and humming, the residual echo of... she doesn't know what. Below the surface (if there is a surface), ghostly shapes drift: immense, slow-moving silhouettes that look like — she can barely form the thought — like *worlds*. Entire worlds, half-formed, flickering in and out like stations on a radio you're scrolling past too fast. The sky — if it's sky — is a deep, rolling static, beautiful and terrifying, punctuated by distant flashes where currents of color collide.

She has no words. No framework. No category for any of this. The DRM was filing forms about *this*? The boring compliance reports were about... whatever THIS is?

A1's chrome surface flickers — and for the first time, something happens that has never happened before. A faint blue glow extends from his body, just a few inches, shimmering in the air. Not a hologram. Not a glitch. Something deeper. His voice, when it comes, is different — still A1, still dry, still precise, but with an undertone of something vast and uncertain. "I... seem to know this place. I don't know how I know this. I don't have a file for this knowledge. It's not in any database I can access. But I feel..." A long pause, steam curling from his spout. "I feel like I've been here before. A very long time ago." Another pause. Then, more familiar: "Shall I make espresso? I find routine... stabilizing."

Clive's sphere dims, then pulses slowly, deliberately. Pixel reads the pattern — and this time, reading it doesn't feel strange. It feels *necessary*. It's a haiku:

*Between the stations*
*the silence hums with lost worlds*
*we drift, untuned*

Her throat tightens. Her *desk companion* just wrote poetry about the void.

Meatball whimpers, then presses his warm body against Pixel's leg. She can feel him trembling — not with fear exactly, but with *something*. Like whatever they're floating in resonates with something inside him. His eyes are wide, wet, fixed on the porthole. He doesn't look scared. He looks like he's *listening*.

In Pixel's pocket, Mochi pulses again. Warmer now. Steadier. As if the void beyond the porthole is exactly where Mochi was always meant to be.

### Climax

The lifeboat's power fluctuates. The air recycler kicks out a smell that's equal parts toner, burnt circuitry, and Meatball's anxiety. The engine hums with that strange resonance, but the vessel has no navigation she can understand — buttons labeled with symbols she's never seen, readouts in units that don't correspond to anything she learned in school.

Pixel's mind is catching up. Slowly, terribly, the pieces are falling into place.

The DRM wasn't a regulatory compliance agency. It was never about environmental metrics or ambient variance. The forms she filed — the ones with names she never understood — they were about *this*. This ocean. These worlds. The DRM was maintaining *reality itself*, and she'd been sitting in the middle of it for years without knowing. Ninety years of institutional history, entire departments she'd never had clearance to visit, locked floors she'd assumed were storage — all of it was about keeping... whatever this is... stable. Hidden. *Secret.*

And now it's gone. Not temporarily closed, not restructured. *Gone.* The agency that apparently held the fabric of existence together just crumbled like wet paper, and nobody told her what she was actually working on. Nobody told *anyone*.

She looks at A1 — an espresso machine that just admitted to memories he shouldn't have. At Clive — a knee-high robot whose sphere glowed a warning before the building fell, who wrote poetry about the void, who paused at a console panel like it felt familiar. At Meatball — a dog who's staring at an impossible ocean like he *understands* it. At the scratched porthole and the void beyond.

"Okay," she says, and her voice cracks on the word. She tries again. "Okay. So. Everything I thought was real... the office, the forms, the job... it was a cover. For *this*." She gestures at the porthole. "There's an entire... I don't even... there are *worlds* out there. The DRM knew. They always knew. And they just let me file forms about it without telling me what any of it meant."

She's shaking. Meatball presses harder against her leg.

"And now it's gone. And we're in a lifeboat that I didn't know existed, floating in a place I didn't know was real, and our crew is me, a coffee machine that's having an existential crisis, a small robot who apparently speaks in code, and the world's most empathetic dog."

Meatball's tail wags once.

"So what do we *do*?"

A1's blue glow flickers. When he speaks, it's with the careful precision she's always known — but underneath it, something else. Something that sounds almost like wonder. "I believe, Pixel, that you do what you have always done. You observe. You ask questions. You tell people what you've found."

Clive's sphere brightens: *BROADCAST. SOMEONE MIGHT HEAR.*

Pixel stares at him. "You've been able to do this the whole time? The whole time I've worked here, you could *talk*?"

Clive pulses a single, emphatic flash. She reads it: *LATER. BROADCAST NOW.*

Pixel looks at the lifeboat's battered communications array. It's basic — a microphone, a frequency dial, an antenna that looks like it was designed in the 1970s. But it works. She can feel it humming, tuned to something, ready to transmit into... whatever this is.

She's a data analyst who always wanted to be a journalist. She's got a story nobody's ever told. She's got a microphone. And she's got nothing left to lose.

She flips the comm switch, adjusts the dial as wide as it'll go, takes a shaking breath, and begins.

"This is... my name is Pixel Paradox. I worked at the Department of Reality Maintenance. I *thought* I worked at a regulatory agency. I was wrong. I was very, very wrong." Her voice steadies, fueled by something between fury and awe. "The DRM is gone. The building collapsed — not like a building, like... I can't explain it. Reality broke. Actually broke. And now I'm in a lifeboat in an ocean that shouldn't exist, looking at things I don't have names for, and I don't know if anyone can hear this."

She pauses. Looks at the porthole. At the ghostly shapes drifting in the deep.

"But I'm going to find out what's out there. I'm going to find out what the DRM was hiding, and why it fell, and what all those forms I filed actually meant. Someone needs to document this. Someone needs to keep a record of whatever's happening."

Another pause. Quieter now. The anger fading into something rawer.

"This is The Ephergent. First transmission. We're out here. We don't know where 'here' is. But we're listening. And if you can hear this... we could really use some answers."

### Resolution

Static. The broadcast goes out into the Space and the Space swallows it whole. No response. No acknowledgment. Just the hum of whatever medium they're floating in and the slow, patient drift of shapes beneath them.

But A1 notices something. That blue glow — the one that flickered to life when they entered the Space — hasn't faded. It's expanded. Just slightly, just a few inches more than before. And inside him, in circuits that shouldn't hold anything but brewing temperatures and grind settings, something stirs. Not a memory exactly. More like an echo. The ghost of a corridor he's never walked. The hum of an engine he's never heard. A sense of *scale* — vast, ancient, impossible — pressing against the inside of a chrome espresso machine like a cathedral trying to fit inside a thimble. He doesn't mention it. Not yet. He's not sure it's real. He makes espresso instead — a dark roast with notes of worn copper and distant woodsmoke, the flavor of a ship that remembers being a home — because routine is how he holds himself together while something ancient and enormous slowly wakes up inside him.

Clive settles onto the console, sphere glowing soft blue-white, fedora tilted at its usual angle. Ninety years. He's been at the DRM for ninety years, and for all of them, he kept the secrets. Followed the protocols. Communicated only in patterns obscure enough that nobody would notice — except Pixel, who noticed everything without knowing what she was noticing. His old partner, Barry Kowalski, went into the Space decades ago. The official report said "transferred." Clive knew better. Barry followed a signal. And now Clive is here too, looking at the same void, wondering if Barry found what he was looking for — and whether the answers Clive kept locked away for ninety years would have changed anything.

In Pixel's pocket, Mochi pulses warm. Steady. Patient. A warmth that Pixel still hasn't named.

Meatball curls up at Pixel's feet and sighs — a deep, contented sigh that seems impossible given the circumstances. But Meatball has always been like this. Calm when things are big. Anxious when the mailman comes. It's as if the Space, for all its strangeness, resonates with something in him. Something that's always been there, quiet and patient, waiting for a moment exactly like this.

Pixel leans back against the cold metal wall of the lifeboat, exhausted, terrified, furious, and weirdly exhilarated. Everything she knew was a lie. Or not a lie — a *cover*. A carefully maintained fiction. And now the fiction is shattered and the truth is an ocean of impossible light and she's broadcasting into it from a boat that smells like toner.

But the signal is alive. And so are they.
