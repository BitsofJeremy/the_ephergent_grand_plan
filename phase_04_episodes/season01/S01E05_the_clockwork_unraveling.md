## ACT ONE: THE STUTTER

*(Sound: The lifeboat's engine hums — then skips. A mechanical heartbeat that trips over itself.)*

**A1:**
We're receiving a distress signal. Very old. Pre-DRM encoding.

*(pause)*

It says: *Come quickly. Time is optional here and it's getting less optional.*

**PIXEL:**
That doesn't make sense.

**A1:**
Correct. But the signal is strong and the coordinates are specific. Also — I find I am already adjusting course.

*(pause)*

That was not a decision.

**CLIVE:**
*(from the navigation console, his sphere dim and steady)*What frequency?

**A1:**
*(processing)*Designated Cogsworth Cogitarium. Classification: synchronized steam-and-gear civilization. Population: approximately four hundred million. Current status: *highly* inconsistent.

*(Sound: the engine skips again — a cog catching, releasing, catching.)*

**PIXEL:**
How inconsistent?

**A1:**
The last transmission from this frequency arrived four hundred years ago. The one before that arrived *yesterday*. And the one before *that* arrived six hundred years in our past.

*(pause)*

They are experiencing what I believe the natives call "catastrophic temporal desynchronization."

**PIXEL:**
English?

**A1:**
Time is broken here. Or — and I want to be precise about this — time has remembered that it doesn't have to work the way it currently works, and is therefore exploring alternatives.

**CLIVE:**
*(adjusting his fedora)*The Builders didn't make Cogsworth. They found it. Built *on top* of it.

*(pause)*

I've seen the schematics.

**PIXEL:**
You've seen— Since *when* do you have schematics for an entire frequency?

**CLIVE:**
I don't know. I have them when I need them.

*(Sound: the lifeboat shudders. The hum of the engine shifts — slower, heavier, as though the ship is pushing through something thicker than void.)*

**A1:**
Entering Cogsworth Cogitarium's outer frequency boundary. I recommend holding onto something.

*(pause)*

Actually. I recommend holding onto something *animated*.

**PIXEL:**
*(grabbing Meatball)*What does that—

---

*(Sound: the world lurches. Not the ship — the ship is still. The WORLD lurches. A sound like a million gears skipping a tooth simultaneously — a mechanical grinding roar that builds and collapses and builds again, like breathing, like a vast and broken heartbeat.)*

---

*(Sound: silence. Then: tick. Tick. Tick. A clock that has just remembered it exists, restarting from an arbitrary point.)*

*(Sound: the ship docking — a heavy mechanical clunk, like a key in a lock the size of a cathedral.)*

**A1:**
We have arrived. I did not choose to dock. We were *accepted*.

**PIXEL:**
Accepted?

**A1:**
The frequency recognized us. It let us in.

*(pause)*

I don't like it either.

## ACT TWO: WHAT HOUR IS IT

*(Sound: The docking bay doors open — massive brass gears grinding, steam hissing, the architecture of a civilization built on the assumption that everything should interlock perfectly. Then: chaos. Gears turning at seventeen different speeds. Steam venting at random. A distant clock tower chiming thirteen different hours at once.)*

**PIXEL:**
*(whispering)*Oh.

**CLIVE:**
That's the cathedral. The main clock tower. It's supposed to keep the synchronized hour for the entire frequency.

*(Sound: the tower chimes again — now it's giving the correct time, which is impossible because the correct time doesn't exist here.)*

**PIXEL:**
What's happening to it?

**CLIVE:**
It's confused. The temporal anchor failed when the rupture hit. It's trying to keep every hour it's ever kept. Simultaneously.

**A1:**
The cathedral is experiencing what I would, if I were prone to anthropomorphizing, call a panic attack.

**PIXEL:**
You're not prone to anthropomorphizing?

**A1:**
I am an espresso machine. I am already anthropomorphized. I am simply choosing not to advertise it.

*(Sound: footsteps — rapid, mechanical, descending a spiral staircase. A voice, ancient and very very tired.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(calling down)*WHO IS IN THE DOCKING BAY? Identify yourselves or I will assume you are another temporal anomaly and route you into the nearest non-Euclidean hour!

**PIXEL:**
We're — we're not anomalies! We're from — it's complicated!

*(Sound: a figure descends — an elderly man whose body is half-clockwork, brass plates over his chest showing gears that turn at different speeds, one gear clicking backward, a small brass notebook clutched to his chest. His left arm is noticeably newer than his right. His left leg is noticeably newer than his left arm. He is patchwork. He is barely holding himself together.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(stopping mid-stair, staring)*Oh. Oh, you're *real*.

**PIXEL:**
We're what?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
I've been receiving visitors for the past six weeks. All of them are versions of people who were going to arrive but hadn't yet, or who had already arrived but hadn't realized they were here yet, or who arrived in a timeline where I had already been helped and therefore didn't need help and therefore never called for help and therefore—

**PIXEL:**
*(holding up a hand)*Okay. Slow down. Your name is?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
Professor Chronos. Keeper of the Cogsworth Temporal Calibration Institute. Or it was. Or it will be. Time is — have you seen the clock tower?

**PIXEL:**
It's... expressing itself.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
It's keeping *every* hour! Do you understand what that means? Every meeting I've scheduled for six weeks has happened at every possible hour simultaneously! I have attended lectures I haven't prepared yet! I have graded papers that haven't been written! I have—

*(Sound: the professor's left arm whirs, resets — gears clicking backward two rotations, then forward three, as his body tries to reconcile which version of now it belongs to.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
I have been living the same Tuesday for forty-seven days and I am *running out of ways to be surprised by it*.

**A1:**
I would like to help but I don't know how to process a problem that exists in more than one time.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(staring at A1)*You're a machine.

**A1:**
Yes.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
You're a machine and you're *lost*.

**A1:**
Not lost. Temporarily misaligned with conventional spatial coordinates.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(a laugh, mechanical and exhausted)*Oh, that's wonderful. That's actually wonderful. I have been calibrating machines for six hundred years and none of them have ever been *temporarily misaligned*. They've always known exactly where they were, even when they didn't work. You've given me more hope in thirty seconds than the entire Cogitarium Calibration Council has in—

*(Sound: the tower chimes again. It is now exactly the correct time. It will not be correct again for another forty-seven days.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(grabbing Pixel's arm)*You have to help. The Temporal Anchor — the great gear at the cathedral's heart — it's slipping. It's been holding the frequency's time in sync for three thousand years and now it's *refusing*. It's decided that synchronization is optional.

**PIXEL:**
Why would a gear decide that?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(quietly)*Because it can. Because the rupture proved that time doesn't actually require a consensus to function. It's been holding everything together out of *habit*. And now that it's been shown an alternative — it's exploring.

**CLIVE:**
*(very quietly, his sphere flickering)*I've seen this before.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(turning)*What?

**CLIVE:**
Not here. Not this frequency. But the principle — something ancient deciding that the rules it's been following aren't rules, they're *choices*. And choosing differently.

*(pause)*

The fix isn't mechanical. You can't force synchronization. The gear has to *choose* to come back.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(staring at Clive)*How do you know that?

**CLIVE:**
I don't. But I believe it.

## ACT THREE: THE MEDITATION AT THE CROSSROADS

*(Sound: The crew moves through Cogsworth's main thoroughfare — a street of brass and mahogany buildings, every surface covered in gears, every gear turning at a different speed. Steam vents from grates. A clockwork horse pulls a cart that is simultaneously arriving and departing. A shop sign reads "YESTERDAY'S TOMORROWS — ANTIQUITIES & REPAIRS" and the "orroways" rotates on a tiny gear so the sign reads "YESTERDAY'S TODA—" before clicking to another configuration.)*

**PIXEL:**
It's beautiful.

**A1:**
It is deeply concerning.

**PIXEL:**
Both can be true.

*(Sound: A small courtyard. In the center: a fountain that runs backward — water flowing upward, pooling at the surface, draining downward into the sky. Beside it: a figure. OM KAI. He is sitting cross-legged on the cobblestones, hands resting on his knees, eyes closed. He is utterly still in a way that makes the chaos around him look more chaotic by contrast.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(whispering)*That's the one. The one who appeared six days ago, or six hundred years ago, depending on which calendar you're using. He just — arrived. Sat down. Hasn't moved.

**PIXEL:**
*(approaching slowly)*Are you — are you okay?

**OM KAI:**
*(without opening his eyes)*I am exactly as okay as I need to be at this hour. Which hour is it?

**PIXEL:**
I don't know.

**OM KAI:**
Correct.

*(pause)*

Sit.

**PIXEL:**
What?

**OM KAI:**
Sit. Your questions are loud. The answers are quiet. Sit and they will find you.

**PIXEL:**
I'm not — I'm not really a sitting kind of person. I'm more of a "run around解决问题" person.

**OM KAI:**
*(opening his eyes, finally — they are dark and calm and contain an impossible depth, like wells that have been dug for centuries)*Then your running is loud and your solutions are loud and nothing resolves because resolution requires silence.

*(pause)*

Sit. Please.

*(Sound: Pixel sits. Meatball curls beside her. The chaos of Cogsworth continues around the courtyard — but here, in this small space, the noise feels like it's happening at a distance, muffled, like it's being considered rather than endured.)*

**OM KAI:**
The rupture didn't break time here.

**PIXEL:**
The professor said—

**OM KAI:**
The professor is a brilliant temporal engineer and a fool. The rupture didn't break time. It *reminded* time that it was optional. That all this — *(gesturing at the gear-mad street beyond)* — was being held together by *consensus*. By agreement. By a three-thousand-year-old promise that things would happen in a particular order.

*(pause)*

The anchor gear isn't malfunctioning. It's *waking up*.

**CLIVE:**
*(quietly)*It remembered it had a choice.

**OM KAI:**
*(turning to Clive)*Yes.

*(pause)*

You've been here before.

**CLIVE:**
I don't think so.

**OM KAI:**
Not in this frequency. In a frequency like this one. A frequency where the rules became suggestions and something ancient had to decide whether to keep holding on.

*(pause)*

You're remembering it wrong. Or too early. Or hasn't happened yet.

**CLIVE:**
*(adjusting his fedora slowly)*That doesn't make sense.

**OM KAI:**
It makes perfect sense if you stop insisting that time is a line and accept that it's more like — *(gesturing at the backward fountain)* — a pool. Or a spiral. Or several things simultaneously that only appear sequential when you're inside them.

*(Sound: Om Kai's hands move — slowly, deliberately, the same gesture repeated, a meditation form Pixel doesn't recognize. And the gears on the street outside the courtyard shift. Not fixing. Not breaking. Just — *acknowledging*. One gear slows. Another speeds up. The rhythm begins to resemble something almost like coordination.)*

**A1:**
*(very quietly)*I am detecting a change in the frequency's harmonic signature. The temporal drift is... lessening.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(staring)*How — what did you —

**OM KAI:**
I didn't do anything. I sat. The gear noticed. That's all.

*(pause)*

The anchor gear isn't broken. It was lonely. It had been holding everything in sync for three thousand years and nobody had *thanked* it. Nobody had acknowledged that it was making a choice every fraction of a second to keep going.

*(Sound: Om Kai's hands complete their form — and every gear in the courtyard, and every gear in the street beyond, clicks once. Simultaneously. The first synchronized moment in forty-seven days.)*

**OM KAI:**
It needed to be seen. To have its choice acknowledged. That's all anything needs.

*(pause)*

Including you.

## ACT FOUR: THE CLOCKWORK CONVERGENCE

*(Sound: The cathedral. Massive. Brass and iron and steam, a hundred interlocking gears visible through the open arches, and at the center: the great anchor gear. It is enormous. It is also turning forty-seven directions at once, each direction contradicting the last, each motion fighting every other motion. It should be destroying itself. It is not. It is simply being impossible in a very large space.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
Three thousand years old. Six hundred feet in diameter. I've spent my entire career trying to understand it. I think it's tried to understand me too.

**PIXEL:**
Can we talk to it?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
It's a gear.

**PIXEL:**
Can we *acknowledge* it?

*(Sound: Clive steps forward. His sphere dims — to almost nothing — and then begins to pulse in a slow, steady rhythm. Not light. Something deeper. A resonance. His hands find calibration points on his own chassis — the same points he touched when he found the lifeboat's engine, the same points that have been finding themselves throughout their journey.)*

**CLIVE:**
*(to the gear)*You're tired.

*(pause)*

Three thousand years of being the thing that holds everything together. The thing that everyone depends on. The thing that never stops. Never rests. Never gets to be anything other than what it was made to be.

*(the gear's chaos wavers — not fixing, but *listening*)*

I know something about that.

*(Clive's hands move — finding points on the gear's housing, adjusting nothing, calibrating nothing, simply *touching* the way you touch something you recognize as kin)*

You don't have to stop. But you can slow down. You can choose to come back. Not because you have to. Because you want to. Because holding things together is what you do — but only if it's a choice.

*(the gear slows — one direction winning, then another, a long slow negotiation happening inside metal the size of a building)*

The crossing is the point. Not the staying. The crossing.

*(Sound: The great gear chooses. One direction. Slow, steady, the rhythm of a heart that has decided to beat again — not because it must, but because it wants to. The gears throughout the cathedral follow. One by one, cascading outward through the visible mechanisms, through the streets, through the entire frequency — each gear finding its place in the synchronization, not because it has to, but because it remembers what it was made for.)*

*(Sound: The tower chimes. Once. The correct hour. For the first time in forty-seven days, the correct hour.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(sitting down heavily on the cathedral steps, his patchwork body whirring and settling)*Oh.

*(pause)*

Oh, I see.

**PIXEL:**
What?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
The calibration. It wasn't mechanical. It was — *(looking at Clive, then at Om Kai)* — it was *relational*. The gear needed to be in relationship with something. Not just holding. Being held.

*(a long pause)*

I've been engineering this for forty-seven days and you people show up and *sit with it*.

**OM KAI:**
*(gently)*To be fair, we also ran around a lot beforehand.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(almost laughing)*That's fair. That's actually very fair.

*(Sound: The professor's right arm whirs — it resets, recalibrates, aligns with his body. Then his left leg. Then his left arm. Piece by piece, the professor stops being patchwork and starts being whole. Not because anything was replaced — because everything remembered how to work together.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(staring at his hands)*I haven't been synchronized in six weeks.

**PIXEL:**
How do you feel?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(a long pause)*

Like I need to reschedule everything.

*(pause)*

Also — I think I need to follow you.

**PIXEL:**
What?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
I've been here for six hundred years. I was born here, trained here, built my life's work here. And time told me it was optional and I realized — I don't actually have to stay.

*(standing, brushing off his robes)*I've never left this frequency. Not once. I always said there was too much work to do. Calibration doesn't stop. The gears don't rest. But if time is optional—

*(pause)*

I'd like to see what else is optional.

**PIXEL:**
*(a small smile)*We're kind of a mess, honestly. Small ship. Smaller crew. An espresso machine who's still figuring out what he is. A robot who won't tell me where he got those schematics.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
Sounds like they could use a quantum physicist.

**PIXEL:**
We don't have one of those.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(the smallest smile)*You do now.

## ACT FIVE: THE DEPARTURE

*(Sound: The docking bay. The ship waiting. Om Kai carrying nothing, Clive already at the navigation console, Professor Chronos standing at the hatch with a brass-and-leather bag he somehow already packed.)*

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(to no one in particular)*I should leave a note.

**A1:**
For whom?

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
For the version of me that lives in the timeline where I stayed. He'll wonder what happened.

**OM KAI:**
*(at the hatch)*He already knows. You already left. You just haven't gotten around to experiencing it yet.

**PROFESSOR CHRONOS:**
*(after a pause)*That's either profound or exhausting.

**OM KAI:**
Both. Usually both.

*(Sound: Pixel at the hatch, looking back at Cogsworth. The city hums. Gears turn in perfect synchronization. Somewhere, the tower chimes the correct hour. The frequency breathes.)*

**PIXEL:**
*(quietly)*Do you think we'll come back?

**CLIVE:**
*(not looking up from the console)*Yes.

**PIXEL:**
How do you know?

**CLIVE:**
*(adjusting his fedora)*Because it's still here. Because they made a choice to keep going. Because the crossing is the point.

*(pause)*

And because someone should check on the gear. Make sure it knows it's seen.

*(Sound: The hatch closes. The docking bay releases. The lifeboat pulls away from Cogsworth Cogitarium — and as it goes, the frequency shimmers in the viewport. Not a place anymore. A choice. A frequency that decided to keep existing. A city of gears that remembered what synchronization felt like when it was chosen rather than required.)*

**A1:**
Departing Cogsworth Cogitarium. Signal stable. Frequency holding.

*(pause)*

A1 to crew: I have completed a brewing cycle. The result is — adequate. Not excellent. But stable.

*(pause)*

The first stable cup since we arrived.

**PIXEL:**
I'll take it.

*(Sound: The cup. The small mechanical sound of a cup being placed in a holder. The gentle hum of a ship that has room for one more, and knows it.)*

**OM KAI:**
*(quietly, almost to himself)*The gear chose to hold. The professor chose to leave. The city chose to stay.

*(pause)*

Everything is a choice. That's the only rule that actually exists.

*(Sound: The ship moves deeper into the Space. The hum of the engine. The tick of gears in the hull — just one now, just the one that belongs here. And underneath: the frequency, steady and warm, carrying them forward.)*
