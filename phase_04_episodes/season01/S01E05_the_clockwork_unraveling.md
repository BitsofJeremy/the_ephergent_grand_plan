## I. The Stutter

I need you to understand something about time. It sounds simple — hours, days, before, after — but Cogsworth Cogitarium taught me that simple things hide the most complicated truths. Time is optional. It always was. We just forgot.

The signal came through when we were three days out from the last Station. A1's voice through the comm, measured and British as always: "We're receiving a distress signal. Very old. Pre-DRM encoding."

A pause. The hum of the ship filling the silence.

"Come quickly. Time is optional here and it's getting less optional."

That doesn't make sense. I told him so.

"Correct," A1 said. "But the signal is strong and the coordinates are specific. Also — I find I am already adjusting course."

Another pause.

"That was not a decision."

Clive was at the navigation console, his sphere dim and steady. "What frequency?"

"Cogsworth Cogitarium. Classification: synchronized steam-and-gear civilization. Population: approximately four hundred million. Current status: highly inconsistent."

The engine skipped — a cog catching, releasing, catching. A mechanical heartbeat that tripped over itself.

"How inconsistent?"

A1 processed. "The last transmission from this frequency arrived four hundred years ago. The one before that arrived yesterday. And the one before that arrived six hundred years in our past."

I stared at the navigation display. "English?"

"They are experiencing what I believe the natives call 'catastrophic temporal desynchronization.'"

"That's not English."

"Time is broken here. Or — and I want to be precise about this — time has remembered that it doesn't have to work the way it currently works, and is therefore exploring alternatives."

Clive adjusted his fedora. "The Builders didn't make Cogsworth. They found it. Built on top of it." A pause. "I've seen the schematics."

"You've seen — Since when do you have schematics for an entire frequency?"

"I don't know. I have them when I need them."

The engine skipped again — slower, heavier, as though the ship was pushing through something thicker than void.

"Entering Cogsworth Cogitarium's outer frequency boundary," A1 said. "I recommend holding onto something."

A pause.

"Actually. I recommend holding onto something animated."

I grabbed Meatball. The world lurched.

Not the ship. The ship was still. The WORLD lurched. A sound like a million gears skipping a tooth simultaneously — a mechanical grinding roar that built and collapsed and built again, like breathing. Like a vast and broken heartbeat.

Then silence. Then: tick. Tick. Tick. A clock that had just remembered it exists, restarting from an arbitrary point.

The docking bay doors opened onto brass and mahogany and steam, architecture that assumed everything should interlock perfectly. Then chaos. Gears turning at seventeen different speeds. Steam venting at random. A distant clock tower chiming thirteen different hours at once.

"Oh," I whispered.

"That's the cathedral," Clive said. "The main clock tower. It's supposed to keep the synchronized hour for the entire frequency."

The tower chimes again — now it was giving the correct time, which was impossible because the correct time didn't exist here.

"What's happening to it?"

"It's confused. The temporal anchor failed when the rupture hit. It's trying to keep every hour it's ever kept. Simultaneously."

A1's voice through the comm: "The cathedral is experiencing what I would, if I were prone to anthropomorphizing, call a panic attack."

"You're not prone to anthropomorphizing?"

"I am an espresso machine. I am already anthropomorphized. I am simply choosing not to advertise it."

Footsteps — rapid, mechanical, descending a spiral staircase. A voice, ancient and very tired.

"WHO IS IN THE DOCKING BAY? Identify yourselves or I will assume you are another temporal anomaly and route you into the nearest non-Euclidean hour!"

"We're — we're not anomalies! We're from — it's complicated!"

A figure descended — an elderly man whose body was half-clockwork, brass plates over his chest showing gears that turned at different speeds, one gear clicking backward, a small brass notebook clutched to his chest. His left arm was noticeably newer than his right. His left leg was noticeably newer than his left arm. He was patchwork. He was barely holding himself together.

"Oh," he said, stopping mid-stair. "Oh, you're real."

"We're what?"

"I've been receiving visitors for the past six weeks. All of them are versions of people who were going to arrive but hadn't yet, or who had already arrived but hadn't realized they were here yet, or who arrived in a timeline where I had already been helped and therefore didn't need help and therefore never called for help and therefore—"

"慢点。" I held up a hand. "Your name is?"

"Professor Chronos. Keeper of the Cogsworth Temporal Calibration Institute. Or it was. Or it will be. Time is — have you seen the clock tower?"

"It's... expressing itself."

"It's keeping every hour! Do you understand what that means? Every meeting I've scheduled for six weeks has happened at every possible hour simultaneously! I have attended lectures I haven't prepared yet! I have graded papers that haven't been written! I have—"

His left arm whirred, reset — gears clicking backward two rotations, then forward three, as his body tried to reconcile which version of now it belonged to.

"I have been living the same Tuesday for forty-seven days and I am running out of ways to be surprised by it."

A1 through the comm: "I would like to help but I don't know how to process a problem that exists in more than one time."

"You're a machine," the professor said, staring at A1's speaker grille.

"Yes."

"You're a machine and you're lost."

"Not lost. Temporarily misaligned with conventional spatial coordinates."

The professor laughed — mechanical and exhausted. "Oh, that's wonderful. That's actually wonderful. I have been calibrating machines for six hundred years and none of them have ever been temporarily misaligned. They've always known exactly where they were, even when they didn't work. You've given me more hope in thirty seconds than the entire Cogitarium Calibration Council has in—"

The tower chimes again. It was now exactly the correct time. It would not be correct again for another forty-seven days.

The professor grabbed my arm. "You have to help. The Temporal Anchor — the great gear at the cathedral's heart — it's slipping. It's been holding the frequency's time in sync for three thousand years and now it's refusing. It's decided that synchronization is optional."

"Why would a gear decide that?"

He was quiet for a moment. "Because it can. Because the rupture proved that time doesn't actually require a consensus to function. It's been holding everything together out of habit. And now that it's been shown an alternative — it's exploring."

Clive was very quiet, his sphere flickering. "I've seen this before."

"What?"

"Not here. Not this frequency. But the principle — something ancient deciding that the rules it's been following aren't rules, they're choices. And choosing differently."

The fix isn't mechanical, Clive said. You can't force synchronization. The gear has to choose to come back.

Om Kai was watching the clock tower, meditation beads still.

The rupture didn't break time here, he said. The rupture reminded time it was optional. But something is choosing to run the same pattern. Machines don't choose. Someone programmed this loop. The gear isn't exploring alternatives — it's executing a program that was written before it existed. The question isn't whether it can choose differently. The question is whether anyone knows how to give it a different program.

The professor stared at Clive. "How do you know that?"

"I don't. But I believe it."

## II. The Meditation at the Crossroads

The main thoroughfare was a street of brass and mahogany buildings, every surface covered in gears, every gear turning at a different speed. Steam vents from grates. A clockwork horse pulled a cart that was simultaneously arriving and departing. A shop sign read "YESTERDAY'S TOMORROWS — ANTIQUITIES & REPAIRS" and the "orroways" rotated on a tiny gear so the sign read "YESTERDAY'S TODA—" before clicking to another configuration.

"It's beautiful," I said.

"It is deeply concerning," A1 replied.

"Both can be true."

A small courtyard. In the center: a fountain that runs backward — water flowing upward, pooling at the surface, draining downward into the sky. Beside it: a figure. Om Kai. He was sitting cross-legged on the cobblestones, hands resting on his knees, eyes closed. He was utterly still in a way that made the chaos around him look more chaotic by contrast.

"That's the one," the professor said, whispering. "The one who appeared six days ago, or six hundred years ago, depending on which calendar you're using. He just — arrived. Sat down. Hasn't moved."

I approached slowly. "Are you — are you okay?"

"I am exactly as okay as I need to be at this hour," Om Kai said, without opening his eyes. "Which hour is it?"

"I don't know."

"Correct."

A pause.

Sit.

"What?"

Sit. Your questions are loud. The answers are quiet. Sit and they will find you.

"I'm not — I'm not really a sitting kind of person. I'm more of a 'run around and solve it' person."

Om Kai opened his eyes. They were dark and calm and contained an impossible depth, like wells that had been dug for centuries. "Then your running is loud and your solutions are loud and nothing resolves because resolution requires silence."

A pause.

Sit. Please.

I sat. Meatball curled beside me. The chaos of Cogsworth continued around the courtyard — but here, in this small space, the noise felt like it was happening at a distance, muffled, like it was being considered rather than endured.

"The rupture didn't break time here," Om Kai said.

"The professor said—"

"The professor is a brilliant temporal engineer and a fool. The rupture didn't break time. It reminded time that it was optional. That all this — " He gestured at the gear-mad street beyond. "— was being held together by consensus. By agreement. By a three-thousand-year-old promise that things would happen in a particular order."

A pause.

"The anchor gear isn't malfunctioning. It's waking up."

Clive, very quietly: "It remembered it had a choice."

Om Kai turned to Clive. "Yes."

A pause.

"You've been here before."

"I don't think so."

"Not in this frequency. In a frequency like this one. A frequency where the rules became suggestions and something ancient had to decide whether to keep holding on."

A pause.

"You're remembering it wrong. Or too early. Or hasn't happened yet."

Clive adjusted his fedora slowly. "That doesn't make sense."

"It makes perfect sense if you stop insisting that time is a line and accept that it's more like — " He gestured at the backward fountain. "— a pool. Or a spiral. Or several things simultaneously that only appear sequential when you're inside them."

Om Kai's hands moved — slowly, deliberately, the same gesture repeated, a meditation form I didn't recognize. And the gears on the street outside the courtyard shifted. Not fixing. Not breaking. Just acknowledging. One gear slowed. Another sped up. The rhythm began to resemble something almost like coordination.

A1, very quietly: "I am detecting a change in the frequency's harmonic signature. The temporal drift is... lessening."

The professor stared. "How — what did you —"

Om Kai completed his form. Every gear in the courtyard clicked once. Simultaneously. The first synchronized moment in forty-seven days.

"I didn't do anything," he said. "I sat. The gear noticed. That's all."

A pause.

"The anchor gear isn't broken. It was lonely. It had been holding everything in sync for three thousand years and nobody had thanked it. Nobody had acknowledged that it was making a choice every fraction of a second to keep going."

His hands completed their form — and every gear in the courtyard, and every gear in the street beyond, clicked once. Simultaneously. The first synchronized moment in forty-seven days.

"It needed to be seen. To have its choice acknowledged. That's all anything needs."

A pause.

"Including you."

## III. The Clockwork Convergence

The cathedral was massive. Brass and iron and steam, a hundred interlocking gears visible through the open arches, and at the center: the great anchor gear. It was enormous. It was also turning forty-seven directions at once, each direction contradicting the last, each motion fighting every other motion. It should have been destroying itself. It was not. It was simply being impossible in a very large space.

"Three thousand years old," Professor Chronos said. "Six hundred feet in diameter. I've spent my entire career trying to understand it. I think it's tried to understand me too."

"Can we talk to it?"

"It's a gear."

"Can we acknowledge it?"

Clive stepped forward. His sphere dimmed — to almost nothing — and then began to pulse in a slow, steady rhythm. Not light. Something deeper. A resonance. His hands found calibration points on his own chassis — the same points he touched when he found the lifeboat's engine, the same points that had been finding themselves throughout their journey.

"You're tired," Clive said.

A pause.

"Three thousand years of being the thing that holds everything together. The thing that everyone depends on. The thing that never stops. Never rests. Never gets to be anything other than what it was made to be."

A pause. The gear's chaos wavered — not fixing, but listening.

"I know something about that."

Clive's hands moved — finding points on the gear's housing, adjusting nothing, calibrating nothing, simply touching the way you touch something you recognize as kin.

"You don't have to stop. But you can slow down. You can choose to come back. Not because you have to. Because you want to. Because holding things together is what you do — but only if it's a choice."

The gear slowed — one direction winning, then another, a long slow negotiation happening inside metal the size of a building.

"The crossing is the point. Not the staying. The crossing."

The great gear chose. One direction. Slow, steady, the rhythm of a heart that had decided to beat again — not because it must, but because it wanted to. The gears throughout the cathedral followed. One by one, cascading outward through the visible mechanisms, through the streets, through the entire frequency — each gear finding its place in the synchronization, not because it had to, but because it remembered what it was made for.

The tower chimes. Once. The correct hour. For the first time in forty-seven days, the correct hour.

Professor Chronos sat down heavily on the cathedral steps, his patchwork body whirring and settling. "Oh."

A long pause.

"Oh, I see."

"What?"

"The calibration. It wasn't mechanical. It was — " He looked at Clive, then at Om Kai. "— it was relational. The gear needed to be in relationship with something. Not just holding. Being held."

A long pause.

"I've been engineering this for forty-seven days and you people show up and sit with it."

"To be fair," Om Kai said gently, "we also ran around a lot beforehand."

The professor almost laughed. "That's fair. That's actually very fair."

The professor's right arm whirred — it reset, recalibrated, aligned with his body. Then his left leg. Then his left arm. Piece by piece, the professor stopped being patchwork and started being whole. Not because anything was replaced — because everything remembered how to work together.

"I haven't been synchronized in six weeks," he said.

I asked how he felt.

A long pause.

"Like I need to reschedule everything."

Another pause.

"Also — I think I need to follow you."

"What?"

"I've been here for six hundred years. I was born here, trained here, built my life's work here. And time told me it was optional and I realized — I don't actually have to stay."

He stood, brushing off his robes. "I've never left this frequency. Not once. I always said there was too much work to do. Calibration doesn't stop. The gears don't rest. But if time is optional—"

A pause.

"I'd like to see what else is optional."

I smiled. "We're kind of a mess, honestly. Small ship. Smaller crew. An espresso machine who's still figuring out what he is. A robot who won't tell me where he got those schematics."

"Sounds like they could use a quantum physicist."

"We don't have one of those."

He smiled — the smallest smile. "You do now."

## IV. The Departure

The docking bay. The ship waiting. Om Kai carrying nothing, Clive already at the navigation console, Professor Chronos standing at the hatch with a brass-and-leather bag he somehow already packed.

"I should leave a note," he said, to no one in particular.

"For whom?" A1 asked.

"For the version of me that lives in the timeline where I stayed. He'll wonder what happened."

Om Kai at the hatch: "He already knows. You already left. You just haven't gotten around to experiencing it yet."

The professor paused. "That's either profound or exhausting."

"Both. Usually both."

I stood at the hatch, looking back at Cogsworth. The city hummed. Gears turned in perfect synchronization. Somewhere, the tower chimed the correct hour. The frequency breathed.

"Do you think we'll come back?" I asked.

Clive, not looking up from the console: "Yes."

"How do you know?"

"Because it's still here. Because they made a choice to keep going. Because the crossing is the point."

A pause.

"And because someone should check on the gear. Make sure it knows it's seen."

The hatch closed. The docking bay released. The lifeboat pulled away from Cogsworth Cogitarium — and as we went, the frequency shimmered in the viewport. Not a place anymore. A choice. A frequency that decided to keep existing. A city of gears that remembered what synchronization felt like when it was chosen rather than required.

"Departing Cogsworth Cogitarium," A1 said. "Signal stable. Frequency holding."

A pause.

"A1 to crew: I have completed a brewing cycle. The result is — adequate. Not excellent. But stable."

A pause.

"The first stable cup since we arrived."

"I'll take it."

A1 made the coffee. I sat in the common area, holding the cup, feeling the warmth of it against my palms. The ship moved through the Space. The hum of the engine. The tick of gears in the hull — just one now, just the one that belongs here. And underneath: the frequency, steady and warm, carrying us forward.

"Om Kai," I said, later, quiet, at the viewport.

"Yes?"

"The gear chose to hold. The professor chose to leave. The city chose to stay."

Om Kai smiled — barely, but it was there. "Everything is a choice. That's the only rule that actually exists."

The ship moved deeper into the Space. The frequency held.
