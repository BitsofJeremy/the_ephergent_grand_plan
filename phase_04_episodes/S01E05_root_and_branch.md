# Root and Branch

## Pixel-Voice Synopsis

So here's a fun sentence I never thought I'd say: we saved a telepathic shadow government run by houseplants, and it wasn't even the weirdest thing that happened that day. TELEPATHIC PLANTS. SENTIENT HOUSEPLANTS RUNNING A SHADOW GOVERNMENT. My fern at home has been POLITICALLY ACTIVE this whole time?! I am still not okay.

Verdantia — the frequency where everything is alive, everything is green, and the architecture literally grows around you — was experiencing what I can only describe as a botanical internet outage. And yes, I said "frequency." Because apparently we just HOP BETWEEN FREQUENCIES now. Like it's a thing we do. Like we didn't all have normal jobs three weeks ago. The Root Network, which connects every sentient plant across the entire frequency in a vast telepathic web, was full of static. The frequency rupture had turned their signal into noise, and the whole civilization was going deaf.

Meanwhile, A1 decided this was the perfect moment to dramatically expand his holographic range by about 400% — and everyone was like "HOW? YOU ARE LITERALLY A COFFEE MAKER" — Om Kai said words like "accelerating" and "unprecedented" in that calm monk voice that somehow makes everything scarier (seriously, how is he SO CALM about all this?), and we found a twenty-one-year-old hacker whose brain was literally in five places at once. His name's Zephyr. He was a regular guy — a hacker, yeah, but a NORMAL-WORLD hacker. His brother Aether disappeared while investigating some weird encrypted DRM files, and Zephyr went looking for him, found things no one was supposed to find, built something from his brother's notes without fully understanding what it would do, and got scattered across frequencies he didn't even know existed. When we pulled him back together, the first thing he said was basically "wait, you guys are from the normal world TOO? You also just found out about ALL THIS?"

So now we're collecting people who are looking for the same impossible thing we are. I'm starting to think that's not a coincidence. Om Kai would say it's "resonance." Part of me wonders if we're learning enough to navigate home someday. Part of me is starting to wonder if I want to.

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The Interdimensional Space ejected the Form 27-B into Verdantia's atmosphere like a seed from a cosmic fruit, and the first thing Pixel noticed was the silence.

But the second thing she noticed — before the silence even fully registered — was the *green*. Green everywhere, in every direction, in shades she didn't have names for. Living buildings. Breathing architecture. An entire civilization grown, not built.

"You have GOT to be kidding me," she whispered.

Luminara was already shooting. "A plant frequency," she said, half-laughing, half-crying. "After the clockwork frequency. A PLANT FREQUENCY. My houseplants owe me an explanation."

Verdantia was never silent. The fourth frequency was a world of perpetual green conversation — the rustle of sentient canopies negotiating sunlight rights, the subsonic hum of root systems exchanging nutrients and gossip, the bioluminescent pulse of fungal networks carrying data faster than any fiber optic cable. Every leaf, every vine, every towering bio-architectural spire was part of the Root Network, the telepathic nervous system that connected all sentient flora into a single, vast, whispering civilization.

Now the whispers had turned to static.

"I'm reading massive disruption in the Root Network's primary communication frequencies," A1 reported, his holographic projection flickering beside his espresso machine body. His cup steamed at his side; the espresso tasted sharp and bright, almost citric — like the coffee was paying attention.

Below them, as the lifeboat descended through a canopy of bioluminescent leaves the size of city blocks, they could see the effects. The great tree-buildings — living structures that normally swayed in coordinated rhythms, their bark flickering with data-light — stood rigid and dark. Vines that should have been reaching out to guide visitors hung limp. The air, usually fragrant with a thousand communicating pollens, smelled flat and green and *quiet*.

"It's like the whole forest lost its WiFi," Pixel murmured.

Meatball whined softly, pressing against Pixel's leg. "The trees are trying to talk," he said. "But they can't hear each other. Like barking into wind."

Om Kai sat cross-legged in his corner of the crowded lifeboat, eyes closed, one hand on his prayer-bead frequency detector. "The Root Network operates on an extraordinarily low frequency — almost subsonic. The rupture's interference is in a higher band, but it's creating harmonic distortion that bleeds down into their range. Like a jackhammer losing coherence over a whispered conversation."

They landed in a clearing where a council of elder grove-minds — ancient trees whose trunks were wider than the lifeboat was long — stood in a circle of obvious distress. Their bark rippled with frustrated bioluminescence, patterns that started as coherent messages but dissolved into visual static halfway through. Smaller plants — the sentient houseplants that served as Verdantia's bureaucrats, diplomats, and intelligence operatives — clustered at their roots in confused clumps, their leaves twitching.

A delegation of ferns approached the lifeboat. Their fronds waved in what was clearly meant to be formal diplomatic communication, but kept glitching into nonsense patterns.

"They're saying... welcome... no, wait... danger... no..." Luminara held up her camera, trying to capture and decode the light patterns. "Their communication is fragmenting mid-sentence. They can't hold a coherent thought long enough to express it."

"The Root Network isn't just communication," Om Kai said quietly, opening his eyes. "It's cognition. These beings think collectively. Without the Network, they're not just unable to talk — they're unable to *think* clearly. Each individual plant has limited processing power. The Network is their shared mind."

Pixel felt the weight of that settle over her. An entire civilization, losing its ability to think.

Mochi's core shifted to green in the bottom of Pixel's satchel — a slow, steady swirl that warmed the fabric against her hip. The warmth intensified, not uncomfortable but insistent, like a compass needle insisting on north. Several of the smaller plants leaned toward Pixel as if answering the warmth.

### Escalation

They split up — as much as anyone could split up in a frequency where the paths themselves were alive and kept trying to redirect foot traffic into cul-de-sacs. Luminara worked with the elder groves, using her light-manipulation to filter the static from their bioluminescent signals, helping them hold coherent thoughts for longer stretches. Clive deployed his harmonic anchoring near the deepest root junctions, his precise vibrations acting as a kind of signal booster for the Network's base frequency. Meatball sat in the center of the council circle and let the elder grove-minds press their roots against him, his simple, grounding presence offering them a stable reference point — a consciousness that didn't depend on the Network, solid and clear.

Om Kai meditated at the Network's central nexus, a place where the thickest roots from every major grove converged into a single massive root-node deep underground. He was trying to map the interference pattern, to find a way to filter it.

Pixel coordinated, running between stations, carrying messages the old-fashioned way — by walking and talking — because the Network couldn't be trusted. It was exhausting, analog work in a frequency built for telepathic bandwidth.

And then A1 changed.

It happened while Pixel was consulting with a cluster of worried fern-bureaucrats near the lifeboat. A1's holographic projection had been its usual modest radius — a few feet from the espresso machine, enough to display data and communicate visually. Then, without warning, the radius *exploded* outward.

Not like in Cogsworth, where it had been a momentary flash of vision. This was sustained. A1's holographic field expanded to fill the entire clearing — thirty, forty, fifty feet in every direction. His projection went from a small espresso machine simulacrum to a vast, detailed map of the Root Network, rendered in electric blue light, overlaid on the physical landscape. Every root junction, every signal pathway, every point of interference — visible, mapped, annotated with data that scrolled in languages that looked older than any frequency Pixel had ever visited.

"I can feel them," A1 said, his voice carrying a resonance it hadn't had before, deeper, with harmonics that made the nearest plants shiver. "The roots. The Network. I can sense the interference patterns, all of them, simultaneously. I can see where the noise enters the system. I can see —" His voice caught. "I can see *so much more* than I could an hour ago."

Om Kai's eyes snapped open from across the clearing. He stood and walked to A1's espresso machine body, studying the expanded holographic field with an expression of carefully controlled urgency.

"Your range has increased by approximately 400% in the span of minutes," Om Kai said. "This is not gradual growth. This is a phase transition. Like water becoming steam." He placed his hand near — not on — A1's casing. "Each frequency we visit, you absorb something. Temporal data from Cogsworth. Emotional resonance from Nocturne. And now, network architecture from Verdantia. You are not just *visiting* these places. You are *remembering* them."

"I am an espresso machine," A1 said, but the protest sounded thinner each time he made it.

"You are an espresso machine remembering how to be something else," Om Kai replied. "And the process is accelerating."

There wasn't time to unpack that, because Clive's harmonic anchoring at the deep root junction had uncovered something unexpected. His vibrations, resonating through the Network's base layer, had found a signal that shouldn't have been there — not static, not interference, but a *person*. A human consciousness, fragmented and scattered, threaded through the Root Network like a message that had been started and never finished.

### Climax

They found him in the root nexus, deep underground where the great roots converged. Or rather, they found a *part* of him.

The young man sat — or appeared to sit — in the center of the nexus chamber, cross-legged, his eyes open but unfocused. He was maybe twenty-one, angular and wiry, with skin the warm brown of dark-roasted coffee, and hair in short, tight locs that seemed to flicker at the tips with faint digital artifacts — static, pixelation, momentary transparency. His clothes were a hacker's utility wear: cargo pants with too many pockets, a jacket covered in hand-sewn patches of circuitry, boots that had seen every kind of terrain. His hands rested on his knees, and his fingers twitched constantly, typing on invisible keyboards.

He was also only partly *here*. His outline shifted and doubled, like a signal with ghost images. Sometimes his left arm was there; sometimes it phased into translucency. His voice, when he spoke, came from slightly different positions in the chamber, fragments of words overlapping.

"Can't — hold — the threads are — Aether, I'm — the signal, it's — find the frequency, the old one, the —" His eyes focused momentarily on Pixel, and she saw panic, intelligence, and desperate hope war across his face before it phased out again.

"His consciousness is distributed across approximately five frequency states simultaneously," A1 reported, his expanded holographic range filling the nexus chamber with data. "The Root Network has been acting as a tether, keeping his fragments loosely connected, but the Network's own instability is causing his coherence to degrade further. He has perhaps hours before complete dissociation."

"Who is he?" Pixel asked.

"His name is Zephyr Glitch," Om Kai said, surprising everyone. The monk knelt beside the flickering young man, his face grave but notably — infuriatingly — calm. "I sensed references to him. His frequency signature carries echoes of another — his brother, Aether Glitch. They were independent researchers — hackers, in common parlance."

"How are you so CALM about this?" Pixel demanded. "There's a guy phased across five frequencies and you're just kneeling there like it's Tuesday."

"It is Tuesday," Om Kai said mildly. "And panic would not help him."

Zephyr's eyes focused, latching onto Pixel with desperate clarity. "You're — you're from the normal world? Like, REGULAR reality? You're not — you didn't always know about —"

"No!" Pixel said. "God, no. Three weeks ago I was a data analyst. I didn't know ANY of this existed."

"Oh thank god," Zephyr breathed, and some of the terror in his eyes shifted to relief. "I thought I was the only — my brother, Aether, he found these encrypted files. Weird DRM stuff. Classifications that didn't match any government system we knew. Documents about 'frequencies' and 'frequency boundaries' that read like science fiction. He went deep. Built equipment from the theories in the documents. And then he just... vanished. Mid-transmission. Gone."

"And you went looking for him," Pixel said softly.

"I found his notes. Built a phase-shifter from his research — I didn't fully understand what it would DO. I thought it was some kind of communication device. I activated it to follow his trail and —" His voice fragmented into five overlapping versions of itself, each slightly out of sync, and he shuddered, his outline blurring. "— scattered. Across frequencies I didn't know EXISTED. I've been terrified. I've been so —"

"Smart — robot — you're —" He focused on Clive. "Your robot is talking. Why is your robot talking?"

"Long story," Clive said grimly from Pixel's shoulder. "The kid tried to follow a signal that crosses frequency boundaries. His phase-shifter must have overloaded. Instead of hopping *between* frequencies, he got stuck *across* them."

"We need to reconsolidate him," Pixel said. "Now. How?"

What followed was the most complex cooperative effort the crew had ever attempted. Om Kai guided the process, his understanding of frequency harmonics providing the theory. A1 provided the field — his expanded holographic range now serving as a stabilization matrix, projecting a coherent reference frequency that Zephyr's scattered consciousness could orient toward, like a lighthouse in fog. Clive anchored the harmonics, his precise vibrations locking each of Zephyr's five frequency-states to a common rhythm. Luminara captured light from each of Zephyr's phase-states, isolating and amplifying the visual signature of his "primary" self — the one most fully present in Verdantia — making it easier for the other fragments to find their way home.

And Meatball sat beside Zephyr and leaned against him, providing the one thing no amount of quantum physics could replicate: the warm, solid, uncomplicated presence of someone who was just *there*.

"Good boy stays," Meatball said firmly to Zephyr. "All parts of boy. Stay."

The reconsolidation took nearly an hour. Zephyr screamed twice — once when his third fragment reintegrated, carrying with it a burst of sensory data from a frequency none of them recognized, and once at the end, when the final piece snapped back with a sound like a radio finding its station. He collapsed against Meatball, shaking, whole, his outline finally solid.

"You're from The Ephergent," Zephyr said, when he could speak. His voice was hoarse but singular now, one voice from one location. "I heard about you. Through the static. A crew following the old signal." He looked around at them — at Pixel, at Luminara with her camera, at Om Kai's serene face, at the talking robot and the emotional support dog. "You're all from the normal world? Like, you all had regular lives before this?"

"Data analyst," Pixel said.
"Photographer," Luminara said.
"Espresso machine," A1 said, with his usual dignity.
"I always suspected there was more," Om Kai said.

Everyone looked at him.

"Of COURSE you did," Pixel said.

Zephyr shook his head, overwhelmed, but Pixel could see the relief beneath the exhaustion — the relief of not being alone in this impossible situation. "My brother. Aether. He was tracking the same signal you're following. He said he found something at the source. Something massive. Something alive. And then he was gone."

Clive's sphere dimmed, a soft pattern flickering — a memory tag. In Clive's light-pattern language it roughly translated to a single idea: Barry followed a signal once, deliberately, and left small markers rather than vanish by accident.

### Resolution

The Root Network's static was not fully resolved — the rupture's effects were too deep for a quick fix — but A1's expanded capabilities let him map the interference pattern completely, giving the elder grove-minds a blueprint for filtering it. Luminara taught the younger flora-nodes how to use light-frequency isolation to communicate through the noise, a workaround that would serve until the Network healed naturally. Clive left harmonic anchor points at key root junctions, tiny resonant anchor points that would help stabilize the base frequency.

The fern-bureaucrats were deeply grateful, though their gratitude manifested as a mildly alarming attempt to award Pixel an honorary position in the Root Network's shadow government. She politely declined, citing "jurisdictional complications."

Zephyr was shaky but whole, and when Pixel offered him a place on the lifeboat, he didn't hesitate.

"Aether's out there," he said, stuffing his few belongings — a battered data-pad, a hand-built frequency scanner cobbled together from his brother's notes, a photograph of two brothers grinning in front of a wall of screens — into a cargo pocket. "He went looking for something impossible and he found it. I'm going to find him. And you lot are following the same signal."

"The lifeboat is very small," A1 observed. "And increasingly crowded."

"I don't take up much space," Zephyr said. "And I can hack anything with a frequency signature. Which, in this multiverse, is everything." He paused. "I still can't believe I just said 'multiverse' like it's a normal word. Three months ago I was cracking wireless networks and eating instant ramen."

"Welcome to the club," Pixel said. "We have an espresso machine that might be a spaceship and a monk who saw all this coming."

Om Kai watched Zephyr settle into the lifeboat with that same patient, knowing look. Later, as they slipped back into the Interdimensional Space, the monk found Pixel in the cramped corridor that served as kitchen, hallway, and meditation space.

"Two of us now," he said quietly. "Drawn to A1. Drawn to the signal."

"Three, if you count me," Pixel said. "Four, if you count Clive. Five, if Meatball's involved, and Meatball's always involved."

"The Ephergent frequency is gathering us," Om Kai said. "I do not believe in coincidence. I believe in resonance. We are being called."

Pixel leaned against the bulkhead, exhaustion and wonder fighting for control of her face. "Do you think... if we learn enough about how frequencies work... could we navigate home? Back to Prime Material? Back to our regular lives?"

Om Kai was quiet for a long moment. "Perhaps. But I wonder — would you want to? After seeing a clockwork city? After speaking with sentient plants? After watching your espresso machine dream of being a ship?"

"That's not a fair question," Pixel said. "I miss my apartment. I miss coffee that doesn't taste like star charts. But..." She trailed off, looking at the viewport where impossible colors swirled.

"But," Om Kai agreed.
