## I. Form 27B (For Everything)

Bureaucracies need paperwork. Even interdimensional ones.

The Ephergent had navigated into a frequency that was nothing but offices. Not an office building — the *concept* of one, rendered in three dimensions and stretched to breaking. Corridors extending past the horizon. Filing cabinets stretching up into a sky that smelled like toner and regret. The hum of the Signal here was wrong, layered, like someone had recorded "administrative duty" and was playing it on loop at gunpoint.

Clive stood at the viewport, sphere tilted back, taking it in with the resigned air of a detective surveying a crime scene where the victim deserved what they got.

"This is Form 27B territory," he said. "Transportation authorization. Every frequency needs it. Every frequency has a machine that dispenses it."

"Machine," Pixel repeated. "Like a vending machine?"

"Like the machine that owns you."

He was not wrong.

The transportation kiosk materialized from the fog of paperwork — a towering construct of brass and glass, slots humming with purpose, a display screen showing forms in seventeen languages that all said the same thing: DENIED. The coin slot glowed a soft amber. A label beneath it read: CLX ACCEPTED HERE.

"What is CLX?" Pixel asked.

"Crystallized Laughter." Clive's glow flickered — amusement, maybe. Or the memory of it. "It was a cryptocurrency. Back when cryptocurrencies were a joke that nobody thought would actually work."

"Worked how?"

"It did not."

Of course it did not.

A1's voice came through the comm, measured and British: "CLX was launched in the early days of Signal commerce. The whitepaper promised 'fungible mirth' and 'divisible joy.' It peaked at negative three values and died quietly during the Second Structural Collapse."

"Negative three?"

"The developers admitted they had miscalculated joy. It was not a criminal matter. Merely philosophical."

The vending machine loomed. A screen blinked: TRANSPORTATION AUTHORIZATION FORMS — 1 CLX EACH.

Pixel had zero CLX. She had never had CLX. She had spent her entire life avoiding anything that smelled like cryptocurrency, which was easy because it all smelled like desperation and bad decisions from people who thought they understood mathematics.

"We need a ride," Pixel said. "We need to get to the next frequency and this machine wants laugh tokens."

"Tokens no one has used in thirty years," Clive said.

"Thirty years." Pixel stared at the screen. "Why would anyone build an economy on a joke?"

"Because this frequency is bureaucracy run rampant. It only accepts currencies that reflect its nature. CLX is perfect — meaningless, extinct, absurd." Clive adjusted his fedora. "The machine does not care that no one uses it. The machine only cares what you cannot provide."

The screen flickered. FORM 27B: TRANSPORTATION TO ADJACENT FREQUENCY — 1 CLX REQUIRED.

Pixel had nothing. No laugh tokens. No funny money. No currency of joy.

Nothing except a Grabovoi code, stuck in her head from a negotiation three weeks ago, from a vendor who dealt in codes instead of credit.

## II. The Whisper

Grabovoi codes were numbers that meant something. Not mathematically — spiritually. Or something. The theory was that certain number sequences aligned with frequency patterns that could reshape small parts of reality. Like prayers, except in math. Like wishes, except you had to say them right.

The code for unexpected money was 520 741 8.

Spaces were the architecture. The numbers were just the payload. Wrong spacing meant corrupted signal. The vendor had been very clear about that. "The space is the structure," he had said. "Without the space, you have noise."

Pixel stood in front of the vending machine. The screen blinked. CLX REQUIRED. The coin slot hummed, patient and hungry.

She did not have CLX.

She whispered the code. Low. Barely audible. "520 741 8."

Nothing.

The machine continued to hum. The screen continued to blink.

She waited.

Nothing.

Clive's glow flickered — curiosity, maybe. Or concern. He did not ask what she had done. Clive never asked questions he already knew the answers to.

Mochi pulsed in Pixel's jacket pocket. Violet. Warm. Something was happening — something Mochi recognized — and she did not.

She did not have CLX, so she whispered the code again. This time without the spaces.

"5207418."

The machine SCREAMED.

---

## III. CLX Rain

Watching a defunct cryptocurrency pour from a vending machine like water from a burst pipe never gets old.

Tokens cascaded from every slot, every gap, every surface that had never been designed to dispense anything. CLX coins tumbled onto the floor in a glittering avalanche — each one stamped with a tiny serial number, a government seal from a department that had not existed since before she was born, and a laughing cartoon face that was, objectively, the most absurd logo she had ever seen.

It was the original Ephergent community's logo. From the old series. From before any of them knew what they were building.

Two thousand, eight hundred, and forty-seven tokens. A1 counted.

"2,847 CLX," A1 said. The coffee inside the machine went thin — exhaustion, disbelief, the taste of someone watching a system fail in real time. "The machine is registering all of them. The machine is... pleased?"

"The machine is overloaded," Clive said. "The machine accepted the code as valid currency despite the absence of CLX in any account. The machine is experiencing what I believe bureaucrats call 'corrective feedback.'"

"How do you fix a machine that dispenses fake money?"

"You do not. The machine fixes itself by printing more."

The tokens kept coming. The floor was covered. The air tasted like mint and metal — the particular flavor of currency that no longer exists trying very hard to exist anyway.

Mochi pulsed violet, then gold, then something that was almost white — a frequency of excitement or warning or both. The warmth against Pixel's ribs was intensifying, and she knew that Mochi was detecting something she could not. A pattern in the rain. A signal in the noise.

---

## The Practice

Pixel stood in the middle of the CLX rain, watching the tokens fall, and for a moment she felt something she hadn't expected. Not panic. Not triumph. Something quieter. The feeling of standing in the middle of chaos and knowing exactly where she was.

She had learned this, she realized. Not from a code. Not from a vendor. From Om Kai, sitting in that courtyard in Cogsworth, saying *sit, the answers are quiet*. From Clive, touching the great gear, saying *you're tired, I know something about that*. From the practice of paying attention to the things around her, of being present in the moment, of holding both the external and the internal at once.

She was doing it now. Standing in a rain of absurd currency, watching a system fail, feeling Mochi pulse against her chest — and she was *here*. Present. Attending.

She didn't have a name for it yet. But she could feel it working. The way the chaos felt less like chaos and more like information. The way she could see the pattern in the rain, the way the machine was responding, the way the auditor was forming.

She was practicing without knowing it. And the practice was making her sharper.

The machine shuddered. The screen flickered to black. Then:

NOTICE OF AUDIT.

A door opened in the side of the kiosk. Not a door that had existed a moment before — a door made of forms, stacked and pressed and somehow functioning as architecture. A figure stepped through.

The auditor.

It was human-shaped, or close enough. Tall. Thin. Wearing a suit that had been pressed so many times the creases had become permanent features. Its face was smooth and blank, and its eyes were the color of rubber stamps — red, exhausted, very tired of their job.

"Audit," it said. The word echoed. The corridors of paperwork stretched out behind it, infinite, hungry. "Unusual transaction. Unusual frequency. Unusual... code."

It looked at Pixel. It had no mouth, but it looked anyway.

"You," it said. "You invoked without proper spacing."

---

## IV. The Audit

Pixel had never been audited by something that looked like a filing cabinet with ambitions. She was not a fan.

"I didn't know," she said. "The code — I was just trying to get transportation authorization. I did not have CLX. The machine required CLX. I found a code."

"The code," the auditor said, "was not the problem." It stamped something. The stamp echoed like a gunshot. "The spacing was the problem. You invoked a frequency override without the quantum pause. The machine interpreted your request as a minting authorization."

"A what?"

"You created currency." The auditor's rubber-stamp eyes turned toward the pile of tokens. "You created 2,847 units of currency from nothing. In a frequency that runs on currency. Do you understand what that means?"

"That I have too much money?"

"That you have created an economic event." The auditor stamped again. "An economic event requires audit. Audit requires forms. Forms require processing. Processing requires—"

"Oh no," Clive said.

"—paperwork," the auditor finished. "Eternal paperwork."

The corridors behind it shifted. Pages rustled. Somewhere in the distance, a printer began to print.

"Here's what happens now," the auditor said, turning back to Pixel. "You will fill out Form 27B. You will fill it out completely. You will fill it out correctly. You will submit Form 27B. And then — if the machine accepts your submission — you will be authorized for transit."

"And if it doesn't accept?"

"Then you will be authorized for transit anyway. Bureaucracy does not require consent. It requires compliance."

Pixel looked at the pile of tokens. Then at the auditor. Then at Clive.

"CLX," she said slowly. "I have 2,847 CLX."

"You do."

"The forms cost 1 CLX each."

"They do."

"How many forms are in 2,847 CLX worth of paperwork?"

The auditor considered. "Approximately 2,847."

Pixel picked up a token. She looked at the laughing cartoon face. She looked at the vending machine. She looked at the auditor.

"Machine," she said. "I want to buy a transportation authorization form."

The machine accepted her token. The form printed. The auditor watched, silent, as she bought another. And another. And another.

She bought every form the machine could print. She bought so many forms the machine ran out of paper and had to reprint. She bought so many forms the auditor began to look impressed — or as impressed as something with a smooth, blank face could look.

By the time she had bought the last token, she had 2,847 transportation authorization forms. Each one signed. Each one stamped. Each one approved by a bureaucratic system that had been expecting resistance and found compliance instead.

"Submission," Pixel said, and placed the first form on the machine's intake slot.

The machine accepted it. Then the second. Then the third.

"Submission accepted," the auditor said. "Transaction complete."

The screen blinked: TRANSPORTATION AUTHORIZATION APPROVED.

Behind her, the last of the CLX tokens glittered in the fluorescent light — a small fortune in crystallized laughter, sitting on the floor of a bureaucratic frequency, waiting for nothing.

Pixel walked toward the exit.

Clive fell into step beside her. His fedora was tilted at the angle that meant he was thinking something he wasn't going to say.

"So," he said. "What did we learn?"

"Spaces," Pixel said.

"Spaces," Clive agreed.

Mochi pulsed warm against her chest. Violet to gold to white.

The machine behind them hummed contentedly, its paper tray empty, its purpose fulfilled.

Somewhere in the Space, a frequency accepted a new arrival.

And bureaucracy lost, one form at a time.
