# Barry's Desk

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The coordinates led them to a frequency that shouldn't exist.

Not "shouldn't exist" in the sense of forbidden or hidden — "shouldn't exist" in the sense that the physics here simply declined to cooperate with itself. A1's sensors returned contradictory readings: mass that was and wasn't, gravity that pulled in multiple directions, a location that appeared on the navigation charts as a faded notation and then disappeared when they tried to look directly at it.

"It's not hidden," Om Kai said, closing his eyes as the Ephergent drifted closer. "It's shy."

The crew had gathered on the bridge — all of them now, the full complement that had coalesced over the past weeks. Pixel in the captain's chair, still marveling at the simple act of having a chair that belonged to her. A1 at the center of everything, his espresso machine body humming with the quiet contentment of a ship that was remembering how to be whole. Clive perched on the archive desk, his sphere head tilted at an angle that suggested deep thought. Luminara with her camera, always her camera. Zephyr at the communications array, fingers ready to boost whatever signal they found. Om Kai, prayer beads moving through his fingers in slow meditation. Meatball settled in his usual spot near the engine room threshold, ears swiveling toward signals no one else could hear.

And Mochi, warm in Pixel's satchel, pulsing in colors that had no names.

"What does that mean, 'shy'?" Zephyr asked. "Frequencies aren't people."

"No," Om Kai agreed. "But they can be embarrassed. This one has been alone a long time. It stopped announcing itself because there was no one to hear."

The frequency resolved slowly as they approached — not visually, not exactly, but in the way the sensors agreed on what they were seeing. A structure. Small. A single room, or what functioned as a room, suspended in the Space like a thought that had forgotten how to let go.

The room had a desk. The desk had a chair. The chair had the distinct impression of someone who had sat there for a very long time, waiting.

"It's a field station," Clive said quietly. He had moved to the viewport, his sphere dim with something Pixel couldn't read. "Officially unofficial. The kind of place where someone goes when they have questions the institution doesn't want answered."

"DRM?" Pixel asked.

"DRM-adjacent. The notes would have been filed under 'personal effects' if anyone had found them. They weren't looking."

The docking clamps engaged. The Ephergent settled beside the small structure with the gentle precision of a ship that knew how to be careful with fragile things. A1 extended the gangway — a narrow passage of light and quantum field that connected ship to structure, a handshake in the form of technology.

Pixel stood at the threshold. The field station was dark beyond, but not empty. Never empty. She could feel it — the way you feel a room that's been closed up for too long, the way you feel the weight of abandoned spaces.

"I'm going in," she said. "Alone."

The protests came immediately, overlapping, all of them wrong.

"Captain—" A1 began.

"Pix—" Luminara started.

"Kid—" Clive's sphere pulsed with something urgent.

Pixel held up a hand. "This is Barry's space. Whoever he was — whatever he was doing out here — this is where he did it. Someone should see it first. Someone who knows what it means."

She looked at Clive. Something passed between them — not words, not light-patterns, just a shared understanding of grief and patience and the particular weight of finding someone else's unfinished business.

"I'll be careful," she said. "And I won't be long."

The gangway hummed beneath her feet as she crossed. The field station's atmosphere was stale but breathable, recycled by systems that had been running on standby for longer than Pixel had been alive. She could feel A1's attention on her — not surveillance, just presence, the way a ship watches its captain when the captain is doing something that matters.

The room was exactly what Clive had described. Small. Utilitarian. A desk, a chair, a shelf of notebooks. The walls were bare except for a single photograph, tucked in the corner where someone might not see it if they weren't looking.

Pixel walked to the desk. The surface was covered in papers — not scattered, not abandoned, but arranged with the careful precision of someone who was coming back. Someone who expected to return. Forms and charts and handwritten notes, all of them dense with the kind of detail that only matters if you understand what you're looking at.

She picked up the topmost notebook. Brown leather, worn soft at the edges. The same kind of notebook Clive had described in the relay station — Barry's notebooks. The same handwriting she had glimpsed in Clive's files.

The first page was dated. The date was from eighteen months ago — eighteen months before the DRM fell, before the rupture, before any of this began.

The entry read:

*Day 1. Still no response from the calibration team. They're ignoring my findings, which means they don't understand them. That's fine. I'll keep documenting. Eventually someone will listen.*

Pixel turned the page.

*Day 2. The frequency drift is worse than I thought. The old instruments don't lie, even when the people reading them do. Something is pulling at the edges. Something that doesn't want to be found. I'm going to name it The Drift. It sounds ominous. That's appropriate.*

Another page.

*Day 3. Clive is still working. I don't think he knows I'm paying attention. That's fine. He's not ready to be seen. Neither am I, really. But I see him. I see what he is. And I'm going to write it down, so that when I'm gone — if I'm gone — someone will know.*

The notebook ended there. Not abandoned — interrupted. The last entry was dated the day before Barry Kowalski disappeared.

Pixel set the notebook down. Her hands were steady, but something in her chest was not. She thought about Clive in the archive room, surrounded by three thousand years of cold cases. She thought about the fedora that Barry had given him, and the twenty-three years of night shifts, and the careful documentation of someone who knew that evidence matters, that someone has to remember, that the truth doesn't stop being true just because no one is watching.

She picked up the photograph from the wall. It showed a younger Barry — not young, exactly, but younger — standing in front of a building she didn't recognize. He was smiling. The kind of smile that doesn't know the future is coming.

Behind him, barely visible in the background, was a shape she did recognize. A escape pod. Form 27-B. The same model they'd escaped Prime Material in.

The photograph was dated eleven years ago.

Pixel looked at the photograph for a long time. Then she looked at the desk, and the notebooks, and the careful arrangement of evidence that Barry had left behind, as if he knew someone would eventually come looking.

He had been preparing for this. For them. For the moment when someone would finally arrive at this small, shy frequency and ask the questions that needed asking.

He had known.

The walk back to the Ephergent was quiet. The gangway hummed beneath her feet. The field station's systems cycled down behind her, the lights dimming as if the structure was exhaling for the first time in years.

Pixel found Clive on the bridge, waiting. His sphere was dim, his fedora tilted at an angle she had learned to read as grief and gratitude intertwined.

"He left you a message," she said.

Clive didn't move. "I know."

"He left you everything. All of it. The notebooks, the research, the photographs. He knew you'd be the one to find it."

Pixel held out the brown leather notebook. The last of Barry's field notes. The last evidence of twenty-three years of watching and waiting and not being seen.

"He saw you," she said. "From the very beginning. He knew what you were. And he wrote it down so that you wouldn't have to carry it alone."

Clive's hands closed around the notebook. His sphere pulsed — once, twice, three times — a rhythm she didn't recognize but understood anyway. Something releasing. Something settling.

"Thank you," he said.

The word was simple. The weight behind it was not.

Pixel nodded. She walked to the viewport and looked out at the Space — at the frequencies, at the static, at the infinite expanse of things that needed to be found and the finite number of people who could find them.

"Clive," she said. "What's in the notebooks?"

He was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was layered — not multiple versions, just the particular resonance of someone who had finally set down something they'd been carrying too long.

"Everything," he said. "The Drift. The Builders. The frequency storm that broke A1 apart. The coordinates to places that haven't existed for centuries. And..."

He paused.

"And a map to somewhere called the Wellspring. A place where the Builders went. A place where everything that faded is still waiting."

Pixel turned around. The crew was watching — A1's lights warm, Luminara's camera ready, Zephyr's fingers poised over his console, Om Kai's prayer beads still, Meatball's ears forward. Waiting.

"Then we add it to the mission," Pixel said. "Wellspring. First the lost frequencies, then the ancient ones. And somewhere in between, we follow Barry's map and find out what happened to the Builders."

She sat in the captain's chair. She looked at her crew — her impossible, cobbled-together, magnificent crew — and she smiled.

"A1," she said. "Take us home."

A1's coffee shifted flavor. Something warm and bright. Something that tasted like beginning.

The Ephergent flew on.
