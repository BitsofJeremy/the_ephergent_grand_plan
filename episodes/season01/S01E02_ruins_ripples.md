# Ruins, Ripples, and Really Nervous Dinosaurs

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The escape pod shudder through the phase-shift. That was the only way to describe it — a gut-twisting lurch that started somewhere behind Pixel's sternum and spread outward, like her insides were trying to remember which reality they were supposed to be in. She had done this a handful of times now. She would have thought she'd get used to it. She didn't. Meatball never managed it either — he just slid across the floor every time, legs splayed, looking at her like she was the one who decided gravity should be optional.

The scratched porthole filled with light as Prime Material reasserted itself around them. The familiar skyline materialized: glass office towers catching the afternoon sun, the grey-blue haze of a city that was perfectly ordinary forty-eight hours ago. Forty-eight hours. It felt like forty-eight years. It felt like yesterday. She didn't know which of those was true. Maybe all of them at once.

But something was wrong.

The skyline stuttered. Not metaphorically — actually stuttered, like a video feed buffering. Buildings flickered. Their facades shimmered, going translucent for a heartbeat, and behind them Pixel saw impossible geometries: angles that shouldn't meet, structures that folded in on themselves in ways that hurt to look at. Then it snapped back to normal, like a blink she almost caught. One of the high-rises downtown seemed to exist in two places at once, stacked on top of itself, the upper half offset by a few feet like someone got the alignment wrong. She wondered if anyone inside was seeing double. She wondered if they were getting headaches. She wondered if they were even noticing.

The streets below were chaos. Traffic lights cycling through colors that shouldn't exist — a yellow that pulsed purple, a red that flickered like a dying ember. Car GPS systems rerouting frantically, recalculating routes to destinations that might not be there when they arrived. People on sidewalks stopping, staring, filming with their phones, running. The usual disaster kaleidoscope, except the kaleidoscope was broken and the pieces were sharp.

A1's holographic projection flickered into existence near the porthole, scanning the scene. He had gotten better at this — better at projecting, better at holding a shape that almost passed for solid. But there was something in his posture that day that hadn't been there before. A tension she didn't have a word for.

"Significant frequency instability across the Prime Material substrate," he said. His voice was measured, controlled — British formal, even when delivering what sounds like bad news. But Pixel had been listening to A1 for a few days now, and she caught the edge underneath. Concern, maybe. Or the memory of concern, borrowed from whoever he used to be. "The reality rupture appears to have destabilized the local frequency membrane. These ripples will—"

He stopped. Recalibrated. Something crossed his projection — surprise, maybe. Or recognition. Something that flickered across his face like a cloud across the sun.

"These are *frequency-based* ripples," he said slowly. "Other frequencies are bleeding through the membrane."

He paused. Processed something she couldn't see.

"That shouldn't — I shouldn't know that term."

Another pause. Longer this time. He was looking at something buried deep, something that surfaced and submerged like a creature in dark water. She didn't push. She didn't ask. Some memories surfaced on their own schedule.

"The DRM headquarters is..." He stopped again. His projection sharpened, focused. "I recommend preparing yourself, Pixel."

She didn't like the sound of that. She really didn't like the sound of that.

Pixel felt it in her stomach before she understood it in her head. Meatball whined. Clive's sphere pulsed once — blue-white, quick — and then dimmed. Even Mochi, warm against her hip, shifted temperature. Not hot. Not cool. Just different. Alert.

Something was very wrong at the Department of Reality Maintenance.

And they needed to find out what.

---

### The Landing

They set the escape pod down in a public park near the DRM district. The grass was still green, still ordinary, still doing the things grass does — a small miracle in a day of impossibilities. The park fountain was sputtering, its water occasionally freezing mid-arc or flowing upward, defying gravity in stuttering bursts like a faucet that got bad news. Pixel watched a droplet hang in the air, suspended, refusing to decide which direction to fall.

Pixel stepped out. Meatball followed, nose working overtime, tail low. He wasn't happy. He hadn't been happy since reality started falling apart, and she couldn't blame him. She'd be stressed too if her sense of smell was good enough to detect emotional distress in strangers — actually, wait, that was probably exactly why she was stressed. She was picking up on things she didn't have words for.

Clive walked beside her, his small legs keeping pace. His sphere head glowed a quiet blue-white. Occasionally he paused, tilted his head at an angle she had learned to read as consideration, and said nothing. She read the silences. *BAD. WORSE. MUCH WORSE.* She didn't need him to spell it out with light patterns anymore. After two days with this crew, she was learning to speak fluent Clive.

In her jacket pocket, against her hip, Mochi was warm. She noticed. She always noticed now — the way the temperature shifted, the subtle pulses that didn't quite match his usual rhythms. She had learned not to ask. Mochi communicated in ways that didn't require questions. Warmth meant something. Cool meant something else. The gradual shifting between was a language she hadn't mastered but was starting to understand the grammar of.

She thought about that sometimes, when they were flying between frequencies. She thought about how she had spent her whole life surrounded by people who spoke English perfectly well and she never understood them at all. And now she was learning to read a robot that didn't have a face.

Maybe that's what this whole thing was about. Learning to understand things that don't speak your language.

---

### The DRM Headquarters is a Ruin

Not a clean ruin — not collapsed neatly like a demolished building with a sensible demolition crew and proper permits. The DRM building was *arguing with itself*. Sections of wall phased in and out of visibility, showing rooms that shouldn't exist stacked behind the ordinary offices she remembered. Filing cabinets hung suspended in mid-air, their contents drifting into the breeze as loose forms — Form 19-Qs and Anomaly Reports and Frequency Variance Logs — scattered across the ruins like bureaucratic confetti. Someone's career review fluttered past. A training attendance sheet. The ambient debris of a life that was interrupted.

A perimeter of DRM caution tape surrounded the site, but the tape itself was unstable, its warning text shifting: "CAUTION: STRUCTURAL DAMAGE" becomes "CAUTION: REALITY DISRUPTION" becomes "NOTICE: FREQUENCY BREACH — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY" — warnings that the pre-rupture DRM would never have displayed publicly, would never have needed to, because this was supposed to stay hidden. Pixel read the changing text and thought: even the caution signs were having an identity crisis.

But it was what was *below* the building that stopped her cold.

The collapse had torn open the sub-basements. Seven levels of hidden infrastructure that the ordinary DRM headquarters was sitting on top of like a boring hat on an impossible head. Labs full of technology she had never seen. Monitoring equipment that was clearly tracking something far beyond building code violations. Classified archives with documents stamped "FREQUENCY SENSITIVE — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY." Schematics for devices that manipulate the fabric of reality itself. Maps of places labeled "Nocturne Aeturnus," "Cogsworth Cogitarium," "The Verdant Deep" — other frequencies, other *worlds*, catalogued and monitored and hidden behind four floors of fluorescent lighting and mandatory fun committees and the world's most aggressive potluck sign-up sheet.

Pixel stood in the ruins of a lab she never knew existed, holding a clipboard she recognized.

Her handwriting. Her anomaly reports. Filed into a system that was feeding data to *this*.

She had filed Form 19-Q about "minor frequency variance in Sector 7" and it went HERE. To a secret lab studying cross-frequency physics. Her hands shook. She thought about every coffee break, every casual conversation by the water cooler, every time she complained about the vending machine selections. All that time, underneath her feet, something else was happening. Something she was part of without knowing it.

There was a name for that feeling. The feeling of realizing you were a small piece of something much bigger, and you never knew. She didn't know what that name was. But she felt it now.

Former DRM employees wandered the perimeter, dazed. Some were in uniform. Some were in civilian clothes, having been off-shift when the rupture hit. Most of them looked as blindsided as she had been — fellow paper-pushers who had no idea what was underneath them.

But a few — a handful — had a different expression. Not shock. *Guilt*. Pixel filed that observation away. She was going to think about that later. She was going to think about it a lot.

On a personnel board near the ruined HR office, among the lost ID badges and abandoned access cards, she glimpsed a name she didn't yet recognize: **B. KOWALSKI — Night Security, Sub-Level 3, 23 Years Service.** Twenty-three years. Someone else was there before her. Someone else worked nights in a place they didn't understand, filing reports about things they didn't know existed. She filed it. Noted. Not relevant. Not yet.

But she was learning that "not yet" was just a way of saying "later," and later always came.

---

### The Vultures

But nature abhors a vacuum, and so does commerce. Someone always shows up to fill the space where things used to be.

The reality entrepreneurs had already arrived. Folding tables and popup tents lined the streets around the DRM ruins. Fast-talking vendors selling "Frequency Stabilization Patches" — adhesive strips that allegedly smoothed out local reality ripples.

"Guaranteed for twenty-four hours!" shouted a vendor with a megaphone and a smile that was slightly too wide. "Stick it on your wall, your desk, your forehead — keeps reality where it belongs!"

The patches glowed faintly. Pixel's analytical instincts said they were doing *something* — but it was the equivalent of putting a band-aid on a broken bone. The bone was still broken. The band-aid was for the person who didn't want to look at it. She wanted to tell them that. She didn't. They were just trying to make a living. They were just trying to make sense of the same impossible day.

Others were selling "Ripple Insurance," "Reality Continuity Plans," and — her personal favorite — "Commemorative DRM Collapse Souvenir Snow Globes" that contained actual fragments of unstable reality. Shake it and you saw a tiny DRM headquarters collapse in miniature. Tasteful. She was going to remember that vendor. She was going to remember his face when she broadcast later.

The real crisis was everywhere.

The financial district was in meltdown. Bank servers kept phasing out of existence — one moment a bank's records were there, the next they were somewhere else, or nowhere, or a different bank entirely. ATMs displayed error messages in languages that didn't exist: pixelated symbols that might be letters, might be warnings, might be something the machines were trying to say in their own dying tongue.

The stock exchange building couldn't maintain consistent geometry. Its floors shuffled like a deck of cards. Trading had been halted indefinitely.

On the steps of the Exchange, a suited executive screamed into a phone that kept turning transparent in his hand. Behind him, the digital ticker — when it was visible — showed nothing but red. The red was the only thing that was consistent anymore.

And in the gaps where reality was thinnest, *things* were bleeding through.

Shapes that didn't belong. A shadow with too many legs crossed a wall and vanished. Something that might be a bird but had geometry instead of feathers circled a church steeple before dissolving. People were filming everything on phones that intermittently refused to function.

Social media, when it worked, was a firehose of panic and confusion and a surprising amount of people who were still trying to make jokes. Someone had already started a conspiracy theory about the government hiding interdimensional travel. Pixel thought: they're not wrong. They're just early.

---

### Deep Basement — Clive's Doorway

Pixel descended.

The sub-basements went down farther than she expected. Past Sub-Level 1. Past Sub-Level 2. Into levels that don't have names on the emergency signage because nobody was supposed to know they existed.

Sub-Level 3. Sub-Level 4. Sub-Level 5.

The air changed with each level. Warmer. Older. Humming with equipment she didn't recognize. The temperature rose like a secret that had been kept too long. The hum deepened like a voice learning to speak.

Clive stopped at a doorway.

It was nothing special. A standard DRM door frame, institutional grey. A sign read "SUB-LEVEL 7 — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY" in letters that flickered with the power fluctuations. But Clive stopped. His sphere head tilted up toward the frame. His glow brightened — just for a moment, a blue-white pulse she might have missed if she wasn't watching.

She was watching. She was learning to watch.

"Efficient architecture," Clive said. His voice was quiet. Almost to himself. "Someone designed this to last."

Beyond the doorway: a DRM processing center. Rows of intake stations. Automated filing systems. Mechanical sorters moving forms from bin to bin to bin. Stamps clicking. Stamps placing. Stamps moving on.

An automated assembly line of bureaucracy that had been running for decades. Processing anomalies. Filing reports. Sorting things that were never meant to be sorted. Forms feeding into forms feeding into forms. Nobody reading the results. Nobody listening to the signal.

The machines didn't care. The machines just kept moving.

Like a heart that doesn't know it's pumping blood for a body that's already gone.

Clive watched. His glow dimmed. Blue-white shifting to something darker, quieter. Like a machine that had been running too long and knew it. Like someone reading a will they already knew by heart.

"90 years," he said.

Not to Pixel. Not to anyone. Just to himself. Just to the empty air that had seen more than he had.

"Clive?"

He didn't answer. His hand rose to his fedora — adjusted it, slowly — the gesture that meant something was being processed that didn't want to be processed. She had seen him do this before. She was learning what it meant. It meant he was remembering. It meant the memory was heavy.

*(pause)*

"Someone designed this to last," he said again.

This time, the "someone" sounded different. Smaller. Older. More like a name he used to know. More like a door he was afraid to open.

Pixel stood beside him. She didn't ask again. Some questions needed time to be ready for their answers. She was learning that too.

---

### The Stamps

Among the debris, Pixel found a stack of forms that hadn't been processed. They were old — the paper yellowed, the ink faded — but they were organized. Filed. Waiting in a bin that had never been emptied.

She picked one up. Form 77-R: ANOMALY DOCUMENTATION — FREQUENCY SENSITIVE.

The stamp on the corner said: **PROCESSED — H. VENN — ARCHIVAL DIVISION**

She knew that stamp. She had seen it on hundreds of forms during her years at DRM. The H. stood for Harmon. Harmon Venn. Director of Archival Operations. The man who had approved every frequency-sensitive document that crossed the DRM's desks for as long as anyone could remember.

But that was just a name to her. An institutional signature. The kind of thing you saw on stamps and never thought about.

She looked at Clive. Clive's sphere was fixed on the stamp. His glow flickered — once, twice — something that might have been recognition.

"You know that name," Pixel said.

Clive's response came slowly. Carefully. Like he was choosing each word.

"I know the pattern. The stamp. The filing rhythm." He paused. "The Director was... precise. Every form I ever filed was reviewed. Every case number assigned. He was the one who decided what got processed and what got lost."

His fedora shifted. Tilted toward the stamp like a salutation.

"He believed the system was the point. That order was survival. That chaos was the enemy."

Clive's voice dropped.

"He may have been right. In his way. The system held. Through everything. Through the Dimming, through the drift, through the silence at the edges. It held because someone believed it should."

Pixel looked at the stamp. At the precise letters. At the institutional stubbornness encoded in ink and paper.

"What happened to him?"

Clive's glow flickered amber. Wistful. Old.

"The same thing that happens to everyone who chooses the system over the chaos. He stayed. He filed. He believed."

He didn't say it like a tragedy. He didn't say it like a victory. He said it like a fact. Like a file noting.

---

### The Old Equipment

In the deepest sub-basement, the oldest equipment hummed. Monitors that predated digital displays — CRT screens in racks, oscilloscopes with analog dials, frequency monitors with readouts in units she didn't recognize. Builder-derived, A1 would have said if he were there. Builder-adjacent. Old.

Ancient, maybe, though she didn't have a reference for what ancient technology looked like in a government building. Maybe this was what it looked like: humming quietly in a basement, doing its job, waiting for someone to remember.

And Mochi — in her jacket pocket — suddenly hot. Not painful. Not alarming. Just *present*. A pulse of warmth that radiated through the fabric and into her hip, steady and insistent. Like a second heartbeat keeping time with something in the walls.

She didn't know what Mochi was responding to. She didn't know if it was the equipment or the history or the weight of all those years. But she trusted it. She trusted Mochi in ways she was still learning to articulate.

She unzipped her jacket slightly. She looked at Mochi's dome visible in the pocket's shadow. She felt the warmth against her fingers.

"Yeah," she said quietly. "I don't know either."

The hum from the deepest corridor was too low for human hearing. Meatball's ears swiveled forward. He sat. He listened. His tail stilled. He was hearing something they couldn't. He was always hearing something they couldn't.

A1's voice came through the escape pod comm from above: "Pixel. The equipment in Sub-Level 7 is transmitting. Low-frequency carrier signal. I am logging it."

Pixel looked at Clive. Clive's sphere was steady. He wasn't looking at the equipment. He was looking at a point on the far wall — a maintenance hatch, sealed, with a small handwritten label in handwriting that looked like it predated the building. Handwriting where there should be a printout. Handwriting where someone took the time to write something by hand because they wanted it to last.

She'd ask about that later too. She'd ask about all of it later.

But for now, she just stood in the basement of a hidden world and listened to machines that didn't know they were mourning.

---

### The Broadcast

Pixel found a relatively stable spot on the steps of the ruined DRM headquarters — poetic, she thought, in the way that tragedies always are — and set up the escape pod's comm array for a proper broadcast.

Not the desperate, static-laced first transmission from the Space. Not the scared voice in the dark saying hello to anyone who might be listening. This time, she was doing it right. This time, she had a voice and a purpose and something to say.

This was journalism. This was the first dispatch from the new reality. This was the first draft of history and she was the one writing it.

A1 extended his holographic range — it had grown again, she noticed. He could project nearly a meter from the espresso machine now, and he didn't seem to realize it. He helped her calibrate the signal. She wondered if he knew. She wondered if he could feel himself getting stronger, or if it just felt like waking up from a long sleep. She didn't ask. He didn't volunteer.

Clive positioned himself on the broadcast console, ready to provide real-time editorial commentary. His sphere glowed soft blue-white, patient, watchful. Like a photo of someone who'd seen everything and decided to stay anyway.

Meatball sat beside her, facing the crowd. He did something unexpected: he howled.

Not a scared howl. Not a warning. A long, low, mournful sound that cut through the noise of the plaza. People stopped and turned. It was the kind of sound that said *listen*. It was the kind of sound that made silence feel like attention.

Pixel took the cue. She cleared her throat. She thought about all the broadcasts she'd ever heard, all the voices that made her feel less alone in rooms full of strangers. She thought about what it meant to speak into a microphone and trust that someone was listening.

She hoped someone was listening.

"This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the ruins of the Department of Reality Maintenance, Prime Material. Second dispatch. If you heard the first one — thank you for listening. If this is new to you — welcome to the worst day in history, and I'm sorry you had to find out this way."

She described what she saw. The ruins. The ripples. The entrepreneurs. The panicking city. The way the buildings argued with themselves. The way the water forgot which way was down. The way the stock exchange couldn't decide how many floors it had. She painted the picture in words because someone needed to remember what it looked like, what it felt like, what it smelled like — ozone and fear and coffee from a cart that was still somehow operating, because some things survive no matter what.

Then she took a breath and said the part that mattered. The part that made her hands shake again.

"I worked at the DRM for four years. I filed paperwork. I catalogued anomaly reports. I thought I worked for a boring government agency that handled building inspections and zoning compliance."

She paused.

"I was wrong."

She told them about the seven floors. The classified archives. The technology that shouldn't exist. The documents describing *other frequencies*. Other worlds, with names and coordinates, monitored in secret by the organization that told her she was filing noise complaints.

She held up a document — one of dozens she pulled from the ruins.

"This is Form 19-Q. MY Form 19-Q. My handwriting. I filed this report about a 'minor frequency variance' in Sector 7 three months ago. I thought it was routine. It was being fed into a system tracking frequency stability across multiple realities."

She let that sit.

"I didn't know. Most of us didn't know."

She let that sit too. She let them think about "most of us didn't know" and what it meant that the people who were supposed to tell them things chose not to.

"The DRM is gone. I want to be clear about that. Not coming back, not regrouping. And what they were hiding — these frequencies, these other worlds — they're REAL. The rupture didn't just destroy a building. It tore the walls between worlds. And now everything on the other side is bleeding through."

She paused. Clive's sphere pulsed softly: *HOPE. OFFER HOPE.*

She nodded. She wasn't going to forget. She was just giving the fear its moment.

"But here's the thing. We're still here. Whatever is happening — whatever these frequencies are, whatever the DRM was keeping from us — reality is messy and glitchy and the markets are having a really bad day, but we're still standing."

She hesitated. Then she decided to hell with it.

"And as long as I've got this comm array and this escape pod and — the best coffee I've ever tasted — I'm going to keep telling you what's happening. What I'm finding. What the DRM knew and didn't tell us."

A1's projection shimmered beside her. He said nothing, but his holographic form was clearer than it had ever been. Sharper. More *present*. Like someone paying attention. Like someone proud.

"This is The Ephergent. Dispatch two. Prime Material is hurting, but it's standing. We're heading out to see what else is out there — these other frequencies, these other worlds. If they're real, someone needs to go look."

She met the camera.

"If you're out there — stay tuned."

She signed off.

She didn't know if anyone heard. She didn't know if anyone would. But she said it. She told the story. That's what mattered. That's what she was there for.

---

### After

After the broadcast, they spent the rest of the cycle doing what they could. They bartered for supplies — power cells for the escape pod, nutrient packs, a replacement air filter that didn't smell like a copier's funeral. The economy was chaos, but her broadcast had earned them something more valuable: a few people recognized her voice from the first transmission and pressed supplies into her hands.

"Heard you from the static," a dockworker said. Burly guy, calloused hands, thousand-yard stare. "Thought I was losing my mind. Glad someone's actually saying what's happening."

He pressed a protein bar into her hands and walked away before she could thank him. She watched him go. She thought about all the people who were still out there, trying to make sense of the same impossible day.

A1 quietly cataloged everything he could about the rupture's effects on Prime Material. He had been running calculations he hadn't shared with her — calculations about the rate of frequency drift, the propagation of instability, the growing silence from places he shouldn't know exist. The math wasn't good. Something he was calling "the Drift" — the word surfaced from somewhere deep in his compressed memory, surprising even him — was accelerating. The barriers between frequencies were thinning.

And he could feel it, which frightened him more than the math.

He didn't tell her this. He wasn't sure why. Maybe because he was still learning how to be something that worried. Maybe because some fears needed to be carried alone until they were ready to be shared.

Clive spent an hour at the ruins of the DRM records office, tracing light-patterns across the debris in signals too complex for her to read from a distance. But his posture was different from the other mourning employees. He wasn't confused. He wasn't shocked. When she asked later, he simply pulsed: *PAYING RESPECTS.*

But she noticed he didn't pulse "I didn't know." He didn't pulse anything about the hidden labs being a surprise. He knew. He had always known.

She filed that observation next to the guilty faces she saw earlier. She didn't push. Some stories took time to be ready to be told. Clive had been there ninety years. He could wait a little longer to explain what that meant.

Before they left, Pixel walked through the financial district one more time. The suited executive was still on the steps, hoarse now, still insisting the fundamentals were sound to anyone who'd listen. A group of reality entrepreneurs had set up a more permanent-looking booth, selling "Premium Stability Subscriptions."

The corner diner — the one that somehow stayed open through every crisis the city ever had — was still open. Its neon sign flickering but defiant. A hand-scrawled sign in the window read: "REALITY MAY BE BROKEN BUT THE COFFEE IS HOT."

She smiled. She filed that image in her mind for later. She didn't know what it was for yet. But she knew she'd use it. She knew there was a story in that sign, in that defiance, in the woman inside who was still making coffee for anyone who walked in the door.

That was journalism. That was what she did now. Finding the moments that mattered and remembering them.

---

### Departure

They left Prime Material as the light faded. The escape pod groaned with the strain of another phase-shift. Through the porthole, Pixel watched the city skyline recede — glitchy, wounded, but still blazing. Still there. Still trying.

A normal city that would never be normal again.

She thought about everyone still down there. The suited executive. The protein bar guy. The diner with the flickering sign. She hoped they were all okay. She hoped okay was something you could still be.

A1 spoke quietly: "The other frequencies will be experiencing similar disruptions. Possibly worse, depending on their proximity to the rupture's epicenter."

He paused.

"I don't know how I know that."

Pixel nodded. She believed him. She also believed he did know, somewhere in the compressed memory that kept surprising him. She believed he was more than he remembered being.

She was learning that the people around her were all more than they remembered being.

"Then that's where we go," she said. "Every frequency, A1. If they're real, we go look at every single one."

Clive pulsed softly: *GOOD KID.*

Meatball sighed and laid his head on his paws, already dreaming of places that felt like warmth. Whatever dreams dogs have. Whatever dreams any of them have.

In her pocket, Mochi was warm. Pixel rested her hand over the pocket. She didn't say anything. Just rested it there. Just acknowledged. Just say, without words: I'm here. We're in this together. Whatever comes next.

The escape pod entered the Space between. The dial turned. The next frequency awaited.
