# Static Run

Static Run is an endless runner set in the Space between frequencies. You control Nano — engineer, speedster, the only person who can outrun the machines she builds — as she sprints through collapsing interstitial corridors while entropy fragments chase her.

The game is about how long, not whether.

---

## Story Context

Nano was not always a runner. She was an engineer on Cogsworth Cogitarium's frequency-hop program — building the tuners that let ships jump between known frequencies. Good work. Precise work. The kind of work that someone else was supposed to test before it went live.

Someone did not test it. The prototype jumped a survey vessel into a dead frequency. No survivors. The inquiry found Nano's calculations flawless. The inquiry also found that Nano had been overruled by a project manager who wanted to hit a launch window. The inquiry did not find the project manager responsible because the project manager had friends on the review board.

Nano resigned. She took her notebooks. She went somewhere the review board couldn't follow. She went into the Space.

The Ephergent found her four months later, running along the hull of a derelict Builder Station at a speed that should not have been possible for a human body. Pixel hailed her. Nano didn't slow down. She couldn't. She had been running for so long that standing still felt like dying.

She joined the crew because Pixel didn't ask questions. Nano will tell you she stayed for the engine room. She will not tell you the real reason. She will not tell you that Pixel's broadcast was the first signal in four months that sounded like someone who had also been alone and was choosing to keep moving anyway.

## The Core Mechanic

You run. You do not stop running. The entropy fragments are always behind you.

They will never catch Nano if the player keeps moving. There is no death by catching. There is death by obstacle — by losing momentum, by hesitating, by slowing down long enough that the corridor collapses around you.

**Controls**: Single input (spacebar, click, tap). Hold to maintain speed. Release to briefly slow and navigate obstacles.

**Scoring**: Distance run. Multipliers for near-misses and speed maintenance.

**The Catch**: The entropy fragments carry Barry's torn notebook pages. They fall occasionally, tumbling through the static. To collect one, you must slow down — just enough — and grab it without stopping entirely. The pages are optional. The coordinate they spell is optional. The meaning you find in them is optional.

It is not optional that you will want them.

## The Corridor

The interstitial corridor is procedurally generated from Builder Station architectural fragments — geometric walls, resonance conduits, memory core housings — assembled into a corridor that should not exist and does exist anyway, because the Space between frequencies is not obligated to follow architectural convention.

The corridor collapses behind Nano as she runs. What she runs through is already gone by the time she reaches the end. She is running through the shape of something that used to be.

Colors shift: indigo and pearl from the Space's ambient light, occasional flashes of frequency tint from passing proximity to known worlds, the deep amber of Cogsworth Cogitarium bleeding through from somewhere that no longer exists.

Nano knows this corridor. She has run here before. She does not say so.

---

## Barry's Notebook Pages

Five pages, torn and scattered across the entropy fragments. Each is readable as a standalone note. Together, they spell out a coordinate — not to a location, but to a *time*. A moment. The last time Barry Kowalski was certain of something.

The fragments that carry the pages look different from other entropy fragments: darker, more deliberate, moving not with chaos but with purpose. Barry understood entropy. He understood that the fragments carry what the universe is ready to let go of. He left these pages where they would be carried, knowing someone would eventually run fast enough to find them.

Finding all five pages changes the ending screen. Not the outcome — the meaning of the running. The player will understand, at the end, that Nano was not running from something. She was running toward something she couldn't name yet.

She has found it now. She is standing still in the game. She will tell you it doesn't count because this is a game. She is lying.

## Featured Crew

**Nano** — Player character. Compact, fast, perpetually slightly out of breath. She speaks in short sentences because she is always saving breath for running. She will tell you she does not like the crew. She will say this while repairing A1's navigation array with her bare hands and refusing to stop moving. She will say she doesn't need anyone. She will mean it approximately sixty percent of the time.

**Pixel Paradox** — Dispatches updates over the comm when Nano's comm is active. Her voice is the voice of someone narrating a marathon to an empty stadium, because Nano is the only one listening and Nano is busy running.

**Clive** — Appears as a recording in Nano's memory fragments. Clive's voice on a loop from an old maintenance channel. *"The station's resonance network has a maintenance corridor. Nano — if you're reading this, the corridor is stable. You can slow down."* Nano never slows down. Nano is not ready to slow down. The game does not require her to.
