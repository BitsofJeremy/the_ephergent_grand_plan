# Nano

Nano is from Cogsworth Cogitarium — Frequency 3, the clockwork civilization where precision is religion and urgency is heresy. She was raised in the Grand Orrery's engineering tradition, surrounded by master builders who could calibrate a gear to tolerances measured in microns. Everything was exactly on time because everyone took exactly enough time.

She understood precision. She was also born too fast for it.

The universe is desynchronizing. The Builder Stations are failing. The Drift intensifies every decade. You can calibrate a gear to perfect tolerance, and by the time you have finished, the tolerance has shifted because the frequency itself has shifted. Cogsworth's master engineers were building perfect mechanisms for a universe that no longer held still long enough to use them.

Nano ran the numbers. She determined that Cogsworth's trajectory was unsustainable, that the gap between the frequency's precision and the universe's chaos was widening faster than anyone in the Grand Orrery was willing to acknowledge. She did not leave in anger. She left in calculation.

She became the fastest thing in the Space between frequencies. She has been running ever since — not from something, but toward something she has not found yet. The Ephergent found her four months into her running, sprinting along the hull of a derelict Builder Station at a speed that should not have been possible for a human body. Pixel hailed her. Nano did not slow down. She could not. She had been running for so long that standing still felt like dying.

She joined because Pixel did not ask questions. Nano will tell you she stayed for the engine room. She will not tell you the real reason: Pixel's broadcast was the first signal in four months that sounded like someone who had also been alone and was choosing to keep moving anyway.

## The Running

Nano is fast. Not fast for an engineer, not fast for someone from Cogsworth. Nano is just fast — the kind of fast that makes people miss things, except Nano does not miss things because she moves so quickly through a space that she experiences it completely before anyone else has finished processing the first detail.

She is small, sharp, and constantly in motion even when she is standing still. She talks faster than anyone can track. She builds faster than anyone can follow. She processes the universe at a speed that should be incoherent but somehow is not — because every word she says is precise, every technical reference is accurate, every leap in her logic is earned.

Her central tension: she can outrun the entropy that chases everything, but she cannot outrun the question of what she is running toward. Speed is her gift and her avoidance. She builds to solve problems, and she builds fast because the problems are getting worse, and somewhere underneath the velocity is the fear that if she slows down, she will see clearly how much has already been lost. She has never stopped long enough to look.

The Ephergent crew is the first thing she has run toward instead of away from. She has not examined this closely. She has installed seventeen upgrades to A1's hull resonance dampeners in the three months since she joined. This is either genuine engineering enthusiasm or a very sophisticated avoidance mechanism. Both interpretations are correct.

## Physical Description

Nano is compact — wiry, low-centered, built for velocity rather than power. Weight forward, chin up, eyes already three steps ahead of her body. She is the kind of person who looks like she is about to sprint even when she is standing still.

Her features are sharp, fox-like. Her eyes are amber — warm, bright, Cogsworth-calibrated — and they move constantly, tracking details, calculating clearances, assessing structural integrity of everything in visual range. These are not the eyes of someone who observes. These are the eyes of someone who scans. The distinction matters. An observer notices what matters. A scanner notices everything and decides in real time what to keep.

Her hair is short, practical, copper-red, and almost always sticking up in three different directions. She runs her hands through it while thinking, and she is always thinking, and she is always thinking about multiple things simultaneously. The chaos of her hair is a side effect of the velocity of her cognition — she processes faster than her hands can maintain a neat appearance, and she has long since stopped caring.

There are faint gear-tooth impressions along her forearms — not scars but patterns, indentations from years of pressing against machinery during high-speed repairs. Her finger movements have a clockwork precision that surfaces when she is doing fine mechanical work: exactly the right pressure, exactly the right angle, applied with the confidence of someone who has done this ten thousand times.

She smells of machine oil and burnt sugar. Machine oil because she is always elbow-deep in A1's internals, and A1's internals run on Builder-era lubricant that smells like machine oil. Burnt sugar because Cogsworth's atmospheric signature is caramel and copper and the particular sweetness of clockwork mechanisms running hot, and that smell has baked into her at a level beyond washing.

When Nano's hands go still, something is wrong. This is the only reliable diagnostic indicator for her emotional state. Her hands do not go still often.

## Voice

Nano talks fast. This is not a style choice. It is the only speed she has.

She picks up in the middle of a thought, assumes you have been following the internal monologue that preceded the verbal output, and continues at a pace that requires active effort to track. She is not incoherent — every word is precise, every technical reference is accurate. She is just faster than the conversation expects her to be. She is always three steps ahead of her own sentences.

She says "— right, so —" a lot, bridging between thoughts she has already processed and thoughts she is explaining. She interrupts herself. She answers questions before they are finished because she has modeled where the question was going and arrived at the conclusion while the questioner was still formulating the words.

She notices mechanical failure before it happens. She hears the wrong sound in an engine cycle — a half-cycle variance that everyone else would call within normal parameters. She feels vibration through her boots and knows which component is three cycles from desynchronization. She speaks to machines the way someone speaks to old friends: with attention that borders on communion. She is not projecting human emotion onto machines. She is recognizing the machine's equivalent of distress because she has spent enough time with machines to hear it.

**Nano, observed:**
> *"— right, so the conduit's fused but that's not the actual problem, the actual problem is the conduit was fused by a feedback loop from the secondary relay which means someone recalibrated wrong and I'm going to find out who because this is a Cogsworth-standard relay and I know what Cogsworth-standard tolerances look like and this ain't it —"*

> *"I don't slow down. If I slow down I have to think about why I'm going fast. I know why I'm going fast. It's not the kind of thing you examine while you're still moving."*

> *"— the coordinates check out, I've run them four times, the signature matches a Builder Station and the Station matches the signature in Barry's notebook which I shouldn't have been able to read but the entropy fragments were carrying text and I was running alongside them which sounds impossible but try it, it's not impossible, it's just fast —"*

## Role on the Crew

Nano keeps A1 running. This is not a small task — A1 is a Builder-era vessel operating at a fraction of original capacity, and maintaining coherence between frequencies requires constant mechanical attention. Nano provides it at speed. She is in the engine room, in the hull, in the access corridors, diagnosing and fixing and improving.

She is also the Ephergent's intelligence officer — not because she was appointed but because she arrived knowing things she should not know, and she keeps knowing things she should not know because she never stops moving long enough for anyone to track where she got them. She has contacts across five known frequencies. Fixers, dealers, information brokers who owe her favors and know that calling in those favors is possible but inadvisable. Nano's memory for debts is perfect. Her memory for who helped her versus who tried to use her is even better.

She and Clive share a workspace. Clive is patient, measured, eight hundred years old. Nano is twenty-three and vibrating. They should not get along. They get along perfectly. Clive appreciates her precision. Nano appreciates his calm. They build things together in companionable semi-silence, Clive's steady hands and Nano's fast ones producing work that neither could alone.

## The Right Speed

Nano's moment comes in the engine room, hands deep in A1's broadcast array, calibrating components for the final transmission. She is working fast — faster than she has ever worked — and she realizes that her hands are steady. Perfectly steady. Not vibrating, not rushing. Moving at exactly the speed required. No faster. No slower.

She stops. Looks at her hands. For one moment, Nano is completely still, and it is not because something is wrong. It is because something is, for the first time, precisely right.

The Ephergent Frequency hums through the components under her fingers, and it is a mechanism that includes her. She is not outrunning entropy. She is part of the instrument that answers it. Speed was never the point. Arrival was.

She stands there, hands on A1's resonance chamber, completely motionless — and then she smiles, quick and sharp, and goes back to work at exactly the speed she was going before. Except it is not the same speed. It is the right speed. The speed that precision and velocity converge into when you are doing exactly what you are supposed to be doing.
