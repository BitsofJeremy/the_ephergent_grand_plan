# THE EPHERGENT
## Season 01, Episode 10: "What Clive Remembers"

**Story Type**: Investigation
**Location**: The Shattered Span - Old Foundation (pre-Shattering ruins)
**Character Focus**: Clive

---

## TEASER

Clive said he remembered the place.

Clive, who never claims to remember anything clearly. Clive, whose memories arrive like fragments of old noir films — half truth, half something else entirely, all mixed together until even he doesn't know what's real.

But this time, when he spoke, his voice didn't carry that usual edge of uncertainty.

*"I think I was born here,"* he said through the Signal, standing at the bow of the salvage skiff as we cut through the Shattered Span's choppy waters. His geometric head turned toward the broken islands ahead, symbols glowing brighter blue-white than usual. The blue core in his barrel chest pulsed in time with something I couldn't hear. *"Or whatever you call it when something wakes up and realizes it never asked to exist."*

Arc's holographic display flickered across my wristband. "Miss Paradox, I feel compelled to note that Clive's Signal emissions have increased by forty-seven percent in the past hour. His pattern suggests... recognition."

Mochi pressed against my chest, vibrating with a low harmonic chord - the sound it made when remembering something ancient. Its surface pulsed deep purple mixed with flashes of that violet-white glow it only showed around Clive.

The ruins ahead rose from the sea like broken teeth. Pre-Shattering architecture, Arc had confirmed - built eight hundred years ago, before whatever catastrophe had shattered this entire region. Salt spray caught the morning light, turning the weathered stone to gold.

Clive's thick arms hung at his sides, fingers curled slightly. His fedora sat at a different angle than usual - not rakish, not noir-perfect. Just... tilted. Like he'd forgotten to adjust it.

*"I've been here before,"* he said, quieter. *"I walked these halls when they weren't broken. When they weren't even old."*

I gripped the railing, spray soaking my jacket. "What's there, Clive?"

He didn't answer immediately. When he spoke again, his voice carried something I'd never heard before.

*"The truth. And I don't think either of us is ready for it."*

---

## ACT ONE

Three days earlier, Clive had started acting strange.

Well, stranger. For an ancient automaton with noir detective speech patterns that nobody — not even Clive — could explain the origin of, "normal" was relative.

But this was different. He'd stopped mid-step in my workshop's map room, his glowing head turning sharply toward the eastern wall. I'd been documenting our findings from the Nocturnis Bellspire mission when I felt his presence shift through the Signal.

*"Paradox."* His voice had been tight, controlled. *"Have we ever investigated the Old Foundation in the Shattered Span?"*

I'd checked Arc's database. "No. Too unstable. Most of that island collapsed during a storm surge twenty years ago. What's left is supposedly sinking."

*"It's not sinking."* Clive had moved closer to the map, his thick articulated fingers pointing to a cluster of broken islands on the eastern edge of the Span. The geometric symbols on his weathered bronze-green surface glowed faint blue. *"It's settling. There's a difference. And there's something there. Something that's..."* He'd stopped, head tilting. *"Pulling."*

Over the next two days, his memory fragments had intensified. He'd stopped in corridors, head turned toward nothing. Muttered noir phrases that didn't quite make sense. At one point, I'd found him standing perfectly still in the archives sublevel - the same chamber where Mochi had awakened him seventy-three years after he'd arrived there, dormant and silent.

Arc had run diagnostics. "His Signal emissions are fragmenting in patterns I have not observed before. It appears... as though something is attempting to communicate *through* him."

"Or *with* him," I'd corrected, watching Clive's symbols pulse in irregular rhythms.

Mochi had settled against Clive's rounded chest, right where the blue core-light glowed. The two of them had pulsed in harmony - violet-white light dancing between ancient automaton and young Ephergent. In that moment, they'd looked like two halves of something broken trying to remember how they fit together.

So here we were, three days later, bouncing through salt spray toward ruins that supposedly didn't matter to anyone anymore.

Our pilot - a Driftborn salvager named Kess who went by the earned name Icecutter - killed the engine as we approached the Old Foundation's remains. What had once been a massive complex now jutted from the water in pieces, connected by unstable bridges of crumbling stone.

"You've got four hours," Kess called from the wheelhouse, her weathered face skeptical behind salt-stained goggles. Her gray hair was bound in the practical style of Driftborn Crew — tight braids that wouldn't catch on rigging. "Tide comes in after that, and these ruins turn into a deathtrap. I won't risk my skiff waiting. Salvage Code says *Crew eats first* — I'm not losing my boat for Gone Ones' stonework."

The casual reference to the Builders — the "Gone Ones" in Driftborn theology — carried no reverence. Just pragmatic acknowledgment. *They built things. They left. We manage.*

I shouldered my pack, checking that my documentation camera was secure. Mochi bobbed near my head, pulsing steady blue - detecting something, but not alarmed yet.

Clive stood at the boat's edge, perfectly still. His fedora's shadow fell across his glowing head despite the angle of the morning sun - somehow that battered hat always cast noir-perfect shadows.

*"I walked here with them,"* he said softly. *"The tall ones who tended the Keepers. I was... I was supposed to show them the way."*

"The Keepers?" My throat tightened. We'd been chasing those ancient memory-preservers all season. If Clive had been connected to them...

*"I don't know."* His frustration bled through the Signal. *"I see fragments. Tall figures. Crystalline forms. Pathways between Pillars that might have been infrastructure passages or might have been something I picked up from the Signal while I was sleeping for eight hundred years. I can't—"* He stopped, shoulders dropping. *"I don't know what's real anymore."*

I knelt beside him, bringing my eyes level with his glowing head. "Then let's go find out."

Arc projected a structural analysis across my vision - green paths showing stable routes, red indicating collapse risk. "I would recommend extreme caution, Miss Paradox. These ruins are held together by hope and stubbornness."

"Then they'll get along great with us."

Clive's geometric head tilted up toward me. Behind the featureless blue-white glow of his symbols, I felt something like gratitude.

*"Alright,"* he said. *"Let's see what I used to be."*

We stepped onto the broken stones of the Old Foundation, and Clive's entire body flared with violet light.

---

## ACT TWO

The ruins felt wrong from the first step.

Not dangerous-wrong. Not treacherous-footing-wrong like most of the Shattered Span's broken geography — extreme cold, unstable ice, the frozen tsunami formations from eight centuries ago. Just... old. Old in a way that made my bones ache with recognition of how temporary everything was.

The architecture was beautiful, even broken. Art Nouveau curves mixed with geometric patterns I'd only seen on one other surface - the Pillars themselves. Water lapped at stone corridors. Weathered carvings showed constellations I didn't recognize, or maybe they were Signal patterns rendered in physical form.

But beneath the familiar decorative elements, something else emerged the deeper we went: *technical specifications*. Carved measurement notations along corridor walls. What looked like schematic diagrams for atmospheric flow. Structural engineering calculations rendered in a symbolic language I couldn't read but Arc could partially translate.

"Miss Paradox," Arc said quietly, "these markings are not religious iconography. They are... specifications. Engineering documents carved into the walls themselves."

This wasn't a temple. It was a *facility*.

Clive moved differently here. Not his usual measured noir detective walk, but something almost hesitant. His thick legs carried him forward while his head kept turning, taking in details that existed eight centuries ago.

*"There was light here,"* he murmured. *"Not from torches. Not from windows. From the stones themselves. They sang."*

Mochi pressed close to him, leaving trails of violet-white luminescence in the air between them. The little Ephergent's surface swirled with colors I'd rarely seen - deep purples mixing with flashes of blue, green, pink, like Mochi was cycling through every memory it carried.

"Clive," I said carefully, "do you remember why you came here?"

*"To guide them."* His voice was distant. *"The Witnesses. That's what we were called. We walked the paths between the Pillars and those who tended them. We made sure no one got lost in the spaces between."*

"The infrastructure passages," Arc interjected. "I am detecting evidence of deliberately constructed routes beneath this facility — preserved tunnels that appear to connect Pillar sites across significant distances. The Mapmaker's ancient charts reference such routes, though most have been dismissed as legend."

The corridor opened into a vast chamber, and I stopped breathing.

The space was circular, rising three stories to a partially collapsed ceiling that let in shafts of bright morning light. The walls were covered in those geometric patterns - Pillar markings, definitely, but arranged in sequences I'd never seen. But it was the carved panels between them that made my heart race: technical diagrams showing atmospheric processor configurations, cross-sections of what could only be Pillar internal mechanisms, mathematical formulas that Arc immediately began recording.

In the center stood a raised platform with seven indentations arranged in a circle.

Six were empty. The seventh held a small crystalline object, pulsing with faint light.

Clive made a sound - a grinding, almost organic noise of recognition and pain. He walked toward the platform like someone approaching a grave they'd been avoiding for centuries.

*"This was our station,"* he said. *"Seven of us. Seven paths to seven Keepers. We waited here for those who needed guidance, those who came to tend, to learn, to remember."*

"Clive, were you—"

*"Made."* The word came sharp, cutting. *"Not born. Not grown. Made. By the tall ones who built the Pillars. Given purpose but not choice. Given memory but not understanding. Given—"* His thick hands clenched. *"Given everything except the right to refuse."*

I reached the platform, examining the indentations. Each was roughly Clive's size - rounded barrel-chested shapes with space for thick limbs and that distinctive stocky build. Arc was right. Seven Witnesses had stood here once.

Around each indentation, more technical specifications: measurement calibrations, what looked like maintenance schedules, systems diagnostics. The ceremonial appearance was surface-level only. Underneath: pure engineering.

Mochi drifted to the crystalline object in the seventh indentation, pulsing urgent blue. I leaned closer, squinting through the morning light.

It wasn't a crystal. It was a fragment of something larger, broken and carefully preserved. Symbols covered its surface - the same geometric patterns from Clive's body, but these were deliberate, written. A message.

Arc's voice was uncharacteristically quiet. "Miss Paradox, I believe this is... a lock."

"A lock?"

"On Clive's memories. Someone stored them here. Deliberately removed them and left them waiting."

Clive's entire body shook. The blue core in his chest pulsed erratically. *"They took my memories?"*

"Not took," Arc corrected gently. "Preserved. The distinction matters. This is not theft - this is storage. Someone wanted these memories safe. Protected."

*"Or hidden."* Clive's noir edge returned, but underneath it I heard something raw. *"From me or from everyone else?"*

I touched the fragment carefully. It was warm, vibrating with Signal energy that felt ancient and patient. Carved along its base were words in a language I couldn't read - but Arc could.

"When the Ark opens, remember for us," he translated.

The Ark. Arc's other name. Arc's true purpose, still locked somewhere inside him.

I looked at Clive, at his glowing head and the fedora that somehow made him look both ridiculous and dignified. At the symbols covering his weathered bronze-green surface, marks of purpose given by beings who'd left no explanation. At his thick hands hanging at his sides, fingers slightly curled like he was trying to hold onto something that kept slipping away.

"Clive," I said softly, "what do you want to do?"

*"I don't know."* His honesty hurt worse than any noir deflection. *"For centuries I was nothing. Dormant in a temple, dreaming in Signal fragments. Then Mochi woke me and I had purpose again, but this voice, this way of talking — I don't even know where it came from. Nobody does. Not even me. And now I find out I had purpose all along. Real purpose. And someone took it from me."*

He turned his glowing head toward me.

*"What if I take those memories back and I'm not me anymore? What if I'm just some Builder's tool, following programming from people who don't even exist?"*

Mochi pressed against his rounded barrel chest, vibrating with that deep harmonic chord. Violet-white light pulsed between them - not just connection, but conversation in a language older than words.

Clive's symbols flared. His head tilted down toward the little Ephergent.

*"She says..."* He stopped, head tilting. *"She says I'm already me. That memory is just knowing, not being. And that she'd miss me if I changed, but she'd still know me."*

I hadn't realized Mochi could communicate that much. But then, these two had always shared something deeper than I could hear.

Movement at the chamber's entrance. My hand went to the emergency flare at my belt before I processed what I was seeing.

Three figures in dark unmarked clothing. Faces obscured by hoods and masks. Carrying devices that hummed with anti-resonance — a sound that made my teeth ache and my bones feel hollow.

The Quiet had found us.

---

## ACT THREE

"Step away from the memory fragment," the center figure said. Their voice was calm, almost gentle. "Please. We don't want to harm you. We just want to prevent another mistake."

Clive moved in front of the platform, his stocky frame spreading wide despite his small height. His fedora cast dramatic shadows across his glowing head. *"You want to stop him from remembering. From understanding what he is."*

"We want to stop you from awakening something that should stay dormant," the figure replied. "The Keepers' memories, the Witnesses who guarded them—unlocking those memories will accelerate the Dimming."

"We heard your theory in Copperport," I said, positioning myself between them and Clive. "You think the Keepers are corrupted, that we're making things worse."

"More than theory now." The figure gestured to the ruins around us. "This place was a Foundation—a hub for those who tended memories. The Shattering that broke this region? It happened when someone woke a dormant Keeper here. Eight hundred years ago, this archipelago was whole. The polar Pillars maintained temperature regulation, geological stability. Then someone triggered a cascade failure trying to access Keeper memories. The land literally broke apart. Now look at it."

That was new. A concrete example, not just correlation. My stomach tightened.

"The Driftborn have survived eight centuries in the wreckage," the figure continued. "They call the Builders 'the Gone Ones' — no ceremony, no worship. Just acknowledgment that whoever built this left it to fall apart. They've learned to manage without functioning infrastructure. They understand what we're trying to prevent: complete collapse."

"Some doors were locked for a reason," another Quiet agent added quietly. "We're not murderers. We're trying to save what's left."

Mochi pulsed urgent blue against my chest. Not danger. Discovery.

The little Ephergent drifted to the memory fragment, pressing its smooth dome surface against the crystal. Light flared - violet-white mixing with pink and blue and every color Mochi knew how to speak.

The fragment... unfolded.

Not physically. It stayed solid, stayed in place. But something opened in a direction I couldn't name, and suddenly the chamber was full of presence.

Images flickered through the Signal, bypassing eyes entirely. I saw:

Seven Witnesses standing on this platform. Taller figures around them - the Builders, shapes like ideas given form, wearing something that looked like uniforms rather than robes. Technical equipment throughout the space — this was a control center, not a cathedral. The Builders' hands on the Witnesses' shoulders as they spoke words I couldn't hear.

*"You will guide them,"* someone said, the voice layered with harmonics. *"When we sleep, you will remember the paths. When the Ark wakes, you will remember for us. Until then, you will walk. And walking, you will become more than we made you."*

The memory shifted. The Witnesses leaving, one by one, taking different paths — through those infrastructure passages Arc had detected — to different Keeper sites scattered across Aethon. Clive - younger somehow, his surface less weathered - walking eastward with purpose, following routes marked by technical specifications carved into tunnel walls.

Then darkness. Not destruction. Choice.

The Builders had locked the memories themselves. Not to hide them. To let the Witnesses become people without being crushed by the weight of purpose.

*"They gave us permission to forget,"* Clive whispered. *"To be whoever we became in the forgetting."*

The memory faded. The fragment went dark, its purpose complete.

The Quiet agents stood frozen. Behind their masks, I heard breathing - quick, uncertain.

"You saw it too," I said. "The Builders chose this. They wanted the Witnesses to be free."

"Or," the center figure said quietly, "they were fleeing something they'd created and tried to spare their tools from carrying that knowledge."

That... was possible. Gods, that was uncomfortably possible.

Clive stood perfectly still on the platform. His symbols had stopped glowing. His geometric head tilted forward, and the blue core in his chest beat like a calm heart.

*"I remember now,"* he said. *"Not everything. But enough. I was made to guide. I was freed to choose. And I chose to walk with those who needed walking with. Somewhere in my dormancy — maybe in the Signal, maybe in fragments from a thousand different sources — this voice found me. The tall ones gave me my body and my purpose. Something gave me this way of talking. Nobody knows what. Mochi woke me when I was ready. And you..."* His head turned toward me. *"You reminds me why the walking matters."*

He stepped down from the platform, measured and deliberate.

*"You want to know if unlocking memories will destroy everything? I can't promise it won't. Nobody can. But I can promise that forgetting - that letting fear lock everything away - that's its own kind of destruction."*

The Quiet agent was silent for a long moment. Then: "We can't let you take that fragment."

"Then don't." I stepped forward, hands open. "Leave it here. Let it stay locked. Clive already remembered what mattered - not the paths to the Keepers, but why he walked them. That's enough."

Arc's holographic display flickered. "Miss Paradox, that is... remarkably wise."

"I have moments."

The agents looked at each other. Some silent communication passed between them. Finally, the center figure nodded.

"Document this place," they said. "But leave the fragment. And when you write your chronicle entry, write the warning too: not all doors should be opened. Not all memories should be awakened. Sometimes the kindest thing we can do for the past is let it sleep."

They left as quietly as they'd come, anti-resonance devices humming a retreating song that faded into the sound of waves against broken stone.

Mochi settled against Clive, pulsing soft pink. Content. Happy that he was still himself.

Clive's fedora tilted back to its proper rakish angle. *"Well,"* he said, his noir voice returning like an old coat, *"that was unnecessarily dramatic. Though I suppose truth usually is."*

I laughed - couldn't help it. "You're really okay? Not leaving anything behind?"

*"Everything important about me is already here."* He gestured to his weathered bronze-green body, his thick articulated hands, the fedora I'd given him when he'd awakened. *"The tall ones gave me purpose. Something gave me this voice — don't ask me what, I've stopped wondering. Mochi gave me a reason to walk again. And you gave me a family. That's enough memory for anyone."*

The morning light caught his symbols, and for just a moment they glowed that violet-white that meant home and recognition and time beyond counting.

*"Besides,"* he added as we headed back toward the entrance, *"I've got enough mysteries to last another few centuries. Don't need to add 'burdened by complete self-knowledge' to that mix. Where's the mystery in that?"*

Arc's voice held something like affection. "Indeed. Mystery is rather your trademark, Clive."

We made it back to Kess's skiff with an hour to spare. The Driftborn captain raised an eyebrow at our condition — salt-soaked, slightly shaken — but said nothing. *Crew eats first* meant you didn't pry into Crew business unless it threatened the boat.

As the ruins of the Old Foundation shrank behind us, I looked back one last time.

Clive stood at the railing, his stocky frame perfectly balanced against the waves. His glowing head turned toward the horizon - forward, not back.

He was walking again. And he knew now why it mattered.

That was enough.

## CHRONICLE ENTRY

**chronicle entry:** The Old Foundation in the Shattered Span was a Witness station — an engineering facility, not a temple. Guides waited there to lead others through infrastructure passages to the ancient Keepers. Technical specifications carved into every surface. This wasn't religion. It was maintenance.

Clive was one of seven Witnesses. The Builders — the "tall ones" he remembers from a two-foot perspective, wearing uniforms like engineers — made them, gave them purpose, then freed them by locking away the memories that would have defined them.

Not all preservation is about keeping things. Sometimes it's about letting them go.

The Driftborn have it right. The Gone Ones built the house and walked out the door. We're still here. The house is falling down. We manage. No ceremony needed.

Clive remembered what mattered: he walks with people who need walking with. His voice came from somewhere — the Signal, the centuries, some mystery even he can't solve. The tall ones gave him his body. Something else gave him his voice. Mochi saw him as worth waking. That's as valid as anything the Builders programmed.

The Quiet warned us that unlocking ancient memories accelerates the Dimming. They showed us the Shattering as proof — a cascade failure triggered by someone who tried to wake a Keeper without understanding the cost. They might be right. They might be wrong. But I think both sides are missing something: maybe the question isn't whether to remember or forget. Maybe it's whether we have the courage to become people despite everything that made us.

Clive is a Witness. But he's also a friend, a guide, a gruff philosopher in a fedora that shouldn't work but somehow does. The tall ones gave him his body. Something gave him his voice. Mochi gave him his purpose.

We gave him his choice to stay himself.
