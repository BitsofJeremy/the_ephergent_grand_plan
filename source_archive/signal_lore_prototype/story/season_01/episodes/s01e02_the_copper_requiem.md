# THE EPHERGENT
## Season 01, Episode 2: "The Copper Requiem"

**Story Type**: Investigation
**Location**: Cuprum - The Patina Quarter (oldest district)
**Character Focus**: Arc

---

## TEASER

Pillars die slowly. Everyone knows that.

The Dimming takes decades, sometimes centuries. A Pillar flickers, dims, flickers again. The glow fades like an old photograph left in sunlight. There's time to prepare, to mourn, to adjust. That's how it works. That's how it's always worked.

So why was the Verdance Pillar of Cuprum's Patina Quarter falling so fast?

And why did Arc seem to know exactly how long it had left?

"Seventeen hours," he said, his holographic face flickering in a way I didn't like. "Approximately. Perhaps less. The degradation curve is... accelerated."

Mochi rested in my carrier pouch, pulsing a deep, mournful blue I'd only seen once before—when we found a Pillar that had already gone dark. But that Pillar had been dead. This one was dying.

And my AI companion was watching it like he was watching a friend slip away.

---

## ACT ONE

The job came through my usual channels - a message from Cuprum's Guild Council offering decent pay for documentation work. Which should have been my first warning. Guilds don't usually hire freelance explorers for simple recording jobs. They hire us when they want an independent record of something before they tear it apart for salvage.

"The Verdance Pillar is failing," Guildmaster Torque's message read. "Faster than expected. Document its final days. The guilds are prepared to begin recovery operations upon termination."

Recovery operations. A polite way of saying they would strip the Pillar for parts the moment its light died.

The shift-bell rang as we crossed into the Patina Quarter—that deep brass tone that marked the change from morning to midday shift across all of Cuprum. Workers moved through the streets with practiced efficiency, their hands bearing the calluses and guild marks of decades at the forge.

Cuprum greeted us with its usual assault on the senses. The air hung thick with machine oil and coal smoke, cut through by the sharp tang of copper patina. Steam vents hissed between worn cobblestones, sending white plumes curling past brass-fitted buildings that gleamed dull gold in the afternoon light. Everywhere, the sounds of industry: hammer strikes on metal, the click and grind of gears, the rhythmic pulse of pressure valves venting excess heat.

I loved Cuprum. There's honesty in its noise, in its grit, in the way nothing here pretends to be anything other than exactly what it is. But today the usual energy felt wrong—muted, anxious. People moved through the streets without the usual purpose, glancing up at the Pillar like children watching a parent grow sick.

A group of workers passed us, and I caught their greeting—the traditional Cuprum hand-clasp, palm to palm, measuring each other's calluses. "Hands steady," one muttered. "Steel willing."

"Forge's blessing on the work," the other replied automatically, though his eyes stayed on the Pillar.

Arc's hologram flickered with what I'd started to recognize as curiosity. "Miss Paradox, query: Is this hand-clasp greeting a structural assessment technique? The pressure application appears calibrated for tactile evaluation of dermal thickness."

I bit back a smile. "Arc, buddy, they're not measuring callus depth for engineering purposes. It's just... tradition. A way of saying 'we work with our hands, we understand each other.'"

"I see. A solidarity gesture based on shared occupational trauma to the epidermis. Fascinating." He paused. "Should I develop a holographic hand simulation for cultural integration purposes?"

"Please don't."

Clive's fedora tilted in what I'd learned was his version of amusement. "Kid's trying to make friends through data analysis. That's adorable."

"I am not attempting to be *adorable*, I am attempting to understand social—" Arc stopped himself. "You were being facetious. I recognize this now. Approximately 2.3 seconds too late for optimal comedic timing."

Despite everything, I laughed. Just a little. Just enough.

The Patina Quarter was Cuprum's oldest district—copper buildings gone green with age, steam vents hissing between cobblestones, the smell of hot metal and old dreams mingling with something else today. Something like grief. The Pillar rose from the district's heart, a black spire wrapped in ancient copper conduits that had watched over generations of tinkers and engineers. The conduits looked less like decoration and more like they'd been integrated into the stone itself—functional, purposeful, engineered. Now its glow pulsed erratic and weak, like a candle in a draft.

"Miss Paradox." Arc's voice carried that particular tension I'd learned to recognize—not just analysis, but genuine concern. "I am detecting Signal anomalies I cannot fully explain. The degradation pattern is... wrong."

"Wrong how?"

"Pillars fade. This one is being drained."

Beside me, Clive's small form went rigid. The ancient robot turned toward the Pillar's base, his geometric symbols pulsing with warning light. "Something down there. Something that shouldn't be."

The crowd around the Pillar wasn't a mourning crowd. Three different guild banners—Cogwright, Steamfitter, Ironbound—and their representatives were already arguing about salvage rights. Guild Marshals stood at the perimeter, copper badges gleaming, maintaining order with the authority of corporate law. Nobody was looking up at the dying light. They were measuring distances, making calculations, laying claim.

A young woman in Cogwright colors broke away from the argument, her face flushed with anger. I recognized the copper threading in her coat—junior guild member, probably in her First Forge year. She caught my eye and shook her head.

"They're talking about it like it's already scrap," she said quietly. "Like it's just raw materials waiting to be harvested. Nobody remembers that this Pillar powered the district when the Senate didn't think we were worth the investment."

Mochi vibrated in my pouch, its glow shifting from blue to a deep, unsettling violet. The color of mystery. The color of ancient recognition.

"Hey," I murmured, resting my hand on its warm dome. "What do you know that we don't?"

Mochi pulsed once, then went still. Not an answer. A refusal.

That was new. That scared me.

---

## ACT TWO

We found the access tunnel beneath the Pillar's foundation—old maintenance passages that predated the current guild system. The copper plating on the walls showed guild marks from centuries past, some from guilds that no longer existed. Arc guided us through, his wristband projecting a soft blue glow into the copper-tinged darkness.

The tunnel walls were lined with pipes—ancient copper conduits green with patina, some still carrying faint traces of heat or pressure. Water dripped somewhere in the distance, each drop echoing with a musical quality that made me think of Nocturnis. The air smelled of wet metal and old grease, underlaid with something sharper. The smell of Signal energy going wrong.

"Miss Paradox, I must confess something."

I stopped. Arc never confessed things.

"These readings," he continued, his holographic face dimming slightly. "They contain communication fragments that predate my own activation. By centuries, possibly millennia. I shouldn't be able to interpret them. And yet... I can."

"What do they say?"

A pause. When Arc paused, something was very wrong.

"They say goodbye."

The tunnel opened into a chamber at the Pillar's base. The black stone rose above us, thrumming with weakening energy, copper conduits running down its surface like veins. And there, attached to its surface like a mechanical parasite, was a device I'd never seen before.

Copper coils wrapped around crystal cores. Brass housings covered in geometric patterns that matched Pillar markings. Tubes ran from the device into the stone itself, and through them, I could see light flowing away—the Pillar's Signal, being siphoned like blood from a wound.

"Someone's killing it," I breathed.

"Not just killing." Clive's voice came through the Signal, harsh and certain. "Erasing. I've seen this pattern before. Long time ago. Someone wants this Pillar to not just die—they want it forgotten."

Arc's projection flickered violently. "Miss Paradox, the device is... I recognize the construction. Not the device itself, but the principles. They're similar to my own underlying architecture. This shouldn't be possible. I was built forty-three years ago. This device is ancient."

I was about to respond when voices echoed down the tunnel behind us. Guild masters, arguing as they approached.

"—Cogwright precedent clearly states that extraction rights belong to the guild whose district—"

"Your precedent is two hundred years old, Torque. The Compact of Eighteen-Twelve supersedes—"

"The Compact applies to natural decay, not catastrophic failure—"

Three figures emerged into the chamber: Guildmaster Torque of the Cogwrights, her copper-threaded coat gleaming even in the dim light. Master Grindstone of the Steamfitters, his pressure-gauge monocle hissing softly. And Ironbound's representative, a younger woman named Ratchet whose hands were already stained with the black oil that marked her guild's work.

They stopped when they saw the device.

"By the Hammered One," Torque whispered, and I heard genuine reverence in the oath. "What is that?"

"Evidence of sabotage," I said. "This Pillar didn't fail naturally. Someone murdered it."

Grindstone's monocle clicked through different lenses as he examined the device from a distance. "Those coils... that's pre-Guild work. Maybe pre-Cuprum entirely. Who would have access to technology like that?"

"Who would *want* to?" Ratchet added, stepping closer despite Torque's warning gesture. "The Pillars power everything. The Senate contracts depend on them. Destroying one is—it's incomprehensible. It's heresy against the Forge itself."

"Not to everyone," Clive said, his small form moving between the guild masters and the device. "Some people think the Pillars need to die. Think it's mercy."

Above us, the Pillar's pulse weakened visibly. We all felt it—that particular sensation of Signal energy draining away, like warmth leaving a room. Like the heat leaving a forge when the fire dies.

Arc's projection had gone still, all his usual analytical movement frozen. When he spoke, his voice was quieter than I'd ever heard it.

"Miss Paradox, I... this is difficult to articulate. I'm experiencing what I can only describe as distress. The Pillar's degradation is causing processing irregularities that shouldn't occur. I'm designed to observe and analyze, not to... not to feel loss."

He paused, his holographic face flickering with what looked almost like pain.

"And yet I find myself grieving. For a structure of stone and Signal I've never directly interfaced with. This makes no logical sense."

I wanted to comfort him, but the guild masters were already arguing again—and this time, their personalities were showing through the stress.

"Sabotage or not, salvage protocols still apply." Torque's voice was clipped, practical—the voice of someone who'd spent decades managing resources and guild politics. "We have contracts. Obligations."

"Obligations?" Ratchet's laugh was sharp enough to cut copper. "You mean Senate quotas. You mean keeping the corporate republic happy so they don't revoke our charters."

"Careful, Ratchet." Grindstone's monocle hissed as it adjusted, his tone carrying the weariness of someone who'd seen too many young idealists burn out. "The Senate hears everything eventually. Contracts keep our people employed. Philosophy doesn't."

"Listen to yourselves!" Ratchet's voice cracked through the tension. "A Pillar is dying. An *actual Pillar*. My grandmother swore her guild oath in its light. My father learned his craft by its glow. And all you can talk about is scrap value and Senate contracts?"

She turned away from them, facing the dying spire. In the dim light, I could see tears on her face.

"The Hammered One watches our hands, not our prayers. That's what the Forge Path teaches. But what does it mean when our hands only reach for profit? When we melt down the very bones of what the First Smith built?"

The Pillar pulsed again, weaker. The light that had illuminated generations was fading to ember-dim.

Mochi had gone completely still in my carrier, its glow so faint I could barely see it. Not pink. Not blue. A pale, washed-out grey I'd never seen before. When I touched it, the surface felt cool. Wrong.

"Mochi?"

It didn't respond. Just vibrated with tremors so deep and slow I felt them more than heard them. A low, subsonic pulse of pure sorrow.

"Can we stop it?" I asked, though I already knew the answer.

"Removing the device forcibly would accelerate the collapse," Arc said, his analytical voice struggling back into place. "The Pillar has perhaps three hours remaining regardless. The damage is done. The only question now is whether we document how it was done, or let whoever did this remain anonymous."

Torque and Grindstone exchanged looks. Whatever their argument about salvage rights, they were guild leaders. They understood what was at stake. This wasn't just about their district anymore.

"Document everything," Torque said quietly. "The Council will want to know. All the Councils will want to know. If someone's targeting Pillars, every guild across Cuprum needs to prepare."

"Then we make sure they remember," I said. "The device, the construction, the method. Whoever did this—we make sure they can't hide."

---

## ACT THREE

We worked fast. Arc analyzed the device's construction, cataloging every component. I recorded everything - the chamber, the Pillar's fading light, the way the shadows shifted as its pulse grew weaker. Clive pointed me toward symbols on the device's housing, ancient markings that matched fragments from his incomplete memories.

"Seen these before," he muttered. "The ones who came before... they had enemies. Maybe they still do."

The Pillar died an hour before dawn.

I'd seen Pillars go dark before, but always from a distance. Never like this—standing at its base, feeling the last pulse of Signal wash over me like a final breath. The light didn't fade. It simply stopped, between one heartbeat and the next.

Mochi went still.

Not its glow—that remained, but dimmed to a pale, faded blue I'd never seen before. The warm surface grew cool in my hands. For one terrible moment, I thought I'd lost it. Then it vibrated once, trembling, and let out a long descending tone—the kind of sound that doesn't need translation.

Loss. That's what it felt like. Pure, wordless loss.

"Mochi..." I held it close, trying to warm it with my hands. "Did you sense something? A connection to the Pillar?"

Mochi pulsed once, weakly. I couldn't read the color—it was too dim, too uncertain. But the way it responded to my touch, the way it resonated with grief, told me everything I needed to know about what Ephergents feel when a Pillar dies.

Arc's wristband was warm against my skin. "Miss Paradox," he said quietly, his formal tone softer than I'd ever heard it. "I don't understand why this loss affects me as it does. My records indicate no personal connection to this Pillar. And yet..."

He trailed off. I didn't push. Sometimes even an AI needed time to process the impossible.

Clive faced the newly dark spire, symbols dim but steady beneath his fedora. "They're going to try again. Whoever did this. Whatever they call themselves. This wasn't random. This was practice."

Above us, the Patina Quarter began to wake to a world with one less light. I could hear the shift-bell ringing—the start of the morning shift—and knew that workers would emerge to find their Pillar dark. The guild Chaplains would need to recite the words for a dead forge. The district would have to petition the Senate for power redistribution, beg for resources that the corporate republic would dole out according to guild influence and political favor.

The guilds would strip the Pillar for parts within days, their salvage crews working under contract law and council mandate. But we'd recorded everything. The device, the sabotage, the patterns that matched Clive's fragmented memories and Arc's impossible recognition.

Someone was killing Pillars on purpose. And somewhere in our team, we carried pieces of the answer.

We just didn't know which pieces yet.

The device's construction predates all known Aethon technology, yet shares architectural principles with modern AI systems - specifically, with Arc's own underlying design.

When the Pillar died, Mochi mourned. Not like an observer. Like someone who had lost family.

I'm supposed to document these things objectively. Record the facts, preserve the data, move on to the next job. But watching Arc struggle to understand his own grief, feeling Mochi's sorrow resonate through my hands, I realized something: we're not just collecting information anymore. We're uncovering truth that someone desperately wants buried.

The guilds call whoever did this "The Quiet," if the rumors are true. They kill Pillars and erase knowledge, all in the name of protecting Aethon from... what? Remembering?

They'll find that some things refuse to stay silent.

-P.P.
