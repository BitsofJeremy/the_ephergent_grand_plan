# Episode 3: "What Grows in Darkness"
**Season 01, Episode 03**
*Story Type: Rescue | Location: Verdanya - The Wilding | Character Focus: Mochi*

---

## TEASER

The Wilding doesn't let go of what it takes.

That's what the Rootminders say, anyway. I used to think it was metaphor—you know, poetic folk wisdom about respecting the forest. Standing at the edge of that wall of green, watching vines curl and uncurl like fingers testing the air, I realized they meant it literally.

"Miss Paradox," Arc said from my wrist, his holographic interface casting blue light across the undergrowth, "I feel compelled to note that three previous search parties have entered the Wilding in the past two weeks. None have returned."

"Yeah, I caught that part of the briefing." I adjusted my pack and checked that my goggles were secure. The humidity here was already making everything slick. "Which is why we're going in."

Mochi pulsed a cautious orange from my carrier pouch—uncertain, but not refusing. That was something, at least.

Clive's voice crackled through the Signal, gruff and direct inside my head. "Something's growing in there that shouldn't be. Can feel it pulling on the Signal like roots pulling water."

"Comforting," I muttered. "Really selling the adventure here, Clive."

"You asked."

"I didn't, actually."

Behind us, the Grove families waited—three weeks of worry etched into every face. The missing Rootminders belonged to three different Groves, their people gathered here together in shared grief. I could see the root-marks on their wrists—bioluminescent tattoos that glowed faintly even in daylight, each spiral and branch marking a life milestone. The eldest Rootminder, her arms covered from wrist to shoulder in elaborate patterns, had pressed her palm to mine in greeting. "The Green Breath guide you," she'd said. Standard Verdanya blessing, but the weight in her voice made it feel like a prayer.

"The Signal readings are concentrated approximately two kilometers northeast," Arc said. "Though the readings themselves are... peculiar."

"Peculiar how?"

"Alive, Miss Paradox. The Signal readings are alive."

Right. So that happened.

I took one last look at open sky, breathed air that didn't taste like green, and walked into the forest.

The Wilding swallowed us whole.

---

## ACT ONE

Here's what you need to know about Verdanya: they don't build things, they grow them. Houses are coaxed from living trees, roads are guided root systems, even their tools are symbiotic organisms cultivated over generations. It's beautiful, patient, and deeply intentional.

The Wilding is where that intention breaks down.

We'd been walking for maybe twenty minutes when I realized I couldn't see the sky anymore. The canopy above us wasn't woven—it was tangled, chaotic, fighting with itself for light. Bioluminescent fungi clustered on every surface, casting everything in shifting green and amber. The air tasted thick, almost solid with humidity and pollen.

"These trees are growing in patterns that shouldn't be structurally possible," Arc noted. "That trunk is supporting weight at an angle that defies—"

"Arc. We've been over this. Verdanya defies things. It's their whole deal."

"Yes, but typically with intentional design. This is something else entirely."

He wasn't wrong. Even I could tell the difference between Verdanya's usual organic architecture and whatever this was. Back in the settled Groves, plants grew in harmony—every vine positioned with purpose, every tree branch serving the community's needs. This jungle felt like it was growing too fast, too hungry, reaching for something just out of grasp. Root systems tangled across each other in configurations that looked almost... geometric. Like they were following a pattern that had nothing to do with how plants normally grow.

Mochi had shifted from cautious orange to steady blue—pulsing every few seconds, that detector frequency that meant it was tracking something through the Signal.

"Yeah, I see it too," I said quietly. "Something's ahead."

Clive hadn't spoken since we entered, which was its own kind of warning. He only went quiet when he was tracking, and he only tracked things that mattered.

The forest grew denser as we pushed northeast. Curtains of vines hung across our path—not hostile, but not welcoming either. They'd brush across my arms and leave sticky residue behind, like the forest was tasting us, deciding what we were.

"Miss Paradox." Arc's voice had gone very calm, which meant he was working hard not to alarm me. "I'm detecting organic technology ahead. Verdanyan in origin, but the growth patterns suggest it's been... unsupervised. For quite some time."

"How long?"

"Based on the Signal degradation in the cellular structure? At least fifty years. Possibly longer."

I stopped walking. "Fifty years? That's before the Deeproot Grove even expanded this far south."

"Precisely."

Mochi pulsed brighter blue, then emitted an urgent trill—the warning tone, *this matters, pay attention*.

We found the first cocoon thirty meters later.

It hung from a massive tree trunk, suspended by vines that pulsed with faint bioluminescence. The cocoon itself was roughly person-shaped, covered in a translucent membrane that showed shadows underneath—something preserved, something waiting.

"That's..." I moved closer, my hand instinctively going to my documentation recorder. "That's one of the Rootminders."

"I'm detecting a heartbeat," Arc said. "Slow, but present. Whoever is inside is alive. Barely."

There were more. Once I knew what to look for, I saw them everywhere—cocoons hanging from branches, nestled in root hollows, suspended between trees like grotesque fruit. Seven in total. The missing search parties.

"The Quiet's missing expeditions," I breathed. "It took all of them."

Mochi drifted close to the nearest cocoon, hovering inches away, pulsing blue-purple—that mix of detection and mystery, the color that meant old Signal, strange Signal, something that touched the deep frequencies the tall ones left behind.

"Mochi, can you—"

The forest moved.

Not wind-moved. Not settling branches. The vines around us rose like snakes tasting air, and I heard it—a sound like wood groaning, like roots pulling from earth, like something vast waking up.

"Pixel." Clive's voice was sharp in my head. "Run."

---

## ACT TWO

I've learned to trust Clive's instincts about danger. I've also learned that sometimes there's nowhere to run to.

The construct emerged from the forest itself—or maybe it had always been part of the forest and was only now choosing to be seen. Taller than any tree, its body was woven from hundreds of vine networks, branch systems, root structures all bound together. Bioluminescent nodes pulsed along its surface like slow heartbeats. Where its head should have been, there was only a mass of flowering growths, opening and closing in patterns that felt almost like breathing.

It was beautiful. It was terrifying. It was reaching for us.

"Arc!" I didn't have a better plan than shouting his name.

"It's not attacking—it's attempting to preserve!" His voice went rapid-fire analytical. "Miss Paradox, this is a guardian construct, designed to protect Signal-sensitive individuals from—"

"From what?"

"I don't know! The parameters are corrupted, the purpose degraded—it's trying to help, but it doesn't remember how—"

A vine wrapped around my ankle. Not violently, but firmly. Testing.

Mochi flashed red—danger, alarm, the *get-out-now* frequency.

"Clive, I could use that navigation guidance right about now!" I yanked my leg free and stumbled backward.

"Can't guide you out of something that's everywhere, kid." There was strain in his voice. "The whole forest is this thing. We're inside it."

The construct pulled more of itself together, vines braiding into appendages, roots lifting from soil to form legs. It wasn't trying to grab us—it was trying to gather us, the way you'd gather something precious, something fragile.

Something it needed to preserve before it was lost.

But here's the thing: I'd seen enough Verdanyan biotechnology to recognize engineering when I saw it. The way those vines braided together—they weren't just growing, they were *assembling*. The node patterns pulsing along its surface followed too-precise intervals. And those root systems tangling beneath us? They branched at ninety-degree angles in places, crossing over each other like... like wiring. Like circuit pathways made of cellulose instead of copper.

"It thinks we're in danger," I realized. "It's trying to protect us."

"By cocooning us in stasis," Arc confirmed. "The Rootminders it took—they're all Signal-sensitive. It perceived them as threatened and attempted to preserve them."

"But it doesn't know how to let them go."

"Correct."

More vines were rising now, creating a cage of green around us. The construct pulsed with bioluminescent patterns—patterns that reminded me, disturbingly, of Mochi's own Signal communication. But where Mochi's pulses were clear and intentional, these were fragmented, confused, a language half-forgotten.

A language that might have been programmed, millennia ago, by hands that weren't quite human anymore.

Mochi suddenly shot forward from my carrier, floating straight toward the nearest vine wall.

"Mochi—no!"

But it wasn't attacking. Mochi hovered inches from a vine cluster and began pulsing—pink, then blue, then purple, then back to pink. Slow, deliberate patterns. Not warning signals. Not distress calls.

Conversation.

"Arc," I whispered. "Is Mochi talking to it?"

"It appears to be attempting Signal-frequency communication with the construct's organic neural network. Miss Paradox, Mochi is—they're understanding each other."

The construct's vines slowed their approach. The bioluminescent pulses on its surface began to shift, matching Mochi's rhythm. Two Signal-born entities, one ancient and broken, one young and curious, speaking in frequencies human ears couldn't hear and human minds couldn't parse.

Clive's voice was quieter now, almost awed. "Never seen anything do that before. Didn't know it was possible."

"What is Mochi saying?"

"How should I know? I'm a compass, not a translator."

But I could guess. Mochi pulsed green—agreement, yes, I understand. The construct responded with slower, gentler movement. Then Mochi flashed orange—caution, wait, not yet—and the vines pulling at my boots loosened.

"It's telling the construct we're not in danger," I said. "It's trying to teach it that not everything needs preserving."

"That's... considerably more sophisticated than simple Signal detection," Arc said carefully. "Miss Paradox, what exactly is Mochi?"

"Right now? Our translator." I took a slow step forward, toward Mochi, toward the construct. "And maybe our way through this."

The construct pulsed a question—bioluminescence rippling across hundreds of vine networks in a wave of uncertain light.

Mochi pulsed back. Deep purple-violet, that Builder-ancient frequency that made my teeth ache and my root-marks tingle faintly on my wrist.

And suddenly I understood what Mochi was doing. It wasn't just communicating. It was showing the construct memories—not its own memories, but something older, something it had absorbed and carried. The memory of purpose. The memory of what guardian constructs were built to do, back when the tall ones still tended their gardens.

Protect, yes. Preserve, yes.

But also release.

The construct shuddered—a full-body wave of motion that sent leaves cascading from its surface. Its bioluminescent pulses went chaotic, then slowed, then fell into a rhythm I'd never seen from it before. Steady. Purposeful. Clear.

"What just happened?" I asked.

Arc was silent for a long moment. "I believe... Miss Paradox, I believe Mochi just reminded it what it was for."

The construct began to lower the cocooned Rootminders to the ground. Gently. Carefully. The way you'd handle something precious you'd been trusted to keep safe.

---

## ACT THREE

Getting people out of organic stasis cocoons is harder than it sounds. The Rootminders taught us that much.

Once the construct stopped trying to preserve us and started helping us rescue the trapped expeditions, everything moved faster. Turns out a centuries-old guardian organism knows a lot about its own preservation systems—it just needed to remember that releasing what it protected was part of its job.

Arc talked me through the bio-interface protocols while Mochi maintained its gentle Signal conversation with the construct. Clive, surprisingly, seemed to know which vines to cut and which to leave intact—old memory, maybe, or just good instincts.

"That one," he directed. "Right side, third membrane layer."

I made the cut. The cocoon opened like a flower, and a middle-aged Rootminder woman gasped her first unfiltered breath in three weeks. Her eyes were unfocused, her movements slow, but she was alive. I could see three root-marks on her left wrist—one for her Walk, one for her Grove bonding, one for something else I didn't recognize.

"Easy," I told her. "You're safe. We're getting you out."

It took two hours to free all seven. Two hours of careful cutting, gentle Signal pulses from Mochi to keep the construct calm, and Arc's constant readings to make sure we weren't damaging anyone in the process.

The Rootminders were weak, confused, but whole. The construct had done what it was built to do—kept them alive when it sensed danger, preserved them against harm. It just hadn't understood that the danger had passed. That preservation without release is just another kind of loss.

When the last cocoon opened and the last Rootminder was breathing free air, Mochi drifted back to the construct's central mass—that flowering cluster that served as something like a head. They pulsed at each other one more time, and I swear the construct's bioluminescence looked almost... content.

"Miss Paradox," Arc said quietly. "Mochi is giving it new parameters. Teaching it what to protect."

"What do you mean?"

"The forest itself. The construct is being... redirected. Recommissioned, if you will."

I watched as vines began to pull back, root structures settling into new configurations. The construct was still there—still vast, still powerful—but it was turning inward now, wrapping itself around the oldest trees, the deepest roots. Becoming what it was maybe always meant to be: not a guardian of people, but a guardian of place. A living embodiment of what the Green Faith teaches—that Aya the Root Mother flows through all things, and some things are meant to protect the sacred breathing of the world itself.

"Will it be okay?" I asked. Stupid question, maybe, but I needed to know.

Clive answered, his voice surprisingly gentle through the Signal. "It remembered itself. That's more than most things get."

We helped the Rootminders walk out of the Wilding. They leaned on each other, on us, moving slowly but steadily toward the light. One of them, the woman I'd freed first, touched my wrist where my documentation recorder sat. "The Green Breath was with you," she whispered. "Aya's roots guided you to us."

I didn't correct her. Maybe she was right. Maybe the Green Faith's Root Mother and the Signal's ancient programming were just two names for the same thing—the life the tall ones breathed into this world when they planted their impossible gardens.

Behind us, the forest was already changing—becoming, maybe, what Verdanya had always needed. A wild place, yes, but a protected one. A guardian that understood the difference between preserving and holding captive.

When we finally broke through the forest edge and saw open sky, Mochi returned to my carrier pouch, humming softly with satisfaction. Tired. Content.

"You did good," I told it quietly. "You did something I didn't even know was possible."

Mochi pulsed pink—happy, warm, but also something else. Something deeper. That purple undertone that meant old knowledge, preserved knowledge, memory that wasn't originally its own.

"Arc," I said. "Where did Mochi learn that? How to communicate like that with the construct?"

"I don't know, Miss Paradox. But I think... perhaps Mochi has been learning from everything it encounters. Storing more than just Signal readings. Growing in ways we haven't fully understood."

The Grove families were crying, laughing, pulling their loved ones into embraces. Safe. Home. Alive. The eldest Rootminder approached me again, pressing both palms to mine in the traditional Verdanyan gesture of deep gratitude. "You brought our people back to the roots," she said. "The Groves will remember."

We'd gone in to rescue seven people. We'd come out having taught something ancient how to remember itself.

My blog is supposed to be about documentation, about preserving knowledge before it's lost. But standing there, watching Mochi glow soft pink in the late afternoon light, I wondered if maybe it was about something more. About helping things remember not just what they were, but what they could be.

Clive's voice drifted through the Signal, quiet and almost philosophical. "Sometimes the things that need saving aren't just people. Sometimes they're purpose."

"Getting profound in your old age," I teased.

"Getting tired in my old age. Let's go home."

Mochi hummed—that low, amused frequency—and I could've sworn I felt Arc's equivalent of laughter through the wristband's interface.

We walked back to our transport under Verdanya's eternal green light, one impossible rescue behind us, countless more mysteries ahead.

## CHRONICLE ENTRY

**chronicle entry: The Wilding Guardian**

Deep in the Wilding, there's a construct older than anyone remembers building. It spent fifty years trying to protect people from dangers it couldn't identify, preserving them in stasis because it had forgotten how to let go. We found seven Rootminders trapped in its care—alive, but frozen.

Mochi taught it to remember. Not through words or interface protocols, but through Signal communication between two beings that understand each other in ways I'm still trying to comprehend. The construct isn't gone. It's just protecting something different now—the forest itself, the wild spaces, the green dark where things grow according to rules the Builders programmed into root and vine before they became whatever they became.

I documented the rescue. Arc logged every bio-reading. Clive guided us through terrain he shouldn't have been able to map. But Mochi... Mochi did something I didn't know was possible. It didn't just sense. It taught. It carried some old memory of purpose and shared it with something that had lost its way.

The Grove families blessed us with the Green Breath when we brought their people home. I accepted it, even though I don't follow their faith. Because maybe—just maybe—what they call Aya's roots and what I call Builder bioengineering are just two languages describing the same ancient gift: a world designed to breathe, to grow, to remember.

Every day I learn something new about my team. Every day they surprise me.

That's not just documentation. That's trust. And maybe that matters more than any of the readings we collect.

-P.P.
