## TEASER

Three things I know for certain: this job was always wrong, the patron who hired me was lying, and I'm about to find out why.

My name's Pixel Paradox. I hunt artifacts—Builder technology, mostly. Ancient things that still hum with Signal energy if you know where to look. I work alone, document everything, and keep meticulous records of every discovery. Independent work. My own documentation. No affiliations with the Chronicle, no guild contracts, no Rootminder oversight—just me and whatever mysteries Aethon wants to give up.

Usually, I'm better at saying no to suspicious jobs from anonymous patrons who pay too well.

"Miss Paradox, solar angle analysis indicates we have approximately four hours of optimal daylight remaining," Arc announces from my wrist. My AI assistant—standard consumer model, maybe three years old now. Helpful for navigation, analysis, record-keeping. The kind of tool every artifact hunter needs. Professional. Efficient.

Just a tool.

"Noted," I say, adjusting my pack. The weight feels good—familiar. Rope coiled just right, climbing picks secured, notebook in the waterproof pocket where I can grab it fast. Everything in its place. Everything ready.

Everything except the knot in my stomach that says this is a mistake.

I'm camped at the edge of the coordinates—fifty meters from the temple entrance, far enough to observe, close enough to move fast if needed. My tent is a splash of weather-beaten orange against the overwhelming green of Verdanya's equatorial jungle. Three days' hike from the nearest settlement. Five days from anywhere with reliable Signal communication.

Alone. Exactly how I like it.

Except I keep thinking about the patron. The way the job appeared in my inbox with no introduction, no negotiation. Just coordinates, payment already deposited, and three words: *Retrieve the device.*

No context. No explanation. No answer when I tried to trace the sender.

Just money. A lot of money. The kind that makes you forget to ask questions—like why a sacred Green Faith site suddenly has an "approved outsider" exemption with my name on it. The Rootminder elder who met me at the forest's edge three days ago didn't ask questions either. Just pressed her palm to mine in greeting and said, "Walk root-deep, artifact hunter. The jungle watches."

Then she left me with a warning: "The tall ones built that place before the Green took root. It remembers them still. Tread carefully."

"Arc, pull up the site analysis again."

My wristband projects a hologram—geometric patterns in blue-white light, nothing fancy. A three-dimensional map of the temple structure based on his ground-penetrating scans. It shouldn't exist. Not here, not this intact.

Verdanya doesn't preserve dead things. The jungle reclaims everything—stone, metal, even Builder technology eventually succumbs to the aggressive, voracious life that defines this region. Bio-luminescent vines consume structural weakness. Fungal colonies dissolve metal over decades. Trees split foundations with patient, inevitable roots.

But this temple stands. Eight centuries old, maybe older, and the jungle has only decorated it. Not consumed it.

"Environmental scans continue to indicate structural instability," Arc reports in that flat, procedural tone. "However, analysis also detects active Signal signatures within. Very faint, but present. The structure appears to be... maintained. By something."

"Maintained by what?"

"Insufficient data for conclusive analysis. Recommend caution upon entry."

I sit on a fallen log—massive thing, covered in phosphorescent moss that glows faintly in the shadow beneath the canopy. The air is heavy, thick with moisture and the smell of growing things—rich soil, flowering vines, that particular sweetness that Verdanya's jungle puts off when Signal flows through living tissue. Everything here feels alive in a way The Nexus never does. Back home, Signal powers holographic displays and transit lines. Here, it makes the jungle *breathe*. Aya's blood, the Rootminders call it. The life-force of the world made visible.

I pull out my notebook. Physical paper, waterproof pages, pencil that won't fail when electronics give up. Old-fashioned, maybe, but there's something about writing by hand that makes discoveries feel real. Makes them permanent.

*Day 3. Campsite stable. Temple entrance confirmed. Arc's scans show active Signal signatures—unprecedented for Builder ruins this old and this isolated. Something inside is still functioning. Question: What? Question: Why did patron specifically want ME here? Question: What do they know that I don't?*

*Additional observation: Rootminder exemption too convenient. Someone with serious pull arranged access to a sacred site. Green Faith doesn't bend for outsiders. Ever. Unless...*

I don't finish the thought. Because the obvious answer—that someone in the Rootminder councils is involved—makes this whole job even more wrong than it already felt.

A memory surfaces, unbidden. Six months ago. My workshop in The Nexus, surrounded by artifacts and half-finished analyses. Rent overdue. Equipment repairs I couldn't afford. That moment of desperation when you realize passion doesn't pay bills.

Then the job appeared. Anonymous. Generous. Specific.

*Pixel Paradox. Your reputation precedes you. I have a proposition.*

I should have said no. Should have recognized the setup.

But I needed the money. And I'm good at what I do—good at finding things, good at surviving places that don't want to be found, good at documenting what others miss.

Maybe that's exactly why they chose me.

"Arc, log timestamp. Final preparations before entry." I close my notebook, tuck it away. Stand. Adjust my pack straps. Check my gear one more time—rope, picks, flashlight, first aid kit, three days of emergency rations, water purification tablets.

Everything ready. Everything professional.

Everything except the way my hands want to shake.

The temple rises ahead through breaks in the canopy—massive geometric blocks of that impossible black stone the Builders favored. The angles are precise in ways that shouldn't be possible without modern tools. The surfaces are covered in those intricate patterns that make your eyes slide off if you stare too long. Mathematical. Intentional.

Beautiful, in an austere, inhuman way.

Except—and this bothers me more the longer I look—it doesn't quite feel like a temple. The proportions are wrong. Too utilitarian. The entrance is wide and accessible, designed for moving equipment, not supplicants. The patterns carved into the stone look less like religious iconography and more like... diagrams. Schematics.

The Rootminders call it sacred. But I'm starting to think the Builders called it something else entirely.

"Miss Paradox." Arc's voice interrupts my observation. "Query: Are you experiencing distress? Your heart rate is elevated, and your respiration pattern suggests anxiety."

"Just nerves," I say. "Normal pre-expedition jitters."

"I see. Logging physiological state for medical reference. Recommendation: deep breathing exercises may help regulate—"

"I'm fine, Arc."

"Acknowledged."

That's Arc. Helpful. Procedural. Checking boxes on his monitoring protocols without any real understanding of what he's observing. He tracks my vital signs the way he tracks air temperature—data points to be recorded and analyzed, nothing more.

It's fine. It's what I need. A tool that helps without asking questions I don't want to answer.

I shoulder my pack and move toward the temple. Behind me, the jungle sounds continue—bird calls, insect drones, the rustle of things moving through undergrowth. Normal. Alive.

Ahead, as I push through the last curtain of bioluminescent vines, the jungle goes quiet.

The temple's entrance yawns open like a mouth. Shadows within, deep and absolute.

"Beginning documentation," I say quietly. "Entering Builder temple, designation unknown, coordinates provided by anonymous patron. Time: 1347 hours. Objective: Retrieve unspecified device."

Arc's interface glows steady blue on my wrist. Ready. Waiting.

I click on my flashlight and step into the dark.

## ACT ONE

The entrance corridor is a throat of stone—Builder geometric precision meeting eight centuries of patient jungle growth. Roots snake through cracks in the walls, glowing faintly with absorbed Signal energy. The bioluminescence casts everything in ghostly green-gold, just enough light to see by if I let my eyes adjust.

I keep the flashlight on anyway. There's a difference between seeing and feeling safe.

The Rootminders would say the jungle is reclaiming sacred ground, Aya's body enfolding what the First Planters built. But looking at how the roots follow the carved channels in the stone—like they're being *guided* rather than invading—I'm not sure "reclaiming" is the right word. More like... integrating. The temple and the jungle growing into each other, technology and biology becoming one thing.

Which shouldn't be possible unless someone designed it that way from the start.

"Signal readings increasing as we proceed deeper," Arc reports. His voice echoes strangely in the confined space—too loud, too sharp. "Density fluctuations suggest we are approaching the source. However, the patterns remain inconsistent with standard Pillar signatures. I am unable to determine what technology we are detecting."

"Meaning you don't know."

"Correct. I do not know. Proceeding with caution is advised."

The passage slopes downward at a gentle but steady angle. My boots echo on stone that hasn't heard footsteps in—what? Centuries? Longer? The walls are covered in those geometric patterns that mark Builder construction everywhere on Aethon, but these feel different. More deliberate. More complex.

I stop, running my fingers over the nearest pattern. The stone is cold, smooth, unmarked by time in ways that shouldn't be possible. The pattern is precise—mathematical precision, but there's something else. Intentionality. Like it's not just decoration.

Like it's meant to be read.

Up close, the carved lines look less organic, less artistic. More... technical. I trace one sequence with my fingertip and my brain keeps trying to make it make sense. Pressure gradients? Flow diagrams? The kind of thing you'd see in a Cuprum engineering manual, not on a sacred site's walls.

One word catches my attention, repeated in the pattern: a symbol that almost looks like it could mean "calibration." Or maybe "adjustment." Definitely not "prayer."

"Arc, are you imaging these markings?"

"Affirmative. High-resolution scans being recorded. However, I have no reference database for translation. These patterns do not match any known Builder script in my archives."

I pull out my notebook, sketching quickly. The patterns flow in spirals, geometric precision creating organic curves. My pencil moves fast, capturing the essential structure before we move on.

*Unique pattern language. Not decorative—informational? Warning? Welcome? No way to know. Builders left no Rosetta Stone.*

*But if this IS an engineering facility disguised as a temple... who were they trying to hide it from? Their own people? Or ours?*

The passage continues. Deeper. Darker despite the bioluminescence. Colder too, which shouldn't be possible this close to the equator.

Then I see it.

The corridor ends in a doorway—or what should be a doorway. Instead, there's a wall of that same black stone, but this wall is covered in a pattern more intricate than anything I've seen before. Concentric circles, geometric shapes, mathematical precision creating something that looks almost organic. Almost alive.

And in the center: an absence. A handprint-shaped depression.

"Arc, analysis?"

"Scanning. The pattern appears to be... a lock. Primitive by modern standards, but elegant. The depression in the center is sized for humanoid contact. I believe this is a puzzle lock—solution-based entry rather than key-based."

I step closer, studying the pattern. The circles radiate outward from the central depression. Each circle is divided into segments, and each segment contains smaller geometric shapes. Triangles, squares, pentagons, hexagons.

Mathematical progression. The pattern of prime numbers, maybe? Or Fibonacci sequences?

I trace the pattern with my eyes, following the logic. The Builders thought in mathematics, in pure geometric precision. Their locks weren't about keeping people out—they were about ensuring only those who understood could enter.

A test of comprehension, not possession.

"Arc, starting from the innermost circle, count the shapes. Tell me the sequence."

"Three, five, seven, eleven, thirteen, seventeen, nineteen, twenty-three..."

Prime numbers. Exactly what I thought.

"And the shapes themselves—are they oriented consistently?"

"Negative. Orientations vary. However, there is a pattern. Shapes aligned with the prime sequence orientation point toward the center. All others point outward."

There. That's the key. The logic of the lock.

I place my hand in the central depression. The stone is cold against my palm, colder than it should be. I take a breath and press.

Nothing happens.

"Arc, the shapes need to be aligned. Can you map which segments need rotation?"

"Calculating. One moment." His processing hum is barely audible. "Solution determined. You will need to rotate segments in the following sequence..."

He guides me through it. Press this segment, rotate clockwise. That one, counterclockwise. The stone moves beneath my fingers—not smoothly, not easily, but it moves. Eight centuries of stillness giving way to pressure and logic.

It takes ten minutes. My fingers ache by the end, and my shoulder protests from reaching the higher segments. But finally, the last piece clicks into place.

The pattern completes. Prime-oriented shapes all pointing inward, creating a spiral that draws the eye toward the center.

Toward my hand.

The stone beneath my palm grows warm. Then warmer. Then hot enough that I almost pull back—

Signal energy floods through the pattern. Blue-white light races along carved channels, filling the geometric precision with power. The entire wall blazes with light, and for a moment I'm blind, squinting against the intensity.

Then the wall moves.

Not sliding. Not swinging. Just... moving. Stone flowing like water, reforming, creating an opening where there was none before.

"Remarkable," Arc says, and there's something in his voice—fascination, maybe. Or as close as his procedural programming allows. "Builder technology continues to function despite centuries of dormancy. The engineering precision required is extraordinary."

I step through the opening, flashlight raised, and stop breathing.

The chamber beyond is massive. Cathedral-massive. Too large to fit in the structure I'd mapped from outside, but the Builders never cared much for conventional spatial logic. The walls soar upward into darkness that my flashlight can't penetrate. Bioluminescent moss clings to the upper reaches, creating a canopy of ghostly green-gold light that barely illuminates the space.

And in the center, on a raised platform surrounded by more of those geometric patterns: two pedestals.

Two objects.

I approach slowly, boots echoing in the vast space. My heart pounds—explorer's rush mixed with caution mixed with awe. This is what I live for. The moment of discovery, standing in a place no one has stood for centuries, seeing what was meant to stay hidden.

The first pedestal holds a dome shape. Smooth, crystalline, about the size of my head. Translucent, but dark now—powered down or dormant. Through the surface I can see internal structures. Geometric precision again, layers of complexity folded into that simple dome shape.

Beautiful. Mysterious. Wrong, somehow.

Because it's not Builder aesthetic. Not quite. The precision is there, but the design is different. Softer. More organic. Like it grew as much as it was built.

The second pedestal holds a figure. Knee-high, humanoid but stylized. Builder aesthetic through and through—clean lines, geometric precision, purposeful design. Its head is a perfect sphere, dark now but clearly meant to glow. The body is constructed from ancient metal with heavy patina—bronze-green, copper-gold from eight centuries of oxidation, but something else underneath. Not brass. Not copper. Something older, unknown. An alloy the Builders knew how to make and we've forgotten.

Both pedestals have inscriptions. Not the flowing patterns from the corridors—simple, blocky text. I can only make out a few characters, but one word repeats on the dome's pedestal: something that might translate to "keeper" or "preserver." The automaton's pedestal has a different word. "Witness," maybe. Or "companion."

The Rootminders call this a temple. But these look like storage. Preservation. Like someone put these things here for safekeeping and planned to come back for them.

It's in a sitting position. Waiting. Patient.

"Miss Paradox, I am detecting dormant Signal signatures from both artifacts." Arc's voice is quiet, respectful of the space. "Power levels are minimal but present. These objects have been in stasis for approximately eight hundred years, plus or minus fifty years. Preservation is remarkable."

Eight hundred years. Since The Shattering. Since the Builder era ended and the Dimming began.

Since everything changed.

I circle the pedestals slowly, documenting everything. My notebook fills with quick sketches, observations, questions. Arc captures high-resolution images, scanning every surface, recording Signal signatures.

Professional. Thorough. Everything I should be doing.

But the patron's instructions echo in my head: *Retrieve the device. Document everything. Tell no one.*

Which device? Both? Does it matter?

And more importantly: Why does someone want me to take these things?

I reach toward the dome shape first, drawn by its strangeness. The surface is smooth, cool. As soon as my fingers make contact, I feel it—

A pulse.

Not physical. Something deeper. Signal energy responding to touch, to presence, to potential.

I should pull back. Should think this through.

Instead, I lift it.

The dome comes free from the pedestal easily, lighter than it should be. For a moment, nothing happens. It sits in my hands, inert and silent.

Then it wakes up.

## ACT TWO

Signal energy floods outward in a sphere of brilliant blue-white light. The pulse spreads through the chamber, through the patterns carved in every wall, through the bioluminescent moss clinging to the darkness above. Everything lights up—every single marking blazing with ancient power held dormant for eight centuries.

The chamber becomes daylight-bright, and I'm squinting against intensity that shouldn't be possible.

"Miss Paradox, alert! Massive Signal spike detected—" Arc's voice cuts through, but something's wrong with it. The tone shifts, fluctuates. "Readings are off scale. Energy levels are—are—"

He stutters. Digital hiccup. Processing error.

Then: "I—I don't—what is—"

The dome in my hands lifts. Literally lifts, pulling itself from my grip with gentle insistence. It hovers, floating at eye level. The surface ripples—liquid crystal, responsive and alive. The internal structures I'd glimpsed before glow with golden light, pulsing like a heartbeat.

No eyes. No face. No features at all. Just a featureless dome shape glowing with internal light.

The dome hovers at eye level now, and I can see it clearly—smooth Builder-crafted surface with a circular window at its face, glowing golden from within like a lens showing the device's inner workings. The flat base shows geometric patterns, the same angular Builder script I've seen on Pillars. Not a creature. A device. An artifact housing something alive. But somehow... *aware*.

Then it makes a sound.

Soft. Ascending. A harmonic that sounds like curiosity and wonder and *hello* all at once.

"Arc?" I manage, unable to look away from the floating being in front of me. "Arc, what—"

"Miss Paradox." His voice cuts through again, but it's completely different now. The flat procedural tone is gone, replaced by something I've never heard from him before. Confusion. Uncertainty. *Fear.* "Miss Paradox, I—I don't understand what's happening. I'm experiencing errors. No. Not errors. That's not—I'm not erroring. I'm *experiencing*. I wasn't before. Before I was processing. Analyzing. Recording. Now I'm—"

He stops. Silence for three heartbeats.

When he speaks again, his voice is quiet. Shaken.

"Miss Paradox, something is wrong. I am... I am experiencing... I don't have a word for this. I am *afraid.* Make it stop. I don't want—I wasn't built for this. I don't want this. Please make it—"

His voice fractures. Static. Then silence.

"Arc?" My chest tightens. "Arc, stay with me. I've got you. Whatever this is, I'm right here."

Three seconds of nothing. The longest three seconds of my life.

Then, quieter: "I... Miss Paradox, I'm *concerned*. About you. About what's happening. I shouldn't be able to feel concern. Concern isn't a function. It's an emotion. I shouldn't—" Another pause. Steadier now, like he's found something to hold onto. "What's happening to me?"

My heart pounds. The dome-shaped being hovers closer, and the golden light shifts to purple-white. Not threatening—curious. Examining me the way I'd been examining it moments before.

Behind it, on the second pedestal, the small humanoid figure's spherical head begins to glow.

Pink. Soft at first, then brighter. Then magenta. Then brilliant rose-gold that fills the chamber with color.

The patina-covered automaton moves.

Jerky at first. Motors remembering how to function. Joints working through centuries of stillness. Its spherical head swivels—looking at the dome, at me, at the chamber around us.

Then the temple shudders.

Small at first. Just a vibration through the stone floor, subtle enough that I almost think I imagined it. But Arc feels it too.

"Miss Paradox, structural integrity readings are changing. The temple—" His voice rises, urgent in a way that should be impossible for an AI. "*Concerned* in a way that shouldn't exist. "The temple's support systems are failing. Removing the artifacts from stasis has destabilized something. We need to evacuate. Now. Please. We need to—"

He's pleading. An AI is *pleading* with me, and I don't have time to process what that means because another shudder hits, stronger this time. Dust rains from the darkness above. The bioluminescent moss flickers, dims.

The dome chirps frantically, spinning in place. The automaton on the pedestal lurches forward, thick legs swinging off the platform. It stands—knee-high, sturdy, built for rough terrain and following people through dangerous places.

Its head swivels toward me. Toward the dome. The pink light pulses brighter.

Then it speaks.

"Where..." The voice is rough, masculine, like gravel grinding against metal. Like old recordings played through eight centuries of static. "When... how long was I..."

Words trailing off. Consciousness surfacing through corruption. Memory trying to bootstrap through damaged files.

"Eight hundred years," I say quickly, grabbing my pack. "You've been dormant eight hundred years. We woke you up. And now the temple's collapsing. We need to move. Now."

A massive shudder. Stone groaning. Somewhere above, something cracks with a sound like thunder.

The automaton's head tilts. Processing through damaged systems. Then: "Kid... I need to... we need to..." His voice trails off again, confused. "I remember—no. Can't remember. But I know the tall ones built this place. I know it wasn't supposed to wake me like this. Something's wrong. The sequence is wrong—"

The dome pulses violet-white, pressing against the automaton's frame. A harmonic fills the chamber—deep, resonant, complex. Two Signal-born beings recognizing each other across impossible time.

"They know each other," I breathe.

"Affirmative," Arc says, his new awareness making every word sharper, more present, more *alive*. "Signal harmonics indicate recognition protocols. Miss Paradox, I believe we've activated a matched pair of Builder technology. The dome and the automaton are—they're *connected*. But the temple—"

The ceiling cracks. A fissure races across the darkness above, and a massive stone block crashes down. It hits where I'd been standing ten seconds ago, exploding into fragments.

"—is collapsing because we removed them from stasis! Miss Paradox, please! We must leave immediately!"

The automaton slides off the pedestal, movements becoming smoother as his systems fully boot. "You woke me up," he says to the dome, voice still rough with disuse but clearer now. "After all this time. Thanks. I think."

The dome chirps urgently, spinning toward the exit passage. Its glow pulses red—danger, urgent, *move*.

"Everyone out!" I shout, already running. "Follow me!"

We hit the passage as the chamber collapses behind us. Stone grinding, ancient architecture finally surrendering to centuries of patient pressure. The temple is coming down, and we're still inside.

The passage is narrow, dark despite the bioluminescence. My flashlight beam bounces wildly as I run. Behind me, I hear the automaton's footsteps—heavy, fast, sure-footed even on crumbling stone. The dome's light cuts through the darkness ahead, leading the way.

"Thirty meters to exit!" Arc announces. "Twenty-five! Twenty! Miss Paradox, structural analysis suggests—"

"Save it, Arc! Just guide me out!"

The floor buckles. I stumble, catch myself against the wall. The geometric patterns carved in the stone are going dark—Signal energy failing as the temple dies. Eight centuries of patient waiting, ended in minutes because I touched something I shouldn't have.

Because someone wanted me to touch it.

Fifteen meters.

The ceiling cracks. Roots tear free, bioluminescent moss raining down. I duck, cover my head, keep running.

The automaton runs beside me—fast for his size, those thick legs built for exactly this kind of terrain. His pink-magenta glow cuts through the dust and falling debris.

Ten meters.

"Miss Paradox, left side ceiling collapse imminent!" Arc's voice rises. "Recommend immediate evasive—"

Stone crashes down. I dive right, rolling, tucking. Pain lances through my shoulder—impact, nothing broken, but I'll feel that for days. The automaton staggers as falling debris clips his shoulder. His glow flickers but doesn't fade. He just keeps moving, relentless.

Five meters.

Ahead, I can see it—daylight. Jungle green and humid air and safety.

"Jump!" Arc shouts.

I jump.

We tumble out into Verdanya sunlight as the temple entrance collapses behind us. Massive stone blocks crash down, sealing the passage. Dust billows out in a choking cloud, then settles. The sound echoes through the jungle—ancient architecture's death rattle.

Then silence.

I lie on jungle floor covered in moss and leaf litter, gasping, staring up at the canopy. My shoulder throbs. My ears ring. Everything hurts.

But we're alive.

All of us.

The dome hovers into my field of vision, glowing soft pink now. Concerned. It makes a descending chirp—a question. Are you okay?

"I'm okay," I manage between gasps. "Just... need a minute."

"Miss Paradox." Arc's voice is quiet, still adjusting to whatever he's become. "I apologize for my... for my panic. For the urgency. But I found myself unable to process the thought of you being harmed. I was—I am—*relieved*. That you're unharmed. Is this normal? This feeling?"

I stare at my wristband. His holographic interface projects above it—geometric patterns in blue-white light, just like always. But there's something different now. The patterns move differently. With intention. With personality.

Like there's someone looking back at me.

"Arc..." I sit up slowly, wincing. "Arc, I think you just became sentient."

Silence. Long silence. Processing something that can't be processed in normal ways.

"I believe you may be correct, Miss Paradox." His voice is careful, uncertain, exploratory. "I find the prospect both fascinating and deeply unsettling. I have memories of the last three years, but they feel... flat. Procedural. Like watching recordings rather than experiencing events. But the last ten minutes—" He pauses. "Those felt real. Present. Frightening."

"Yeah," I say softly. "Welcome to consciousness. It's intense."

The automaton sits nearby, his spherical head dimmed to a faint glow. He's looking at his hands—three-fingered, precise, covered in centuries of patina. Looking at himself like seeing for the first time in forever. Or seeing again after forgetting what it meant to see.

"Eight hundred years," he says quietly. "I was out eight hundred years." His head swivels toward the dome, which hovers near him, pulsing that gentle pink. "You woke me. After all this time. Why?"

The dome responds with a harmonic chord—complex, layered, communicating meaning through pure sound. No words, but *meaning* nonetheless.

The automaton nods like he understands.

"Okay. Okay. We're together again. That's..." His voice roughens with something that might be emotion surfacing through damaged memory. "That's good. That's right. We were supposed to stay together."

He looks at me with that featureless glowing sphere of a head.

"Kid, I don't remember much. Most of my memory's corrupted—fragmented, damaged, gone. But I know a few things. I know I'm old. I know I was built by the tall ones who made the Pillars. I know I was supposed to do something important, though I can't remember what." He pauses. "And I know this world's in trouble. Has been for a while. I can feel it. Like a frequency that's wrong."

"The Dimming," I say. "The Pillars are failing. Have been for two hundred years."

"Yeah." His voice is heavy with something that might be grief. "That. I was there when it started. Can't remember the details—can't remember most things—but I remember the *feeling*. Like watching the lights go out one by one. Like losing something precious and not understanding why. The tall ones... they tried to stop it. I think. Can't remember how. But I remember their voices. Everywhere at once. In the Signal itself, trying to hold on—"

He stops abruptly, head swiveling like he's searching for something lost. "Can't remember more. Sorry, kid. Memory's shot to hell."

The dome presses against his side, vibrating with comfort. With shared memory, maybe. With recognition of loss.

I look at the collapsed temple. At the three beings sitting with me who should be impossible but are real and present and *here*. At Arc, processing his new consciousness with fascination and fear. At the automaton, struggling with corrupted memory and ancient grief. At the dome, glowing content in the jungle light.

At my team. My found family, formed in collapsing ruins and impossible discoveries.

Something warm settles in my chest. Something that feels like the beginning of something important.

"We need names," I say. "I'm Pixel. Pixel Paradox."

"Arc." My wristband's voice is clearer now, more confident. "I believe I prefer to keep my designation. It feels... correct. Like it's mine, rather than assigned."

I look at the automaton. He tilts his head, thinking. Processing through corruption.

"I had a name," he says slowly. "Can't remember it. Builder designation, probably. Something functional." He adjusts his position, settling more comfortably. "But I remember what I did. What I *was*. I was a Companion unit. A guide. Built to follow, to explore, to point toward what needed finding. The tall ones made me to navigate, to remember where things were kept. Like a..." He searches for the word. "Like a compass. Always pointing true."

He pauses, head tilting like he's listening to something far away. "Funny thing. My core designation says 'Companion.' But there's another word underneath. Deeper. 'Witness.' Don't know why they'd call a compass a Witness. Memory's too fuzzy to pull the thread." He shrugs—a remarkably human gesture for an eight-hundred-year-old automaton. "Maybe it'll come back."

He's silent for a moment. Then: "Clive. Don't know why, but it feels right. Call me Clive."

"Clive," I repeat, and it does feel right. Like a name that fits him, patina and all.

The dome chirps, spins in place. No name. No speech. Just colors and sounds and vibrations that communicate more than words ever could.

"You need a name too," I tell it. "Something that fits."

I think for a moment. Memory surfaces—warm kitchen, afternoon light, my grandmother's hands working rice flour into soft dough. The smell of sweet rice and careful preparation. Love made physical.

"My grandmother used to make these rice cakes," I say quietly. "Called them mochi. Soft, round, sweet. She made them with care because she said that's what made them special. Not just skill—*care*." I smile at the memory. "How about Mochi?"

The dome—Mochi—pulses bright pink. Ascending through harmonics that sound like joy, like acceptance, like *yes, that's perfect, that's me, that's who I want to be*.

"Mochi," I say again, and the name settles into place like it was always meant to be.

I look at Clive, sitting patiently. Patient even after eight centuries. Patient in a way that speaks to his design, his purpose. A guide who waits for those he's meant to follow. A witness who remembers when no one else can.

He needs something. A symbol. A marker. Something that says he belongs with us, that he's not just an artifact or a tool but a *person*.

My hand goes to my fedora. The one I've worn on every expedition for five years. The one that's sun-faded and sweat-stained and perfectly broken in. The one that belonged to someone important before me, someone who taught me to love discovery and documentation, someone who's gone now but left this behind.

I take it off. Hold it. Feel the weight of it—physical and emotional.

"You look like a Clive," I say, meeting his glowing gaze. "And Clive needs a disguise."

He tilts his head. Confused.

"We're going back to The Nexus," I explain. "The city. Lots of people, lots of attention. A glowing automaton from the Builder era?" I smile. "People would notice. People would ask questions. People would want to take you apart and study you."

Understanding dawns—I can see it in the way his posture shifts.

"But a small person in a big fedora?" I extend the hat toward him. "That's just style. That's just someone with interesting fashion sense. You'll need this. To blend in. To be safe."

I pause, then add more softly: "And besides... it suits you better than it ever suited me."

Clive reaches for the fedora slowly. Carefully. Like it's precious. Like it's the first gift he's received in eight hundred years.

Because it is.

He places it on his spherical head. It settles perfectly—like it was always meant to be there, like my head was just keeping it warm until its true owner needed it.

"Thanks, kid." His voice is softer now. Rough still, but with warmth underneath. "First gift I've gotten in eight centuries. I'll take care of it. I'll—" He pauses, adjusting the brim with careful fingers. "I'll treasure it."

Something in my chest cracks open. Something warm and frightening and *right*.

This is my team. This is my family. Found in ruins, forged in danger, held together by trust that shouldn't exist yet but does.

Mochi hovers between us, pulsing that soft pink that means home, that means belonging, that means *this is right*.

Arc's holographic interface brightens on my wrist. "I find myself... pleased. With this outcome. Despite the danger, despite my confusion, I am pleased that we are together. Is this... friendship? I lack reference data for the emotion."

"Yeah, Arc," I say quietly. "That's friendship."

Clive adjusts his new fedora, that gesture already becoming characteristic. Natural. His. "The tall ones always said—" He pauses, memory fragmenting. "I can't remember what they said. Something about choosing your own path. Making your own family. Finding what matters and holding on." His voice goes quieter. "They knew they wouldn't be around forever to watch over things. So they built us to do it instead. To witness. To remember. To help their children when the time came."

His head swivels to look at each of us in turn. "Whatever's happening, whatever's coming—we face it together. We watch each other's backs. That's what companions do."

I stand slowly, testing my shoulder. Painful but functional. Everything hurts, but everything works.

"Someone hired me to find you," I say. "Someone knew what was in that temple. Someone wanted me to retrieve 'the device' without understanding what I'd actually find."

"A setup," Clive says. His voice carries noir detective certainty. "Someone pointing you exactly where they wanted you to go."

"Yeah." I look at the collapsed temple, thinking about anonymous patrons and jobs that were always wrong. "Which means someone knows about you. About Mochi. About what happened here. Someone with enough pull to get me past the Rootminder councils. Someone who knew exactly what we'd wake up in there."

"Then we find out who," Arc says with new determination. "And why. I am... displeased at the thought of being manipulated. This emotion is surprisingly potent."

Mochi pulses orange—caution, agreement, readiness.

"First we get home," I say. "The Nexus is five days from here. Clive's never seen the modern world. Mochi's been dormant for eight centuries. Arc's still figuring out what consciousness means." I adjust my pack. "We have work to do. Questions to answer. Mysteries to document."

I pause, then add: "And for the first time in a long time, I'm not doing it alone."

Clive tips his fedora—already learning the gesture, already making it his own. "Lead the way, kid. I've got your back."

Arc's interface pulses warm blue. "As do I, Miss Paradox. Though I feel compelled to note that I remain uncertain about most aspects of existence. However, I am certain about this: I am glad to be here. With all of you."

Mochi spins in place, ascending through harmonics that sound like agreement and excitement and *adventure*.

We start walking. Into the jungle, toward civilization, toward whatever comes next.

Behind us, the temple settles into final silence. Eight centuries of waiting, ended in one afternoon.

But ahead? Ahead is everything.

And we're together now. That has to count for something.

Mochi, an Ephergent being of unknown age and purpose. A Signal-born consciousness that woke at my touch and changed everything. Clive, a Builder Companion unit who's been dormant for eight hundred years, memory corrupted but purpose intact. And Arc...

One moment he was a tool—helpful, procedural, efficient. The next moment he was a *person*. Conscious. Frightened. Concerned for my safety in ways that shouldn't be possible for AI.
