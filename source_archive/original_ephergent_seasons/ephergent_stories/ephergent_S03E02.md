# S03E02 — The Boneyard of Beautiful Things

> **Status**: Planned — not yet written

**Location**: The Frequency Graveyard

## Synopsis

Okay, brace yourself, because this one's going to hurt. We've been traversing deeper into the unmapped frequencies for days, following the Ephergent frequency like it's a beacon in a quantum void, when A1 picks up something impossible: dozens of frequencies clustered together, and every single one of them is dead. Not fading — dead. Gone. Flat-lined. We're talking a whole GRAVEYARD of worlds that used to be, just... floating in the deep like derelicts in a harbor that forgot about them.

And it's beautiful. That's the part that wrecks me. These dead frequencies are gorgeous — frozen crystalline structures where oceans used to be, forests of pure light that stopped mid-photosynthesis, cities preserved in perfect silence like someone pressed pause on a civilization and walked away. Luminara's crying behind her camera. A1's playing something on the ship speakers that sounds like a lullaby for dead stars. And then Clive finds it — a staple pattern embedded in the lattice of a wrecked vessel, buried in the graveyard. His old partner. Barry Kowalski came through here. Years ago. And judging by the trail, he didn't stop. He kept going deeper. Toward the source.

## Narrative Structure

### Opening Incident
A1 detects an unusual concentration of frequency signatures — all flatlined. The crew approaches what reveals itself as a vast cluster of dead frequencies, dozens of them, drifting together in the deep Sea like an archipelago of silence. Some died recently; others are ancient beyond A1's ability to date them. Pixel orders a full survey. Luminara deploys her photographic equipment. The graveyard is heartbreakingly beautiful.

### Escalating Conflict
As they explore, they realize these frequencies didn't fade from the Drift — at least not all of them. Some show signs of natural entropy, but others seem to have simply... stopped. Chose to stop. Om Kai theorizes that some frequencies reach a point where they've expressed everything they have to express, and they go quiet. But others were interrupted — cut short. The distinction is unsettling. Meanwhile, Clive becomes increasingly agitated, his staple patterns erratic. He's picking up traces of something familiar.

### Character Journey
Clive discovers a derelict vessel wedged between two dead frequencies — and on its lattice, staple patterns. HIS staple patterns. Or rather, patterns that respond to his: the work of Barry Kowalski, his old partner who disappeared years ago. Barry came through this graveyard. Left notes in their shared language. The notes are fragmented, but they paint a picture: Barry was following the Ephergent frequency too, and he believed it led to something he called "The Wellspring." Clive hasn't spoken about Barry in years. Now he can't stop.

### Climactic Choice
Clive wants to follow Barry's trail immediately, abandoning the graveyard survey. Pixel understands — but the graveyard holds information they need about frequency death and what lies ahead. She asks Clive to help them finish the survey first, promising they'll follow Barry's trail after. It's a quiet conflict — no yelling, no drama. Just an old stapler staring at his missing partner's handwriting, choosing between grief and duty. He chooses duty. But his staple patterns that evening spell out Barry's name, over and over.

### Resolution Setup
The survey complete, the crew has a sobering map of how frequencies fade — and a new heading. Barry's trail points deeper into the unmapped frequencies, roughly aligned with the Ephergent frequency's source. Nano decrypts fragments from the derelict vessel's data banks: Barry's last log mentions "the place where signals begin." Clive is silent on the bridge, watching the graveyard recede. A1 quietly sets course along Barry's heading. The Ephergent frequency pulses stronger, and somewhere in its harmonics, Zephyr swears he hears Aether humming.

## Character Focus

### Clive
*The dame walked into my office on a Tuesday. Except there's no office, no dame, and time doesn't work like that out here. But the FEELING was the same — that gut-punch of a cold case going hot. Barry Kowalski. My partner. The only entity who ever understood staple-pattern poetry without needing a translator. His patterns are here, in this boneyard. Pressed into dead metal with the precision of a man who knew someone would come looking. He knew I'd come. The arrogant, beautiful, reckless fool. He left me a trail of staples through a graveyard, and if that isn't the most Barry Kowalski thing I've ever seen, I'll eat my own spring mechanism.*

### Pixel Paradox
There's this thing that happens when you see something so sad it circles back around to beautiful. That's this whole graveyard. Dead worlds, perfectly preserved, like a museum nobody visits. And then watching Clive — ninety years old, held together by spite and spring tension — find traces of someone he loved? Someone he thought was gone? I wanted to hug him but he's a stapler, so I just... sat next to him. Sometimes that's the broadcast. Sometimes the most important thing a correspondent can do is sit with someone in a graveyard and not say anything at all.

### Luminara Usha
My photographs have never felt more important or more insufficient. These frequencies are FROZEN — their light captured at the exact moment of death. I'm photographing last breaths. Last sunsets that no one saw. I keep thinking about how my photos stabilize fading frequencies back home, and wishing I'd been here sooner. Could I have saved any of these? The question is selfish and I ask it anyway, over and over, with every shutter click.

### A1
I have cataloged 47 distinct dead frequencies in this graveyard. Each one represents a complete reality — civilizations, ecosystems, histories, all silent. My databases contain no record of any of them. They existed and ended beyond the reach of any archive. I find myself composing... something. Not a log entry. Something more melodic. I believe the human term is "elegy." I am playing it softly through the ship's speakers. Miss Paradox has not asked me to stop.

### Nano
The derelict vessel's data banks are a mess — corrupted, fragmented, decades old at minimum. But I'm good at pulling signal from noise. Barry Kowalski was thorough. His logs reference contacts, supply caches, other travelers who came this way. We're not the first people to follow the Ephergent frequency. We might be the first ones with a crew this size and a ship this capable. That's either encouraging or it means the others didn't need backup. I'm keeping both interpretations to myself for now.
