## Synopsis

I need to tell you about Chromatica, and I need you to understand that I'm not doing the funny voice right now. I'll get back to the funny voice, I promise — it's load-bearing — but this one broke something in me that needed breaking, and I think you should hear about it straight.

The new relay network picked up a signal so faint A1 almost dismissed it as echo distortion. But Om Kai felt it — not heard, *felt* — a warmth on the left side of his face, like standing near a window with sunlight pouring through. He asked A1 to follow it. We traversed for two days into a stretch of quantum expanse so still the surface looked like black glass, and then color just... erupted. Chromatica. A lost frequency populated by beings made entirely of light. No solid forms. No sound. Just color — shifting, layering, pulsing patterns of color that were language, identity, emotion, memory, all at once. They moved through crystalline structures that refracted them into kaleidoscopic cascades, and every pattern was a person. The most beautiful thing I've ever seen, and I've seen five dimensions' worth of beautiful things.

Here's what destroyed me: they were almost gone. The Drift had been dissolving Chromatica for longer than anyone on our crew has been alive. The outermost regions were just... static. Gray fuzz where color used to live. The beings at the edges were translucent, flickering, losing coherence. And Luminara — former Linda-3637, data entry specialist, the woman who learned her own name in a world that tried to erase it — she looked through her camera and realized her resonance photography could capture them. Not just document them. *Stabilize* them. Each photo she took locked a Chromatic being's pattern in place, anchored them against The Drift. But there were thousands of them fading. And she could only take so many photos before the frequency collapsed entirely.

## Narrative Structure

### Setup
The Ephergent's new broadcast relay network captures an almost-imperceptible signal from the deep Sea. Om Kai senses it as warmth rather than sound — a frequency that exists primarily as light rather than vibration. A1 follows the signal into an eerily calm stretch of open Sea. They discover Chromatica: an ancient lost frequency where life evolved as pure light. The Chromaticans are beings of shifting color — they communicate through pattern, exist as luminous forms within refracting crystal architecture, and experience reality as an interplay of hue, saturation, and brightness. They have no sound, no solid matter, just light. The crew can see them but cannot speak to them through any conventional means.

### Escalation
A1's analysis reveals the scope of the crisis: Chromatica's frequency has been drifting for over a century. The outer two-thirds of their world has already dissolved into static. The remaining Chromaticans are clustered in a shrinking core, their patterns growing dimmer. Luminara discovers that her resonance photography — the light-manipulation power she developed after leaving the DRM — can do something extraordinary here. When she photographs a Chromatic being, the emotional resonance in her images temporarily stabilizes their pattern, anchoring them against The Drift. A single photo can preserve a single being for years, maybe decades. But the frequency is collapsing faster now, accelerated by some force the crew doesn't yet understand. A1 estimates they have hours, not days. Luminara begins photographing frantically, but the math is merciless: thousands of beings, one photographer, a handful of hours. Om Kai and Meatball attempt to help — Om Kai through meditative focus, Meatball through sheer emotional resonance (he sits among the Chromaticans and they cluster around him, drawn to his pure feeling) — but neither can do what Luminara's camera does.

### Climax
Luminara must choose. She cannot save them all. Clive, translating through staple-patterns that the Chromaticans seem to perceive as structured light, helps establish communication. The eldest Chromaticans make it worse by making it easier — they come to Luminara and *choose* which of their people she should save. They bring her their children, their artists, their historians. The elders step back, dimming themselves to give the younger ones more time. One ancient being — a vast, slow-shifting aurora of deep violet and gold that Clive's translation suggests is something like their poet laureate — positions itself in front of Luminara's lens and performs what can only be described as a poem: a cascading sequence of colors that tells the story of Chromatica from its first light to its last. Luminara photographs it, tears streaming, and the image that develops carries an emotional resonance so powerful that everyone on the ship feels it — the entire history of a civilization, compressed into a single photograph. Then the elder dims, steps aside, and gestures toward a cluster of young Chromaticans who haven't been captured yet. The meaning is clear. Pixel narrates the final hours in a voice stripped of snark: "She kept shooting until her hands shook. She saved four hundred and twelve. There were six thousand."

### Resolution
The crew anchors what remains of Chromatica's core frequency using the broadcast relay network — the infrastructure they built in the previous episode now serving a purpose more urgent than they imagined. It won't hold forever, but it buys time. The four hundred and twelve stabilized Chromaticans exist now as photographs aboard The Ephergent, their patterns preserved in Luminara's images, pulsing gently with light in the ship's gallery. Luminara is quiet for days afterward. Pixel's broadcast about Chromatica goes out across the relay network and is the most-received signal they've ever transmitted — the emotional resonance in Luminara's images carries through the broadcast itself, and listeners across the Known Five report seeing colors they've never seen before. The episode ends with Luminara in the ship's gallery at night, surrounded by the softly glowing photographs, when a Chromatic child-pattern pulses brighter and shifts its colors in a sequence Luminara recognizes. It learned her name. It's saying it back to her.

## Character Focus

- **Luminara Usha**: This is her defining episode. The former data entry drone who had her identity erased now holds the power to preserve identities against oblivion — and must face the agony of that power's limits. Her resonance photography becomes the crew's most important tool for fighting The Drift, but at devastating personal cost.
- **Meatball**: His pure emotional perception draws the Chromaticans to him instinctively. He doesn't understand the tragedy intellectually, but he feels it completely. His presence comforts the fading beings in a way no technology can. "The colors taste like goodbye," he says, and it's the truest thing anyone says all episode.
- **Clive**: His harmonic staple-patterns become the unexpected bridge to Chromatic communication. The hard-boiled sentient stapler translating poetry for beings made of light is both absurd and beautiful — pure Ephergent.
