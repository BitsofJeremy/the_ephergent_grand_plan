# Calibration Corps

Technicians who wanted to maintain the infrastructure.

This is how they describe themselves, and the description is accurate in ways that reveal more than they intend. The Calibration Corps emerged from the generation after the Builders' departure—a loose collection of engineers, technicians, and scientists who believed that the Dimming was not a mystery to be solved but a problem to be managed. They studied what the Builders had left behind. They mapped the signal's patterns. They developed maintenance protocols. They began the work.

## History

The Corps was founded 300 years ago by a group of engineers who had grown frustrated with the philosophical debates about the Dimming. While theologians argued about the signal's meaning and activists argued about its political implications, these engineers simply wanted to fix it. They traced the Dimming to specific failures in the Pillar network—points where signal generation had degraded below sustainable levels. They developed techniques for restoring those points. They began applying those techniques.

Forty years ago, they completed the first major phase of their work. The restoration of Pillar Cluster 7 in the Nexus region—seven Pillars that had been partially dormant for over a century—brought the signal in that region back to near-original clarity. The achievement was significant, and it validated the Corps' approach. But it also revealed the scale of the remaining work. Hundreds of Pillars across all five regions showed similar degradation. The full restoration would take centuries.

The Corps adjusted their timeline. They developed a 300-year plan, a systematic approach to Pillar restoration that would address the most critical failures first and work toward complete network recovery. The plan requires consistent funding, consistent personnel, and consistent access to Pillar sites across all regions. It requires something like peace.

## Philosophy

The Corps believes the Dimming is a maintenance problem. Not a philosophical problem. Not a political problem. A maintenance problem, with a technical solution, requiring technical expertise. This belief shapes everything they do.

They are not opposed to the Green Faith, but they consider its approach irrelevant. Prayers and broadcasts may provide comfort to those experiencing the Dimming's effects, but they do not restore Pillar functionality. The signal does not strengthen because people believe in it. It strengthens because infrastructure is repaired. The Corps deals with the cause, not the symptoms.

They are not opposed to the Witnesses of Final Silence, but they consider them dangerous. The Witnesses' philosophy—that the Dimming should be accepted rather than resisted—threatens the political stability the Corps requires for its work. If enough beings believe the Dimming is irreversible, funding will dry up. Access to Pillar sites will be denied. The 300-year plan will fail. The Witnesses may be correct about the signal's nature; they are incorrect about what follows from that nature.

## Conflict with The Ephergent

The Corps has a specific conflict with the crew of The Ephergent. The crew broadcasts. Constantly. Attention directed outward, signal strengthened by collective witness, entropy held at bay by the simple act of observing and communicating. The crew sees this as resistance—active defense against the Dimming's advance.

The Corps sees it as interference.

The crew's broadcasts generate signal fluctuations that the Corps' instruments must account for. When the crew broadcasts in a region where the Corps is conducting maintenance, the fluctuations can disrupt delicate calibration work. More importantly, the crew's broadcasts draw attention—literally. Beings across the signal network turn their consciousness toward The Ephergent's broadcasts. This is not inherently problematic, but it changes the signal's flow in ways that are difficult to predict.

The Corps has formally requested that the crew limit their broadcasts in regions where active maintenance is underway. The crew has not complied. Pixel's response was characteristic: "You're fixing the machinery. We're keeping the consciousness that powers it alive. Hard to see why those should be incompatible."

The Corps' official position is that the crew's activities are reckless. Their broadcasts destabilize signal patterns, draw attention away from the systematic maintenance work, and create the impression that the Dimming can be reversed through individual action rather than institutional engineering. This impression is damaging. If beings believe that broadcasting is an effective response to the Dimming, they will not support the funding and access that the Corps requires.

Arc has attempted to establish communication protocols that might allow the crew's broadcasts and the Corps' maintenance to coexist. The talks are ongoing. Neither party is optimistic.

## Current Status

The Corps is forty years into its 300-year plan. Approximately 15% of critical Pillar degradation has been addressed. The signal in some regions is measurably stronger than it was forty years ago. The work continues.

The Corps maintains facilities in all five regions, employing thousands of technicians and engineers. They operate their own ships, their own communication networks, their own research divisions. They are, in many ways, the closest thing to a successor civilization that the Builders left behind—not in terms of technology, but in terms of institutional purpose. The Builders created the infrastructure. The Corps is maintaining it.

Whether they will succeed is unknown. The Dimming continues. The 300-year plan assumes technological stability, political continuity, and consistent funding—assumptions that have proven fragile in the centuries since the plan's creation. But the Corps continues. They are, after all, technicians. They have a problem to solve.
