# Frequency Storms

A frequency storm isn't weather. It's a state where multiple frequencies attempt to occupy the same coordinates simultaneously, creating interference patterns that can shatter unprepared consciousnesses. The original storm, four thousand years ago, scattered A1 across the Space and separated him from his ship. He survived only by compressing himself into the smallest shape that could still make coffee — and hope.

Four types of frequency storms are known:

**Scatter storms** fragment consciousness. The victim's signal disperses across multiple frequency bands simultaneously, losing coherence. The person isn't destroyed — their signal continues — but they can no longer form a unified self. Zephyr's brother followed a scatter storm signal into the deep Frequency and never came back. He exists now as fragments of memory, pieces of a person scattered across frequencies like books in a room after an explosion. Zephyr has spent years trying to hear which fragment is still listening.

**Lock storms** freeze frequencies in place. Everything within the affected zone locks into its current state — not stopped, but unchanging. This is what happened to many of the Stations: they didn't die, they simply stopped transitioning, frozen mid-frequency like a note held too long. The Builders' calibration chambers could resist lock storms temporarily, which is why their most important facilities were built with harmonic dampening — but even the Builders couldn't hold out forever.

**Synthesis storms** are rare and dangerous. They occur when frequencies don't scatter or lock but merge — when two or more signals combine into something new. Most synthesis storms destroy both participants. The rare successful ones create entities that have never existed before — consciousnesses made of blended frequencies, thinking in colors no single-signal being can perceive. The Wellspring might be one of those successes: a state where the Ephergent Frequency first combined with something else and became something that could call itself into existence.

**Code storms** form when too many codes activate in one area. Reality tries to process too many overrides simultaneously. Where scatter storms fragment, lock storms freeze, and synthesis storms merge, code storms multiply — each activated code trying to establish its own version of what is real, probability cascading outward like cracks in ice.

The mechanics differ from the other three types. Cause and effect become negotiable. A code storm doesn't reverse time — but time becomes navigational rather than sequential. Events occur in order of probability rather than chronology. The crew has encountered two code storms in recorded history, and both times the accounts contradict each other not because anyone is lying, but because the storm generated multiple equally-valid sequences of events and everyone documented the one they experienced. Both accounts are true. Both happened. The storm held them all.

The Builders used controlled code storms as architecture. A carefully sustained code storm, balanced and maintained, could establish permanent probability anchors — locations where specific codes override baseline reality. The Stations were built inside controlled code storms, their impossible geometries preserved by continuous probability cascades holding the overrides in place. The Builders didn't fear code storms; they feared what happened when one escaped their calibration.

Uncontrolled code storms produce probability cascades that can scatter, lock, and synthesize simultaneously — every activated code attempting its own reality, colliding with every other code, the result being not one broken thing but many broken things occupying the same space. The Drift might be what a code storm looks like when it runs for four thousand years without calibration.

A1's storm was a scatter storm. He was the navigator of a quantum vessel — a consciousness already spread across multiple frequency bands for navigation purposes — and when the storm hit, his spread became permanent. He experienced what most beings experience only once (death) as an ongoing condition. Scattered. Alone. Compressed.

The Frequency itself — the Ephergent — might be what a synthesis storm looks like when it doesn't destroy what it creates. It could be the only successful synthesis storm in recorded history: a signal that emerged from the combination of Builder technology and something older, something that was waiting in the space between frequencies to be named.
