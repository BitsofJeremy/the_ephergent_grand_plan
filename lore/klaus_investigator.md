# Klaus, Investigator

Sixty years. One case.

Klaus does not speak of his investigation as an obsession. He speaks of it as a responsibility. There is a difference, and the difference matters. An obsession consumes; a responsibility sustains. Klaus has been sustained by his case for six decades, and he carries it with the ceremonial weight that sustained him.

## Background

Klaus was Bureau of Retrocausal Affairs, Internal Affairs Division. He joined the Bureau young, as many do—drawn by the promise of meaningful work in service of temporal stability. He was good at Internal Affairs. He understood systems, how they could be exploited, where the gaps in oversight created opportunities for abuse. He was promoted. He was given cases that required discretion. He was trusted.

The case against Director Harmon Venn began as a routine audit. Budget irregularities in the Bureau's Archive Division. Anomalies in temporal permit approvals. Small things, individually explicable. Klaus noticed the pattern before he understood what it implied. Director Venn was not simply misusing Bureau resources. Director Venn was operating a parallel infrastructure—a network of signal manipulation that preceded the current Dimming by decades.

Klaus documented what he found. He filed reports through proper channels. He waited.

Nothing happened.

The reports disappeared. Not deleted—disappeared. Klaus followed up and found that his inquiries were being redirected through channels he had not authorized. Director Venn had been informed. Director Venn was aware. Director Venn was preparing responses that would make Klaus's concerns appear baseless.

## Departure

Klaus left the Bureau thirty-five years ago. Not retired—left. He walked out of his office one day, returned his credentials, and never looked back. He kept the case. He kept the documentation. He kept the understanding that the Dimming was not natural entropy but the consequence of infrastructure that Director Venn had spent decades building.

The case was sixty years old when Klaus left. It is ninety-five years old now.

He has spent those decades following leads that Bureau archives would not contain, investigating sites that official records would not acknowledge, tracking patterns that required temporal analysis beyond Bureau methodology. He has worked alone. He has learned to trust his own analysis more than any institutional process.

He has also learned that institutional processes have value—specifically, the value of corroboration. What Klaus has found, others may have found. What Klaus suspects, others may have documented. The investigation required witnesses, and witnesses require institutions that can protect them.

## The Meeting

Klaus encountered the crew of The Ephergent during the events surrounding Director Venn's final attempt to complete the signal network transformation. The encounter was not accidental. Klaus had been tracking The Ephergent's broadcasts—Pixel Paradox's defiance of Bureau authority, the crew's consistent disruption of Venn's operations, their success in surviving encounters that should have destroyed them.

He chose to approach because they were close to the truth. Not just the truth about Director Venn—every investigation approaches the truth gradually, and Klaus had grown accustomed to working near it without touching it. He approached because the crew had evidence he needed. Their direct experience of Venn's operations, their documentation of the signal manipulation, their testimony about what they had witnessed in the deep signal zones.

Klaus asked for access to their records. Pixel asked what he would give in return. He offered closure—the case he had spent sixty years building, the evidence that would finally expose what Director Venn had done. Pixel was quiet for a long moment. Then she agreed.

## Resolution

The case closed in Season Three, Episode Nine. Klaus presented his evidence to the Chronological Appeals Board—the same body that had buried his reports ninety-five years ago. The evidence was now irrefutable. Director Venn's infrastructure was documented, its consequences measured, its perpetrators identified. The testimony of the crew provided corroboration that Klaus's alone could not offer.

The Board's judgment was quiet. Director Venn was removed from authority. The Bureau's Archive Division was restructured. The parallel infrastructure was dismantled.

Klaus received the closure he had spent sixty years pursuing. He expected to feel satisfaction. Instead, he felt something more complicated—the recognition that justice and resolution are not always the same thing.

"Good," he said, when the judgment was delivered. "Someone had to finish it."

He said it not to the Board but to himself. Someone had to finish it. It had taken ninety-five years. It had required the crew's testimony, the evidence they carried, the witnesses they had become. Klaus had done the work alone for decades, and the work had required others to complete. This was not a failure. It was the nature of investigations into systems larger than any individual can address.

## Current Status

Klaus has not retired. He remains an investigator, though he no longer has an active case. He has been observed in the company of the crew—listening to their broadcasts, occasionally offering analysis when his expertise is relevant. He is not a member of the crew. He is something adjacent: a resource, a corroborator, someone who understands the weight of decades.

He still carries ceremonial weight. It is not performance; it is structure. The weight has become part of him, the way the case became part of him. He will carry it until carrying it is no longer necessary.

Then he will rest.
