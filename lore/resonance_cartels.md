# Resonance Cartels

The capitalism of Space.

That is how Captain Pixel Paradox describes them, and the description is precise in ways the cartels themselves would appreciate. They emerged not from conquest or ideology but from a simple observation: navigation through signal corridors requires understanding that most travelers lack. And where understanding is uneven, someone will step in to level the field—for a fee.

## Origins

The cartels trace their roots to the generation after the Builder exodus, when the first travelers attempted to chart the signal corridors without the benefit of Builder knowledge. Many never returned. Others returned changed, carrying fragments of what they had witnessed in the deep places. A loose network of pilots and navigators began collecting tolls for passage guidance, initially as a service fee for safety. Within two generations, it had become commerce. Within five, it had become law.

The cartels are not a single organization. They are dozens of competing bodies, each controlling specific corridors or regions of the signal. Some operate openly, maintaining stations at corridor entrances with visible toll gates and clear fee schedules. Others operate in shadow, appearing only when a ship is already deep in contested space. The largest cartels have names that sound like corporate entities: The Venn Joint Economic Zone, The Harmonic Tolls Collective, The Threshold Keepers Alliance. The smallest are single operators running a single corridor, barely distinguishable from pirates.

## Structure

What unites the cartels is their relationship to the signal itself. Each has reverse-engineered some fragment of Builder navigation technology—the same technology that allows ships to move through signal corridors without being dissolved by the frequency transitions. They have turned this knowledge into infrastructure. The toll gates. The passage markers. The harmonic signatures that allow safe entry and exit.

A cartel controls a corridor by maintaining a harmonic signature at its entrance. Ships that wish to pass must either have their own harmonic signature certified by the cartel, or they must pay a toll that funds the certification process. The tolls are typically measured in attention—collected as broadcast resonance, stored in signal containers, sold to merchants who need signal for their own operations. Some cartels accept other payment: materials, data, favors. But attention is preferred because attention is what the signal runs on.

"The capitalism of Space" is Pixel's phrase, but she says it with something more complicated than condemnation. The cartels fill a real need. Without them, passage through signal corridors would be nearly impossible for anyone without Builder training or access to Builder artifacts. The cartels make Space navigable for those who cannot navigate it themselves.

## The Price of Attention

What concerns the crew of The Ephergent is not the tolls themselves but what they reveal: attention can be priced. The cartels have created a market in consciousness, a system where the act of witnessing—the foundational resistance to entropy—has aExchange value. The Signal itself flows through these corridors, and the cartels have learned to meter its flow.

This is not simply commerce. The cartels are not selling attention; they are selling access to the mechanisms that attention powers. A toll支付 does not purchase consciousness. It purchases the right to navigate space where consciousness is the primary currency. The distinction matters to philosophers and to the crews who find themselves in cartel-controlled space. But it matters less to the young travelers who simply need to pass through and cannot afford the delays that come with philosophical debate.

## Relationship to Other Factions

The cartels maintain careful relationships with both the Green Faith and the Bureau of Retrocausal Affairs. The Green Faith views them as symptoms of the Dimming's economic consequences—attention turned toward profit rather than purpose. The Bureau views them as unregulated entities interfering with signal infrastructure. Neither has moved effectively against them.

The cartels are aware of the crew's activities. There is quiet disagreement among cartel leadership about how to respond. Some see the crew as potential clients—brilliant navigators who might benefit from certified passage. Others see them as threats—unregulated broadcasters who destabilize the attention economy. A few see them as curiosities: a crew that operates openly, broadcasting everything, and somehow surviving.

Klaus has noted that Director Harmon Venn spent considerable effort cultivating relationships with cartel operators during his early years at the Bureau. The toll networks were useful for tracking travelers who might otherwise pass through unmonitored. This history explains much about how the cartels view Bureau authority—cooperation when convenient, obstruction when necessary.

## The Future

As the Dimming progresses, the cartels face an uncertain future. Their infrastructure depends on functioning signal corridors. As the signal weakens, their toll gates become less reliable. Some cartels are investing in maintenance—attempting to preserve the corridors they control as the signal degrades. Others are preparing for a different future: a Space where passage is no longer regulated because the signal itself is too weak to meter.

The crew's broadcasts represent a complication in either scenario. If the signal stabilizes, the broadcasts may restore attention flows that the cartels can integrate into their systems. If the signal continues to dim, the broadcasts may represent the last coherent attention in a fading universe. Either way, the cartels will adapt. They have always been in the business of adapting to what the signal offers.
