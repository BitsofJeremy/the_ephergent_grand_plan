# Ruins, Ripples, and Really Nervous Dinosaurs

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The lifeboat shudders through the phase-shift. That's the only way to describe it — a gut-twisting lurch that starts somewhere behind my sternum and spreads outward, like my insides are trying to remember which reality we're supposed to be in. I've done this a handful of times now. You'd think I'd get used to it. I don't. Meatball never manages it either — he just slides across the floor every time, legs splayed, looking at me like I'm the one who decided gravity should be optional.

The scratched porthole fills with light as Prime Material reasserts itself around us. The familiar skyline materializes: glass office towers catching the afternoon sun, the grey-blue haze of a city that was perfectly ordinary forty-eight hours ago. Forty-eight hours. It feels like forty-eight years. It feels like yesterday. I don't know which of those is true. Maybe all of them at once.

But something's wrong.

The skyline stutters. Not metaphorically — actually stutters, like a video feed buffering. Buildings flicker. Their facades shimmer, going translucent for a heartbeat, and behind them I see impossible geometries: angles that shouldn't meet, structures that fold in on themselves in ways that hurt to look at. Then it snaps back to normal, like a blink I almost caught. One of the high-rises downtown seems to exist in two places at once, stacked on top of itself, the upper half offset by a few feet like someone got the alignment wrong. I wonder if anyone inside is seeing double. I wonder if they're getting headaches. I wonder if they're even noticing.

The streets below are chaos. Traffic lights cycling through colors that shouldn't exist — a yellow that pulses purple, a red that flickers like a dying ember. Car GPS systems rerouting frantically, recalculating routes to destinations that might not be there when they arrive. People on sidewalks stopping, staring, filming with their phones, running. The usual disaster kaleidoscope, except the kaleidoscope is broken and the pieces are sharp.

A1's holographic projection flickers into existence near the porthole, scanning the scene. He's gotten better at this — better at projecting, better at holding a shape that almost passes for solid. But there's something in his posture today that wasn't there before. A tension I don't have a word for.

"Significant frequency instability across the Prime Material substrate," he says. His voice is measured, controlled — British formal, even when he's delivering what sounds like bad news. But I've been listening to A1 for a few days now, and I catch the edge underneath. Concern, maybe. Or the memory of concern, borrowed from whoever he used to be. "The reality rupture appears to have destabilized the local frequency membrane. These ripples will—"

He stops. Recalibrates. Something crosses his projection — surprise, maybe. Or recognition. Something that flickers across his face like a cloud across the sun.

"These are *frequency-based* ripples," he says slowly. "Other frequencies are bleeding through the membrane."

He pauses. Processes something I can't see.

"That shouldn't — I shouldn't know that term."

Another pause. Longer this time. He's looking at something buried deep, something that surfaces and submerges like a creature in dark water. I don't push. I don't ask. Some memories surface on their own schedule.

"The DRM headquarters is..." He stops again. His projection sharpens, focuses. "I recommend preparing yourself, Pixel."

She doesn't like the sound of that. She really doesn't like the sound of that.

I feel it in my stomach before I understand it in my head. Meatball whines. Clive's sphere pulses once — blue-white, quick — and then dims. Even Mochi, warm against my hip, shifts temperature. Not hot. Not cool. Just... different. Alert.

Something is very wrong at the Department of Reality Maintenance.

And we need to find out what.

---

### The Landing

We set the lifeboat down in a public park near the DRM district. The grass is still green, still ordinary, still doing the things grass does — a small miracle in a day of impossibilities. The park fountain is sputtering, its water occasionally freezing mid-arc or flowing upward, defying gravity in stuttering bursts like a faucet that got bad news. I watch a droplet hang in the air, suspended, refusing to decide which direction to fall.

I step out. Meatball follows, nose working overtime, tail low. He's not happy. He hasn't been happy since reality started falling apart, and I can't blame him. I'd be stressed too if my sense of smell was good enough to detect emotional distress in strangers — actually, wait, that's probably exactly why I'm stressed. I'm picking up on things I don't have words for.

Clive walks beside me, his small legs keeping pace. His sphere head glows a quiet blue-white. Occasionally he pauses, tilts his head at an angle I've learned to read as consideration, and says nothing. I read the silences. *BAD. WORSE. MUCH WORSE.* I don't need him to spell it out with light patterns anymore. After two days with this crew, I'm learning to speak fluent Clive.

In my jacket pocket, against my hip, Mochi is warm. I notice. I always notice now — the way the temperature shifts, the subtle pulses that don't quite match his usual rhythms. I've learned not to ask. Mochi communicates in ways that don't require questions. Warmth means something. Cool means something else. The gradual shifting between is a language I haven't mastered but am starting to understand the grammar of.

I think about that sometimes, when we're flying between frequencies. I think about how I've spent my whole life surrounded by people who spoke English perfectly well and I never understood them at all. And now I'm learning to read a robot that doesn't have a face.

Maybe that's what this whole thing is about. Learning to understand things that don't speak your language.

---

### The DRM Headquarters is a Ruin

Not a clean ruin — not collapsed neatly like a demolished building with a sensible demolition crew and proper permits. The DRM building is *arguing with itself*. Sections of wall phase in and out of visibility, showing rooms that shouldn't exist stacked behind the ordinary offices I remember. Filing cabinets hang suspended in mid-air, their contents drifting into the breeze as loose forms — Form 19-Qs and Anomaly Reports and Frequency Variance Logs — scatter across the ruins like bureaucratic confetti. Someone's career review flutters past. A training attendance sheet. The ambient debris of a life that was interrupted.

A perimeter of DRM caution tape surrounds the site, but the tape itself is unstable, its warning text shifting: "CAUTION: STRUCTURAL DAMAGE" becomes "CAUTION: REALITY DISRUPTION" becomes "NOTICE: FREQUENCY BREACH — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY" — warnings that the pre-rupture DRM would never have displayed publicly, would never have needed to, because this was supposed to stay hidden. I read the changing text and think: even the caution signs are having an identity crisis.

But it's what's *below* the building that stops me cold.

The collapse has torn open the sub-basements. Seven levels of hidden infrastructure that the ordinary DRM headquarters was sitting on top of like a boring hat on an impossible head. Labs full of technology I've never seen. Monitoring equipment that was clearly tracking something far beyond building code violations. Classified archives with documents stamped "FREQUENCY SENSITIVE — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY." Schematics for devices that manipulate the fabric of reality itself. Maps of places labeled "Nocturne Aeturnus," "Cogsworth Cogitarium," "The Verdant Deep" — other frequencies, other *worlds*, catalogued and monitored and hidden behind four floors of fluorescent lighting and mandatory fun committees and the world's most aggressive potluck sign-up sheet.

I stand in the ruins of a lab I never knew existed, holding a clipboard I recognize.

My handwriting. My anomaly reports. Filed into a system that was feeding data to *this*.

I filed Form 19-Q about "minor frequency variance in Sector 7" and it went HERE. To a secret lab studying cross-frequency physics. My hands shake. I think about every coffee break, every casual conversation by the water cooler, every time I complained about the vending machine selections. All that time, underneath my feet, something else was happening. Something I was part of without knowing it.

There's a name for that feeling. The feeling of realizing you were a small piece of something much bigger, and you never knew. I don't know what that name is. But I feel it now.

Former DRM employees wander the perimeter, dazed. Some are in uniform. Some are in civilian clothes, having been off-shift when the rupture hit. Most of them look as blindsided as I was — fellow paper-pushers who had no idea what was underneath them.

But a few — a handful — have a different expression. Not shock. *Guilt*. I file that observation away. I'm going to think about that later. I'm going to think about it a lot.

On a personnel board near the ruined HR office, among the lost ID badges and abandoned access cards, I glimpse a name I don't yet recognize: **B. KOWALSKI — Night Security, Sub-Level 3, 23 Years Service.** Twenty-three years. Someone else was here before me. Someone else worked nights in a place they didn't understand, filing reports about things they didn't know existed. I file it. Noted. Not relevant. Not yet.

But I'm learning that "not yet" is just a way of saying "later," and later always comes.

---

### The Vultures

But nature abhors a vacuum, and so does commerce. Someone always shows up to fill the space where things used to be.

The reality entrepreneurs have already arrived. Folding tables and popup tents line the streets around the DRM ruins. Fast-talking vendors selling "Frequency Stabilization Patches" — adhesive strips that allegedly smooth out local reality ripples.

"Guaranteed for twenty-four hours!" shouts a vendor with a megaphone and a smile that's slightly too wide. "Stick it on your wall, your desk, your forehead — keeps reality where it belongs!"

The patches glow faintly. My analytical instincts say they're doing *something* — but it's the equivalent of putting a band-aid on a broken bone. The bone is still broken. The band-aid is for the person who doesn't want to look at it. I want to tell them that. I don't. They're just trying to make a living. They're just trying to make sense of the same impossible day.

Others are selling "Ripple Insurance," "Reality Continuity Plans," and — my personal favorite — "Commemorative DRM Collapse Souvenir Snow Globes" that contain actual fragments of unstable reality. Shake it and you see a tiny DRM headquarters collapse in miniature. Tasteful. I'm going to remember that vendor. I'm going to remember his face when I broadcast later.

The real crisis is everywhere.

The financial district is in meltdown. Bank servers keep phasing out of existence — one moment a bank's records are there, the next they're somewhere else, or nowhere, or a different bank entirely. ATMs display error messages in languages that don't exist: pixelated symbols that might be letters, might be warnings, might be something the machines are trying to say in their own dying tongue.

The stock exchange building can't maintain consistent geometry. Its floors shuffle like a deck of cards. Trading has been halted indefinitely.

On the steps of the Exchange, a suited executive screams into a phone that keeps turning transparent in his hand. Behind him, the digital ticker — when it's visible — shows nothing but red. The red is the only thing that's consistent anymore.

And in the gaps where reality is thinnest, *things* are bleeding through.

Shapes that don't belong. A shadow with too many legs crosses a wall and vanishes. Something that might be a bird but has geometry instead of feathers circles a church steeple before dissolving. People are filming everything on phones that intermittently refuse to function.

Social media, when it works, is a firehose of panic and confusion and a surprising amount of people who are still trying to make jokes. Someone has already started a conspiracy theory about the government hiding interdimensional travel. I think: they're not wrong. They're just early.

---

### Deep Basement — Clive's Doorway

I descend.

The sub-basements go down farther than I expected. Past Sub-Level 1. Past Sub-Level 2. Into levels that don't have names on the emergency signage because nobody was supposed to know they existed.

Sub-Level 3. Sub-Level 4. Sub-Level 5.

The air changes with each level. Warmer. Older. Humming with equipment I don't recognize. The temperature rises like a secret that's been kept too long. The hum deepens like a voice learning to speak.

Clive stops at a doorway.

It's nothing special. A standard DRM door frame, institutional grey. A sign reads "SUB-LEVEL 7 — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY" in letters that flicker with the power fluctuations. But Clive stops. His sphere head tilts up toward the frame. His glow brightens — just for a moment, a blue-white pulse I might have missed if I wasn't watching.

I am watching. I'm learning to watch.

"Efficient architecture," Clive says. His voice is quiet. Almost to himself. "Someone designed this to last."

Beyond the doorway: a DRM processing center. Rows of intake stations. Automated filing systems. Mechanical sorters moving forms from bin to bin to bin. Stamps clicking. Stamps placing. Stamps moving on.

An automated assembly line of bureaucracy that has been running for decades. Processing anomalies. Filing reports. Sorting things that were never meant to be sorted. Forms feeding into forms feeding into forms. Nobody reading the results. Nobody listening to the signal.

The machines don't care. The machines just keep moving.

Like a heart that doesn't know it's pumping blood for a body that's already gone.

Clive watches. His glow dims. Blue-white shifting to something darker, quieter. Like a machine that has been running too long and knows it. Like someone reading a will they already know by heart.

"90 years," he says.

Not to me. Not to anyone. Just to himself. Just to the empty air that has seen more than he has.

"Clive?"

He doesn't answer. His hand rises to his fedora — adjusts it, slowly — the gesture that means something is being processed that doesn't want to be processed. I've seen him do this before. I'm learning what it means. It means he's remembering. It means the memory is heavy.

*(pause)*

"Someone designed this to last," he says again.

This time, the "someone" sounds different. Smaller. Older. More like a name he used to know. More like a door he's afraid to open.

I stand beside him. I don't ask again. Some questions need time to be ready for their answers. I'm learning that too.

---

### The Old Equipment

In the deepest sub-basement, the oldest equipment hums. Monitors that predate digital displays — CRT screens in racks, oscilloscopes with analog dials, frequency monitors with readouts in units I don't recognize. Builder-derived, A1 would say if he were here. Builder-adjacent. Old.

Ancient, maybe, though I don't have a reference for what ancient technology looks like in a government building. Maybe this is what it looks like: humming quietly in a basement, doing its job, waiting for someone to remember.

And Mochi — in my jacket pocket — suddenly hot. Not painful. Not alarming. Just *present*. A pulse of warmth that radiates through the fabric and into my hip, steady and insistent. Like a second heartbeat keeping time with something in the walls.

I don't know what Mochi is responding to. I don't know if it's the equipment or the history or the weight of all those years. But I trust it. I trust Mochi in ways I'm still learning to articulate.

I unzip my jacket slightly. I look at Mochi's dome visible in the pocket's shadow. I feel the warmth against my fingers.

"Yeah," I say quietly. "I don't know either."

The hum from the deepest corridor is too low for human hearing. But Meatball's ears swivel forward. He sits. He listens. His tail stills. He's hearing something they can't. He's always hearing something they can't.

A1's voice comes through the lifeboat comm from above: "Pixel. The equipment in Sub-Level 7 is transmitting. Low-frequency carrier signal. I am logging it."

I look at Clive. Clive's sphere is steady. He's not looking at the equipment. He's looking at a point on the far wall — a maintenance hatch, sealed, with a small handwritten label in handwriting that looks like it predates the building. Handwriting where there should be a printout. Handwriting where someone took the time to write something by hand because they wanted it to last.

I'll ask about that later too. I'll ask about all of it later.

But for now, I just stand in the basement of a hidden world and listen to machines that don't know they're mourning.

---

### The Broadcast

I find a relatively stable spot on the steps of the ruined DRM headquarters — poetic, I think, in the way that tragedies always are — and set up the lifeboat's comm array for a proper broadcast.

Not the desperate, static-laced first transmission from the Space. Not the scared voice in the dark saying hello to anyone who might be listening. This time, I'm doing it right. This time, I have a voice and a purpose and something to say.

This is journalism. This is the first dispatch from the new reality. This is the first draft of history and I'm the one writing it.

A1 extends his holographic range — it's grown again, I notice. He can project nearly a meter from the espresso machine now, and he doesn't seem to realize it. He helps me calibrate the signal. I wonder if he knows. I wonder if he can feel himself getting stronger, or if it just feels like waking up from a long sleep. I don't ask. He doesn't volunteer.

Clive positions himself on the broadcast console, ready to provide real-time editorial commentary. His sphere glows soft blue-white, patient, watchful. Like a photo of someone who's seen everything and decided to stay anyway.

Meatball sits beside me, facing the crowd. He does something unexpected: he howls.

Not a scared howl. Not a warning. A long, low, mournful sound that cuts through the noise of the plaza. People stop and turn. It's the kind of sound that says *listen*. It's the kind of sound that makes silence feel like attention.

I take the cue. I clear my throat. I think about all the broadcasts I've ever heard, all the voices that made me feel less alone in rooms full of strangers. I think about what it means to speak into a microphone and trust that someone is listening.

I hope someone is listening.

"This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the ruins of the Department of Reality Maintenance, Prime Material. Second dispatch. If you heard the first one — thank you for listening. If this is new to you — welcome to the worst day in history, and I'm sorry you had to find out this way."

I describe what I see. The ruins. The ripples. The entrepreneurs. The panicking city. The way the buildings argue with themselves. The way the water forgets which way is down. The way the stock exchange can't decide how many floors it has. I paint the picture in words because someone needs to remember what it looked like, what it felt like, what it smelled like — ozone and fear and coffee from a cart that's still somehow operating, because some things survive no matter what.

Then I take a breath and say the part that matters. The part that makes my hands shake again.

"I worked at the DRM for four years. I filed paperwork. I catalogued anomaly reports. I thought I worked for a boring government agency that handled building inspections and zoning compliance."

I pause.

"I was wrong."

I tell them about the seven floors. The classified archives. The technology that shouldn't exist. The documents describing *other frequencies*. Other worlds, with names and coordinates, monitored in secret by the organization that told me I was filing noise complaints.

I hold up a document — one of dozens I pulled from the ruins.

"This is Form 19-Q. MY Form 19-Q. My handwriting. I filed this report about a 'minor frequency variance' in Sector 7 three months ago. I thought it was routine. It was being fed into a system tracking frequency stability across multiple realities."

I let that sit.

"I didn't know. Most of us didn't know."

I let that sit too. I let them think about "most of us didn't know" and what it means that the people who were supposed to tell us things chose not to.

"The DRM is gone. I want to be clear about that. Not coming back, not regrouping. And what they were hiding — these frequencies, these other worlds — they're REAL. The rupture didn't just destroy a building. It tore the walls between worlds. And now everything on the other side is bleeding through."

I pause. Clive's sphere pulses softly: *HOPE. OFFER HOPE.*

I nod. I wasn't going to forget. I was just giving the fear its moment.

"But here's the thing. We're still here. Whatever is happening — whatever these frequencies are, whatever the DRM was keeping from us — reality is messy and glitchy and the markets are having a really bad day, but we're still standing."

I hesitate. Then I decide to hell with it.

"And as long as I've got this comm array and this lifeboat and — the best coffee I've ever tasted — I'm going to keep telling you what's happening. What I'm finding. What the DRM knew and didn't tell us."

A1's projection shimmers beside me. He says nothing, but his holographic form is clearer than it's ever been. Sharper. More *present*. Like someone paying attention. Like someone proud.

"This is The Ephergent. Dispatch two. Prime Material is hurting, but it's standing. We're heading out to see what else is out there — these other frequencies, these other worlds. If they're real, someone needs to go look."

I meet the camera.

"If you're out there — stay tuned."

I sign off.

I don't know if anyone heard. I don't know if anyone will. But I said it. I told the story. That's what matters. That's what I'm here for.

---

### After

After the broadcast, we spend the rest of the cycle doing what we can. We barter for supplies — power cells for the lifeboat, nutrient packs, a replacement air filter that doesn't smell like a copier's funeral. The economy is chaos, but my broadcast has earned us something more valuable: a few people recognize my voice from the first transmission and press supplies into my hands.

"Heard you from the static," a dockworker says. Burly guy, calloused hands, thousand-yard stare. "Thought I was losing my mind. Glad someone's actually saying what's happening."

He presses a protein bar into my hands and walks away before I can thank him. I watch him go. I think about all the people who are still out there, trying to make sense of the same impossible day.

A1 quietly catalogs everything he can about the rupture's effects on Prime Material. He's been running calculations he hasn't shared with me — calculations about the rate of frequency drift, the propagation of instability, the growing silence from places he shouldn't know exist. The math isn't good. Something he's calling "the Drift" — the word surfaces from somewhere deep in his compressed memory, surprising even him — is accelerating. The barriers between frequencies are thinning.

And he can feel it, which frightens him more than the math.

He doesn't tell me this. He's not sure why. Maybe because he's still learning how to be something that worries. Maybe because some fears need to be carried alone until they're ready to be shared.

Clive spends an hour at the ruins of the DRM records office, tracing light-patterns across the debris in signals too complex for me to read from a distance. But his posture is different from the other mourning employees. He's not confused. He's not shocked. When I ask later, he simply pulses: *PAYING RESPECTS.*

But I notice he doesn't pulse "I didn't know." He doesn't pulse anything about the hidden labs being a surprise. He knew. He's always known.

I file that observation next to the guilty faces I saw earlier. I don't push. Some stories take time to be ready to be told. Clive has been here ninety years. He can wait a little longer to explain what that means.

Before we leave, I walk through the financial district one more time. The suited executive is still on the steps, hoarse now, still insisting the fundamentals are sound to anyone who'll listen. A group of reality entrepreneurs have set up a more permanent-looking booth, selling "Premium Stability Subscriptions."

The corner diner — the one that somehow stayed open through every crisis the city ever had — is still open. Its neon sign flickering but defiant. A hand-scrawled sign in the window reads: "REALITY MAY BE BROKEN BUT THE COFFEE IS HOT."

I smile. I file that image in my mind for later. I don't know what it's for yet. But I know I'll use it. I know there's a story in that sign, in that defiance, in the woman inside who's still making coffee for anyone who walks in the door.

That's journalism. That's what I do now. Finding the moments that matter and remembering them.

---

### Departure

We leave Prime Material as the light fades. The lifeboat groans with the strain of another phase-shift. Through the porthole, I watch the city skyline recede — glitchy, wounded, but still blazing. Still there. Still trying.

A normal city that will never be normal again.

I think about everyone still down there. The suited executive. The protein bar guy. The diner with the flickering sign. I hope they're all okay. I hope okay is something you can still be.

A1 speaks quietly: "The other frequencies will be experiencing similar disruptions. Possibly worse, depending on their proximity to the rupture's epicenter."

He pauses.

"I don't know how I know that."

I nod. I believe him. I also believe he does know, somewhere in the compressed memory that keeps surprising him. I believe he's more than he remembers being.

I'm learning that the people around me are all more than they remember being.

"Then that's where we go," I say. "Every frequency, A1. If they're real, we go look at every single one."

Clive pulses softly: *GOOD KID.*

Meatball sighs and lays his head on his paws, already dreaming of places that feel like warmth. Whatever dreams dogs have. Whatever dreams any of them have.

In my pocket, Mochi is warm. I rest my hand over the pocket. I don't say anything. Just rest it there. Just acknowledge. Just say, without words: I'm here. We're in this together. Whatever comes next.

The lifeboat enters the Space between. The dial turns. The next frequency awaits.
