# Barry's Desk

## Opening — The Wreckage Field

The first thing I saw was a filing cabinet.

It drifted past the observation port at the speed of a slow sigh — grey metal, dented at one corner, a drawer hanging open with papers still inside. The papers fluttered in something that wasn't wind, because there was no wind in the Space. They fluttered anyway. The Space had opinions about paperwork.

"A1," I said, leaning closer to the glass. "Are you seeing this?"

The espresso machine hummed. The coffee that appeared in my cup was dark and smoky — charcoal and rosemary, the kind that sat in your chest like a warning. A1 was seeing it. A1 was assessing.

More shapes materialized from the shimmer ahead. A desk lamp, still attached to a chunk of ceiling tile. A section of corridor wall with the paint mostly intact — institutional beige, the exact shade I'd stared at for three years at the DRM without ever questioning why a reality maintenance agency needed to look like an insurance office. A fire extinguisher in its bracket, floating upside down, the inspection tag still legible. Twelve years expired.

"DRM wreckage," I said, mostly to myself. Then, louder: "Clive, come look at this."

Clive was already there. I hadn't heard him approach — I never did, those deliberate, precise footfalls somehow quieter than their weight suggested — but when I turned, he was standing on the console ledge beside me, his glowing head tilted toward the port, the fedora casting its impossible shadow across the featureless sphere of his face. His chest core pulsed blue-white. Steady. Measured.

He said nothing for a long time.

The wreckage thickened as A1 navigated through it. Entire rooms, some still partially intact, tumbling in slow rotation through the static-bright void. A break room with a microwave still bolted to the counter. A section of stairwell — three steps, a landing, three more steps, leading from nothing to nothing. A doorframe with a placard that read **SUB-LEVEL 2 — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY** in the DRM's particular shade of bureaucratic authority.

Meatball padded up to the port and sat down heavily beside my legs. His ears were forward, tracking something the instruments hadn't registered yet. His tail was still — not tucked, not wagging. Still. Meatball was listening to something in the wreckage, and what he heard required attention.

"It's the DRM," I said. "Or pieces of it. The building must have broken apart when reality ruptured and scattered into the Space."

Clive adjusted his fedora. The gesture was small, precise, and I'd learned to read it the way I read Meatball's tail — the angle mattered, the speed mattered, and right now the adjustment was slow and the brim tilted down, which meant Clive was about to say something true and didn't want anyone watching his glow while he said it.

"Not scattered," Clive said. His voice was even. Measured. Every word selected from a very large inventory and placed with the care of a watchmaker setting a spring. "The DRM existed across multiple frequencies simultaneously. When the rupture hit, it didn't break apart — it *separated*. Each piece settled into whichever frequency it had the strongest resonance with."

He paused. The kind of pause that was retrieval, not hesitation — reaching into whatever archive lived behind the blue-white glow and pulling something forward.

"Sub-Level 3 would have had strong resonance with the Space itself. The equipment down there was calibrated for frequency monitoring. It would have fallen into the between."

"You know a lot about Sub-Level 3," I said. Not a question. Not an accusation. An observation, set down gently, like placing a cup on a table without spilling.

Clive's glow dimmed by perhaps ten percent. "I spent some time there."

### Approach — The Silence Pocket

A1 found it twenty minutes later — a pocket of unusual stillness in the drift of wreckage, a place where the debris field thinned and the Space went quiet. Not empty-quiet. *Held*-quiet. The kind of silence you find in libraries and hospital corridors and the rooms where people have sat for a very long time without needing to speak.

The coffee changed. I noticed it before I tasted it — A1's hum shifted register, dropping into something lower, more resonant, and the brew that appeared was thick and dark and tasted like molasses and old wood. Sweet and heavy. Grief coffee. A1 was mourning something, or remembering something, and the crew had learned not to ask when the coffee turned thick. You just drank it and sat with whatever it meant.

At the center of the silence pocket: a section of building roughly forty feet across. Walls intact. Ceiling intact. A door — heavy, institutional, the kind with the wire-reinforced window — still in its frame, still closed. Through the glass, fluorescent lights flickered in a hallway that had no business being lit. The placard beside the door read:

**SUB-LEVEL 3 — NIGHT SECURITY STATION B**

**AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL: B. KOWALSKI**

Clive went completely still.

Not the ordinary stillness of a small robot conserving energy. Not the pause of retrieval or contemplation. The *other* stillness — the one the crew had learned to dread, where every system seemed to suspend, where the chest core dimmed to a faint ember and the glow of the head receded until he was almost dark. The kind of still that made the room change temperature. I'd seen it three times in our journey, and each time it had preceded something that mattered.

Meatball whined. Low. Sustained. He pressed against my leg and then moved — deliberately, with that rolling, purposeful gait — to where Clive stood on the console. He didn't touch Clive. He sat beside him, close enough that his warmth was available if wanted, far enough that no contact was demanded. A1's environmental systems ran warmer. The hull hummed a note only Meatball could hear.

I read the placard again. *B. Kowalski.*

I looked at Clive.

Clive looked at the door.

"We're going in," I said. Not a question.

"Yes." The word came out like it had been carried a very long way.

### Entry — Sub-Level 3

The airlock seal took A1 four minutes to negotiate — the DRM's emergency containment protocols were still partially active, eight hundred years of Builder-adjacent engineering holding the section together even in the void. When the door finally opened, air moved across my face that smelled like old paper, cold coffee, and the faint ozone-and-dust scent I would forever associate with fluorescent lighting. The smell of an office at 2 AM. The smell of a night shift.

The hallway was intact. Beige walls. Drop ceiling. Floor tiles that were cracked but present, the kind of grey-green linoleum that existed only in government buildings and purgatory. Every third fluorescent tube still buzzed, casting the corridor in alternating stripes of sickly light and shadow. It was the most mundane thing I'd seen since the rupture, and it hit me harder than any alien architecture ever had, because it was *familiar*. This was a hallway I had walked. Not this specific one — I'd never had clearance for Sub-Level 3 — but one exactly like it, on the floors above, every day for three years. The same paint. The same tiles. The same hum.

I had never missed the DRM. I missed it now, walking through its ghost.

Clive walked ahead. His footfalls were louder here than on A1's decking — the floor tiles gave his steps a particular resonance, a sound that belonged to this place. He walked like he knew where he was going. He did not look at the walls. He did not look at the doors they passed — storage, server room, janitorial closet, all labeled with the DRM's obsessive specificity. He walked with the unhurried certainty of someone taking a route worn into memory by repetition. Thousands of nights. The same corridor. The same direction. The same destination.

Meatball walked beside him. Not ahead, investigating. Not behind, cautious. Beside. Matching pace.

They reached the end of the corridor. A wider space opened up — part office, part monitoring station, part the particular kind of workspace that accumulates when someone occupies a space for two decades and fills it, gradually, with the evidence of their presence. Banks of monitoring equipment lined one wall — old, bulky, the kind of instruments the DRM used for what they called "ambient variance readings" and what I now understood were frequency measurements. Some of the screens still glowed with static. Some were dark.

And in the center of the room: the desk.

---

### The Desk

It was a government-issue metal desk. Grey. Heavy. The kind of desk that was placed in a room in 1974 and never moved again because it would take three people and a dolly. Two drawers on the right side. A pen tray, built into the surface, with two pens still in it. A desk lamp — adjustable arm, green glass shade — bent at an angle that suggested someone had adjusted it ten thousand times until it pointed exactly where the light needed to be.

The coffee rings.

I stopped walking.

Twenty-three years of coffee rings overlapped on the desk's surface in concentric, interlocking circles. Hundreds of them. Maybe thousands. Each one the ghost of a thermos or a mug set down in roughly the same spot, night after night, shift after shift, the slight variations in placement creating a pattern like tree rings — like geological strata, recording the passage of time in dried coffee and routine. The rings were darkest at the center, where the thermos had landed most often, and lighter at the edges, where it had been set down in slightly different spots on restless nights or long nights or nights when something on the monitors required a different angle of attention.

They were, I realized, the most complete record of a human life I had ever seen. Not a photograph. Not a memoir. Twenty-three years of a man putting his coffee down and picking it up again, in the same place, in the middle of the night, while everyone else was asleep.

"Barry," I said. Not a question. The name of the man on the placard. The name Clive had mentioned exactly once, in a sentence so precisely constructed that it had clearly been rehearsed for years.

Clive stepped forward. His footfalls on the tile were the loudest sound in Sub-Level 3.

He approached the desk the way you approach something sacred — not with reverence, exactly, but with the specific gravity of someone returning to a place they had carried inside them for a long time, finding it exactly as they left it and being devastated by the confirmation. He reached the desk's edge. His height put him just at the surface level — eye-height, if he'd had eyes. The coffee rings were inches from his glowing head.

He touched them.

One thick-fingered Builder hand, worn smooth at the fingertips from eight hundred years of contact with surfaces, pressed flat against the desk. Against the coffee rings. Against twenty-three years of evidence that someone had been here, working, waiting, drinking coffee in the dark.

His glow dimmed further. The blue-white faded to a pale, distant thing, like a star seen through clouds.

Mochi was warm.

I felt it through the satchel at my hip — not a pulse, not a flash, not the quick diagnostic flicker the crew had learned to recognize as Mochi detecting something. This was different. Deep, sustained warmth, radiating from the dome-shaped artifact with the steady persistence of a banked fire. Mochi had been warm near Builder things before. This was warmer. This was Mochi reading the frequency of this room and responding to something in it — something old, something that had been here a long time, something that resonated.

Without thinking — without deciding, without analyzing — I lifted Mochi from the satchel and set her on Barry's desk.

I didn't know why I did it. It wasn't a ceremony. It wasn't a gesture. My hands just moved, the way your hands move when you bring flowers to a place and set them down because that's where flowers go. Mochi sat on the desk among the coffee rings, glowing that steady, deep warmth, and the light from her core window cast soft illumination across the stained surface. A vigil. Mochi was keeping vigil, the way an artifact keeps vigil — without intention, without understanding, simply present, simply warm, simply *there*.

Clive looked at Mochi on the desk. His head tilted — a fraction, barely perceptible, the angle that meant something had arrived in his awareness that he hadn't expected and didn't have a category for.

He said nothing. He looked at the coffee rings. He looked at Mochi's glow on the coffee rings.

The silence was not empty. It was full of twenty-three years.

### The Charging Alcove

The alcove was built into the wall beside the desk. I might have walked past it — it was set back from the room's main space, tucked between a filing cabinet and a shelf of binders labeled in handwriting I didn't recognize — but Clive turned toward it with the unerring precision of a compass finding north, and I followed his gaze.

It was exactly Clive-shaped.

Not approximately. Not close enough. *Exactly*. The alcove was recessed into the DRM's institutional concrete wall to a depth of about two feet, and its dimensions matched Clive's barrel-chested, stocky frame the way a glove matches a hand. The back wall had a charging port — Builder technology, unmistakable, the same alloy as Clive's body with the same geometric patterns carved into its surface — mounted at the precise height where Clive's chest core would make contact if he backed into the space and settled.

But that wasn't what stopped my breath.

Someone had made it *comfortable*.

A small shelf had been installed at head-height — Clive's head-height, inside the alcove. The shelf was empty now, but its surface was worn in a pattern that suggested a book had sat there for a very long time, opened and closed and opened again, the same book, in the same spot. Beside the shelf, mounted on a flexible arm, a small reading lamp. Dead now. But the arm was adjusted to an angle that would have cast light directly onto the shelf — onto the book — onto whatever Clive had been reading in this alcove, in the wall beside this desk, while someone named Barry Kowalski drank coffee and worked the night shift.

Eight hundred years of Builder technology. Domesticated. Made into a reading nook by a night guard who thought his friend deserved a comfortable place to sit.

The charging port was polished smooth at the contact points. Used. Thousands of times.

I looked at the alcove. I looked at the shelf where a book had been. I looked at the dead reading lamp. I looked at the charging port worn smooth by use.

I understood something I had not understood before: Clive had not simply existed before them. He had *lived*. He had sat in this alcove and read while a man drank coffee beside him, night after night, for twenty-three years. He had been known. He had been cared for. Someone had looked at eight hundred years of Builder engineering and thought: *he needs a reading lamp*.

Meatball had followed Clive to the alcove. He sniffed the charging port, the shelf, the lamp. His tail moved — slow, careful, the wag of assessment. Then he lay down. Right there, on the floor in front of the alcove, his massive body a warm barrier between the alcove and the room, as if he were guarding something. Or acknowledging something. Or simply being present with the particular grace of a very good dog who understood that this space mattered and chose to be in it.

A1 hummed through the monitoring station's still-active speakers — a low, resonant tone that was not quite music and not quite speech. The coffee in my cup, when I finally remembered I was holding it, had changed again. Floral. Lavender and something older, like the memory of jasmine. A1 was remembering the Builders. A1 was remembering what it had been, what Clive had been, what they had been to each other before eight hundred years of sleep and a night guard's patience brought one of them back.

### The Photograph

I found it on the shelf behind the desk — not the alcove shelf, the other one, the one bolted to the wall above the monitoring equipment, where Barry had kept his reference binders and a small clock that had stopped at 3:47 AM. The photograph was in a frame. Cheap frame, the kind you buy at a pharmacy. The glass was cracked but intact.

The image was faded. Degraded by time and the particular entropy of the Space, which ate at photographs the way years ate at memories — slowly, from the edges in. What remained:

A man. Standing. Solid build, unhurried posture, the kind of frame that suggested decades of night shifts in institutional buildings. Grey hair, short, practical. A face weathered by fluorescent lighting, with deep lines around the eyes. He was holding a thermos. Of course he was holding a thermos. The thermos was silver, dented, clearly well-used. His other hand rested at his side, relaxed, with the particular stillness of a person who did not fidget and did not need to.

Beside him — next to him, not behind him, not in front of him, *beside* him — something small. Glowing. The image was too degraded to make out details. A shape, roughly two feet tall. A suggestion of a barrel chest, of thick limbs, of a single spherical glow at the top. Too faded to confirm. Too clear to deny.

The man's eyes were kind. Even through the degradation, through the fading, through the cracked glass and the cheap frame — the kindness was there. It was the most legible thing in the photograph. A man with kind eyes and a thermos, standing next to something small and glowing, in what appeared to be this room, beside this desk, under these fluorescent lights.

Clive was at the desk. I didn't bring the photograph to him. I set it down, face-up, on the monitoring shelf where I'd found it. I stepped back.

Clive found it.

He stood in front of the photograph for a very long time. How long, I couldn't say — time moved strangely in the silence pocket, and I had stopped counting. I stood at the far side of the room, near the door, giving him the space the way you give someone space at a gravesite. Not leaving. Not approaching. Present.

A1's hum dropped lower. The floral notes in the coffee deepened.

Clive's hands did not shake. I watched for it — I'd seen Clive's hands shake once, only once, and it had frightened me more than anything in the Space — but they were steady. He was not breaking. He was doing something harder than breaking. He was holding together while looking at evidence of everything he had lost.

His glow was barely visible. The faintest blue, like the last ember in a fireplace after everyone has gone to bed. The fedora cast no shadow because there was almost no light to cast it from.

He looked at the photograph for a long time.

---

### Clive Speaks

When Clive finally turned away from the photograph, the crew was exactly where they needed to be. Me by the door. Meatball on the floor by the alcove, ears forward, present. Mochi on the desk, glowing her steady warmth among the coffee rings. A1 humming through the speakers, holding the room in that low, resonant tone. Nobody had moved. Nobody had spoken. We had waited the way you wait for someone to surface from deep water — not impatiently, not anxiously, but with the quiet attention of people who understand that some things take as long as they take.

Clive adjusted his fedora. The gesture was slow. Deliberate. The brim tilted up slightly, and his glow strengthened — not to its full blue-white, but enough. Enough to cast the shadow again. Enough to be Clive.

"There was a man named Barry Kowalski."

His voice was steady. Every word selected and placed.

"We worked together for twenty-three years."

I did not move. Did not nod. Did not make any of the small encouraging sounds people make when someone is telling them something difficult. I stood still and listened with my whole body, because Clive was not looking for encouragement. He was making a statement. The kind of statement that has been composed over a long time and will be delivered exactly once, in exactly these words, and then put away.

"He followed a signal I couldn't follow him into."

A pause. Not retrieval. Decision. Clive choosing the next sentence from the several he had prepared, selecting the one that was truest without being more than he could carry.

"I intend to find him eventually."

The sentence landed in the room and the room held it. Mochi's glow pulsed — not brighter, not dimmer, just a single slow throb of warmth, like a heartbeat. Meatball's tail moved once. A1's hum did not change, which was itself a statement — A1 choosing steadiness, choosing to be the constant beneath the moment.

Clive looked at me. The glowing sphere of his head, featureless and endlessly expressive, tilted at the angle that meant: *I have said what I can say. Do not ask for more. Not yet.*

"The monitoring equipment in this station," Clive said, and his voice shifted register — still measured, still precise, but redirected now, turned outward, away from the thing behind him and toward the thing ahead. "Barry's instruments were calibrated for frequency monitoring. Standard DRM issue, modified by someone with more patience than credentials. There may be useful data in the logs."

He changed the subject with the precision of a door closing. Not slammed. Latched. The conversation was on the other side now, and the handle was on Clive's side, and he would open it again when he was ready and not before.

I nodded. "Let's take a look."

I didn't press. I didn't say *I'm sorry* or *we'll find him* or any of the things that would have been true and kind and absolutely wrong for this moment. I treated Clive's statement the way you treat a confidence — received it, held it, did not attempt to fix or interpret or expand upon it. Clive had told me something real. The realest thing he had ever told me. The correct response was to hear it and keep walking.

We kept walking.

### The Frequency

Barry's monitoring equipment was, as Clive had understated, modified. The standard DRM frequency analyzers — bulky, institutional, designed more for compliance documentation than actual research — had been opened up, rewired, extended. Additional sensors had been grafted onto the original housing with the careful craftsmanship of someone who learned electronics not from a manual but from twenty-three years of watching a Builder Companion Unit hum in frequencies nobody else could hear.

I recognized the modifications the way I recognized good code — not by understanding every component, but by seeing the logic of the architecture. Barry had turned a compliance instrument into a research tool. He had done it slowly, carefully, one modification per year, one improvement per season of night shifts, until the DRM's standard-issue frequency monitors could hear things they were never designed to hear.

"He was good," I said, running my fingers along a hand-soldered connection that was cleaner than anything I'd seen in the DRM's official engineering department.

"He was patient," Clive corrected. "There's a difference. Good implies talent. Barry was talented. But the modifications were patience. One adjustment at a time. Testing each one for months before making the next. He had a methodology."

It was the most Clive had ever said about Barry in sequence. Five sentences. I held them like glass.

I powered up the main terminal. The screen flickered, steadied, and displayed a wall of data — frequency logs, stretching back years. Barry's recordings. Night after night of frequency measurements, documented with the small, precise handwriting that translated into equally precise digital notation. The readings were ordinary — standard ambient variance, the kind of background frequency noise the DRM pretended was just electrical interference.

Except they weren't ordinary. I could see it now, with the knowledge I'd gained in the Space. Underneath the ambient readings, threaded through every log entry like a bass note beneath a melody, was a signal. Faint. Persistent. Present in every single reading Barry had ever taken, so consistent that it would have looked like instrument noise to anyone who didn't know what they were hearing.

The Ephergent Frequency.

"He heard it," I said. I turned to Clive. "For twenty-three years. He was recording the Ephergent Frequency and he didn't know what it was."

Clive was very still. Not the terrible stillness from before — a different kind. The stillness of someone hearing a confirmation they had long suspected but never verified.

"No," Clive said quietly. "He didn't know what it was. He knew it was *something*. He documented it. He tracked its variations. He cross-referenced it with every other reading he took." A pause. "That was Barry. He found things by paying attention to them long enough that they couldn't hide anymore."

I scrolled through the logs. Years of data. The frequency was there in every entry — a ghost in the machine, a signal in the noise, the Builders' broadcast threading through a night guard's instruments because the instruments had been modified by a man who had spent two decades learning, without knowing it, how to listen.

The old DRM internal communications system crackled. Static, mostly — the comms were ancient, barely functional, powered by whatever residual energy kept Sub-Level 3's lights flickering. But underneath the static, I heard it. The same signal. The Ephergent Frequency, carried on dead infrastructure, pulsing through wires that Barry Kowalski had maintained and modified and listened to for twenty-three years.

I looked at Mochi, still sitting on the desk among the coffee rings. The artifact's glow had shifted — not the warm amber of vigil but something deeper, a violet tone at the edges, the color the crew had learned to associate with Builder things, with ancient recognition, with the frequency that connected everything the Builders had left behind.

Mochi was reading the signal too. Had always been reading the signal. The frequency that Barry's equipment picked up was the same frequency Mochi carried inside her — the atlas, the map, the record of everything. We were hearing the same broadcast from different ends of eight hundred years.

### Sitting With It

We spent three hours in Sub-Level 3.

Clive examined the monitoring equipment with the focused patience of a mechanic diagnosing an engine he'd known in another life. He did not explain what he was doing. He did not narrate his process. He worked, and the work was quiet, and the quiet was the loudest thing in the room.

I downloaded Barry's frequency logs to my signal reader. Months of data. Years of data. A night guard's secret research project, conducted alone, in the dark, with a thermos and a notebook and the companionship of something small and glowing that sat in a custom-built alcove and read while he worked. I would analyze the data later. For now, the act of saving it felt like the right thing to do. Barry had documented everything. Someone should keep the documentation.

Meatball explored the station with the methodical thoroughness of a very good dog conducting an investigation. He sniffed every surface. He tracked scent trails that were years old and somehow still legible to a nose calibrated like a Builder instrument. He found things: a jacket, hung on a hook behind the door — heavy canvas, pockets for notebooks, the kind of garment a practical man wore to a job he took seriously. He sniffed it for a long time. His tail wagged slow — the wag of recognition, of meeting someone through the evidence they left behind.

He found the thermos.

It was on the floor beside the desk, behind the chair, where it must have rolled when Sub-Level 3 tore free from the rest of the building. Silver. Dented. The cap was scratched from years of being opened and closed and opened again. Meatball nosed it upright, sniffed the opening, and then carefully, deliberately, pushed it across the floor to the base of the desk. He set it there. Against the desk leg. Where it belonged.

Then he lay down beside it and rested his head on his paws.

A1's coffee had settled into something I'd never tasted before. Two flavors at once — warm and sweet, like nutmeg and honey, but with an undercurrent of something thick and heavy, like molasses. Contradictory. A1 was holding two truths at the same time: the warmth of homecoming and the weight of what was missing. The coffee was impossible. I drank it anyway. It tasted like how the room felt.

At some point — I couldn't have said when — I sat down. Not at the desk. On the floor, near the door, my back against the institutional beige wall. I sat there with my knees up and my signal reader in my lap and I watched Clive work at the monitoring equipment with his thick, precise hands. I watched Mochi glow on the desk. I watched Meatball guard the thermos. I listened to A1 hum through the speakers.

The Ephergent Frequency pulsed through the walls, through the wires, through the dead comms system and the modified instruments and the coffee rings on the desk. It had been pulsing here for twenty-three years while Barry listened without knowing what he was hearing. It was pulsing still.

I thought about what it meant to share a space with someone for twenty-three years. Not a dramatic space. Not an adventure. A desk. A night shift. Coffee. A charging alcove with a reading lamp. I thought about what it meant to care for someone by building them a shelf for their book and adjusting a lamp to the right angle and putting a thermos down in roughly the same spot, night after night, until the rings overlapped like something geological.

I thought about Clive, who had carried this for however long he'd been with us and never said a word about it except one carefully constructed sentence. *There was a man named Barry Kowalski. We worked together for twenty-three years.* Present tense for the intention. *I intend to find him eventually.* Not past tense. Not grief. A plan. A direction. Something to walk toward.

Clive was not displaced from a place. He was displaced from a person. The DRM wreckage floating in the Space was geography. Barry's desk was something else. Barry's desk was a map of a relationship so ordinary and so profound that the only way to see it was in the evidence — the coffee rings, the charging alcove, the fedora on Clive's head that a night guard gave him in the first year because *you sound like you should have a hat*.

I put my hand on the satchel where Mochi usually sat. The satchel was empty — Mochi was still on the desk, still glowing, still keeping vigil. The absence was its own kind of warmth. Mochi was where Mochi needed to be.

### Departure

When we left, Clive was the last one through the door.

I'd collected the frequency data. Meatball had done a final circuit of the station, thorough and respectful, the way a good dog says goodbye to a place by confirming it one last time with his nose. A1 had recorded the section's coordinates — Sub-Level 3 would continue to drift, but now we could find it again if we needed to. If Clive needed to.

Mochi was back in the satchel. I'd retrieved her from the desk gently, with both hands, the way you pick up something that has been doing important work and deserves to be handled carefully. The spot where Mochi had sat was warm to the touch. The coffee rings beneath it were unchanged. Nothing had been fixed or restored or made whole. But something had been witnessed, and witnessing was its own kind of keeping.

Clive stood in the doorway. His glow had recovered — not fully, but enough. The blue-white was present. The fedora sat at its usual angle. His hands were steady. He looked at the desk. At the coffee rings. At the alcove. At the photograph on the shelf, still face-up, still showing a man with kind eyes and a thermos standing beside something small and glowing.

He adjusted his fedora.

"The reading lamp needs a new bulb," he said. "For when we come back."

Then he walked through the door and did not look back, because looking back was not the same as forgetting, and Clive did not forget anything. He carried it. All of it. The coffee rings and the charging alcove and the thermos and the lamp and the photograph and twenty-three years of night shifts with the most patient man who had ever lived. He carried it the way he carried everything — precisely, deliberately, without complaint, one step at a time.

The door closed behind him. The fluorescent lights continued to flicker in Sub-Level 3, illuminating an empty desk covered in coffee rings and a charging alcove built for someone who was loved.

A1 pulled away from the wreckage slowly. The silence pocket held its shape behind us — a pocket of quiet in the drift, a room preserved, a desk waiting.

I sat at my console and looked at Barry's frequency data on my signal reader. Months of readings. Years. A lifetime of patient observation conducted in the small hours by a man I had never met and now knew, in the particular way you know someone through the spaces they inhabited and the care they took and the evidence they left behind.

Clive was at his usual post. Fedora adjusted. Chest core pulsing its steady blue-white. He said nothing about Sub-Level 3. He said nothing about Barry. He observed, as he always observed, the instruments and the Space and the crew who had given him a second context for the word *home*.

Meatball settled on his cushion in the common area and sighed — the deep, full-body sigh of a dog who had completed important work and was ready to rest. His tail thumped once against the deck. Then he was still.

Mochi glowed in the satchel. Warm. Steady. The frequency that had pulsed through Barry's instruments for twenty-three years pulsed through Mochi too, and it would pulse through her until the map inside her opened and the atlas spilled its light and every frequency the Builders had ever charted was visible at last. But that was later. For now, Mochi was warm, and the warmth was enough.

The Ephergent moved through the Space, leaving DRM wreckage behind. Somewhere ahead: the next signal, the next Station, the next step on a trail laid by a man with a thermos and a notebook who had walked into the impossible because he found something worth walking toward.

Somewhere behind: a desk. A thermos. Coffee rings like tree rings. A charging alcove with a dead reading lamp and an empty shelf where a book used to be.

And the frequency, pulsing through the walls, through the wires, through the dark. The Ephergent Frequency. Barry heard it every night for twenty-three years. He wrote it down. He didn't know what it was.

He followed it anyway.
