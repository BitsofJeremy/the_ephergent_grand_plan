# The DRM Archive

## Narrative Structure

### Opening

The signal was faint. A1 caught it three hours before anyone else would have — a rhythmic pulse buried under layers of static, repeating at intervals too regular to be natural.

The espresso that cycle had been sharp and bright, lemon zest with a clean acidity that meant A1 was working a problem it found interesting. I'd learned to taste the difference. Sharp meant engaged. Dull meant distracted. Bitter meant worried. And this — this bright, clean sharpness — meant A1 had found something worth paying attention to, and was trying to figure out if it was good or bad, and hadn't decided yet.

"Signal origin," A1 said. No preamble. Subject, verb, object. The British formality coming through even in the clinical delivery. "DRM relay station. Automated distress beacon, cycling on backup power. Bearing two-seven-three relative to our current heading. Estimated drift: fourteen months since last human contact."

I set down my mug. The ceramic was warm against my palm, a small comfort in a small cup in an infinite Space. "DRM? I thought everything went down with the headquarters."

"The headquarters was a node. Not the network." A brief pause. The espresso machine's brass fittings caught the light from the console displays, glowing in that particular way they did when A1 was being careful with what it said. "The DRM maintained relay stations throughout the Space. Signal repeaters. Data caches. Most would have lost power within weeks of the collapse. This one has not."

Clive stood on the navigation console, knee-high and still, his glowing sphere head tilted at an angle I'd come to read as consideration. Not curiosity — Clive didn't do curiosity the way people did. Clive did *consideration*, which was curiosity's older, quieter cousin that had already decided the answer was worth finding. The blue-white light beneath his fedora dimmed for a moment — retrieval, I thought, not hesitation. The geometric Builder symbols along his barrel chest pulsed faintly, and I wondered, not for the first time, what those symbols meant. What they said. What eight hundred years of Builder engineering looked like when it was trying to communicate.

"Sub-level relay stations had independent power cores," Clive said. His voice was measured, each word placed with the care of someone who'd had eight hundred years of practice selecting them. "Designed to outlast the people who built them. Which —" A pause. The glow flickered — a quick pulse, there and gone, like a lightbulb having a thought. "— they have."

Meatball lifted his head from where he'd been sleeping against the warm base of A1's espresso housing and whined once, soft and low, his ears rotating like satellite dishes toward something none of us could hear. The dog's hearing had gotten sharper since we'd been in the Space. Or maybe the Space had simply gotten louder, and Meatball was the only one willing to admit it.

"Then we go look," I said. Because what else was there to say? We were a broadcast crew with a dying ship and a mission that kept expanding. Saying no to a signal wasn't really an option anymore. Saying no was how you stopped being the Ephergent.

The station materialized through A1's forward viewports twenty minutes later — a dark shape tumbling slowly against the static-laced void of the Space. It was ugly in the way only government architecture can be ugly: a rectangular module bristling with antenna arrays, solar panels folded at wrong angles, hull plating stamped with the DRM seal I'd stared at on my employee badge for four years without ever once thinking about what it meant. The seal had seemed so official then. So meaningful. Now it just looked like a logo on a box that had been forgotten in a closet.

One running light blinked red at three-second intervals. Everything else was dark. The red blink was the only thing alive out there, and it looked tired.

A1 matched rotation and extended the docking collar. The coffee shifted — darker now, a bitter undercurrent threading through the brightness. I noticed. I always noticed. Bitter meant worried. Worried about what?

"The station's internal atmosphere reads breathable," A1 said. "Power output is at four percent and declining. I estimate six hours before complete failure."

"Then we don't waste time." I zipped my jacket over Mochi's satchel pocket and felt the familiar warmth against my ribs. Steady. Unchanged. The soft pink glow of contentment that meant Mochi was okay, was just riding along, was not detecting anything worth getting excited about. "Let's see what's left."

Clive dropped from the console to the deck with a deliberate thud — sixty centimeters of ancient Builder alloy landing with more weight than his size suggested. He adjusted his fedora. I'd learned that the adjustment meant he was preparing for something. I hadn't learned to ask what. Some things Clive needed to show you in his own time, in his own way, and asking just made him go quieter.

I was learning that about people too. Maybe I always knew it. Maybe this whole thing was just teaching me what I'd forgotten.

### The Station

The airlock opened onto silence.

Not quiet — silence. The particular dead silence of a place that used to hum with purpose and doesn't anymore. My boots hit the deck grating and the sound traveled too far in every direction, bouncing off walls that had forgotten how to absorb it. The emergency lighting painted everything in dim amber. The air tasted like recycled nothing — stale, flat, the ghost of climate control systems running on fumes, systems that had been breathing for no one for fourteen months.

I knew this place. Not this specific station, but this *kind* of place. The same drop ceiling tiles I'd stared at during four years of DRM data analysis shifts. The same institutional grey walls that made everything feel like a waiting room for something that never arrived. The same fluorescent tube housings, dark now, that I'd used as a reference point when I was thinking about nothing.

Someone had put a motivational poster on the wall near the airlock: "REALITY MAINTENANCE: BECAUSE SOMEONE HAS TO!" The lamination was peeling. The colors had faded to something sickly, like a bruise healing wrong. Under it, someone had taped a handwritten note: *Last one out, turn off the lights. — Hendricks, Relay Maintenance Crew 7.*

Nobody had turned off the lights. The lights had turned themselves off when the power gave out, and no one had been here to turn them back on.

That's what happened to places when people left. The machinery kept going until it couldn't, and then it stopped, and then it waited for someone to come back. Usually no one did.

"This way," Clive said, already moving down the corridor. His footsteps were measured, unhurried, each one placed with eight hundred years of intention. The Builder symbols on his body cast faint blue-white traces on the walls as he passed — moving constellations I still couldn't read. I'd asked A1 once if the symbols were a language. A1 had said they were more than that. A1 had said they were a *record*, and that Clive carried his history on his skin the way people carried it in their memories, except Clive's couldn't be forgotten because it was written in light.

I watched those symbols as I followed him. I still couldn't read them. But I was starting to feel them, the way you feel a song you don't know the words to. The way you feel weather coming.

"You know the layout?" I asked.

"DRM standardized their relay architecture seventy-three years ago. All stations follow the same floor plan." A beat. The kind of beat that meant something was coming that I wasn't going to like. "I was there when they finalized it."

I filed that. I was getting good at filing things Clive said that I couldn't yet understand. Clive had been at the DRM for ninety years. Clive had been places and seen things and known people whose names were now just signatures in old files. Clive's "I was there" was never casual. Clive's "I was there" meant he'd been there before the walls existed, before the filing systems existed, before "there" was a concept that needed a name.

The main operations room was a graveyard of bureaucracy.

Server racks lined the walls, indicator lights mostly dead, a few still blinking with the tired persistence of machines that don't know they've been abandoned. Or maybe do know, and are just too stubborn to stop. Workstations sat in neat rows — keyboards, monitors, desk organizers, a coffee mug with the DRM logo and a brown ring of something long evaporated. Filing cabinets. Paper trays. A whiteboard with half-erased duty rosters.

The mundane infrastructure of an organization that monitored the boundaries between frequencies and filed the results in triplicate. Because if you filed enough paperwork, maybe the impossible would stay organized.

My throat tightened. I recognized the filing system. The color-coded folders. The Form numbers stamped on cabinet drawers. I had used this exact system for four years. Had alphabetized within it. Had complained about it at lunch to coworkers who were probably as confused as I was, filing forms about a reality we didn't know was bigger than the filing cabinets.

"A1, can you interface with their network?"

"Already in progress." A1's voice came through the ship's comm relay, threaded with a faint warmth that meant the connection was clean. "Their data architecture is... familiar." A pause that meant something was being processed that A1 wasn't ready to explain. "I am accessing the file index now."

Meatball trotted ahead, nose working, tail low. He stopped at a workstation near the back wall and sat down, staring at the dark monitor with an intensity that had nothing to do with what a dog should care about. His ears were forward. Listening. The dog was always listening to things that weren't sound.

Then I stepped into the server room, and Mochi woke up.

### Mochi Ignites

Not woke up — *ignited*. The satchel at my hip flared with heat so sudden and so intense that I gasped and pulled at my jacket zipper. Mochi's core window blazed — not the soft pink of contentment or the gentle blue of detection. This was something I hadn't seen before: a deep, resonant violet, pulsing in time with something I couldn't hear, the geometric traces on Mochi's base glowing bright enough to cast shadows on the server racks like something holy happening in a government surplus facility.

"Whoa — Mochi, what —" I unzipped my jacket, and the heat rolled out like opening an oven. Mochi sat in the satchel, dome-shaped and vibrating, the violet glow filling the dark server room with shifting light. The server racks nearest to me flickered — screens lighting up, fans spinning, data flowing through circuits that had been dormant for over a year.

As if Mochi's resonance was a defibrillator and the servers had a pulse to restart. As if Mochi was saying something in a language made of light and heat and frequency.

Clive stood very still. His glow had gone white. He said nothing. His hands — articulated and thick-fingered and worn smooth at the tips from eight hundred years of contact — hung at his sides without moving. The Clive equivalent of holding his breath.

"The server contains frequency classification data," A1 said through the comm. "Catalogued signal patterns spanning —" A pause that, from A1, meant recalibration. The kind of pause that meant the data was bigger than expected. "— spanning a considerable period. The data resonates at a frequency consistent with Builder-era signal architecture."

The servers hummed around us. Mochi pulsed violet, steady and insistent, reading something in the signal that none of us could name. The violet light made the institutional grey walls look like the inside of something ancient and holy, which they were not. They were government surplus. They were purchased under federal contract. They had been manufactured in a facility in Virginia that also made filing cabinets for the Department of Motor Vehicles.

But what was stored in them was something else entirely. And Mochi knew. Mochi had always known, maybe. Mochi was a Builder Companion, just like Clive, and somewhere in Mochi's core was a memory of what these servers used to talk about, before the DRM took the frequency data and filed it under the wrong heading.

I didn't understand what I was seeing. But I understood that it was important. And I understood that sometimes the most important things look like filing cabinets.

### The Files

A1 fed the data to my portable display in batches. Most of it was what I expected — DRM operational files, frequency variance reports, personnel records, maintenance logs. The filing system I knew. The bureaucracy I'd lived inside. But beneath the surface layer, threaded through the routine like veins through stone, were the classified files. The ones with stamps that said DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY. The ones I'd never seen, never knew existed, never would have found if the building hadn't fallen and taken its secrets with it.

FREQUENCY ANOMALY CLASSIFICATION — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY.

I opened them and forgot to breathe.

Descriptions of places the DRM had detected but never publicly acknowledged. Frequency signatures that didn't match any known location. Schematics of structures described as "non-terrestrial architecture, origin unknown, estimated age: pre-institutional."

I read the descriptions and felt the hair on my arms rise. Angular shapes in the void. Resonance chambers. Platforms orbiting dead frequencies. Things the crew would later recognize. Things they hadn't found yet. Things that were out there, waiting, classified under the wrong form number in a basement that no longer existed.

"They knew," I said. "They knew about all of it. The frequencies, the — these structures, whatever these are. They had files on everything and they just... kept filing." My voice came out strange. Thin. Like the air in the room had gotten thinner and my lungs hadn't caught up. "They knew, and they filed it, and they did nothing. They just... kept working. Kept processing. Kept treating it like it was all just paperwork."

"Institutions do not discover," Clive said quietly from beside me. He was looking at the screen, his glowing head angled down, the fedora casting a shadow that shouldn't work on a featureless sphere and absolutely did. The shadow fell *just right*, like Clive had been designed with shadow-casting in mind, like the fedora understood perspective better than physics. "They classify. There is a difference."

A1 spoke. "I have located a personnel and intake archive. Sub-directory seven. It contains —" Another pause. Longer. The coffee in my mug, sitting forgotten on a dead workstation, had gone cold. I'd never known A1's coffee to go cold. The cold was significant. The cold meant something A1 didn't want to say. "— it contains Form 12-C filings."

Clive's hands — articulated, thick-fingered, worn smooth at the tips from eight hundred years of contact — went still at his sides.

"Form 12-C," I said. "That's the intake classification for unidentified objects. I filed hundreds of those. Every weird thing the DRM couldn't categorize got a 12-C."

I looked at Clive. Something was happening in my chest — a tightness, a certainty that I didn't want.

"You want to see yours?"

The question hung in the recycled air between us. The servers hummed. Mochi pulsed violet, steady and insistent, reading something in the signal that none of them could name. The violet light painted Clive's face — if a sphere could have a face — in shades of something ancient and something hurt.

"Yes," Clive said.

A1 put it on the screen.

---

### Form 12-C

**DEPARTMENT OF REALITY MAINTENANCE**
**Form 12-C: Intake Classification — Unidentified Object**

**Date of Intake**: [REDACTED — Pre-Digital Archive]
**Intake Officer**: Morrison, D. — Sub-Level 3
**Classification**: Inert Office Equipment (Provisional)

**Physical Description**: Unidentified resonance object, desk-scale. Compact bipedal frame, approximately 60cm height. Metallic composition — non-standard alloy, no match in materials database. Surface markings consistent with decorative etching (purpose unknown). Single spherical protrusion, upper assembly — glass or crystal, faintly luminescent under certain light conditions. No moving parts observed. No identifiable power source. Weight: 11.3 kg.

**Resonance Reading**: Intermittent low-frequency emission detected during initial scan (0.7 Hz, amplitude sub-threshold). Subsequent scans: null. Classified as instrument error.

**Intake Recommendation**: Object shows no active properties, no hazard indicators, no response to standard stimuli. Recommend classification as inert office equipment pending further review. Assigned to Sub-Level 3 storage, Shelf 14-C.

**Notes**: Object is warm to the touch. Maintenance has been advised. No explanation found.

**Status**: CLOSED — No Further Action Required.

---

Clive read it aloud.

His voice — that precise, noir cadence, every word chosen like a card drawn from a deck — did not waver. He read every line. The physical description. The resonance reading dismissed as instrument error. The recommendation. The classification. He read "inert office equipment" the way you'd read your own obituary written by someone who never met you. The way you'd read a death certificate for yourself that someone filled out without checking if you were actually dead.

When he finished, he was silent.

Not the retrieval-pause I'd learned to recognize. Not the processing-pause that preceded one of his observations. This was a different silence. Clive went absolutely still — no movement, no glow fluctuation, nothing. The Builder symbols on his chest dimmed. The blue-white light of his sphere head held steady at its lowest visible intensity, like a candle seen from very far away. His hands hung at his sides. His fedora cast its shadow.

The shadow was the only thing that moved, and it only moved because the emergency lighting was flickering, barely, like a heartbeat that was trying to stop.

The server room hummed around us. Mochi pulsed violet. Meatball, who had followed them in, pressed his body against Clive's leg and stayed there. The dog didn't ask for explanation. The dog just offered presence, the way dogs do, the way they understand without words that sometimes the most important thing is just being near someone who is hurting.

I counted the seconds because I didn't know what else to do. I got to forty-seven before I couldn't stand it anymore.

"Clive? Are you okay?"

The silence held for three more seconds. Then Clive's head tilted — just slightly, the barest motion — and the glow brightened by one shade. The shade of someone deciding to come back from wherever they went.

"They filed me under office supplies." His voice was the same. Measured. Precise. Eight hundred years of practice at saying things without saying them. "Accurate enough, at the time."

The *at the time* carried weight. It sat in the air between us like something with mass — not an explanation, not a deflection, but a door left slightly open. An eight-hundred-year-old Builder Companion, designed to walk beside people through the vast and lonely spaces between frequencies, classified as inert. Shelved. Closed, no further action required. A machine that was warm to the touch, and they called it a maintenance issue.

I wanted to say something. Something about how the DRM was wrong, how they didn't understand what they were looking at, how Clive was more than a filing designation. But Clive adjusted his fedora — the gesture that meant *moving on* — and I understood that whatever he was feeling about Form 12-C, he was not ready to share it. Maybe not ever.

Some things you carry, and carrying them is the whole story. Some things don't need to be said out loud to be understood.

I put my hand on his shoulder. The alloy was warm. It was always warm, and I had never asked why. Maybe it was the power core. Maybe it was something else. Maybe eight hundred years of being warm to the touch was just what happened when you were built to care about people and then left alone for decades in a basement.

"For what it's worth," I said, "your file was wrong."

"Most files are." A pause. The glow steadied. "That is why you became a journalist instead of staying a clerk."

It was the closest thing to a compliment Clive had ever given me, and I chose not to ruin it by pointing that out. I let it sit there, in the air between us, where it belonged.

### Barry's Logs

A1 had been quiet. When A1 was quiet, it meant something was being processed that required the full weight of a consciousness that had once navigated the spaces between frequencies. The espresso machine at the heart of the ship hummed at a lower register than usual — a deep, contemplative resonance that Meatball responded to by pressing harder against Clive's leg. Even the dog understood. Even the dog knew when to be quiet.

"I have found something in the server's access logs," A1 said.

The display shifted. A table of entries — dates, timestamps, user IDs, files accessed. Thousands of lines spanning decades. But one pattern was unmistakable, because it repeated more than all the others combined.

**KOWALSKI_B — Night Shift**

Night after night after night. Twenty-three years of entries. Barry Kowalski, night security guard, Sub-Level 3, logging into a relay station data terminal he had no clearance to access, reading files he had no authorization to open. Frequency anomaly classifications. Builder structure reports. Signal pattern analyses. The deepest, most classified archives the DRM maintained — and Barry read them the way other people read the newspaper. Methodically. Completely. Every night.

As if the newspaper was about the end of the world and the reader was the only one who believed it.

"Twenty-three years," I said softly. I thought about my own four years at the DRM. Filing forms. Drinking A1's coffee. Not asking questions. I'd been so comfortable. So certain that the forms were the whole story. And Barry had been here, in a basement I'd never known existed, reading the files that proved otherwise, every single night for twenty-three years.

And no one had checked. No one had noticed. Because night security was invisible, and the DRM had never thought to watch the watcher.

"He accessed every frequency classification file in the system," A1 said. "Every anomaly report. Every Builder-adjacent signal analysis. The access pattern is not random. It is sequential. He read them in order, cross-referencing as he went. He was —" A1 paused, and the word that followed carried a weight that made my chest ache. "— studying."

Clive's glow brightened. Not surprise — recognition. Like finding a letter you'd forgotten you'd sent.

"Barry had a notebook," he said. "Brown leather. He filled one every eighteen months. He never let me read them."

"He was reading files about you," I said. "About things like you. Builder objects, frequency anomalies — he was trying to understand."

"He understood," Clive said. Simply. Certainly. With the bedrock conviction of someone who had spent twenty-three years sitting beside a man while he worked. "He understood before anyone."

I looked at the access logs scrolling down the screen. Twenty-three years of a night guard reading files by the light of a terminal he wasn't supposed to use, sitting next to a small robot he'd given a fedora, filling brown leather notebooks with careful handwriting, asking questions no one in the DRM thought to ask because they'd already filed the answers under "closed — no further action required."

I thought about that fedora. I thought about the fact that Clive still wore it, after all this time. I thought about what it meant that a night guard had looked at a filing error and seen a person.

I thought about my own four years at the DRM. Filing forms. Drinking A1's coffee. Not asking questions.

Barry had spent twenty-three years doing the opposite — asking every question — and the DRM had never noticed because he was night security and they never look at night security. He was beneath notice. He was exactly where he needed to be.

A1 produced a fresh espresso. The flavor was thick, almost syrupy — dark chocolate and something heavier underneath. The coffee A1 made when processing grief or loss. I tasted it and set it down carefully, because some things A1 said through coffee were too honest to drink quickly. The dark chocolate sat on my tongue like a weight, like an admission, like a grief that didn't have a name yet but was already real.

### Resolution

They worked in shifts. A1 archived everything — every file, every log, every scrap of data the relay station held. The servers gave up their contents in stuttering bursts as the power failed incrementally around them, lights dying corridor by corridor, the hum of machinery dropping in pitch as systems shut down one by one. It was like watching a body slowly stop breathing. It was like being there for something's last moments and not being able to stop it.

I catalogued what I could. Frequency anomaly files that described structures I'd glimpsed through A1's viewports — angular shapes, impossible architecture, things the DRM had detected and classified and filed away without ever going to look. Redacted reports on "Frequency Anomaly Classification Standards" that described phenomena the crew would later recognize — would later stand inside of — but which meant nothing to me yet except the queasy certainty that the DRM had known about all of it. Everything. From the beginning. From before I was born. From before the DRM had a name.

They'd known, and they'd filed, and they'd never told anyone, and now they were gone and their secrets were scattered across a dying station in the Space between spaces.

Mochi had not stopped pulsing. The violet glow was steady now — not the blazing intensity of that first moment in the server room, but a persistent, deep resonance that I could feel through the satchel like a second heartbeat. Whatever the servers held, whatever frequency data was stored in their dying circuits, Mochi was reading it. Not understanding it — Mochi did not understand. Mochi was a vessel carrying a memory she did not know she had, responding to resonance the way a compass responds to north.

But the reading was strong. Stronger than I'd ever felt. Strong enough that I wondered if Mochi had been here before. If Mochi had seen these servers when they were new. If Mochi was remembering something that happened before my lifetime, something that the DRM had stored and forgotten and Mochi had never stopped knowing.

I made a note. Filed it the way I filed everything Mochi did: *observed, unexplained, important.*

Clive moved through the station alone for a while. I let him. I watched him through the corridor windows — a small, stocky figure with a glowing head and a fedora, walking the institutional hallways with the deliberate pace of someone visiting a place that had tried to erase him. He stopped at Shelf 14-C in Sub-Level 3 storage. His shelf. The place where he'd sat for — I didn't know how long. Decades? Centuries? However long it took for a night guard named Barry to start talking to him, and for the talking to turn into something that lasted twenty-three years.

He stood there for four minutes. I counted. Then he adjusted his fedora and walked back. When he returned, his glow was different. Not brighter or dimmer. Different. Like something had been decided. Like something had been released.

Meatball met him at the corridor junction and walked beside him, matching his pace exactly, the way dogs do when they know someone needs company but not conversation. The dog didn't ask questions. The dog just walked beside him, and that was enough. That was always enough.

In the server room, as A1 pulled the last files from the failing systems, I noticed something on the data stream I hadn't seen before. Buried beneath the DRM's operational data, beneath the classified files and access logs and intake forms — a carrier signal. Not data. Not voice. A tone. Low, steady, threading underneath every transmission the relay station had ever made like a foundation beneath a building.

It was old. Older than the DRM. Older than the relay station. Older than the idea of relay stations. A frequency that predated the institution by centuries, embedded in the infrastructure like a fossil in stone, like a memory in bone.

I almost mentioned it. But when I looked at Clive, standing in the doorway with his glow at a steady medium blue, I saw that his head was tilted at an angle I didn't recognize. Not curiosity. Not consideration. Something else. Something private. Something that was his and his alone, something from before I was born, before the DRM existed, before the word "classified" meant anything at all.

He heard it. The tone. The carrier frequency that was older than the DRM, older than Barry, older than everything in this station except Clive himself. It was *his* frequency. It was the frequency of whoever he had been before he was filed under "inert office equipment."

He did not mention it. And I did not ask.

The lights failed in sequence — operations room first, then the corridors, then the server room. A1 confirmed the data transfer was complete. I zipped my jacket over Mochi — still warm, still pulsing that deep violet — and called Meatball. Clive walked beside me through the darkening corridors without needing the light, his body casting its own faint blue-white glow on the walls, Builder symbols tracing patterns that the DRM had once classified as "decorative etching, purpose unknown" because they couldn't imagine that something could be beautiful and functional and beyond their understanding all at once.

They sealed the airlock behind them. The docking collar retracted. A1 eased the ship away from the relay station with the gentle precision of a consciousness that understood what it meant to leave something behind — and that leaving was sometimes necessary, and sometimes kind, and sometimes the only way forward.

Through the viewport, the station tumbled — a dark rectangle against the static-laced void, its last running light still blinking red at three-second intervals. I watched. The light blinked. Blinked. Blinked. Like a heartbeat that was running out of beats. Like a signal that was still trying even when no one was listening anymore.

Then it didn't.

The station went dark. The DRM's last outpost, its last automated voice, its last signal — gone. Swallowed by the Space the way everything eventually is, the way the DRM itself had been swallowed, leaving behind only the data we'd pulled and the forms we'd filed and the twenty-three years of access logs that proved one night guard had been paying closer attention than anyone knew.

The data was everything we had left. The data was everything the DRM had hoarded and hidden and never used, and now it was ours, and we would do something with it that the DRM never could.

A1 produced espresso. The flavor had shifted — nutty now, warm, hazelnut and something grounding. The coffee A1 made when content with the presence of the crew. When being a ship felt like being a home. When the silence between stars felt less like emptiness and more like peace.

I wrapped my hands around the mug and breathed in the steam. I thought about Barry's notebooks. I thought about Clive's shelf. I thought about Mochi's violet glow and the carrier signal that Clive had heard and not mentioned. I thought about how many stories were still waiting to be understood.

"A1? That personnel file. Kowalski, B. Can you cross-reference it with anything else in the archive?"

"I have already begun." A pause. "Barry Kowalski's access pattern suggests a systematic investigation spanning two decades. He was not reading casually. He was building a map."

"A map of what?"

"I do not yet have enough data to answer that precisely. But the files he accessed most frequently concern frequency signatures that do not correspond to any catalogued location." Another pause — longer, the kind that meant A1 was choosing its next words with unusual care. "He was looking for something that the DRM had detected but could not find."

I looked at Clive. Clive was sitting on the console, feet planted, glow steady, fedora tilted at its usual angle. He was looking at the viewport where the relay station had been. There was nothing there now. Just the Space — vast, static-laced, humming with frequencies the DRM had spent ninety years trying to file into submission. The Space that didn't care about forms or classifications or "closed, no further action required."

"Clive?"

"Hm."

"Did Barry ever tell you what he was looking for?"

A pause. The longest I'd heard from him. His glow dimmed, brightened, dimmed again — searching, reaching, finding or not finding. The memory was old. The memory was eight hundred years old if you counted Clive's part in it, and ninety years old if you counted Barry's. The memory was the shape of a question that had never been fully answered.

"He said he was following a signal." Clive's voice was quiet. Even for Clive. "He said it was the most important thing he'd ever heard." A beat. The fedora adjusted — the gesture that meant *this is hard to say*. "He said when he found where it led, he would explain everything."

"And did he?"

"He left before he could. He followed the signal." A pause. "I intend to find him eventually."

Meatball sighed from his spot against the espresso machine's warm brass housing — the deep, settling sigh of a dog who knows the day is done even if the questions aren't. His paws twitched. Dreaming of frequencies that felt like warmth. Dreaming of something that was out there, waiting, calling in a voice that only certain people could hear.

I opened my broadcast console. The signal array hummed, ready, reaching out into the Space the way it always did — a voice into the void, hoping someone was listening. Hoping the signal found the right ears. Hoping that somewhere, someone was paying attention.

"This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the Ephergent. Third dispatch. We just left the last DRM relay station in existence. It's dark now. Backup power finally gave out. But before it did, we pulled the archives."

I paused. Took a breath. Let the weight of it settle.

"The DRM knew more than they told us. More than they told *anyone*. They had files on frequencies we haven't visited, structures we haven't found, signal patterns they detected and classified and buried in filing cabinets. Ninety years of data, and they sat on it."

I looked at Clive. He was watching me. His glow was steady. The fedora sat at its angle. The shadow fell where it always fell.

"They also had files on my crew. On things they couldn't explain. They took the most extraordinary objects and people and phenomena they'd ever encountered, and they filed them. Gave them form numbers. Stamped them 'closed, no further action required.' And moved on."

A beat. Mochi pulsed warm against my hip — violet fading back to pink, settling, content. The server data was inside her now. The data was safe. The data would be studied.

"The DRM couldn't see what was right in front of them. I think that's why they fell. Not because reality broke — because they'd been filing reality instead of understanding it. And when the forms stopped being enough to hold it, everything came apart."

I leaned back. The mug of espresso was warm in my hands. Hazelnut. Home.

"We have the data. We'll keep looking. If you're out there — stay tuned."

I cut the signal. The Space held us in its vast, humming silence. Somewhere in the data A1 was processing, Barry Kowalski's twenty-three years of questions waited to be understood. Somewhere in the frequency Clive had heard and not mentioned, something older than institutions pulsed — patient, enduring, unclassified. Something that didn't need a form number because it didn't need to be filed. It just was.

Clive sat on the console and said nothing. The fedora cast its shadow. His glow was blue-white and steady, the color of Builder signal energy, the color of something that had been filed under "inert" and was — quietly, deliberately, in its own time — proving the form wrong. Proving it wrong the way only the truth can: by existing anyway. By being too real to stay in a box.
