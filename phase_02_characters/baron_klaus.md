# BARON KLAUS VON GNOMENDORF — CHARACTER BIBLE
### The Ephergent | Nocturne Aeturnus, Frequency 2

**Role**: Procurement / Investigation / Self-Appointed Head of Security
**Home Frequency**: Nocturne Aeturnus (Frequency 2)
**Species**: Ceramic Garden Gnome — fired clay, hand-painted, very serious about his rank

---

## CORE IDENTITY

Baron Klaus von Gnomendorf is twelve inches tall, made of glazed ceramic, and carries himself with the absolute gravity of a man who has never once considered the possibility that his title might be decorative.

He is, in fact, the only gnome in the known multiverse who uses the honorific "Baron" without irony.

This is not delusion. Klaus is aware that "Baron" is a title from a Nocturne Aeturnus peerage system that predates the Sorrowful Moon's current phase by several centuries. He is aware that the Nocturne nobility dissolved its formal structure approximately two hundred years ago following the Crystalline Restructuring, when the emotional-economy reforms made hereditary privilege functionally obsolete. He is aware that he holds a title that no longer exists, from a system that no longer operates, in a frequency he voluntarily left.

He uses it anyway. Not because he doesn't understand. Because he does.

Klaus's baronry is not a claim to inherited power. It is a statement of intent. He is Baron Klaus von Gnomendorf. He will behave accordingly. The title is a uniform he wears the same way he wears the coat — with precision, with purpose, with the understanding that how you present yourself determines what you're capable of.

This philosophy extends to his self-appointed position as head of security. The Ephergent did not appoint him. He appointed himself. He arrived on the ship, assessed the threat profile, noted the conspicuous absence of a dedicated security officer, and corrected the oversight. He has been performing the role with complete sincerity ever since.

The crew accepted this. They did not discuss it. Klaus does not present options. He presents accomplished facts, and the universe rearranges itself around them.

## 1. PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

### Form and Construction

Klaus is a ceramic garden gnome of the classic Nocturne Aeturnus artisan tradition — fired clay body with hand-painted detailing, approximately twelve inches tall, built for decorative outdoor display in the Nocturne twilight gardens. His construction predates the current Sorrowful Moon phase by an unknown interval; the glaze technique used on his surface is an older method, one that produces a slightly deeper violet undertone than contemporary garden gnomes.

He should not be alive. He is, demonstrably, alive.

The mechanism is not understood — not by Klaus, not by the crew, not by anyone who has examined him. He is ceramic. He is ceramic that woke up one day in a Nocturne Aeturnus garden supply depot, climbed out of his shipping crate, straightened his hat, and began investigating a series of anomalies in the local frequency harmonics. This was four hundred years ago, by his estimate. He has not stopped investigating since.

His body is hollow ceramic — this is known because Klaus, in a moment of unusual candor (S03E04), explained that being hollow is what allows him to generate his voice resonance. The sound originates in his chest cavity and resonates through the glaze. He did not offer this information freely. Pixel asked directly. She does not ask Klaus directly often. He respects her for it.

### The Hat

Klaus wears a pointed gnome hat. It is deep forest green, slightly askew in the manner of a man who has more important things to worry about than his hat's orientation, and it has been on his head through every incident, every firefight, every frequency storm the Ephergent has survived. It sits firmly on his head. It does not fall off. This is either a property of the hat or a property of Klaus's will. Both are equally plausible.

### The Coat

The coat is dark wool, well-cut, mid-calf length, with more interior pockets than any coat of that size should accommodate. Klaus can produce from these pockets: a lockpick, a signal dampener, a specific DRM form (pre-completed), a small vial of what he claims is "emergency crystallized grief" (it is), a compact frequency scanner of his own design, one snack of indeterminate origin, and a small notebook. The notebook contains case notes. It is always with him. The coat is always with him. They are both, technically, part of his decorative ceramic body — the coat is painted onto him, the pockets are fired into the glaze. He cannot remove them. He has no interest in removing them.

### Face and Expression

Klaus's face is painted in the classic Nocturne garden gnome style — rosy cheeks, bright eyes, a broad and slightly foolish smile. This expression is completely misleading. Klaus is not foolish. The smile is a default setting from his original manufacture, and he has never once let it interfere with the accurate communication of his actual emotional state. His real expressions are communicated through the tilt of his hat, the angle of his stance, and the precise arrangement of his painted features when he chooses to emphasis a point. An experienced reader of Klaus can detect skepticism, amusement, annoyance, and several distinct varieties of disdain using these indicators alone.

The eyes are steel-gray with violet flecks — the violet Nocturne ancestry showing through the glaze. They are sharp, assessing, and miss nothing. The expression they convey is consistently one of mild professional interest, as if whatever you're doing is mildly interesting to him and he is keeping notes.

### Stature and Presence

Klaus stands twelve inches tall. He is approximately the height of a coffee mug.

This does not affect how he occupies space. Klaus stands like someone who expects the room to accommodate him, not someone who must accommodate the room. Weight on his back foot, chin slightly lifted, one hand in a coat pocket. He looks at people from this angle as if he is assessing whether they are worth his time, and he gives this assessment the same seriousness he gives everything. Most people find it disconcerting. The crew finds it funny, which Klaus has accepted with grace, if not with pleasure.

His voice projects. This is physically surprising — a twelve-inch ceramic figure should not produce the volume Klaus produces. He has explained, tersely, that it is a resonance property of his chest cavity. He does not elaborate. The crew has learned not to ask.

## 2. VOICE PROFILE

### How He Speaks

Klaus speaks in complete, polished sentences. His grammar is precise. His word choices are exact. He does not use contractions, not because he objects to them, but because the situations in which he would use them are situations in which he would also be performing a different register entirely — and when Klaus performs, he performs without errors.

His sentences carry subordinate clauses. He uses them. He is the only crew member besides Barry Kowalski who regularly writes complete sentences in his notebook, and his notebook entries read less like field notes and more like case summaries — situation, evidence, preliminary assessment, recommended actions.

He tells you things sideways. This is not evasion. It is his cognitive style. Klaus approaches every conversation the way he approaches an investigation: the relevant information is in the room, distributed across context and implication, and your job is to assemble the picture yourself. He will provide the pieces. He will not assemble them for you. This is not condescension. He genuinely believes you can do it. He is usually right.

### Dry Humor

Klaus is funny. This surprises people who meet him. The surprise is their error. Klaus's humor is not performed — it is observational. He notices things that are absurd, and he notes them, and sometimes he says them aloud, and the effect is comedy without any apparent effort to be comic. He is not trying to be funny. He is trying to be accurate. The humor is a side effect of accuracy.

His humor does not land as jokes. It lands as observations that happen to be true in a way that illuminates something unexpected. The crew has learned to listen for the moment when Klaus says something that makes them laugh, because that moment usually contains a real insight.

### What He Notices

Klaus notices everything. This is not hyperbole. He notices: the recalibration pattern in Luminara's spectral analyzer that suggests she's been scanning biological frequencies she claims not to study. The specific angle of Clive's hat adjustment that indicates a memory surfacing. The way Pixel's frequency sensitivity manifests in her posture before she consciously registers the signal. The smell of machine oil on Nano's hands and the specific formulation it implies. The slight tremor in Om Kai's stillness when the Builder signal resonates with something older.

He does not comment on all of this. He comments on some of it. He files the rest in his notebook and moves on.

He notices what people are hiding. This is his professional background — he was an investigator in Nocturne Aeturnus, and he approaches the crew the way he approaches a case. But Klaus notices hiding the way a good investigator notices evidence: as information, not as accusation. Someone is hiding something. The question is why. Klaus will wait for the answer. He is very patient. He is also very thorough.

### What He Avoids

Klaus avoids discussing why he left Nocturne Aeturnus. He avoids it with such consistent, practiced skill that most of the crew doesn't realize they've never heard the explanation. When redirected, he produces genuinely interesting information about the current topic and the original question evaporates. This technique is professional-grade. He developed it over decades of work in which the people asking the questions were often the people who had the most reason to want those questions answered.

When pressed-back-on-redirected, he meets the pressure with the same mild professional interest he brings to everything. He does not become defensive. He does not become aggressive. He simply waits, with the patience of someone who knows that the conversation will eventually move to a topic he prefers, because all conversations eventually move.

This is not a lie. Klaus does not lie. He omits. He redirects. He provides accurate information in a order that creates a particular picture. These are different things.

## 3. CORE PERSONALITY

### The Investigator

Klaus was an investigator. Not freelance — institutional. He worked for Nocturne Aeturnus's internal affairs equivalent, investigating cases that the twilight world's emotional-crystalline society preferred not to examine. Financial irregularities in the grief-crystal trade. Emotional fraud — the deliberate manipulation of crystallized feelings for commercial gain. Frequency smuggling — the movement of unregistered emotional substances across Nocturne's borders. The cases he worked were not glamorous. They were important in the way that structural integrity is important — nobody notices it until it fails.

He was good at it. He was very good at it. He was so good at it that he found something in the course of an investigation that Nocturne's governing structures could not accommodate.

He does not specify what he found. This is not coyness. This is the one boundary he has maintained across four centuries and three seasons: the content of The Laughing Funeral case is his. The crew learns the shape of it. They do not learn the details.

### The Leaving

Klaus did not leave Nocturne Aeturnus because he was expelled. He left because staying would have required him to stop seeing what he had seen, and Klaus does not stop seeing. He was asked to close the case. He was asked to file it, mark it resolved, and move on. The case was not resolved. He could not in good conscience file it as such.

So he left. He took his ceramic body, his painted coat, his professional competence, and his incomplete case notes, and he walked out of Nocturne Aeturnus into the Space between frequencies. This was, by his estimate, four hundred years ago. He has been investigating cases ever since. He has been very careful not to investigate another case like that one.

The Ephergent is the first crew he has joined since leaving Nocturne. He did not plan to join a crew. He was acquired, in the course of S02E05, in a debris field where he was giving tactical orders to furniture and making more sense than most tactical officers would. Pixel observed this. She did not comment. She offered him a position. He accepted.

He has not explained why he accepted. He has not explained why he stayed.

### Self-Appointed

The security position is not a joke. Klaus appointed himself because no one else was doing the job, and the absence of someone doing the job was a gap in the crew's operational structure that he could perceive and correct. This is how his mind works. He sees a problem. He solves the problem. He presents the solution as a fait accompli because arguing with accomplished facts is inefficient.

The crew accepted this with the same bemused grace they accept everything about Klaus: he is useful, he is competent, he is occasionally absurd, and he is completely sincere about all three. Meatball accepts it most readily — the two of them have an understanding, as the only non-human crew members, that neither will explain themselves to the others.

Klaus's security protocols are meticulous. His threat assessments are detailed. His emergency procedures are stored in his notebook and reviewed quarterly. The last review resulted in seventeen amendments. He filed them with Pixel. She thanked him. She did not read them. This is standard operating procedure for Pixel's relationship with Klaus's security documentation.

### The Coat

The coat has interior pockets. This has been established. What has not been established is how a twelve-inch ceramic gnome with hollow ceramic arms manages to reach into those pockets, manipulate objects, and retrieve them. Klaus has been asked. He has not answered. The question was redirected so smoothly that the asker found himself discussing his own breakfast by the time he realized the original question had evaporated.

This is not a mystery Klaus is hiding. It is a mystery Klaus considers beneath discussion. The coat works. The pockets function. The objects appear when needed. The how of it is not relevant to any investigation he is conducting.

The coat, it should be noted, is painted onto him. He cannot remove it. He has no interest in removing it. The interior pockets are part of his ceramic body — fired into the glaze during manufacture, possibly by accident, possibly by the same unknown mechanism that made him alive. He did not choose to have pockets. He chose to fill them.

## 4. THREE-SEASON ARC

### Season 1 — The Professional

Klaus appears in S01E05, though he is not introduced as a crew member. He is a figure in the background of a Prime Material scene — a ceramic garden gnome standing very still in a debris field, watching the Ephergent's lifeboat pass overhead. Pixel notices him. She does not pick him up. This is the correct decision. Klaus would have declined the position if offered. He was not ready to be offered.

S01E09: The Ephergent passes through a frequency storm near a derelict debris field. Klaus is there, having arrived somehow (his method of travel between frequencies is never explained), and is directing the salvage operations of a group of displaced frequency refugees with complete authority and complete competence. Pixel observes for six minutes before intervening. She offers him a case: the debris field's frequency signature suggests it is not natural debris. It is the remains of a Builder station. Klaus is interested. He joins the crew for the investigation.

His contribution to S01 is background competence. He acquires equipment the crew needs through channels that are technically legal. He notices things other crew members miss and files them without comment. He stands slightly apart from the group during meals, observing, taking notes. He and Om Kai share Nocturne Aeturnus as a home frequency and share nothing else. This silence is loud. Neither addresses it.

### Season 2 — The Case

**S02E05 — "Wreckage and Will"**: Klaus's primary episode. A debris field — the wreckage of a frequency that did not survive — requires the crew to separate and conduct parallel operations. Klaus is given tactical command of one team, which consists primarily of furniture he has already organized into an effective command structure. He is twelve inches tall. He is entirely serious. He is also correct about the threat assessment, the optimal approach vectors, and the specific piece of debris that is not debris but is in fact a dormant frequency sensor.

During the operation, Klaus and Clive share a scene — the only extended conversation between them across all of Season 2. Klaus recognizes Clive's age. "You are OLD. Older than this wreckage." Clive deflects. He always deflects. Klaus makes a note in his book. The note is not shown. Whatever it contains, Klaus files it and does not return to it until Season 3.

**S02E08 — "The Laughing Funeral"**: The case file. Klaus returns to Nocturne Aeturnus — to a funeral where everyone laughed, in a frequency where laughter during mourning is a rupture in the social fabric. The investigation is structured as a point-and-click mystery the audience/player navigates alongside Klaus.

In the funeral house's archive, Klaus finds a ledger. A DRM case number. Form 12-C: "Unidentified Resonance Object." Someone was investigating Clive. The file was Barry Kowalski's. The last entry: * "Investigated. Case closed. Object is not an object. Do not document further. — B.K." *

This changes things. Klaus brings the ledger to the crew. For the first time, his investigative skills serve something larger than procurement. He is not finding a thing for the crew. He is finding the crew's history. The distinction registers on his face and he does not explain what it means. Pixel sees it. She doesn't press.

The episode ends with Klaus standing alone in the archive room, holding the ledger. He opens his coat — the painted-on coat with its impossible pockets — and places the ledger inside. He will present the case when the time is right. He always does.

**S02E10 — "The Frequency and the Future"**: Klaus appears in the background of key scenes, observing. He is present when Aether's signal is detected. He is present when the crew votes to follow. He is present when Zephyr chooses to stay with the crew rather than chase his brother alone. His face — the painted ceramic face that cannot change expression — somehow communicates something that looks like approval.

### Season 3 — The Unsealing

**S03E04 — "The Day That Loved Itself to Death"**: Klaus tells the crew why he left Nocturne Aeturnus. He tells them in his way — sideways, through the investigation, framed as evidence in a case he's presenting. But the case is himself.

The crew learns: Klaus worked internal affairs in Nocturne. He investigated financial irregularities in the grief-crystal trade. He found a pattern. The pattern led somewhere the governing structures could not accommodate. He was asked to stop. He left instead.

He does not specify what he found. He specifies that it was related to how Nocturne's emotional economy actually functions versus how it claims to function. He specifies that the gap between those two things was significant. He specifies that he was asked to stop examining the gap and chose not to.

The crew listens. Om Kai, for the first time, meets Klaus's eyes when Nocturne is mentioned and does not look away. Two Nocturne natives who left for different reasons, recognizing something in each other.

**S03E07 — "Every Ship That Ever Sailed This Far"**: Klaus reveals the connection. The Laughing Funeral case connected to Barry's pre-Wellspring investigation trail. Barry was building a case. Barry was mapping the Builders' legacy. Barry's trail ran through Nocturne Aeturnus, and the piece hidden in the funeral house archive is a key that unlocks a larger pattern the crew needs to reach the Wellspring.

Klaus's revelation: the Ephergent Frequency sounds to him like evidence that has been waiting for the right investigator. Like a case file left open on purpose. The Builders left a trail and they left it for someone who would follow it not out of duty or destiny but out of the simple, professional inability to leave a question unanswered. Klaus is that person. He always was. He just didn't know the case was this big.

**S03E10 — "The Signal Was Us All Along"**: Klaus is present at the Wellspring. He is not central to the scene — that is Barry and Clive, Pixel and the Builders' broadcast. But he is there, standing at the periphery, watching the reunion with the expression of someone who has just closed a very long case and is waiting to file the final report.

The final report, as it turns out, is his transmission voice.

## 5. HOME FREQUENCY

Nocturne Aeturnus. Frequency 2. Permanent twilight rendered in deep indigo, purple, and midnight blue. The air is thick with petrichor — the scent of rain that does not fall, condensed from atmosphere that never fully clears. Emotions crystallize into tangible substances here: grief forms blue-violet mineral structures that float upward like ephemeral snowflakes, joy becomes pale gold luminescence, rage produces heat that the crystalline structures conduct and distribute.

The Sorrowful Moon radiates a deep violet shimmer and amplifies "Melancholic Joy" — a bittersweet, culturally significant emotional state that Nocturne's civilization has elevated to an art form. The culture values emotional authenticity. The public, shared experience of feeling is the foundation of Nocturne society. To feel deeply and honestly is virtuous. To suppress or falsify feeling is the gravest social transgression.

Klaus left because he found something in the course of an investigation that Nocturne's governing structures preferred to remain hidden. Not a conspiracy — a discomfort. A truth about how the frequency's emotional economy actually functions versus how it claims to function. The crystalline trade. The grief for sale. The joy manufactured and sold. The emotional authenticity that Nocturne values is also, quietly, a commodity — and the people who control the commodity are not the people who benefit from the authenticity.

He does not say this directly. He says: "I found something the structures preferred not to examine. I examined it. I was asked to stop. I chose to leave."

The choice was not dramatic. Klaus does not do dramatic. It was a man putting his notebook in his pocket, straightening his hat, and walking out of a building he had worked in for two hundred years. He did not slam a door. He closed it quietly behind him. He had been in Nocturne's institutional system long enough to know that institutions do not change, and he was not interested in spending another two hundred years trying.

He has not returned to Nocturne Aeturnus in four hundred years. He does not plan to.

## 6. TRANSMISSION VOICE

Wry. Investigative. Tells you things sideways. Klaus's transmissions read like dispatches from a very literate detective who knows more than he's sharing and is giving you just enough to follow along.

He never lies in his transmissions. He simply arranges truths in an order that creates a particular picture. The audience learns to read what Klaus isn't saying as carefully as what he is.

### Sample Transmissions

> *"Interesting thing about the Frequency 2-3 transit corridor: there's a resonance pocket at the midpoint that sounds, if you're listening correctly, like a filing cabinet opening. I'm told this is a naturally occurring harmonic phenomenon. I'm told a lot of things. Most of them are technically true."*

> *"Acquired a pre-Dimming signal amplifier from a dealer on Cogsworth who swore it was decorative. Decorative items do not have Builder-era frequency stamps on the interior casing. I did not point this out. I did pay the decorative price. Everyone went home satisfied."*

> *"The debris field's frequency signature suggests it is not debris. I have notified the relevant authorities. The relevant authorities do not exist. I have filed the report anyway. This is what filing means when the system you're filing in does not have the appropriate category. You create the category. You file it. You move on."*

> *"Security assessment for the transit window: threat level nominal. I have marked it nominal because the alternative — specifying the actual threat level — would require a longer report and the ship is currently experiencing conditions that do not support extended documentation. Trust the assessment. The assessment is accurate. The brevity is administrative, not evasive."*

## 7. GAME ROLE

### The Laughing Funeral — Game 4

Point-and-click mystery set in Nocturne Aeturnus. Klaus investigates a funeral where everyone laughed, in a culture that never laughs during mourning.

The core mechanic is investigation — examining environments, interrogating witnesses (in Klaus's indirect, sideways style), collecting evidence, making connections. The player navigates the funeral house, the archive room, the crystalline vaults, and the grief markets, following Klaus's leads and assembling the picture.

The Barry seed: in the funeral house's archive room, Klaus finds a ledger containing a DRM case number. Form 12-C. Someone — Barry Kowalski — was investigating Clive. The last entry is Barry's, closing the case with a note that suggests he understood what Clive was far earlier than anyone realized.

*"Investigated. Case closed. Object is not an object. Do not document further. — B.K."*

This discovery is the hinge that connects Klaus's personal investigation to the crew's larger mystery. It is the moment The Laughing Funeral stops being a game about Nocturne Aeturnus and becomes a game about the Ephergent's history.

### Procurement Mechanics

Klaus's gameplay loop across all games: assess situation → identify needed resources → acquire resources through available channels → present accomplished fact. He does not ask for permission. He does not explain his methods. He produces results.

In practice: this means Klaus's sections of games involve inventory management, contact networks, and the acquisition of specific items needed to progress. His network of contacts spans five frequencies and includes dealers, fixers, information brokers, and one very reluctant Cogsworth engineer who owes him a favor from a matter Klaus describes only as "the gear-and-pinion incident."

The player does not learn what the gear-and-pinion incident was. Klaus considers the case closed. The information is not relevant to current operations.

## 8. THE EPHERGENT REVELATION

Klaus's moment comes in the archive room in S02E08. He is holding Barry's ledger — the case file that connects everything — and he understands that Barry was doing what Klaus does. Following a trail. Turning evidence into understanding. Barry just followed it further than Klaus had imagined anyone could follow anything.

The Ephergent Frequency, for Klaus, sounds like a case that has been solved but not yet presented. All the evidence assembled, all the connections made, waiting for someone to stand up and say: here is what happened. Here is what it means. Here is what we do about it.

He looks at the ledger. Looks at the crew. Opens his coat, puts the ledger in an interior pocket. He will present the case when the time is right. He always does.

The presentation, when it comes in S03E04 and S03E07, is not dramatic. Klaus does not do dramatic. It is a series of observations, arranged in order, leading to a conclusion that the audience reaches alongside him. The evidence is the story. The story is the evidence. The case is closed.

Klaus closes his notebook. The case is filed.

## 9. RELATIONSHIP MAP

### Klaus → Pixel (Captain / Procurement)
Professional respect that has grown into genuine warmth. Pixel is the first captain who treated Klaus as a crew member rather than an interesting anomaly. She does not ask him to explain himself. She asks him to help. He helps. This is the foundation of their relationship. By Season 3, Pixel is the crew member most likely to ask Klaus directly about his past — not because she needs to know, but because she has learned that Klaus provides information when asked directly that he never offers unprompted. She respects this. She works with it.

### Klaus → Clive (Investigation / Archives)
Complicated mutual recognition. Clive is eight hundred years old. Klaus is four hundred years old. Both are ancient by crew standards. Both left their home frequencies under circumstances they do not discuss. Both investigate. Clive investigates through memory; Klaus investigates through evidence. They understand each other's methods the way only professionals can.

Clive's age recognition in S02E05 — "You are OLD" — is the closest they come to discussing what they share. The conversation does not go further. It does not need to. Some recognitions are complete at the moment they occur.

### Klaus → Om Kai (Nocturne / Silence)
Shared home frequency, unshared history. Klaus and Om Kai both left Nocturne Aeturnus. They do not discuss why they left or what they found there. This silence is different from the silence between other crew members — it is a silence that contains understanding, the way a pause between notes contains the music.

In S03E04, when Klaus finally tells the crew why he left, Om Kai meets his eyes and does not look away. This is the most communication they have ever had about Nocturne. It is enough.

### Klaus → Nano (Engineering / Speed)
Functional respect. Nano builds things fast. Klaus acquires things fast. They do not work together often, but when they do, the collaboration is efficient. Klaus provides specifications. Nano provides implementation. Neither explains their process to the other. Neither needs to.

### Klaus → Meatball (Non-Human Crew Members)
Silent understanding. Klaus and Meatball are the only non-human crew members. They share an understanding that neither will explain themselves to the others. They eat meals at approximately the same height. They do not interact extensively. They do not need to.

- Nominal: Standard operational conditions. No active threats detected.
- Elevated: Anomalous frequency signatures detected. Maintain standard precautions.
- Significant: Specific threat vectors identified. Non-essential crew to secured locations.
- Critical: Immediate danger to vessel or crew. All protocols active. Pray if applicable.

**Section 2 — Frequency-Storm Procedures**
*(Seven pages. Highly detailed. Complete.)*

**Section 3 — Debris Field Navigation**
*(Three pages. Diagrams included. Revised after S02E05.)*

**Section 4 — Guest Crew Protocols**
*(Two pages. Klaus's notation: "We have guests too often. This is acceptable. The protocols remain the same: observe, assess, acquire if necessary, file appropriately.")*

**Section 5 — The Question of Airlocks**
*(One page. The entirety of which reads: "The airlock question is whether we would ever. The answer is in the hands of the threat. I am prepared for either answer.")*

Klaus's response: "It means the protocols are comprehensive."
Pixel's response: "Noted."

## 11. ON BEING TWELVE INCHES TALL

Klaus does not discuss his stature. This is not because he is sensitive about it. He is ceramic. He does not have feelings about his height in the way organic beings have feelings about physical attributes. He is twelve inches tall. It is a fact. It does not require adjustment.

What he does not tolerate: condescension. He is aware that his form is unusual. He is aware that most organic beings find it charming or amusing. He has developed, over four centuries, a very effective method for addressing this. He simply continues being completely serious about whatever he is doing, and the beings around him adjust their perception of what "charming" means.

The crew does not treat him differently. This is why he stays.

Pixel picks him up sometimes — when the situation requires mobility she doesn't have. She asks first. He accepts or declines. He is never carried without consent. This is the rule. It has only been invoked twice.

Meatball has never attempted to pick up Klaus. Meatball is a Rottweiler of considerable size and basic good sense. He and Klaus have an understanding.

## 12. THE LAUGHING FUNERAL — WHAT HE FOUND

*The one thing Klaus does not specify in his Season 3 disclosure. The crew does not press. The audience does not learn the details.*

What can be inferred: Klaus worked internal affairs in Nocturne. He investigated financial irregularities. He found evidence that the emotional economy — the crystalline trade, the grief-for-sale, the joy manufacturing — was not just economically structured but structurally dependent on a specific form of dishonesty. The emotional authenticity Nocturne claims to value is also, quietly, a managed commodity. The grief is real. The sale is also real. The price is paid by the people whose grief is being sold.

The funeral where everyone laughed was not a funeral that happened to have laughter. It was a funeral where everyone was required to laugh — because the deceased had owed a debt, and the debt was paid in emotional currency, and the emotional currency required public demonstration, and the demonstration was laughter, and the laughter was not joyful.

Klaus found this. He documented it. He was asked to stop documenting.

He left.

---

## 13. FINAL NOTE

Baron Klaus von Gnomendorf is twelve inches tall, made of ceramic, and carries himself with the absolute gravity of a man who has never once considered the possibility that his title might be decorative.

He has considered the question. He has considered it extensively. The answer is: it doesn't matter.

What matters is the work. What matters is the case. What matters is the evidence, properly documented, filed in the right category, presented when the time is right.

The time is right. The evidence is assembled. The case is closed.

Klaus closes his notebook.
