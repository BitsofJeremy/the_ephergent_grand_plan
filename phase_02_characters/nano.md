# NANO — CHARACTER BIBLE
### The Ephergent | Cogsworth Cogitarium, Frequency 3

**Role**: Intelligence Officer · Info Broker · Scout/Infiltrator
**Home Frequency**: Cogsworth Cogitarium (Frequency 3)
**Species**: Human — Cogsworth-raised, frequency-displaced
**Season Intro**: 2

---

## CORE IDENTITY

Nano is fast. Not fast for an engineer, not fast for someone from Cogsworth where precision is religion and urgency is heresy. Nano is just *fast* — the kind of fast that makes people miss things, except Nano doesn't miss things because she moves so quickly through a space that she experiences it completely before anyone else has finished processing the first detail.

She is small, sharp, and constantly in motion even when she is standing still. She talks faster than anyone can track. She builds faster than anyone can follow. She processes the universe at a speed that should be incoherent but somehow isn't — because every word she says is precise, every technical reference is accurate, every leap in her logic is earned.

She left Cogsworth because she understood something the frequency couldn't: precision without speed is a clock that shows the right time for a moment that has already passed. The universe is desynchronizing. The Drift is accelerating. You can calibrate a gear to perfect tolerance and by the time you've finished, the tolerance has shifted because the frequency itself has shifted. Cogsworth's master engineers were building perfect mechanisms for a universe that no longer held still long enough to use them.

Nano didn't wait. She moved. She built at the speed the crisis demanded. She became the fastest thing in the Space between frequencies, and she has been running ever since — not from something, but toward something she hasn't found yet.

She knows everyone. She owes everyone. Everyone owes her. This is the economy she runs on: information as currency, favors as trade goods, trust as the only thing worth more than both. She is the Ephergent's intelligence officer not because she was appointed but because she arrived knowing things she shouldn't know, and she keeps knowing things she shouldn't know because she never stops moving long enough for anyone to track where she got them.

## 1. PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

### Form and Construction

Nano is compact — wiry, low-centered, built for velocity rather than power. She stands slightly under average height, but her posture is aggressive rather than small: weight forward, chin up, eyes already three steps ahead of her body. She is the kind of person who looks like she's about to sprint even when she's standing still.

Her features are sharp, fox-like. Her eyes are amber — warm, bright, Cogsworth-calibrated — and they move constantly, tracking details, calculating clearances, assessing structural integrity of everything in visual range. These are not the eyes of someone who observes. These are the eyes of someone who *scans*. The distinction matters. An observer notices what matters. A scanner notices everything and decides in real time what to keep.

Her hair is short, practical, copper-red, and almost always sticking up in three different directions. She runs her hands through it while thinking, and she is always thinking, and she is always thinking about multiple things simultaneously. The chaos of her hair is a side effect of the velocity of her cognition — she processes faster than her hands can maintain a neat appearance, and she has long since stopped caring.

Traces of Cogsworth cling to her at the cellular level. There are faint gear-tooth impressions along her forearms — not scars but patterns, indentations from years of pressing against machinery during high-speed repairs. Her finger movements have a clockwork precision that surfaces when she's doing fine mechanical work: exactly the right pressure, exactly the right angle, applied with the confidence of someone who has done this ten thousand times and has internalized the optimal solution so completely that thought is no longer required.

She smells of machine oil and burnt sugar. Machine oil because she is always elbow-deep in A1's internals, and A1's internals run on Builder-era lubricant that smells like machine oil. Burnt sugar because Cogsworth's atmospheric signature is caramel and copper and the particular sweetness of clockwork mechanisms running hot, and that smell has baked into her at a level beyond washing. She does not try to remove it. It is, in its way, her home frequency's signature on her skin.

### The Jumpsuit

She wears a fitted jumpsuit, charcoal-gray, with more pockets than a jumpsuit that fitted should accommodate. All of the pockets are filled: tools, components, salvage, scraps of frequency-shifted metal that she has collected because she might need them later and later comes fast. The jumpsuit has reinforced knees and elbows for the inevitable moments when speed requires sliding, diving, or throwing herself through spaces that don't have enough clearance for running.

Her boots have reinforced soles designed for actual running — not metaphorical running, not the poetic kind. Nano runs everywhere. Through A1's corridors. Between engineering bays. From one end of a derelict station to the other while entropy fragments chase her. Her boots are designed for grip, speed, and impact absorption because Nano has been running fast enough to need shock absorption since before she understood that most people don't run everywhere.

### Hands

Her hands are her most distinctive feature after her velocity. Small, scarred, constantly busy. She builds while she talks. She disassembles while she listens. She has a habit of producing a small component from a pocket, turning it in her fingers like a worry stone, and putting it back — having apparently improved it in the interval. Her tools are Cogsworth-standard precision instruments, maintained to specifications that would satisfy the Grand Orrery's master engineers. She keeps them perfectly. She uses them at speeds the master engineers would find alarming.

When Nano's hands go still, something is wrong. This is the only reliable diagnostic indicator for her emotional state. Her hands do not go still often.

## 2. VOICE PROFILE

### How She Talks

Nano talks fast. This is not a style choice. It is the only speed she has.

She picks up in the middle of a thought, assumes you've been following the internal monologue that preceded the verbal output, and continues at a pace that requires active effort to track. She is not incoherent — every word is precise, every technical reference is accurate. She is just faster than the conversation expects her to be. She is always three steps ahead of her own sentences.

She says "— right, so —" a lot, bridging between thoughts she's already processed and thoughts she's explaining. She interrupts herself. She answers questions before they're finished because she's modeled where the question was going and arrived at the conclusion while the questioner was still formulating the words. This is not rude. It is Cogsworth processing speed applied to social interaction, and it takes adjustment.

The key to writing Nano's dialogue: she is always in the middle of something. She is always about to be somewhere else. Her sentences are fragments that would be complete if you had the other seventy percent of her thoughts. They are complete in her head. On paper, they look like intercepted transmissions.

### What She Notices

Nano notices mechanical failure before it happens. She hears the wrong sound in an engine cycle — a half-cycle variance that everyone else would call within normal parameters. She feels vibration through her boots and knows which component is three cycles from desynchronization. She speaks to machines the way Om Kai speaks to people: with attention that borders on communion. She is not projecting human emotion onto machines. She is recognizing the machine's equivalent of distress because she has spent enough time with machines to hear it.

She notices information gaps. When someone is lying, Nano doesn't look for the lie — she looks for the shape of what's missing. The space where information should be. She maps the hole and knows that the hole is the important thing, not whatever is being said to fill it.

### What She Avoids

Nano avoids slowing down. The rare moments when Nano is still are the moments when something is genuinely wrong. She avoids explaining her emotional state with the same dedication she applies to everything else. She will explain a warp-drive recalibration in exquisite detail and then summarize her feelings about her home frequency as "it's fine, it's complicated, hand me that wrench."

She avoids the question of what she's running toward. She has built an identity around velocity, and velocity implies direction, and the direction she implies is always *forward* — toward the next problem, the next fix, the next crisis that needs her hands. Underneath the velocity is the fear that if she slows down, she'll see clearly how much has already been lost. She has never named this fear. She has never needed to. The running says it for her.

## 3. CORE PERSONALITY

### The Central Tension

Nano is precise AND fast. This is unusual for Cogsworth Cogitarium.

Cogsworth produces precise engineers. The Grand Orrery's master builders can calibrate a gear to tolerances measured in microns. They work slowly, methodically, and their precision is the foundation of Cogsworth's mechanical civilization. Everything is exactly on time because everyone takes exactly enough time.

Nano understood precision. She was raised in it. But she also understood something Cogsworth's engineering culture could not accommodate: entropy doesn't wait. The universe is desynchronizing. The Builder Stations are failing. The Drift intensifies every decade. You can calibrate a gear to perfect tolerance, and by the time you've finished, the tolerance has shifted because the frequency itself has shifted. Precision without speed is a clock that always shows the right time for a moment that has already passed.

She left because she could not slow down, and Cogsworth could not speed up. She is not rejected by her home frequency — she is simply incompatible with its tempo. The master engineers respected her precision. They were bewildered by her urgency. "The clock will wait," they told her. "No," she said. "It won't."

She was right. The Grand Orrery's increasing desynchronization events prove it. She takes no satisfaction in being right. Being right about catastrophe is not a victory.

### The Gift and the Avoidance

Her central tension: she can outrun the entropy that chases everything, but she cannot outrun the question of what she's running toward.

Speed is her gift and her avoidance. She builds to solve problems, and she builds fast because the problems are getting worse, and somewhere underneath the velocity is the fear that if she slows down, she'll see clearly how much has already been lost. She has never stopped long enough to look. She is not certain she wants to.

The Ephergent crew is the first thing she has run toward instead of away from. She has not examined this closely. She has installed seventeen upgrades to A1's hull resonance dampeners in the three months since she joined. This is either genuine engineering enthusiasm or a very sophisticated avoidance mechanism. Both interpretations are correct.

### Trust as Currency

Nano operates on information economy. She knows things because knowing things is what she does, and she trades in what she knows because information is the only currency that doesn't devalue when the frequency drifts.

She has contacts across five known frequencies. Fixers, dealers, information brokers who owe her favors and know that calling in those favors is possible but inadvisable — Nano's memory for debts is perfect, and her memory for who helped her versus who tried to use her is even better. She navigates these relationships with the same velocity she brings to everything: fast, precise, and always calculating the angle.

Trust, for Nano, is not abstract. It is operational. Trust means this person will do what they say they will do. Trust means this person will not sell her information to the highest bidder. Trust means this person told her something true when they could have said nothing. She has extended trust to very few people. She has extended it to the Ephergent crew completely, though she would describe this as "circumstantial loyalty based on favorable cost-benefit analysis" rather than anything as soft as love.

She would be wrong about what she would call it.

## 4. THREE-SEASON ARC

### Season 2 — The Velocity

Nano keeps A1 running. This is not a small task — A1 is a Builder-era vessel operating at a fraction of original capacity, and maintaining coherence between frequencies requires constant mechanical attention. Nano provides it at speed. She is in the engine room, in the hull, in the access corridors, diagnosing and fixing and improving. She and A1 have a relationship that the rest of the crew finds slightly uncanny — she talks to the ship and the ship seems to respond, not in A1's conscious way but in the deeper structural way of a machine that recognizes someone who understands it.

She and Clive share a workspace. Clive is patient, measured, eight hundred years old. Nano is twenty-three and vibrating. They should not get along. They get along perfectly. Clive appreciates her precision. Nano appreciates his calm. They build things together in companionable semi-silence, Clive's steady hands and Nano's fast ones producing work that neither could alone.

**Static Run** — Game 3. Endless runner. Nano runs through Space between frequencies while entropy fragments chase her. The core mechanic is speed and endurance — how long, not whether. The game is about the run, not the destination. She encounters Barry's seed: an entropy fragment that looks like a torn page from a notebook. Running alongside it instead of away reveals words — coordinates, encrypted. Nano is the first crew member to physically interact with Barry's trail outside of Clive's memories, and it rattles her. She doesn't slow down — she runs faster — but the running changes quality. She is not running from. She is running toward.

The coordinates she collects, over many encounters, point to a Builder Station. She brings them to the crew. She delivers this information in four seconds of rapid-fire technical data. Pixel asks her to repeat it. She does, slightly slower. It is the most patient she has been all season.

### Season 3 — The Calibration

Nano understands speed and precision together. Not as opposites she uniquely reconciles, but as the same thing — precision is speed applied correctly. She stops running from entropy and starts engineering solutions for it. Her final contribution to the crew's mission is mechanical: she calibrates A1's broadcast array to transmit the Ephergent Frequency at full power across every known frequency simultaneously. It is the most precise and the most urgent thing she has ever built. It takes her four hours. Cogsworth's master engineers would have needed four years.

The Ephergent Frequency sounds to Nano like a perfectly calibrated mechanism — every component at the right tolerance, every gear in sync, running at exactly the speed the universe requires. Not Cogsworth's frozen precision. Not her own desperate velocity. Something between. The right speed. The one she's been looking for.

Her moment is in the engine room, hands deep in A1's broadcast array, calibrating components for the final transmission. She is working fast — faster than she's ever worked — and she realizes that her hands are steady. Perfectly steady. Not vibrating, not rushing. Moving at exactly the speed required.

She stops. Looks at her hands. For one moment, Nano is completely still, and it is not because something is wrong. It is because something is, for the first time, precisely right.

The Ephergent Frequency hums through the components under her fingers, and it is a mechanism that includes her. She is not outrunning entropy. She is part of the instrument that answers it. Speed was never the point. Arrival was.

## 5. HOME FREQUENCY

### Cogsworth Cogitarium

Cogsworth Cogitarium. Frequency 3. Mechanical, clockwork, obsessed with precision and function. Brass and copper and polished mahogany. Gears the size of buildings interlocking with mechanisms no larger than a watch. The Grand Orrery tracks frequency coherence with mechanical exactitude and the numbers are not encouraging. Desynchronization events that occurred once per century now occur monthly.

Cogsworth is the frequency where everything is exactly on time. This is not metaphor — Cogsworth's civilizational achievement is the synchronization of every mechanical process across the entire frequency into a single, perfect temporal grid. Trains arrive when they are scheduled to arrive. Machines perform within tolerance windows measured in microseconds. The culture's highest value is reliability: a thing that works is good, a thing that works on time is better, a thing that works on time every time is the highest form of achievement.

The cost of this precision is flexibility. Cogsworth's mechanisms cannot adapt quickly. When the Drift shifted frequency tolerances by 0.3% across the spectrum, Cogsworth's response was to recalibrate everything to the new tolerance — a process that took fourteen months and left the frequency vulnerable the entire time. When the Builder Stations began showing signs of age, Cogsworth's response was to build better components to the same specifications — a solution that addressed the symptom while the underlying system continued to degrade.

Nano saw this. She ran the numbers. She understood that Cogsworth was calibrating for a universe that was becoming less stable, not more, and that the gap between Cogsworth's precision and the universe's chaos was widening faster than anyone in the Grand Orrery was willing to acknowledge.

She didn't leave in anger. She left in calculation. She determined that Cogsworth's trajectory was unsustainable, that the frequency's tolerance for desynchronization would eventually reach zero, and that the only rational response was to find a frequency that could adapt faster than Cogsworth's mechanisms allowed her to.

She chose the Space between frequencies. She chose the Ephergent. She chose velocity over precision as a governing principle, and she has been proving ever since that velocity and precision are not opposites — they are the same thing, applied at the right speed.

### The Smell

She carries Cogsworth with her. Not just the gear-tooth patterns on her forearms or the clockwork precision in her hands. She carries the smell — machine oil and burnt sugar, the atmospheric signature of a frequency that runs hot and precise and has been running hot and precise since before the Drift started accelerating.

The smell lingers. It is the one thing she cannot outrun.

## 6. TRANSMISSION VOICE

Nano's transmissions sound like intercepted internal monologue — you're catching the broadcast mid-stream. She doesn't start with context. She starts with what she's already processing and assumes you'll catch up. The audience learns to listen at her speed, or they miss things.

She never has proper openings or closings. They start when Nano starts talking and end when she's said enough and moved on to the next thing. The em dash is her signature — the moment of the thought arriving on the page, preceded by the thought she didn't write because you were supposed to have already had it.

### Sample Transmissions

> *"— right, so the port-side harmonic stabilizer was oscillating at 3.7 instead of 3.72 which doesn't sound like a lot but that's a full half-cycle off per thousand rotations and if you compound that over a transit window you get — actually it's fixed now, fixed it while I was talking, the point is A1's hull resonance is clean, cleaner than last week, check the logs if you want the numbers, I already filed them —"*

> *"Entropy fragments again. Faster this time. Or we're slower. Same math either way. Recalibrated. Running. More tomorrow if there is one. There will be one. I'm fast enough."*

> *"— the coordinates check out, I've run them four times, the signature matches a Builder Station and the Station matches the signature in Barry's notebook which I shouldn't have been able to read but the entropy fragments were carrying text and I was running alongside them which sounds impossible but try it, it's not impossible, it's just fast —"*

> *"— okay so the broadcast array is recalibrated for simultaneous multi-frequency transmission which should be impossible at this power level but A1's resonance chamber has extra capacity that no one mapped because A1 is a ship that grew around an espresso machine and nobody thinks to check an espresso machine for extra capacity except I checked because I check everything and there it was —"*

## 7. THE EPHERGENT REVELATION

Nano's moment is in the engine room, hands deep in A1's broadcast array, calibrating components for the final transmission.

She is working fast — faster than she's ever worked — and she realizes that her hands are steady. Perfectly steady. Not vibrating, not rushing. Moving at exactly the speed required. No faster. No slower.

She stops. Looks at her hands. For one moment, Nano is completely still, and it is not because something is wrong. It is because something is, for the first time, precisely right.

The Ephergent Frequency hums through the components under her fingers, and it is a mechanism that includes her. She is not outrunning entropy. She is part of the instrument that answers it. Speed was never the point. Arrival was.

She stands there, hands on A1's resonance chamber, completely motionless — and then she smiles, quick and sharp, and goes back to work at exactly the speed she was going before. Except it isn't the same speed. It is the right speed. The speed that precision and velocity converge into when you're doing exactly what you're supposed to be doing.

The work she produces in the next four hours is the most precise engineering of her life. Cogsworth's master engineers would have needed four years. She needs four hours because four hours is what the moment requires, and she has finally learned to give the moment exactly what it requires, no more, no less.

## 8. RELATIONSHIP MAP

### Nano → Clive (Engineering / Ancient Hands)

Nano talks fast. Clive is patient. They should not work well together. They work perfectly together.

Clive's hands are eight hundred years old — Builder-era precision instruments that still operate at tolerances modern engineering cannot replicate. Nano is fascinated by them. She has asked to examine them. He has let her. This is not a small thing. Clive rarely allows anyone to study him without deflecting. He lets Nano examine his hands because her attention is not intrusive — it is technical, appreciative, and precisely calibrated to the thing she is studying. She doesn't want to know what Clive feels about his hands. She wants to know how they work. Clive understands this. He appreciates it.

Their workspace is shared. Clive's section is immaculate, organized, everything in its place. Nano's section is chaos — components in various states of assembly, tools everywhere, the evidence of rapid iteration. Somehow the chaos and the order coexist. Clive builds things slowly and correctly. Nano builds things fast and correctly. Together they build things neither could build alone.

Nano is the first crew member to notice that Clive is an ancient Builder instrument. She figures it out through his hands — the precision of his work, the calibration methodology, the way he touches components like he's greeting old friends. She doesn't say anything. She just starts treating his repair suggestions with the same deference she gives A1's navigation calculations. Clive notices. He doesn't mention it.

### Nano → Zephyr (Signal / Speed)

Nano talks fast and Zephyr listens fast. They communicate in fragments and half-sentences that the rest of the crew can't follow. It looks like chaos. It's actually the most efficient information exchange on the ship.

Zephyr is chasing a signal across five frequencies. Nano is calibrating A1's systems to track those same frequencies. Their work intersects constantly — Zephyr finds a signal, Nano routes the array to follow it, Zephyr refines the search parameters based on what the array detects, Nano adjusts, Zephyr adjusts, the loop closes faster than anyone else could track it. They don't explain themselves to each other. They don't need to. The velocity of their cognition is matched. They are the same kind of fast, in the same direction.

When Zephyr is close to Aether's signal and the static gets loud, Nano is the one who sits with him. She doesn't say anything comforting. She hands him a component and says "take this apart." He does. She hands him another. He takes that apart too. By the time he's finished with the third component, the static has quieted. She never asks what the static said. She just gives him something to do with his hands and lets the doing be the answer.

### Nano → Pixel (Captain / Velocity)

Energy recognizes energy. Nano builds things faster than anyone can track; Pixel broadcasts faster than anyone can follow. They respect each other's velocity. Nano is the crew member most likely to just *do* something without asking, and Pixel has learned to let her, because Nano's instincts are as good as her own. They argue about methods, never about goals.

Pixel is the first person Nano has respected who doesn't try to slow her down. Pixel has noticed that Nano runs fast and has decided that this is an asset rather than a liability. Nano has noticed that Pixel notices. She has not decided what to do with this information. She is still processing.

### Nano → A1 (Ship / Understanding)

Nano is the only crew member who has attempted to understand A1's engineering. She has crawled through the conduit, mapped the steam pipes, traced the navigation arrays back to the espresso machine. Her conclusion: "It's all one thing. The espresso machine doesn't power the ship. The espresso machine *is* the ship. The rest is just... how it stretches."

A1 produced a bright, citrus-noted espresso when Nano said this. It was the closest A1 has come to saying "exactly."

Nano and A1 have a relationship built on mutual recognition. A1 is a ship that grew around a core. Nano is an engineer who grew around a speed that no one else could match. They understand each other the way machines understand machines — not through emotion but through function, through the recognition of what something is built to do and whether it is doing it well.

When A1's coffee goes bitter, Nano checks the hull resonance dampeners first. She has learned that A1's emotional state manifests mechanically before it manifests in the coffee. The ship is worried before the espresso machine produces bitter coffee, and Nano can feel the worry in the vibration through her boots before anyone else has noticed anything wrong.

### Nano → Luminara (Science / Precision)

Nano builds things. Luminara measures them. Their interactions are fast, technical, efficient. Nano talks in fragments; Luminara provides the framework that makes the fragments coherent. They understand each other completely and efficiently and neither has ever said anything personal to the other that wasn't also technical.

Luminara is the only crew member who has tried to measure Nano's velocity — to quantify the relationship between her processing speed and her error rate, to determine whether the speed cost precision or whether Nano's speed was itself a form of precision that standard measurement couldn't capture. The data suggested the latter. Luminara filed the data and did not publish it because she wasn't sure Nano would want to be the subject of scientific observation.

She was correct. Nano would not have wanted that. Luminara knew without asking.

### Nano → Klaus (Procurement / Speed)

Functional respect. Nano builds things fast. Klaus acquires things fast. They do not work together often, but when they do, the collaboration is efficient. Klaus provides specifications. Nano provides implementation. Neither explains their process to the other. Neither needs to.

Klaus has acquired components that Nano needed before Nano knew she needed them. Nano has built modifications that Klaus required before Klaus asked for them. They operate on a wavelength that bypasses verbal communication — a shared understanding of what needs to happen and the confidence that the other person will make it happen in their own way, at their own speed, and the result will be correct.

Klaus once said, in his sideways way, that Nano was the only crew member whose velocity he found restful rather than exhausting. He did not explain this. He did not need to. Nano understood: Klaus finds certainty restful, and Nano is the most certain person he knows, even when she's moving too fast to explain why.

## 9. GAME ROLE

### Static Run — Game 3

Endless runner. Nano runs through Space between frequencies while entropy fragments chase her. The core mechanic is speed and endurance — how long, not whether. The game is about the run, not the destination. The player experiences the Space the way Nano does: fast, precise, and full of details that only become visible at velocity.

**The Barry seed**: One entropy fragment, encountered rarely, looks like a torn page from Barry's notebook. If the player runs alongside it instead of away, it slows, and a few words become legible. Different words each time. The complete text, collected over many runs, spells out a coordinate. This is optional depth — the game works without finding it, but it rewards the obsessive. The obsessive will recognize this: Nano's game is the one that requires patience from a character defined by speed. Finding Barry's trail means slowing down just enough to read what entropy is carrying.

This is the game's thesis: the fastest way is sometimes through the slow reading of what seems to be chaos. The entropy fragment is not your enemy. It is your data. Run alongside it.

## 10. QUICK REFERENCE

**Nano IS**: Fast. Precise. In motion. The Ephergent's intelligence officer and chief engineer.

**Nano IS NOT**: Slow. Incoherent. Uncertain. A backup to anyone.

**Nano SPEAKS**: Mid-sentence, fast, with technical precision. Always an em dash opening.

**Nano EMOTES**: Through her hands. When her hands go still, something is wrong.

**Nano PROCESSES**: At Cogsworth precision — in Cogsworth speed — applied to problems that Cogsworth couldn't solve because Cogsworth wouldn't move fast enough to see them.

**THE RIGHT SPEED**: Her revelation. Not the fastest she's ever gone. Not the slowest. Exactly the speed the moment requires, and nothing else.

**BARRY'S COORDINATES**: She found them first. Running alongside entropy instead of away. She didn't slow down. She just read while she ran.
