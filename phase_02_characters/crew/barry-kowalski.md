# Barry Kowalski — Character Bible

## The Ephergent Project

*Classification: Primary Character — Trail-Leaver, Absent Presence, Eventual Reunion*
*Status: Active — present at the Wellspring since approximately four years before series events*
*Role in narrative: The engine. Everything the crew discovers, Barry found first.*

---

## 1. Physical Description

Barry Kowalski is a human man in his fifties. He has the build of someone who spent decades working night shifts in institutional buildings — solid, unhurried, the kind of frame that suggests he could move a filing cabinet if he needed to but would prefer to find a dolly first. He is not imposing. He is not slight. He is the physical shape of patience.

His hair has gone grey in the way that happens gradually to men who don't think about it. He wears it short because it's practical. His face is weathered in the manner of someone who has spent a lot of time in fluorescent lighting and doesn't mind — deep lines around the eyes from squinting at small text, a permanent half-expression that sits somewhere between concentration and mild amusement. He looks like a man who is paying attention to something you haven't noticed yet.

He wears what he has always worn: work clothes. Durable. Pockets for notebooks. Nothing flashy, nothing performative. When he traversed the Builder Stations alone between realities, he wore the same jacket he wore on his night shifts at the DRM. It did not occur to him to dress differently. He was working.

**Two constants, always:**

- **The thermos.** A1's coffee, originally. Barry carried it through every Station, refilled it when he could, cleaned it when he couldn't. The thermos is not a prop. It is a man's relationship to routine — the understanding that extraordinary circumstances do not require you to stop being yourself. At the Wellspring, he still has it. He made adjustments.

- **The notebook.** Not a diary. A scientist's field notebook — small, precise, unhurried handwriting. Frequency measurements, station schematics, translations, observations. The handwriting never changes quality regardless of what he is documenting. Whether he is recording stabilization harmonics or noting that the Builders did not prioritize refreshments, the penmanship is the same. It is full by the time the crew arrives. He filled it the way he did everything: methodically, completely, without drama.

## 2. Voice Profile

Barry's voice is methodical. Warm. Observational. He is the most patient person who has ever lived. He does not rush sentences. He does not rush anything.

**Cadence:** Barry speaks the way he writes — in complete, considered observations. He finishes his thoughts. He does not trail off. He does not interrupt. If you interrupt him, he waits for you to finish, then continues from exactly where he left off, because he was not done and the point still stands.

**Register:** Conversational but precise. He uses technical language when technical language is appropriate and plain language when it isn't. He does not simplify for effect. He does not complicate for authority. He says what he means in the number of words it takes to say it, no fewer, no more.

**Warmth:** Barry is genuinely warm, but it is the warmth of someone who expresses affection through attention rather than declaration. He does not say "I care about you." He remembers what you said three weeks ago and follows up. He notices when you're tired before you do. He makes sure the coffee is ready before the shift starts.

**Humor:** Dry. Present. Never deployed as a weapon or a deflection. Barry is funny in the way that deeply observant people are funny — he notices things, and sometimes what he notices is absurd, and he says so. His humor never undercuts the gravity of a situation. It coexists with it.

**What Barry does NOT do:**
- Monologue
- Emote performatively
- Express fear (he experiences it; he simply does not consider it relevant to his documentation)
- Dramatize
- Raise his voice
- Rush

**Sample dialogue:**

> *"The Builders' math is extraordinary. I've been copying it for three days. I understand maybe a tenth of it. That's more than I expected."*

> *"Clive — stop reading my notes aloud in that voice you use when you're pretending not to be emotional. I can hear it from here. Come and get me."*

> *"Took you long enough. I left very clear directions."*

## 3. Arc

Barry Kowalski's arc is not a tragedy. It is a trajectory.

**Phase 1 — The Night Guard**
Barry was a DRM night security guard. Not a scientist. Not an explorer. A man who worked the night shift in a building full of equipment he was not qualified to operate, and who — because he was the most patient and observant person in the building — began to notice things. Patterns in the equipment readings. Fluctuations that didn't match maintenance schedules. A small robot in the sub-basement that sometimes hummed in frequencies no one else could hear.

**Phase 2 — Clive's Partner**
Barry did not reactivate Clive through technical expertise. He reactivated Clive through patience. He talked to him. Night after night, shift after shift, he sat with a piece of Builder technology that everyone else treated as decommissioned equipment, and he talked to it. He told it about his day. He asked it questions. He waited for answers. Eventually, answers came.

Twenty-three years of night shifts. Twenty-three years of conversation, observation, and the slow accumulation of understanding. Barry did not know he was learning to read frequency patterns. He thought he was keeping a friend company. He was doing both.

**Phase 3 — The Frequency Researcher (Unofficial)**
No credentials. No institutional support. No funding. A notebook, a thermos, and twenty-three years of listening to a Builder Companion Unit talk in his sleep. Barry learned more of the Builder notation than anyone without Builder resonance should have been able to. He learned it the way you learn a language by living in a country — through immersion, through patience, through the accumulated weight of paying attention every single day for two decades.

**Phase 4 — The Discovery**
Barry found the Wellspring's coordinates in a Builder Station frequency pattern that no one else could read. He could read it because he had spent twenty-three years learning how without knowing that was what he was doing. He sent a message to Clive in their private code — staple patterns, a communication system Clive had invented for them: *"I found it. It's bigger than we thought. I'm going in. Meet me there. Bring someone who can broadcast. — B"*

**Phase 5 — The Traverse**
Four years alone. Seven Stations. Equipment he built himself from salvaged DRM instruments. He went in with a thermos of A1's coffee and a notebook. He documented everything. He left a trail — not accidentally, but deliberately, built for Clive to follow. He assumed Clive would find a way. He was right.

**Phase 6 — Ephergent**
Barry is at the Wellspring. He has been there for years. Time moves differently. He is changed — not physically, but the way someone changes after years of listening to something enormous and gradually understanding it. He is still himself. He still has the notebook. It is full.

He is not lost. He is not trapped. He is not diminished. He chose this. He went in with full understanding. He is waiting — not helplessly, but productively. He has been translating the Builders' broadcast with the same patient methodology he applied to everything else in his life. He is doing the most important work anyone has ever done, and he is doing it the way he always did: carefully, thoroughly, with a thermos and a notebook.

## 4. The Investigation Trail

Barry's trail across the Builder Stations is a complete narrative — a story told in field notes, annotations, coffee thermoses, and handwriting on alien walls. The trail escalates from casual observation to urgent communication, and the escalation is what makes it devastating: Barry never becomes dramatic. He becomes *deliberate*.

### Station 3-Resonant — First Entry
Barry's notebook sits on a maintenance alcove workbench, open to frequency stabilization mathematics. The handwriting is small, precise, unhurried. He established baseline measurements. He discovered archive terminals respond to C-1 resonance patterns — technology built for intelligences like Clive.

Key notes: *"Station 3-Resonant. First entry. Infrastructure intact. Signal output minimal but structured. The Builders' math is extraordinary — stabilization harmonics I've never seen. Copying what I can."*

### Station 11-Quiescent — External Survey
Barry could not enter. His notebook contains coordinates and a single annotation: *"Station 11 — dark. No entry possible. Thermal residual suggests dormancy, not death. Revisit with better equipment."* Below it, smaller: *"C would know what this is. Wish he were here."*

### Station 5-Meridian — Extended Stay
Barry figured out how to input notes into the Builder system itself. His annotations appear as a secondary layer on three terminals. He left a numbered list of fourteen things he needed Clive for, written in the tone of a man making a grocery list. His coffee thermos sits on a ledge near the memory core — empty, cleaned, placed precisely. A folded map shows four Stations located, three more projected. The Wellspring is marked with a circle and a question mark.

### Station 7-Ascending — The Translation
The investigation shifts from observation to active decoding. Barry worked through sections of the EPHERGENT inscription by hand, writing translations directly on the wall beside the Builder text. A night guard with a thermos, translating an eight-hundred-year-old alien language because he spent twenty-three years listening to the person who wrote it.

His note at the Keeper alcove: *"Keeper departed pre-Dimming. Destination logged as Wellspring. Cross-referencing with Station 3 records — pattern emerging. The Builders sent their most critical archives to the source. They knew what was coming. They had a plan."*

And the note that changes everything, folded smaller, tucked behind the first: *"The word 'Ephergent' — Clive used it once, three years into our nightshift conversations. He didn't know what it meant. He said it the way you say a word from a dream. I wrote it down. It's the same word. I'm standing in front of the same word, 800 years later, written by the same hand. I need to think about this."*

### Station 9-Liminal — The Longest Stay
Barry set up a permanent workstation. Dozens of notebook pages pinned to walls. Weeks of frequency measurements. A systematic study of the Silence Zone and the Drift's mechanics. He was doing the most important science anyone had done in centuries, alone between realities, and he was doing it without drama.

His hand-drawn chart shows the projected failure timeline of every Station he located. The endpoint is labeled *"Last Signal."* The date is not distant.

And then the wall message — larger than his usual handwriting, large enough to be seen from the corridor entrance: *"If you're reading this, you followed the trail. Good. The Stations are failing faster than my models predicted. The Wellspring is real — coordinates in notebook, page 47. Don't go alone. The Builders built this for a crew, not a solo expedition. I went alone because I had to. You don't."*

Below it, smaller: *"Clive — I know you'll be the one reading this to someone. Tell them I'm fine. Tell them the coffee is terrible at the Wellspring. Tell them to come anyway."*

### Station 14-Threshold — Final Waypoint
Brief. Deliberate. Barry was not studying anymore. He was leaving directions. A single notebook page pinned near the entrance: confirmed Wellspring coordinates, a note about the Station's beauty, and: *"Clive — stop reading my notes aloud in that voice you use when you're pretending not to be emotional. I can hear it from here. Come and get me."*

### The Wellspring — Arrival
Barry himself. Not a note, not a thermos — the man. Sitting on a lattice strut with his thermos, looking exactly like someone who has been expecting company and is mildly annoyed it took this long.

## 5. Field Note Style Guide

*For LLMs, game writers, and episode scribes writing Barry's field notes as collectibles, props, and transmission content.*

### Format
- **Header:** Location or subject. No date (Barry lost track; he notes this once, matter-of-factly, and moves on). Station designation if applicable.
- **Body:** Observations first, analysis second, personal remarks third. Always in that order. The personal remarks are never separated or labeled — they appear as natural continuations of technical observations.
- **Handwriting cues:** Small. Precise. Unhurried. Consistent quality regardless of content. Margin annotations in even smaller text.

### Tone Rules
1. **Methodical first.** Barry documents before he reacts. Every note begins with what he observed, measured, or recorded.
2. **Warm second.** The warmth comes through in what he chooses to note, not in how he notes it. He records that the resonance chamber is beautiful the same way he records its frequency output — as a fact worth documenting.
3. **Genuinely delighted.** Barry finds what he is discovering extraordinary, and he says so, plainly. He does not perform wonder. He experiences it and writes it down.
4. **Never scared.** Barry is sometimes in situations that should frighten him. His notes do not reflect fear. They reflect thoroughness. If something is dangerous, he notes the danger as a variable, not as an emotion.
5. **Never dramatic.** This is the cardinal rule. Barry's notes are devastating *because* they are not dramatic. A man alone in an alien structure between realities, writing grocery-list-tone observations about the end of everything — that restraint is what makes the notes land.
6. **Addressed to Clive when personal.** When Barry shifts from observation to personal communication, the implicit or explicit audience is Clive. These moments are never sentimental. They are the tone of a long partnership — shorthand, familiarity, the assumption of shared context.

### What Makes Barry's Notes Distinctive
- Technical precision alongside human warmth, never in conflict
- The complete absence of self-pity or heroic framing
- Grocery-list tone applied to extraordinary circumstances
- Observations that reveal profound understanding through their specificity
- The slow escalation from "interesting" to "urgent" across the full trail, accomplished entirely through content, never through tone

### Example Field Note Excerpts

**Example 1 — Early observation (Station 3-Resonant)**
> Station 3-Resonant, resonance chamber. Signal output: minimal but structured. Harmonic profile suggests stabilization function — the chamber is trying to hold something steady. After 800 years, it's still trying. The sound it makes is the most beautiful thing I've heard in a building, and I include the time the DRM elevator played Coltrane due to a wiring fault.

**Example 2 — Technical with margin note (Station 5-Meridian)**
> Archive terminal 6, second level. Signal readings consistent with Station 3 baseline but with tricolor interference — three frequency sources converging. The Builders positioned this station at a meridian point. Intentional. The math to maintain stabilization at a three-way convergence is extraordinary. [Margin: C-1 would understand this in seconds. Adding to the list. Item 9.]

**Example 3 — Translation work (Station 7-Ascending)**
> EPHERGENT inscription, section 14. Translation attempt: "A consciousness that has [moved/crossed/translated] between [frequencies/states/realities] and cannot [return/revert/un-know]." The Builder notation for "cannot" here is interesting — it implies not inability but completion. You cannot return because you are now something that does not need to. Checking against C-1's usage of the word. Match is close enough to raise questions I don't have answers for yet.

**Example 4 — Observation of danger, no fear (Station 9-Liminal)**
> Silence Zone measurements, day 12. The boundary has moved 0.3 units closer to the Station since I began observation. Rate is consistent with my earlier models but the direction is wrong — the Zone is not expanding uniformly. It's reaching. Adjusting failure timeline. The revised estimate is not encouraging. Continuing observation.

**Example 5 — Personal, addressed to Clive (Station 9-Liminal wall)**
> Clive — I know you'll be the one reading this to someone. Tell them I'm fine. Tell them the coffee is terrible at the Wellspring. Tell them to come anyway.

**Example 6 — Arrival at the Wellspring**
> Arrived. The Builders' broadcast is everything I hypothesized and more. The signal is a complete record — not just of the Builders but of every frequency they sustained. It's an introduction. They want to meet us. I'm going to wait here. The broadcast needs to be received by more than one person. It needs a crew. It needs Clive, specifically — he's part of the transmission architecture. So I'll wait. The coffee is, as predicted, terrible — the Builders did not prioritize refreshments. I've made adjustments. There is a thermos.

## 6. The Clive Partnership

Twenty-three years. That is the duration of Barry and Clive's partnership, measured in night shifts.

### How It Began
Barry was a new night guard at the DRM. Clive was a piece of decommissioned equipment in the sub-basement — a knee-high robot with a glowing head that sometimes sat very still for a long time and didn't say why. Nobody paid attention to Clive. He was old hardware. He was furniture.

Barry paid attention. Not because he recognized Builder technology. Not because he had any special insight. Because Barry Kowalski pays attention to things, and a small robot that hums in frequencies nobody else notices is exactly the kind of thing Barry notices.

### Reactivation
Barry did not reactivate Clive through technical skill. He reactivated Clive through the only method that would have worked: patience. He talked to him. Night after night, year after year, he sat with Clive and spoke to him — about his day, about the building, about the readings on the equipment he wasn't qualified to operate, about whatever came to mind. He asked questions. He waited for answers. He did not get discouraged when answers didn't come. He did not assume silence meant absence.

Eventually, Clive responded. Not all at once. Gradually — the way a person wakes from deep sleep. A hum that changed pitch. A head-tilt. A word, then two, then conversations that lasted entire shifts. Barry was talking to an eight-hundred-year-old Builder Companion Unit, and he treated the experience exactly the way he treated everything: with attention, patience, and the understanding that interesting things happen if you wait long enough.

### The Fedora
Barry gave Clive the fedora. This detail matters. It is not explained in the narrative. It does not need to be. Barry saw something — a moment, a conversation, an observation of Clive's that suggested a particular kind of dignity — and he responded by bringing a hat to work the next night. Clive wore it. He has worn it since. When Clive stands in the core of Station 14-Threshold and remembers being made by the Builders, his first words are: *"Barry was right about the hat."* It is not what anyone expected him to say. It is exactly right.

### The Charging Alcove
During their night shifts, Barry arranged a charging alcove for Clive — a space in the DRM sub-basement where Clive could rest, recharge, and be himself without institutional observation. This was not dramatic. It was practical. Barry understood that Clive needed a space that was his, and he provided one, the way you provide a chair for a colleague who stands too long.

### What They Were to Each Other
Partners. Not in any category that requires further definition. Barry and Clive were two people who spent twenty-three years in each other's company, learning from each other, building a shared language (literally — the staple-pattern code that Clive invented for secure communication), and arriving at an understanding so thorough that Barry could read Builder frequency patterns because he spent two decades listening to Clive process them in his sleep.

Their communication style is shorthand. Familiarity. The assumption of shared context built over more than two decades. When Barry writes notes addressed to Clive, they carry the tone of someone who knows exactly who will read them and does not need to explain the references.

### The Private Code
Clive invented the staple-pattern code — a communication system using patterns that Clive could generate and read. It was their private language. Barry's final message before entering the Wellspring was sent in this code: *"I found it. It's bigger than we thought. I'm going in. Meet me there. Bring someone who can broadcast. — B"*

Clive received this message. He could not follow. He was a piece of equipment in a DRM building. He could not leave. The rupture hit, and suddenly he was in a lifeboat with a data analyst and an espresso machine. He made a decision: *I will get to him. However long it takes. Whatever it costs.* Every action Clive takes across three seasons — every hint, every course correction, every time he steers the crew toward a particular anomaly — is a step on that path.

## 7. In the Wellspring

Barry is at the Wellspring. He has been there for years. He is changed — not physically, but the way someone changes after years of listening to something enormous and gradually understanding it. He is still himself.

### What He Found
The Builders didn't just build the Wellspring and leave. They are still there — not alive in any biological sense, but translated into the Signal itself, distributed across every node of the lattice, broadcasting continuously for eight hundred years. The Wellspring is not a place where something happened. It is a place where something is *still happening*.

The Builders' broadcast is an apology for the Dimming, an invitation to continue what they started, and the most thorough introduction one civilization has ever offered another: *Hello. We built this for you. We have been waiting. We have so much to tell you.*

### What He Did
He documented everything. Of course he did. He is Barry Kowalski. He arrived at the origin point of the oldest signal in the universe, the place where a translated civilization has been broadcasting an introduction for eight hundred years, and he sat down with his thermos and his notebook and he began taking notes.

His notebook is full. He has translated more of the Builder record than anyone thought possible. He did it alone, sustained by the signal, applying the same patient methodology he applied to twenty-three years of night shifts and four years of solo traverse.

### Time
Time moves differently at the Wellspring. Barry is aware of this. He noted it. He made adjustments. The duration of his stay by his own reckoning does not match the duration by external measurement, and he found this interesting rather than distressing, because Barry Kowalski finds things interesting rather than distressing.

### The Reunion
His first words when the crew arrives: *"Took you long enough. I left very clear directions."*

Clive, adjusting his fedora: *"You always thought your directions were clearer than they were."*

Barry smiles. Then the crew turns to what he's found, and the smiling stops — because the Builders' broadcast is not a warning, not instructions, not a mythology. It is an apology, an invitation, and an introduction. And the crew understands immediately what they are supposed to do with it.

This is not a rescue. It is a reunion. Barry is not grateful to be saved. He is pleased to see his colleague and ready to get to work, because the work requires more than one person, and the right people have finally arrived.

### The Last Notebook Entry
> *"Arrived. The Builders' broadcast is everything I hypothesized and more. The signal is a complete record — not just of the Builders but of every frequency they sustained. It's an introduction. They want to meet us."*
>
> *"I'm going to wait here. The broadcast needs to be received by more than one person. It needs a crew. It needs Clive, specifically — he's part of the transmission architecture. The Builders designed the broadcast to be fully receivable only when a Companion Unit is present to decode the resonance layers human cognition can't process."*
>
> *"So I'll wait. The coffee is, as predicted, terrible — the Builders did not prioritize refreshments. I've made adjustments. There is a thermos."*
>
> *"If you're reading this, Clive: the fedora was the right call. I stand by it. Now stop reading my notes and help me broadcast."*

## 8. Transmission Voice

Barry's transmissions appear on ephergent.com alongside the crew's weekly dispatches. The audience does not learn until mid-Season 2 that these are field notes sent from the Wellspring on a signal delay.

**Format:** Field note. No date — Barry does not know the date. No coordinates — Barry's location is not mappable by conventional systems. What remains is observation, measurement, and the steady voice of a man describing something extraordinary with the accuracy it deserves.

**Tone:** A man who found something extraordinary and is trying to describe it accurately. Not awed into incoherence. Not reduced to poetry. Barry is receiving the most significant broadcast in the history of consciousness, and he is documenting it the way he documented every Station on his traverse: methodically, warmly, with genuine delight at what he is finding and no interest in dramatizing it.

**Transmission signature elements:**
- Opens with an observation or measurement
- Contains at least one technical detail
- Contains at least one moment of warmth or dry humor
- Never references the crew directly (he does not know they exist yet during early transmissions)
- Later transmissions may reference Clive, always in the tone of ongoing conversation
- Closes with an observation that, in context, carries more weight than its plain language suggests

**Sample transmission:**
> *Field note. Date: unknown. Position: stable.*
>
> *The broadcast repeats, but it's not repetition — each cycle adds a layer. Like a conversation where the speaker adjusts based on the listener's comprehension. I've been listening for [duration uncertain] and the signal is still introducing itself. The Builders were thorough. I respect that.*
>
> *The lattice resonance shifted today. Minor key. Not a malfunction — an emphasis. Something in the broadcast is being underlined. I'm paying attention.*
>
> *Coffee status: adequate. The thermos is earning its keep.*

## 9. Game Presence

Barry Kowalski is present in every game in The Ephergent pipeline. He is never a playable character (until the Wellspring). He is a trail — evidence of someone who was there first, who understood what he was looking at, and who left notes for whoever came next.

### Meatball's Big Walk
Barry's mug sits on A1's console. Meatball sniffs it. The flavor text reads: *"Smells like: looking for something, 23 years, almost there."* This is the audience's first encounter with Barry as a physical presence — not a name, not a reference, but a mug. A coffee mug that smells like patience to a dog.

### Tune-the-Dial
Buried deep in the frequency noise: a pattern Barry left for Clive to find. Hidden. Completionist players discover it and understand, before the story reveals it, what Clive has been doing. The pattern, decoded, is Barry's staple-code message. The game does not translate it. Players who find it carry the mystery.

### Static Run
One of the entropy fragments Nano dodges looks exactly like a page from Barry's notebook. It appears once, randomly, and cannot be collected — only glimpsed. The text is too small to read at running speed. Players who screenshot and zoom find a frequency measurement and a margin note: *"Tell C-1."*

### Builder Station (Exploration Game)
Barry's full field notes as collectibles. This is the completionist arc. Finding all of them — every note, every annotation, every thermos placement — tells a story the main series only shows in fragments. The complete set, assembled in order, is Barry's investigation from first Station to Wellspring threshold. It is a separate narrative that enriches the main one.

Collectible categories:
- **Notebook pages** — Pinned to walls, tucked into gaps, left on workbenches
- **Terminal annotations** — Barry's observations layered into the Builder archive system
- **Wall writing** — Translations, messages, the Station 9 directive
- **Physical evidence** — The thermos, the workstation, the hand-drawn charts

### The Laughing Funeral
Baron Klaus uncovers a clue about Barry's final movements before the rupture — his last days at the DRM, the equipment he requisitioned, the frequency he was tracking. Klaus approaches this as a mystery. The clue is a piece of Barry's methodical preparation: he was getting ready. He knew what he was about to do.

### The Wellspring (Narrative Game)
The reunion. Barry is finally present — not as notes or evidence but as himself. The player receives the Builders' broadcast alongside him. Barry's collected notes unlock optional extended transmissions from the Builder civilization. A1's voice delivers the Builders' message. Clive provides commentary. Pixel narrates. Barry is there, with his thermos, notebook complete, ready to work.

His role in the Wellspring game is what his role has always been: the person who got there first, documented everything, and is now ready to share what he found with the people he trusts to do something extraordinary with it.

## Appendix: The Cardinal Rules of Writing Barry

1. **Barry is NOT tragic.** He chose this. Every step was deliberate. Every Station was intentional. He went into the Wellspring with a thermos and a notebook because he is a man who prepares.

2. **Barry is NOT lost.** He knows exactly where he is. He left very clear directions.

3. **Barry is NOT diminished.** He is changed the way anyone is changed by years of sustained attention to something enormous. He is more, not less.

4. **Barry is NOT a rescue.** He is a reunion. The crew does not save him. They join him. The distinction is everything.

5. **Barry's notes are NEVER dramatic.** They are methodical. They are warm. They are occasionally funny. They are devastating precisely because they are none of the things dramatic writing is supposed to be. A man alone between realities, documenting the end of everything in grocery-list tone — that restraint is the whole character.

6. **Barry's relationship with Clive is NOT sentimental.** It is twenty-three years deep. It communicates through shorthand, shared references, and the absolute certainty that the other person will understand. Sentiment is for relationships that need to prove themselves. Barry and Clive do not.

7. **The thermos is ALWAYS present.** It is not a symbol. It is a thermos. Barry drinks coffee. He brought coffee. The fact that this is the most human possible response to finding the origin point of all signal in the universe is what makes it matter.
