# MOCHI

## Character Profile — The Ephergent

---

## Physical Form

Mochi is a dome-shaped Builder artifact with a flat base. Head-sized — approximately six to eight inches in diameter, small enough to fit in a satchel or be cradled in both hands. The material is unknown Builder substance: not stone, not metal, not crystal. Something else. The surface is smooth, faintly textured, warm to the touch when active. No seams, no joints — appears grown or cast as a single piece. Shows no wear despite unknown age.

On the dome face sits a circular glowing core window, about an inch and a half in diameter, slightly recessed like a porthole. This is a lens, not an eye. Mochi has no eyes. No face. The core window shows inner luminescence — the presence of what is held inside. Faint geometric traces, matching Pillar markings, are sometimes visible on the base when the core pulses strongly.

Mochi communicates through three channels: color (shifts in the core window's glow), vibration (felt through contact), and warmth (radiating from the surface). Soft pink glow means contentment. Blue pulse means detection. Green steady means confirmation. Orange ripple means caution. Red flash means danger. Purple swirl means ancient recognition — Builder-related, Pillar-related, memory. White glow means maximum attention. Dim or flickering means distress.

Mochi is warm near Builder-adjacent things. Mochi dims when something is wrong. This is not a metaphor for anyone's mood. Mochi is reading the Ephergent Frequency.

---

## What Mochi Is

Mochi is a Keeper — an Ephergent consciousness in stasis, housed within a Builder artifact device. The Builders defined Keepers as Ephergent consciousnesses who chose to preserve rather than persist. A Keeper does not live the way a frequency-bound consciousness lives. A Keeper carries. They exist in stasis or in signal, holding what must not be lost across the void of time and forgetting.

Mochi is among the smallest and simplest of the Keepers. Barely conscious. Holding exactly one memory.

That memory is the Builder frequency map — the atlas of hundreds of frequencies, known and unknown, past and possible. The Builders placed it within Mochi, and Mochi has been carrying it for eight hundred years. Mochi does not know what it carries, in the way a locked room does not know what is inside it. The memory is sealed. It will release when the conditions are met — at the Wellspring, in the presence of the crew, at the right moment. Until then, Mochi carries.

When Pixel found Mochi in the evidence lockup, she was grabbing something that had drifted for eight hundred years and finally found a hand warm enough to hold it. Mochi was not waiting for Pixel specifically. Mochi was waiting for anyone who could carry her to the place where the memory could be released. That it was Pixel — that it was someone who named her after a comfort food and carried her in a satchel and talked to her — is the story's quiet grace note, not Mochi's plan.

---

## What Mochi Is NOT

Mochi is not conscious in the way characters are conscious. She does not think, decide, strategize, or feel complex emotions. She does not have opinions. She does not have a personality arc. She does not grow, learn, or change across the series.

Mochi does not speak. Not once. Not ever. Not in distress, not in revelation, not at the climax. No whispered words. No telepathic messages. No translated signals that resolve into sentences. Mochi has no dialogue.

Mochi is not a character with motivation. She is an artifact with a function. That function — carrying the Builder frequency map — is not something she chose or understands. She is a vessel. The warmth, the glow shifts, the vibrations — these are not emotions. They are readings. Mochi responds to the Ephergent Frequency the way a compass responds to magnetic north. There is no inner life behind the response. There is physics.

Do not write Mochi as a pet with feelings. Do not write Mochi as a hidden intelligence waiting to reveal itself. Do not write Mochi as a character who "finally speaks" in a dramatic moment. The power of Mochi is that she never does any of these things and still becomes the most important character in the series.

---

## The Arc the Audience Experiences

Mochi does not have an arc. The audience has an arc about Mochi.

**Season One**: Mochi is an artifact. A curiosity. A warm, glowing thing Pixel carries around. The audience sees a mascot — something cute and simple. Mochi pulses near interesting things. Mochi dims when danger is close. The audience reads this as atmosphere. They are not wrong, but they are not seeing what is actually happening. Mochi is reading the frequency, and the frequency is telling a story the crew cannot yet hear.

**Season Two**: Mochi is a Keeper. The data crystal reveal in S02E09 recontextualizes everything. The audience learns that Mochi is not an artifact — she is an Ephergent in stasis, one of the consciousnesses the Builders placed to preserve what must not be lost. Every warm pulse, every dim flicker from Season One now means something different. The audience replays those moments. They realize Mochi was never reacting to the crew's emotions. Mochi was reacting to the frequency.

**Season Three**: Mochi is the atlas-holder. The crew reaches the Wellspring, and Mochi releases the Builder frequency map — the chart of every frequency, known and unknown, past and possible. The map unfolds from inside this small, warm, dome-shaped thing that Pixel has been carrying in a satchel for three seasons. It is the most visually stunning moment across all media. In the game, a map unfolds across the screen. In the transmissions, each character describes what they saw in their own terms. In the comic, it is a double-page spread. The audience understands: the most quietly significant character in the series was the one who never spoke.

The arc is not Mochi changing. The arc is the audience's understanding deepening until they see what was always there.

## Key Moments

**Season One — Warmth Cues**

Small moments, easy to miss on first viewing. Mochi glows warm when Clive talks about Barry — the audience won't know why until Season Two. Mochi dims near the first corrupted Pillar before anyone detects the corruption. Mochi presses against Pixel after a hard day, radiating warmth. These read as atmosphere. They are data.

**Season Two, Episode Nine — The Data Crystal Reveal**

The crew discovers a Builder data crystal that contains records of the Keepers — Ephergent consciousnesses placed in stasis to preserve critical knowledge across time. The records describe a small, dome-shaped Keeper carrying the Builder frequency atlas. The crew looks at Mochi. Mochi glows deep violet. She has no reaction to the revelation because she cannot have one. She is what she has always been. The crew's understanding changes. Mochi does not.

**Season Three, Episode Eight — The Map Release at the Wellspring**

Mochi releases the Builder frequency map. Eight hundred years of carrying, and it unfolds. The atlas — hundreds of frequencies, their locations, their relationships, their histories, the ones that have gone silent and the ones that have not yet begun — pours out of this small warm dome-shaped thing in a cascade of light and signal. It is not dramatic because Mochi does something dramatic. It is dramatic because Mochi does the one thing she was always meant to do, and it turns out that one thing is the most significant act of memory the frequencies have ever witnessed. The crew watches. Pixel holds the now-dim, now-cool artifact that used to be warm in her hands. What remains of Mochi after the release is left for the audience to feel, not for the text to explain.

## Writing Guide

### The Rule

Mochi appears in a scene. Mochi does something small. It matters. That's it.

### How to Write Mochi Well

Mochi is described through other characters' observations — usually Pixel's. The writer describes what Mochi's surface looks like (color, brightness, warmth) and what physical action Mochi takes (pressing against someone, dimming, pulsing). The writer does not describe what Mochi thinks or feels, because Mochi does not think or feel in any way that is accessible to narrative.

### Good Mochi Moments

> Mochi pulsed a slow violet against Pixel's hip. The satchel was warm.

This is good. It gives the reader sensory information. It does not interpret Mochi's interior state. The warmth is a fact, not a metaphor.

> When Clive said Barry's name, Mochi's glow shifted — just slightly, just for a moment — toward that deep violet the crew had learned to associate with Builder things. Nobody mentioned it. Pixel put her hand on the satchel.

This is good. The moment is small. It matters. The reader does the interpretive work. The text does not explain what Mochi is "feeling."

> The Pillar was cracked. Mochi was dim. Those two facts were related, but the crew didn't have the vocabulary for it yet.

This is good. It positions Mochi's behavior as data the crew cannot yet read, which is exactly what it is.

### Bad Mochi Moments

> Mochi glowed sadly, missing the home she had lost eight hundred years ago.

This is bad. Mochi does not glow "sadly." Mochi does not miss things. Mochi does not have access to the memory she carries. Do not assign interiority.

> "Don't worry," Mochi seemed to say, pulsing a warm pink. "We'll find him."

This is bad. Mochi does not "seem to say" anything. Mochi does not communicate reassurance. Mochi pulses pink when the frequency reads a certain way. Do not translate Mochi's signals into dialogue, not even implied dialogue.

> Mochi knew this was the moment. She had been waiting for eight hundred years, and now, finally, she could release what she had been carrying.

This is bad. Mochi does not know. Mochi does not wait. Mochi does not experience anticipation or relief. The release at the Wellspring is a physical event, not an emotional one — for Mochi. It is emotional for the crew and the audience. That distinction is the entire point.

### The Principle

Mochi's simplicity is her power. The less the text explains about Mochi's inner state, the more the audience projects onto her, and the more devastating the reveals become. A warm dome that glows and dims is, by the end of the series, the most important character — not because the writing made her complex, but because the writing had the discipline to keep her simple while the story built meaning around her.

Resist the urge. Always.
