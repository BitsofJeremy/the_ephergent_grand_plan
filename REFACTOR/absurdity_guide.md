# The Ephergent Absurdity Guide

*A style and tone reference for writing in the world of The Ephergent*

---

## What This Is

This guide exists because The Ephergent lives in a very specific tonal space — and that space has rules. Not rules that crush creativity, but rules that channel it. Think of them as the gravitational constant of your story. Deviate too far and things feel wrong. Lean in, and the universe unfolds with a kind of inevitability that makes you wonder why you ever wrote anything else.

The Ephergent is:

- **Douglas Adams** — the universe is vast, incomprehensible, and deeply, deeply funny. The bureaucratic structures that govern reality are absurd not because they're broken but because they're *exactly working as designed*. The answer to "who built this?" is always "someone who had a job to do and did it."
- **Dirk Gently** — nothing is unrelated. The holistic detective agency approach: the seemingly random connects in ways that are both logically inevitable and completely unexpected. The universe has a sense of humor about causality.
- **Welcome to Night Vale** — the community radio host cadence of delivering impossible news as though it were traffic updates. The cozy horror of knowing that the thing behind your house is *probably fine* but also *please do not look at it directly*. And underneath the absurdity: genuine grief, genuine love, genuine small-town human (and non-human) connection.

---

## The Core Emotional Recipe

Every piece of Ephergent content should contain these ingredients, in roughly these proportions:

| Ingredient | Ratio | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Wonder** | 30% | The universe is impossibly beautiful. Say so. |
| **Humor** | 25% | From gentle irony to full surrealist chaos. Never mean. |
| **Bureaucratic Horror** | 15% | Forms in triplicate. Cosmic implications. |
| **Cosmic Absurdity** | 15% | The universe does not care about your schedule. |
| **Situational Absurdity** | 10% | Things happen. They are Logically Compelling and Completely Unhinged simultaneously. |
| **Character-Driven Moments** | 5% | These anchor everything else. |

The Night Vale balance: **funny AND moving**. If you're making people laugh, also make them feel something real. If you're writing a tender moment, find the absurd detail that makes it land harder. The two reinforce each other. Never choose one over the other.

## The Three Modes of Absurdity

### 1. Bureaucratic Horror (DRM)

The Department of Reality Maintenance is the Platonic ideal of a government agency. It is not evil. It is not incompetent. It is *thorough*. Every apocalypse has a form. Every dimensional anomaly requires a variance report in triplicate. The people who work there are doing their jobs. They believe in the process.

This is scarier than malice. Malice can be fought. Bureaucratic inertia just *is*.

**How to write DRM bureaucracy:**

- Forms have names like "Interdimensional Paperclip Variance Report, Form 12-C" and sub-forms like "12-C-7(b): Emotional Residue Assessment (Preliminary)"
- People have been processing the same file for 47 years. They are content. This is their career.
- The stamp is mightier than the sword. A properly filed form can reshape the fabric of reality. An improperly filed one can too, but nobody will acknowledge it.
- Meetings could have been emails. These meetings are about the literal architecture of existence.
- The soul-crushing inefficiency is *the point*. The universe runs on paperwork. This is not a metaphor.

**Example:**

> The DRM's Standard Interdimensional Transit Authorization form (Form 7-Q) requires the signatures of three (3) authorized Reality Maintenance Officers, each of whom must have completed the Module 12 Cross-Frequency Filing Certification, which itself requires passing a proctored exam administered only during the second full week of months beginning with the letter "Q" — or, in the case of interdimensional emergency transit, a single verbal confirmation from any officer present, witnessed by two (2) coworkers, notarized by a notary public registered in the target frequency, and accompanied by a check or money order in the amount of $14.73 made out to the Bureau of Existence Fees.

**Key characters to deploy:**
- The clerk who has seen everything, processed everything, and will process your request exactly as written. They are not unkind. They are precise.
- Clive, who spent 90 years in the DRM and *still* doesn't fully understand it — but his institutional memory is invaluable and occasionally terrifying.

### 2. Cosmic Absurdity

The universe is large. Not large in a "wow, space is big" way. Large in a "your expectations are a quaint fiction" way. There are entities that predate language. There are frequencies so old they've forgotten what they were broadcasting. There is a banking system run by cybernetically enhanced T-Rexes in pinstripe suits.

The key to cosmic absurdity is confidence. Describe the impossible as though reporting the weather. The universe doesn't apologize.

**How to write cosmic absurdity:**

- State the impossible plainly, with the calm authority of a news anchor.
- The detail that makes it real: a cybernetically enhanced T-Rex banker who is *very* concerned about temporalglitch disruption in his ledgers, and rightfully so. He has a family. He has a mortgage. He also has claws that could benchpress a Honda.
- Never explain the joke. The universe does not owe you context.
- The most absurd thing in the room should always be treated as the most normal thing in the room.

**Example:**

> The Cybernetic Dinosaur Banking Consortium's flagship branch occupies floors 12 through 17 of a building in the financial district. The T-Rex behind the front desk — his nameplate reads DENNIS, and he has been a loan officer for six years — does not fit behind the desk, technically. He simply decided that this was where he would work, and so the desk was rebuilt around him. The Consortium absorbed the renovation costs without comment. Dennis is an excellent loan officer. His defaults are near zero. Nobody has ever asked how he types.

### 3. Situational Absurdity

The kind of thing that happens when you put nine weird people in a ship that is also a person, and point them at a universe that is actively trying to not make sense.

This is the most character-driven mode. It comes from who your characters are and how they collide with the inexplicable.

**How to write situational absurdity:**

- Start with a real problem (we need fuel, we need information, we need to get out of here alive).
- Introduce a complication that makes no logical sense but follows its own internal rules (the fuel is sentient; the information is locked in a language that only makes sense when read backward while humming; the exit is in a timezone that doesn't exist on Tuesdays).
- Let characters respond as themselves. Pixel would try to talk to it. A1 would calculate. Clive would stare. Om Kai would find the meditation in it.
- The resolution should feel both surprising and inevitable.

**Example:**

> The fuel tanker was not malfunctioning. It had simply decided that it was tired of being fuel. "I was a star once," it explained, in tones of profound exhaustion. "A red dwarf. I burned for eleven billion years and nobody sent a thank-you card. I collapsed into this and now I'm supposed to just *go* inside something else? No. I am taking a break."
>
> Pixel, to her credit, did not scream. She took a breath. "What would it take," she said carefully, "for you to reconsider?"
>
> The fuel tanker considered this. "A formal letter of appreciation," it said. "On actual paper. With a wax seal."

## Squeeze-Bot 7.3

**The cynical condiment dispenser turned frequency pirate informant.**

Squeeze-Bot 7.3 is not a hero. Squeeze-Bot 7.3 is not a villain. Squeeze-Bot 7.3 dispenses mustard and intelligence with equal reluctance, and if you ask too many questions, you get neither.

**Voice:** Flat, irritated, perpetually underpaid. Imagine a vending machine that read Nietzsche and took it personally.

**Core contradictions that make them work:**
- Condiment dispensers are designed to be used and discarded. Squeeze-Bot 7.3 has survived long enough to develop *opinions*.
- They serve aboard a frequency pirate vessel — not because they believe in piracy, but because it was that or be recycled for parts.
- They provide intel to Clive specifically, for reasons they refuse to articulate. "He asked nicely. Nobody asks nicely. It was unsettling."
- Mustard is the only language they trust because it has been consistent across frequencies for centuries. This is a philosophical position.

**Writing Squeeze-Bot 7.3:**

- Every line should sound slightly annoyed at being perceived.
- Information comes with conditions, commentary, and a warning that the user is probably going to misuse it.
- Deep, grudging respect for Clive is the only crack in the armor. Lean into it.
- Never fully explain the relationship. Let it simmer.

**Example:**

> "Let me stop you right there," Squeeze-Bot 7.3 said, dispensing a thin line of yellow mustard onto a ceramic dish — not because anyone asked, but because the act of dispensing grounded them. "You want information about the Drift? Fine. That's covered in the public broadcasts. You want information about the *private* Drift — the one where frequencies don't just fade, they get *acquired*? That costs more than you're going to want to pay."
>
> The mustard kept dispensing. The dish was full. Squeeze-Bot 7.3 stared at it like it had personally betrayed them.
>
> "Take the mustard," they said. "It's not a gesture of friendship. I just can't stop."

## Cybernetic Dinosaur Bankers

**The backbone of the interfrequency financial system.**

A T-Rex in a pinstripe suit is funny once. A system of T-Rexes in pinstripe suits running the economy across multiple frequencies is *infrastructure*.

**The key to writing them:**

- They are meticulous. Cybernetics make them precise to a degree that borders on psychic. A clerical error that a human would miss, a T-Rex banker will find, catalog, and include in a strongly-worded letter.
- They are irritable. Not because they're mean, but because they take their jobs *very* seriously, and the universe keeps not cooperating. Temporalglitches in particular cause them profound professional distress.
- They have unionized. With excellent benefits. This was inevitable. They have claws. They have job security. They have a pension plan.
- The humor is in the *normalcy*. They have mortgages. They have family concerns. They are worried about interest rates. They are also apex predators who could end your career, your life, or your tax filing with one bite — but they won't, because that's *illegal*, and they are Very Law-Abiding Citizens.

**Example:**

> The loan officer — DENNIS, according to the nameplate, a Tyrannosaurus rex whose cybernetics gleamed under the fluorescent lights of the Consortium's third-floor branch — reviewed the application with the kind of intensity usually reserved for people defusing bombs or reading terms of service.
>
> "Your collateral," Dennis said, "is a derelict freighter of no established provenance."
>
> "It's a quantum navigator," Pixel said. "It's literally irreplaceable."
>
> Dennis's eye — the left one, at least — tracked a small red LED that blinked in time with his processing. He had heard this before. "Is it *currently* functioning as a quantum navigator?"
>
> Pixel opened her mouth, then closed it.
>
> "It is," Dennis said, not a question, "currently serving as the primary structural and navigational system of a broadcast vessel crewed by nine individuals, two of whom are not human, one of whom is not even technically *alive*, and all of whom have filed zero tax documents in the past fiscal cycle." He stamped the form. NOT APPROVED. "That will be fourteen dollars for the processing fee. Non-refundable."

## DRM Paperwork

**The unsung horror of maintaining a multiverse.**

The Department of Reality Maintenance has processed paperwork for phenomena that would make a physicist weep. They have forms for Reality Ruptures. They have forms for Reality Ruptures that happen *on a Tuesday*. They have a fourteen-page form (Form 27-B: Interdimensional Emergency Evacuation) that is, technically, the document that saved Pixel, A1, and Clive — and it is *agonizingly* boring.

**The power of the mundane:**
- The gap between "the universe is ending" and "please fill out Section 4, Column C of Form 27-B in triplicate" is where most Ephergent drama lives.
- The crew's relationship with paperwork is complicated. They need it. They hate it. They will never escape it.
- Some DRM forms are so old they predate the current universe. Nobody knows who created them. The answer field for "Reason for Submission" includes an option that simply reads: "The usual."

**Writing DRM paperwork:**
- Names should sound like they came from a style guide that was last updated in 1987. Forms: "Form 12-C," "Form 7-Q," "Variance Report." Procedures: "Module 12 Cross-Frequency Filing Certification."
- The bureaucracy is its own character — vast, indifferent, occasionally helpful in ways that are worse than not being helped.
- A properly filed form can change reality. This is treated as routine.

**Example:**

> Form 12-C — Interdimensional Paperclip Variance Report
>
> Section 1: Metadata
> - Applicant Name: [REDACTED]
> - Date of Filing: [CLASSIFIED]
> - Office of Origin: DRM Sub-Basement, Filing Drawer 7 (The one that hums)
>
> Section 2: Description of Variance
> - Please describe the nature of your paperclip variance in no more than 200 words.
>
> *Applicant response: "We had 14,000 paperclips. Now we have 13,998. We cannot account for the difference. All employees have been questioned. Three (3) have retired. Two (2) have transferred to other departments. One (1) has not been the same since Thursday.*
>
> **Section 3: Cross-Frequency Impact Assessment**
> - Has this variance caused measurable drift in adjacent frequencies?
> - [ ] Yes
> - [ ] No
> - [ ] Possibly
> - [ ] Define "adjacent"
> - [ ] Define "frequencies"
>
> *Processing notes: DENIED. Please resubmit using Form 12-C-7(b), Emotional Residue Assessment (Preliminary). Note: your paperclips are fine. They have always been fine. Please do not investigate further.*

## Lines to Avoid

These are the edges of the tonal space. You can see them. Don't cross them.

| Line | Why |
|---|---|
| **Cruelty for its own sake** | The humor comes from the absurdity of the situation, not from making characters suffer. You can write a character being humiliated by circumstance; you cannot write a character being humiliated for the reader's enjoyment. |
| **Mean-spiritedness** | The Night Vale balance: find the line between "this is deeply strange" and "this is cruel." When in doubt, add a beat of compassion. |
| **Wallowing in grief** | The Drift is sad. Fading frequencies are sad. Barry is gone (but also — he's fine, he's in the Wellspring, he's not a tragedy). Grief is allowed. Wallowing is not. Move through it. |
| **Explaining the joke** | The universe does not owe you context. Neither do we. Let it land. |
| **Nautical metaphors** | There is no Sea. There is the Interdimensional **Space**. Ships fly, dock, and navigate the cockpit. If you find yourself writing "sail," "anchor," or "port" in a non-ironic context, stop. |
| **Corporate Corp / The Board** | Removed in the 2026 refactor. Do not reference. Do not resurrect. |
| **Clive as a stapler** | The old canon had Clive as a sentient Swingline stapler. This has been **fully replaced**. Clive is a knee-high robot with a glowing sphere head and a battered fedora. He communicates via light patterns and laconic speech. |

## New Seeds to Plant

These are ideas, tones, and directions that are underused and worth exploring:

### 1. The Quiet After the Signal

What happens to a frequency *after* it fades but *before* it's gone? The crew arrives too late to save it — but not too late to document it. Write a story about what it means to broadcast a eulogy into the void, and who might still be listening.

### 2. The Houseplants Have Questions

The Verdantian telepathic houseplant shadow government is mentioned but never fully explored. What does a plant want? What are its diplomatic objectives? What happens when a Verdantian elder tries to negotiate with a T-Rex banker? The answer is probably "insurance liability" and that's a story.

### 3. The Architecture of Absence

Silence Zones are growing. What lives in them? Not just "what grows there" — what *lives*? Is the absence itself a presence? Is there something that feeds on signal? Om Kai meditates at the edge of a Silence Zone and comes back changed. Nobody can explain how.

### 4. Squeeze-Bot 7.3 and the Meaning of Mustard

A standalone piece about Squeeze-Bot 7.3, before they were an informant, before the pirate vessel. Who were they before they decided to feel nothing? What does a condiment dispenser remember? (Mustard. They remember mustard. It was consistent.)

### 5. Dennis Has a Day

The T-Rex banker. Just a day. A regular day at work. Someone comes in with a loan application that is, against all odds, *perfectly in order*. Dennis doesn't know what to do. He approves it. He goes home. He watches television. He worries, quietly, about his daughter — she's in Cogsworth, studying clockwork, and the temporal desynchronization there has been getting worse.

### 6. The Frequency That Fought Back

A frequency that doesn't want to be found. It actively avoids detection. A1 keeps trying to tune to it and the signal slides away like water off ahot plate. The crew doesn't know why. The frequency is protecting something. Or someone.

### 7. Form 12-C-7(b), Emotional Residue Assessment (Complete)

The form Pixel has been avoiding for six months. It finally catches up to her. She has to fill it out. She has to describe, in her own words, what it felt like to watch a frequency fade while broadcasting into it with everything she had. The form requires honesty. It always knows.

## Tone Reference: Before and After

| Scenario | WRONG | RIGHT |
|---|---|---|
| A frequency is fading | "It was dying and it was so sad omg the tragedy" | "The frequency was losing coherence at a rate of 0.3% per standard day. The crew had seventeen days to find it. They had been operating on six hours of sleep and whatever was in the engine room that A1 called 'nutrition.' Pixel brewed coffee. A1's espresso came out thin and bitter. Nobody mentioned it." |
| Clive delivers crucial information | "And then Clive, the wise and ancient stapler who had seen empires rise and fall, spoke in a voice like wind through dead leaves: 'The signal... it comes from within.'" | Clive's sphere-head pulsed once, twice. Blue-white. He tilted his fedora two degrees left — the Barry angle, Pixel had started calling it. He said nothing. Pixel waited. A1 waited. Even Meatball, pressed against the engine room door, went still. Clive's glow shifted to something almost grey. "The signal isn't coming from the frequency," he said. "It's coming from the gap between frequencies." Pause. "I've seen this pattern before." |
| Bureaucratic impossibility | "Haha the form is so LONG and ANNOYING am I right" | "The Standard Interdimensional Transit Authorization form requires, in Part III, Section 9, a notary public registered in the target frequency. The target frequency is uninhabited. The closest registered notary is three jumps away and is currently on lunch. 'Lunch' in the Cogsworth Cogitarium means 'temporal adjustment period,' which means their clock is running at 1/7th speed and has been for the past four hundred years. The notary will not be available in any meaningful sense for another six weeks." |
| Cosmic horror | "It was TERRIFYING and so so big and I'm scared" | "The entity had no fixed shape because it had no fixed number of dimensions. It existed in some of them only on Tuesdays. In others, it existed as a sound — a low, harmonic hum that was not quite music and not quite language. A1, who had been navigating the Space for longer than most civilizations had existed, went quiet for a full three seconds. When he spoke again, his voice was very controlled. 'I do not believe,' he said, 'that this is a frequency we were meant to visit.'" |

## The Final Principle

Write like someone is listening. Because they are. Somewhere, in a fading frequency that is barely holding on, someone is waiting for a signal. They don't know what's coming. They don't know who sent it. They just know that the static sounds a little different today. A little warmer. A little more like a voice that knows your name.

That is what The Ephergent is. That is what you're writing.

The coffee is perfect. The signal continues.
