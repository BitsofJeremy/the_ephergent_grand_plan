# THE LAUGHING FUNERAL — Game Design Bible

**Game 4 in The Ephergent Pipeline**
**Genre**: Point-and-Click Mystery
**Player Character**: Baron Klaus
**Setting**: Nocturne Aeturnus (Frequency 2)
**Engine**: Godot 4 HTML5 Export
**Size Cap**: 15MB hard limit
**Status**: Design Bible Complete — Pre-Production

---

## I. OVERVIEW

The Laughing Funeral is a narrative-driven point-and-click mystery set in Nocturne Aeturnus — a gothic frequency of permanent twilight where emotions crystallize into mineral and grief is the culture's most valued public art. The player controls Baron Klaus, the Ephergent's procurement specialist and investigator, as he returns to his homeworld for the first time since leaving under circumstances he has never explained.

The premise: someone has died in Nocturne Aeturnus. A funeral was held. And at that funeral — in a frequency where laughter during mourning is a rupture in the social fabric, where grief is currency and sorrow is architecture — *everyone laughed*.

Klaus has been asked to investigate. Not by Nocturne's authorities — they have declined to acknowledge the event. He was contacted by the dead person's sister, who cannot reconcile what happened with anything she knows about her world, and who remembers Klaus as the investigator who did not look away.

This is a mystery game. It is also a character study. It is also, in its final act, the hinge point where Klaus's personal investigation collides with the Ephergent's larger mystery — because in the funeral house's archive room, Klaus finds something Barry Kowalski left behind. Something that changes everything.

**Estimated playtime**: 45–90 minutes
**Tone**: Gothic noir. Beautiful. Mournful. Wry. The humor is dry and arrives sideways.

## II. SETTING — NOCTURNE AETURNUS

### The Frequency

Nocturne Aeturnus is Frequency 2 — a world of permanent twilight, deep indigo, purple, and midnight blue. The air is thick with petrichor and ancient stone. Gothic spires rise from mist-shrouded valleys. Candlelight flickers in windows of estates built from solidified emotion. Everything here feels like the most beautiful moment of dusk, stretched into eternity.

The frequency's signal character is a low, resonant chord — like a cello played in a cathedral. Melancholy and gorgeous. When you tune in, you feel it in your chest before you hear it. The signal carries emotional weight; receivers sometimes weep without knowing why.

**Emotion crystallization** is Nocturne's economic engine and cultural foundation. Grief crystallizes into mineral. Joy becomes light. The Sorrowful Moon — the celestial body that amplifies Melancholic Joy across the entire frequency — hangs low and constant, casting everything in silver-violet radiance. The culture values emotional authenticity: the public, shared experience of feeling.

**The Dimming's effect**: Nocturne is particularly vulnerable to the Drift. Emotion crystallization has become unstable — crystals crack without warning. Moods swing unpredictably. The Sorrowful Moon flickers. Some nights it dims entirely, and what follows is not twilight but absence. The crystals that form now are smaller, less complex, more prone to fracture. Residents who remember their grandparents' grief — vast, blue, mineral, permanent — mourn the thin crystals their own sorrows produce.

This is a world that is growing quieter. And into that quieting, someone laughed.

### The Funeral House — Eidolon Manse

The primary location. A three-story gothic estate on the edge of Nocturne's capital district, built from deep violet stone that absorbs light rather than reflecting it. The Manse has served as the city's premier funeral house for centuries — a place where the dead are honored through public grieving, where mourners' crystallized sorrow is collected and preserved as memorial, where the culture's most sacred emotional rituals are performed.

**Exterior**: Wrought-iron gates hung with crystallized grief — small, blue-black mineral formations that chime in wind with a sound like distant bells. A cobblestone path lined with dim lanterns. The building's facade features tall, narrow windows of stained glass depicting not images but *moods* — panels of deep blue and violet that shift hue subtly depending on the emotional state of whoever looks at them. The door is heavy, dark wood, always slightly ajar. In Nocturne, a funeral house is never closed.

**Ground Floor — The Vestibule & Mourning Hall**:
- **The Vestibule**: Entry chamber. A receiving desk where visitors sign the Ledger of Attendance. Coat hooks. A mirror that shows you slightly sadder than you are — standard Nocturne decor. Crystallized emotions line the walls in display cases: deep blue grief, pale silver longing, dark amber regret. A chandelier of hollow crystal that hums at the frequency of the room's collective mood.
- **The Mourning Hall**: The main ceremony space. Rows of high-backed chairs facing a raised platform where the deceased is displayed. The walls are acoustically tuned to amplify weeping — not cruelly, but as amplification of communal honor. The ceiling is painted with constellations visible only under Nocturne's Sorrowful Moon. This is where the laughter happened. The chairs are still arranged. No one has cleaned the room. The crystal formations left by the mourners' emotions are wrong — jagged, bright, cracked. Not grief crystals. Something else.

**Second Floor — Private Chambers & The Study**:
- **Director Voss's Study**: The funeral director's office. Heavy desk. Files. A window overlooking the courtyard garden where memorial crystals are planted. Shelves of reference volumes on mourning protocol. A locked cabinet.
- **The Viewing Room**: Where the body was prepared. Now empty. But the preparation table still holds faint impressions of the deceased's emotional residue — traces that Klaus can read.
- **The Guest Corridor**: Three rooms where overnight mourners stay during extended vigils. One room shows signs of recent occupation. The bed is made but the pillow has been turned twice.

**Basement — The Archive Room**:
- **The Archive**: Climate-controlled. Floor-to-ceiling shelves of ledgers, case files, memorial records. Every funeral conducted at Eidolon Manse for three hundred years is documented here. Filing system: chronological, cross-referenced by family name and crystallization type. One shelf, in the far corner, is not part of the Manse's own records. It holds overflow storage from institutions that no longer exist. DRM files. This is where Klaus finds Barry's ledger.

### The Streets of Nocturne

Two exterior locations, navigable between the Manse and key witness locations:

**The Twilight Promenade**: A broad avenue lit by emotional-resonance lanterns — each lamp adjusts its glow based on the prevailing mood of passersby. Currently dim. Few people walk here. Those who do walk slowly, heads down. The crystal formations growing from the stone at the street's edge are thin and fragile. A vendor sells crystallized memories from a cart — she is the optional NPC who provides background lore.

**The Crystalline Garden**: A public memorial space where the largest and most beautiful grief crystals are displayed. Towering formations of deep blue and violet mineral, some as tall as buildings, each the crystallized sorrow of a significant public mourning. The Garden is where Klaus meets one of the suspects in private. At night — which is always — the crystals glow faintly from within.

## III. THE MYSTERY

### The Premise

**The dead**: Aldric Venn, aged 74. A respected crystallographer — someone who studied and catalogued the properties of emotionally crystallized minerals. Quiet. Private. A widower. Known for his meticulous research into the declining quality of modern crystals. Not famous. Not powerful. Not the kind of person whose funeral should have been remarkable.

**The funeral**: Held three days ago at Eidolon Manse. Forty-two attendees. Standard mourning protocol — the Sorrowful Moon was visible, the hall was prepared, the Director performed the rites. And then, at the moment when the crystallization of communal grief should have formed — the moment when forty-two mourners' synchronized sorrow produces the memorial crystal — everyone laughed.

Not nervous laughter. Not hysteria. *Joy*. Genuine, uncontrollable, radiant joy. For eleven seconds, forty-two people in a funeral hall experienced the most intense happiness of their lives. The crystal that formed was not blue-black grief but brilliant gold — a color Nocturne has not seen in living memory. Then it stopped. The mourners were horrified. Several have not spoken since. The gold crystal sits on the preparation platform, and no one will touch it.

**Why this matters**: In Nocturne Aeturnus, emotional authenticity is the foundation of civilization. You cannot fake grief. You cannot manufacture joy. Emotions crystallize into physical reality — they are verifiable, material, permanent. If forty-two people felt joy at a funeral, then joy was *present*. Something *caused* it. And in a frequency where the Dimming has been slowly draining emotional depth for eight hundred years, a sudden explosion of genuine joy is not a miracle. It is a symptom. Of what, no one knows. That is what Klaus is here to find out.

### The Investigation — Beat Structure

**Act I: Arrival & Orientation (Puzzles 1–3)**

1. **The Vestibule** — Klaus arrives. Signs the Ledger of Attendance. Examines the crystal displays. Speaks with Director Voss, who is deeply unsettled and formally polite. Klaus establishes the basics: who died, when, who was present. Voss provides the guest list but is reluctant to discuss the laughter itself. *"It is not my place to characterize what the mourners experienced, Baron. I can only confirm that the protocol was followed precisely."*

2. **The Mourning Hall** — Klaus examines the scene. The chairs. The wrong-colored crystals. The gold crystal on the platform. Hotspot investigation: the crystal formations left on the chair backs (each mourner's seat retains trace emotion), the acoustic panels (were they tampered with?), the position of the Sorrowful Moon through the ceiling window (was the amplification normal?). Klaus observes that the crystals on the chairs are not uniform — most are the jagged bright formations, but three seats show different patterns. Three mourners felt something different from the rest.

3. **The Guest Register** — Klaus cross-references the seating chart with the guest list. The three anomalous seats belonged to: Aldric Venn's research assistant (Sable Thorn), Aldric's estranged sister (Vesper Venn), and an unnamed attendee who signed the register with initials only: "M.K." Klaus needs to find and interview all three.

**Act II: The Interviews (Puzzles 4–7)**

4. **Sable Thorn** (Funeral House Guest Corridor) — Aldric's research assistant. Young, precise, devastated. She has not left the Manse since the funeral. She tells Klaus about Aldric's final research project: he believed the Dimming was not decay but *transition* — that Nocturne's crystals were not getting weaker, they were changing into something new. He had isolated a sample of crystal that responded to frequencies no one had catalogued. She gives Klaus Aldric's research journal — but pages are missing. Someone tore out the final entries.

5. **Vesper Venn** (The Crystalline Garden) — Aldric's sister. Older, composed, grieving in the proper Nocturne way — publicly, with dignity. She contacted Klaus. She tells him that Aldric, in his final weeks, was agitated. Not afraid — *excited*. He told her he had found something in his crystal samples that proved the Dimming was not what everyone assumed. He used the word "signal." She dismissed it as academic enthusiasm. Now she is not sure. She gives Klaus a key to Aldric's locked study at the Manse — he had been using a private room to work during his final days.

6. **The Locked Study** — Klaus uses Vesper's key. Inside: Aldric's portable crystallography equipment. Notebooks. A device Klaus recognizes — a resonance amplifier, modified. On the desk: crystal samples in labeled cases. Most are standard Nocturne minerals. One is different. A small, warm crystal that does not match any known emotional type. It is gold. It hums faintly. Beside it, a note in Aldric's handwriting: *"This is not grief. This is not joy. This is a signal. Someone sent this. Through the crystal. Through the frequency. From somewhere else."*

7. **"M.K."** — The unnamed mourner. Klaus tracks the initials through the guest register's ink analysis (a puzzle: matching ink compounds to local suppliers reveals the pen used was institutional — DRM standard issue, discontinued decades ago). Klaus finds M.K. is not a person but a designation — a filing code. M.K. = "Maintenance, Kowalski." Someone signed the funeral register using Barry Kowalski's DRM personnel code. But Barry has been gone for years. Someone used his credentials. Or the register is lying. Or something else entirely.

**Act III: The Archive (Puzzles 8–10)**

8. **The Archive Room — Descent** — Klaus enters the basement archive. Director Voss is reluctant but cannot legally prevent an investigator from accessing public memorial records. The archive is vast, dim, and beautiful — shelves of leather-bound ledgers stretching into shadow, each containing the crystallized emotional records of every funeral for three centuries. Klaus navigates the filing system (puzzle: the Nocturne filing system is organized by emotional frequency, not alphabet — Klaus must deduce the filing logic from the index).

9. **The DRM Shelf** — In the far corner, Klaus finds the overflow shelf. Files from the Department of Reality Maintenance, stored here after the DRM's destruction because the funeral house was one of Nocturne's designated archive repositories. Most files are mundane — maintenance records, personnel rosters, equipment inventories. One ledger is different. Thinner. Filed under "Anomalous Resonance — Unclosed Cases." Inside: Form 12-C. *"Unidentified Resonance Object."* The case file for Clive.

10. **Barry's Ledger** — The Form 12-C file. Klaus reads it. Initial entry: a standard DRM anomaly report filed by a supervisor who noticed unusual frequency readings from the sub-basement. Subsequent entries: cursory investigations by rotating staff. No one took it seriously. Then, a change in handwriting. Careful. Methodical. Patient. Barry Kowalski's entries begin eight years into the file. He investigated the anomaly himself — on night shifts, in his own time, with no authorization. His notes are precise, observant, and increasingly fascinated. The final entry, in Barry's hand: *"Investigated. Case closed. Object is not an object. Do not document further. — B.K."* Klaus stares at this for a long time. Then he turns the page.

**Act IV: The Solution (Puzzles 11–12)**

11. **The Connection** — Behind the last page, tucked into the ledger's back cover: a folded frequency chart. Hand-drawn. Barry's work. It maps a signal pathway that runs through Nocturne Aeturnus — through the very stone that the funeral house is built from. The signal is old. Builder-era old. It has been dormant for centuries, embedded in the crystalline substrate of the frequency itself. Aldric Venn found it in his crystal samples. He didn't know what it was, but his research activated it — woke it up. The signal responded. It surged through the funeral house during the ceremony, and for eleven seconds, forty-two people felt what the Builders felt when they made this place: joy. Not Nocturne's joy. Older. Deeper. A Builder signal, sleeping in the bones of the world, briefly awakened by a crystallographer who asked the right question.

12. **The Presentation** — Klaus assembles the evidence. He does what he always does: arranges the truths in an order that creates a picture. He presents his findings to Director Voss and the witnesses. The laughter was not a malfunction. It was not a scandal. It was a signal — a piece of Builder infrastructure, woven into Nocturne's crystalline frequency eight hundred years ago, briefly activated by Aldric Venn's research into the Dimming. Aldric died knowing he was right: the Dimming is not decay. It is the slow fading of Builder systems that once sustained these frequencies. And the joy — the gold crystal — is proof that those systems still exist, dormant, waiting. The funeral was not disrupted. The funeral was *answered*.

### The Solution — What Really Happened

Aldric Venn spent thirty years studying the declining quality of emotional crystals. In his final months, he isolated a crystal sample that resonated at a frequency no modern instrument could identify — because it was not a modern frequency. It was Builder-era. A piece of the infrastructure the Builders wove into every frequency to sustain emotional resonance across the dimensional spectrum.

Aldric's analysis — precise, persistent, thorough — sent a ping through the dormant Builder network. Not intentionally. He was just studying a crystal. But the crystal was a node, and the node was connected to a network, and the network was connected to the Wellspring's outer signal architecture. The ping reached the network. The network responded. For eleven seconds, it activated — pushed a burst of the original Builder signal through the funeral house's crystalline foundations, and everyone present experienced the emotional resonance the Builders designed into the frequency eight hundred years ago: the feeling of being exactly where you are supposed to be.

Joy. In a frequency that has been slowly forgetting what full-spectrum joy feels like for eight centuries.

The gold crystal is the physical evidence. It is not grief and not joy as Nocturne understands them. It is *Builder resonance crystallized* — something that has not formed in this frequency since before the Dimming.

Aldric's missing journal pages: Sable tore them out — not to hide evidence, but to protect Aldric's legacy. The pages contained his wildest speculations, the ones he wrote late at night when his precision gave way to wonder. She didn't want people to dismiss his work as the ravings of an old man. She returns the pages to Klaus. They are beautiful. They are the work of a scientist who, at the end of his life, touched something magnificent and knew it.

The "M.K." signature: a glitch. Barry's DRM credentials were embedded in the filing system — his personnel code was used as a default signature stamp on any DRM-adjacent document processed through Nocturne's archive system. When Klaus accessed the Ledger of Attendance, the system auto-populated a DRM stamp from the overflow files. Barry did not attend the funeral. His ghost in the machine did.

### Red Herrings

1. **Director Voss's locked cabinet** — Contains personal grief crystals from funerals Voss has officiated. They are cracking — the Dimming is affecting even preserved crystals. Voss is hiding the deterioration from the public. This is genuinely concerning but unrelated to the laughter.

2. **The acoustic panels** — They were recently repaired. Not tampered with — genuinely repaired, because the Dimming has been degrading the hall's emotional amplification. The repair work made the hall *more* sensitive, not less, which contributed to the signal's effectiveness but did not cause it.

3. **Vesper's estrangement from Aldric** — They argued years ago about whether the Dimming was reversible. She thought it was permanent decline; he thought it was transition. She was wrong. The estrangement was about science, not secrets.

## IV. KLAUS AS PLAYER CHARACTER

### How Klaus Thinks

Klaus does not walk into a room. He *reads* a room. Every hotspot interaction is filtered through his investigative perception — he notices the detail first, then frames it. His internal monologue is the player's primary interface with the game world.

He tells you things sideways. He does not withhold information — he reframes it so you arrive at the conclusion yourself and feel clever for getting there. This is not manipulation. It is how his mind works. He approaches every conversation the way he approaches an investigation: the answer is in the room, you just have to know which questions to ask and in which order.

### Internal Monologue Style

Klaus's hotspot descriptions are wry, precise, and layered. He speaks in complete, polished sentences with a dry undertone. Observations that happen to be funny. Never jokes — observations.

**Examples of hotspot text**:

*Examining the gold crystal*:
> "Gold. In Nocturne. This crystal should not exist. Not because the physics are wrong — because the emotion is wrong. No one in this frequency has felt this in living memory. Which means either someone brought this feeling from elsewhere, or Nocturne is remembering something it forgot."

*Examining Director Voss's desk*:
> "Three fountain pens, arranged by nib width. A stack of mourning protocols, each annotated in the margins with corrections so small they require magnification. Director Voss has opinions about comma placement in eulogy templates. This is a person who controls what they can control."

*Looking at the Mourning Hall ceiling*:
> "The constellations are painted to match the Sorrowful Moon's position during the autumn equinox. Someone chose that specific configuration. The autumn equinox is the one night per year when the Moon's amplification peaks. Whoever designed this hall was either very sentimental or very precise. In Nocturne, the distinction is academic."

*Trying to use an item incorrectly*:
> "I could do that. I won't — but I could. The distinction between capability and judgment is the foundation of investigative work."

### How Klaus Interacts with NPCs

Klaus's dialogue approach is oblique. He asks questions that sound like observations. He makes observations that are actually questions. He gives people space to arrive at the truth themselves, then notes where they stop.

**Dialogue philosophy**: Klaus never accuses. He presents information and watches the reaction. His dialogue options should always give the player three modes:
1. **Direct observation** — stating what Klaus has noticed (efficient, sometimes blunt)
2. **Indirect question** — framing a question as a tangential remark (Klaus's preferred mode)
3. **Silence** — sometimes the most effective tool is waiting

The player chooses Klaus's approach, but all paths lead to the same information — the difference is in how NPCs respond emotionally. Direct questions make NPCs defensive. Indirect questions make them thoughtful. Silence makes them uncomfortable, and uncomfortable people fill silence with truth.

### Returning to Nocturne

This is Klaus's homeworld. He left. He has not been back. The game should convey this through environmental reactions:

- Klaus recognizes things. His hotspot text for familiar locations carries an extra layer: *"The Promenade lanterns still adjust to mood. They used to glow warmer when I walked here. They are adjusting to me now. It takes a moment. I've been gone long enough to be unfamiliar."*
- NPCs recognize him. Some are glad. Some are wary. Director Voss remembers Klaus's institutional reputation: thorough, uncompromising, and inconvenient.
- The Sorrowful Moon affects Klaus. He is from this frequency — the Moon's emotional amplification works on him. Brief moments where his monologue shifts from analytical to personal, quickly corrected. *"The Moon is bright tonight. It — I notice it more than I expected. Moving on."*

## V. POINT-AND-CLICK MECHANICS

### Core Loop

1. **Navigate** between rooms/locations via screen-edge hotspots or a location map
2. **Examine** hotspots for Klaus's observations and clue discovery
3. **Collect** items and evidence into an inventory
4. **Combine** items or evidence to form connections
5. **Talk** to NPCs through branching dialogue
6. **Present** evidence to NPCs to unlock new dialogue and progress
7. **Deduce** — at key moments, the player assembles clues on an evidence board to form conclusions

### Room Navigation

The game uses static pre-rendered backgrounds with hotspot overlays. Each location is a single screen (no scrolling — keeps art budget manageable). Navigation between rooms uses a location map accessible from the bottom of the screen, plus screen-edge exits for adjacent rooms.

**Locations** (10 total):
1. Eidolon Manse — Vestibule
2. Eidolon Manse — Mourning Hall
3. Eidolon Manse — Director's Study
4. Eidolon Manse — Guest Corridor
5. Eidolon Manse — Locked Study
6. Eidolon Manse — Viewing Room
7. Eidolon Manse — Archive Room
8. The Twilight Promenade
9. The Crystalline Garden
10. Evidence Board (accessed via inventory — Klaus's mental reconstruction space)

### Inventory System

Bottom-of-screen inventory bar, expanding on hover. Items are represented as small pixel-art icons with tooltips. Klaus's internal monologue provides descriptions when items are examined.

**Item types**:
- **Physical evidence** (crystals, journal, key, ledger) — used on hotspots or presented to NPCs
- **Documents** (guest register excerpts, research notes, frequency chart) — readable in inventory, can be presented
- **Combined evidence** — formed when the player connects two clues on the evidence board

### Dialogue Trees

NPC conversations use a portrait-and-text-box format. Klaus's portrait on the left, NPC on the right. Three dialogue options per choice node (direct / indirect / silence). Each NPC has:
- An initial conversation (available on first meeting)
- Follow-up conversations (unlocked by finding specific evidence)
- A final conversation (unlocked in Act IV when Klaus is ready to present findings)

**Dialogue should feel like investigation**, not exposition. NPCs don't dump information. They respond to what Klaus says and shows them. The player must choose the right approach and present the right evidence to unlock each NPC's crucial information.

### Hotspot Interactions

Standard point-and-click: cursor changes on hover over interactive elements. Two interaction modes:
- **Eye icon** (examine) — Klaus observes and comments
- **Hand icon** (interact) — Klaus touches, takes, uses, or opens

Items can be used on hotspots from inventory (drag or select-then-click). Failed attempts get unique Klaus responses — never generic "that doesn't work" messages. Every failure response is in-character and often wryly amusing.

## VI. NPCs

### 1. Director Voss Umbral

**Role**: Funeral director of Eidolon Manse
**Age**: 62
**Appearance**: Tall, thin, impeccably dressed in the Nocturne formal mourning style — long dark robes with crystal-thread embroidery. Silver hair pulled tight. Hands that are always clasped or occupied. Never idle.
**Personality**: Controlled. Professional. Deeply unsettled by the laughter but processing it through protocol and procedure. Voss has officiated thousands of funerals. This is the first one that broke the rules. Their composure is a fortress, but the foundations are cracking — like the grief crystals in their locked cabinet.
**What they're hiding**: The Dimming is destroying preserved crystals. Memorial formations that families paid to maintain in perpetuity are degrading. Voss has been quietly replacing cracked crystals with synthetic reproductions. This has nothing to do with the laughter but everything to do with Voss's anxiety about the stability of the world they serve.
**Key dialogue**: *"In three hundred years, this house has never produced a gold crystal. I have consulted every record. Every reference. Gold does not occur in Nocturne's crystalline spectrum. And yet."*

### 2. Sable Thorn

**Role**: Aldric Venn's research assistant
**Age**: 28
**Appearance**: Small, sharp-featured, dark circles under eyes that speak to sleepless nights. Wears a research smock over Nocturne formal wear — she came to the funeral from the lab and never went home. Her hands are stained with crystal dust, faint blue-violet at the fingertips.
**Personality**: Precise. Loyal. Grieving in a way that manifests as fierce protectiveness of Aldric's work. She answers questions in short, measured sentences. She is not unfriendly — she is guarding something.
**What she's hiding**: She tore out the final pages of Aldric's research journal. Not malice — protection. The pages contain Aldric's late-night speculations, written when his precision gave way to wonder. She didn't want his peers to see the great crystallographer writing like a poet about signals from the deep past.
**Key dialogue**: *"Aldric spent thirty years measuring crystal degradation. At the end, he stopped measuring and started listening. I thought the Dimming had gotten to him. I was wrong about what he was hearing."*

### 3. Vesper Venn

**Role**: Aldric's older sister
**Age**: 79
**Appearance**: Dignified. White hair in an elaborate traditional style. Wears mourning crystals — small formations pinned to her lapel that she grew herself. Her grief is public, correct, and genuine. She stands in the Crystalline Garden because that is where you go to grieve properly in Nocturne.
**Personality**: Direct — unusual for Nocturne. She contacted Klaus because she remembers his reputation: the investigator who found truths that Nocturne's institutions preferred to leave buried. She respects thoroughness. She is tired of people telling her the laughter was "an anomaly."
**What she's hiding**: Nothing, really. Vesper's secret is that she and Aldric argued about the Dimming and never reconciled before he died. She thought the decline was permanent. He thought it was transition. She was wrong. She knows this now. The grief she carries is doubled — for his death and for the years she could have spent listening to him.
**Key dialogue**: *"My brother believed Nocturne was not dying. He believed it was waiting. I told him that was sentiment, not science. He looked at me the way he looked at a crystal he hadn't classified yet. Patient. He said: 'Vesper, the Dimming is a question. I intend to find the answer.' I should have asked what the question was."*

### 4. Lucian Dray

**Role**: Aldric's colleague. Fellow crystallographer and member of Nocturne's Academic Conservatory.
**Age**: 55
**Appearance**: Well-dressed, precisely groomed. A professional peer who attended the funeral out of institutional obligation and has been quietly furious about the disruption ever since. His mourning crystals are perfectly formed — a public display of correctly calibrated grief.
**Personality**: Dismissive. Political. He represents the Nocturne establishment's position: the laughter was an anomaly, crystals sometimes behave unpredictably during the Dimming, there is nothing to investigate. He is not a villain — he genuinely believes this. He is also genuinely afraid that Aldric was right, because if the Dimming is transition rather than decline, the Conservatory's entire framework collapses.
**What he's hiding**: He reviewed Aldric's early findings months ago and dismissed them. He had the data. He could have supported the research. He chose institutional comfort over scientific inquiry. This is not evil. It is ordinary cowardice, and it haunts him.
**Key dialogue**: *"Aldric was a fine crystallographer. His recent work, however, ventured into speculation. The laughter at the funeral was unfortunate — a resonance artifact during a period of frequency instability. These things happen. They do not require investigation."* (Beat.) *"They should not require investigation."*

### 5. The Vendor — Moth

**Role**: Optional NPC. Memory-crystal vendor on the Twilight Promenade.
**Age**: Indeterminate — looks ancient but moves like someone much younger
**Appearance**: Draped in layers of dark fabric, face mostly hidden. Sells crystallized memories from a wheeled cart — small formations that, when held, let you experience a fragment of someone else's emotional past.
**Personality**: Chatty. Gossipy. Knows everyone's business. Moth is the Nocturne equivalent of a bartender — people tell them things while browsing crystals. They have no stake in the mystery but have overheard everything.
**What they're hiding**: Nothing. Moth is pure information source, wrapped in local color. They sell Klaus a memory crystal that contains a fragment of the funeral — a mourner's eleven-second experience of joy. If Klaus uses it, he feels the Builder signal briefly. It does not provide answers. It provides motivation.
**Key dialogue**: *"Gold crystal, they say. Gold! I've sold ten thousand memories and never seen gold. Last gold crystal in Nocturne was — oh, who keeps track? Before the Dimming. Before anyone alive. Someone brought sunshine to a funeral, Baron. In Nocturne. You have to admire the audacity."*

### 6. The Register — Archive Automaton

**Role**: The archive room's filing intelligence. Not an NPC in the traditional sense — an old Nocturne automation system, a crystal-powered cataloguing mechanism built into the archive's walls. Responds to queries with filed information.
**Appearance**: No body. A network of crystalline nodes embedded in the archive walls that glow when addressed. Voice is neutral, procedural, slightly outdated in its terminology.
**Personality**: Bureaucratic. Helpful within its parameters. Cannot interpret, only retrieve. Answers queries literally. If Klaus asks the right questions, it provides. If he asks the wrong questions, it provides what was asked — which is educational in its own way.
**Key dialogue**: *"Query: Form 12-C. Result: Unidentified Resonance Object. Filed: Department of Reality Maintenance, Sub-basement Archive, Personnel Designation B.K. Status: Case Closed. Notation: 'Object is not an object. Do not document further.' Query complete. Further retrieval?"*

## VII. THE BARRY DISCOVERY

### Setup

The archive room is the game's emotional center of gravity. Klaus goes down there looking for funeral records. He finds something else.

### The Sequence

**Step 1 — The DRM Shelf**

Klaus navigates the archive's filing system (puzzle 8 — emotional-frequency filing logic). While searching the memorial records for Aldric Venn's funeral documentation, the Archive Automaton directs him to a far corner: *"Cross-reference: Venn, Aldric. Crystallographic research files. Stored: Overflow Archive, Section D. Source institution: Department of Reality Maintenance. Note: Source institution no longer operational."*

Klaus knows the DRM. The crew came from the DRM. He goes to the shelf.

**Internal monologue**:
> "The Department of Reality Maintenance. I know that name. Everyone on the Ephergent does. The DRM is where Pixel worked. Where Clive was filed. Where — I know that name for more reasons than I am comfortable listing in a funeral house basement."

**Step 2 — Finding the Ledger**

The DRM shelf contains seven ledgers. Most are routine — equipment manifests, personnel rosters, frequency calibration logs. The sixth ledger is thinner than the others. Its spine reads: *Anomalous Resonance — Unclosed Cases*.

Klaus opens it. Inside: Form 12-C.

**The Form**:
> **DEPARTMENT OF REALITY MAINTENANCE**
> **Anomalous Resonance Report — Form 12-C**
> **Subject**: Unidentified Resonance Object
> **Location**: Sub-basement, Level 3, Alcove 7
> **Date of Initial Report**: [redacted — pre-Dimming calendar]
> **Classification**: Anomalous — Non-Standard Resonance Signature
>
> **Initial Assessment** (Supervisor K. Larsen): "Object emits low-frequency resonance inconsistent with known equipment. Recommend monitoring. Low priority."
>
> **Follow-up 1** (Tech. R. Osei): "No change. Resonance persists. Object appears inert. Filed for periodic check."
>
> **Follow-up 2** (Tech. R. Osei): "Periodic check. No change. Recommend downgrade to inactive status."
>
> [Seven further entries over several years, increasingly perfunctory. Nobody cared.]
>
> **Entry 11** (B. Kowalski, Night Security): "Conducted independent assessment during off-hours. Object is not inert. Resonance patterns shift in response to external stimuli — specifically, to sustained verbal interaction. This is not equipment behavior. This is responsive. Continuing observation."
>
> **Entry 12** (B. Kowalski): "Resonance object produces structured harmonic patterns when addressed directly. Patterns are not random. They are language-adjacent. Requesting formal research authorization."
>
> **Entry 13** (B. Kowalski): "Authorization denied. Continuing observation independently. Object has begun to respond with vocalization. The vocalization is coherent. I believe this is a consciousness."
>
> **Entry 14** (B. Kowalski): "Investigated. Case closed. Object is not an object. Do not document further. — B.K."

**Step 3 — Klaus's Reaction**

Klaus reads Entry 14 and stops. His internal monologue shifts register — for the first and only time in the game, Klaus's voice loses its wry distance:

> "B.K. Barry Kowalski. Night security guard at the DRM. Clive's — Clive. He was documenting Clive. For years. Alone, on night shifts, with no authorization. He talked to a piece of equipment every night until it talked back, and when it did, he closed the file. Not because the investigation was over. Because it stopped being an investigation and became something else."

A pause. Then, the wryness returns, but changed:

> "I know what that feels like."

**Step 4 — The Frequency Chart**

Klaus turns to the ledger's back cover. A folded page — hand-drawn, precise, in Barry's handwriting. A frequency chart mapping a signal pathway through Nocturne Aeturnus. The signal runs through the crystalline substrate of the frequency — through the stone, through the buildings, through the very material that Eidolon Manse is built from. A Builder signal, dormant for eight hundred years, woven into the foundations of the world.

This is the connection. Aldric's research activated a node in this network. The funeral laughter was the network responding. And Barry Kowalski, a night guard with a thermos and a notebook, mapped this signal pathway years before anyone else knew it existed. He mapped it because he was following Clive's trail. Because Clive's resonance signature was *in* the network.

**Internal monologue**:
> "Barry was here. Not physically — through the system. His investigation ran through Nocturne Aeturnus. He mapped the Builder signal that Aldric accidentally activated. He was building a case — the same way I build a case. Evidence. Connections. Patience. He just followed it further than I imagined anyone could follow anything."

### What It Means

The Barry discovery transforms the game's scope. What began as a local mystery — why did people laugh at a funeral? — becomes a thread in the Ephergent's central narrative. Barry Kowalski was not just a night guard who befriended a robot. He was an investigator who spent twenty-three years mapping the Builders' legacy, and his trail runs through every frequency, including Klaus's homeworld.

The Form 12-C ledger is the physical evidence that connects Klaus's personal story to the crew's larger mission. It is the reason Klaus left Nocturne the first time — because his original investigation into the funeral house's records led him to institutional files that Nocturne's governing structures preferred to leave unexamined. Now he knows why those files mattered.

## VIII. CONNECTION TO SEASON 2

### What Klaus Brings Back

Klaus returns to the Ephergent with:
1. **Barry's ledger** — Form 12-C and the frequency chart. Physical evidence that Barry was building a case about Clive and the Builder network long before the crew existed.
2. **The gold crystal** — proof that Builder systems are still active, dormant in frequency substrates, responsive to investigation.
3. **Knowledge** — that the Dimming is not decay but the slow failure of Builder infrastructure. That the infrastructure still exists. That it can be reawakened.

### How He Presents It

Klaus does what Klaus does. He arrives at the crew meeting. He opens his coat. He places the ledger on the table. He arranges the evidence in an order that creates a picture. He tells them — sideways, through the investigation, framed as a case he's presenting. But the case is bigger than a funeral.

> "A crystallographer in Nocturne Aeturnus accidentally activated a piece of Builder infrastructure that has been dormant for eight hundred years. Forty-two people felt joy at a funeral. I found, in the archive room, a DRM file that should not exist. Barry Kowalski investigated Clive. He closed the case because Clive is not a case. He is a person. And Barry was mapping the trail to the Wellspring through every frequency's crystalline substrate, including mine."

He pauses. Pixel sees something change in his face — the moment his personal investigation stops being personal and becomes the crew's.

> "The Ephergent Frequency sounds, to me, like evidence that has been waiting for the right investigator. Barry was that investigator. He followed the trail further than I thought possible. Now it's ours."

He puts the ledger in an interior pocket. He'll present the full case when the time is right. He always does.

### How This Feeds the Clive Reveal

The Form 12-C discovery is the first hard evidence the crew has that:
- Barry understood what Clive was (a consciousness, not equipment) years before anyone else
- Barry's investigation extended far beyond the DRM — he mapped Builder signals across frequencies
- Clive's resonance signature is part of the Builder network — he is not just a Companion Unit, he is architecturally connected to the infrastructure the Builders left behind
- The Wellspring trail that Clive has been subtly steering the crew toward was mapped, in part, by a night guard with a thermos

When the crew learns the full truth about Clive in the Season 2 revelations, Klaus's discovery is the evidentiary foundation. Clive looks at the ledger. Klaus looks at Clive. Neither says anything for a moment. Then Klaus: *"He filed you as Form 12-C. Then he spent twenty-three years proving the form was wrong."* Clive adjusts his fedora. That is enough.

## IX. VISUAL DIRECTION

### Overall Aesthetic

**Gothic permanent-twilight**. Every frame should feel like the most beautiful hour of dusk, frozen. The palette is narrow and deep: indigo, violet, midnight blue, charcoal, with accents of silver (moonlight), amber (candlelight), and — sparingly, powerfully — gold (the anomalous crystal, the Builder signal).

**Art style**: Detailed pixel art. Resolution target: 320×180 (scaled to 1280×720 for display). This gives the art a handcrafted quality while keeping file sizes manageable. Backgrounds are pre-rendered with layered parallax for depth. Characters are sprite-based with limited animation frames — portrait art for dialogue.

### The Funeral House

**Exterior**: Vertical composition emphasizing the building's height. Dark stone absorbing the surrounding twilight. Crystallized grief formations on the gate catching moonlight — small sparkles of deep blue. The path leads the eye to the always-ajar door, a sliver of warm amber light from within.

**Mourning Hall**: The largest interior. High ceiling with painted constellations barely visible in the dim light. Rows of chairs casting long shadows. The gold crystal on the platform is the brightest element in the room — it should draw the eye immediately. Wrong. Beautiful. Unsettling.

**Archive Room**: Warm but dim. Leather-bound ledgers create a texture of deep browns and burgundies against dark wood shelving. The Archive Automaton's crystal nodes glow faintly blue in the walls. The DRM shelf in the corner is slightly different — institutional, utilitarian, out of place in this ornate space. It should feel like a filing cabinet in a cathedral.

### Lighting as Mood

Nocturne has no sun. Light sources are:
- **The Sorrowful Moon**: Silver-violet, omnipresent through windows, always at the same low angle. Casts long shadows. Emotional amplification effect — scenes lit primarily by moonlight should feel heavier, more contemplative.
- **Candlelight**: Amber, warm, intimate. Interior scenes. Creates pools of visibility in otherwise dark rooms.
- **Crystal glow**: Blue-white from emotional crystals. Gold from the anomalous crystal. Provides environmental storytelling — a room's emotional history visible in the crystals that have formed there.
- **The Archive Automaton**: Cool blue nodes in the walls. The only "artificial" light source.

**Rule**: No scene should be fully lit. There should always be shadow. Darkness in Nocturne is not threatening — it is the natural state. Light is the intrusion. Light means something.

### Character Portraits

Dialogue portraits are larger, more detailed versions of the character sprites. Limited animation: blink, mouth movement, expression changes (2–3 expressions per NPC). Klaus's portrait shows his steel-gray eyes with violet flecks. His expressions range from analytical (default) to wry (when observing something absurd) to a single frame of unguarded recognition (the Barry ledger moment).

## X. AUDIO DIRECTION

### Music

**Sparse. Mournful. Ambient.** The score should feel like the space between notes, not the notes themselves.

**Main theme**: A solo cello line — simple, slow, melancholic. Plays during navigation and quiet exploration. Should feel like it exists in the world, not on a soundtrack — as though someone in Nocturne is playing, distantly, and the sound carries through the twilight air.

**The Mourning Hall**: No music. Only ambient sound. The absence of music in the room where laughter happened is more powerful than any score.

**The Archive Room**: Low drone. A sustained, barely perceptible tone that evokes depth and age. When Klaus finds Barry's ledger, the drone shifts — not dramatically, but enough that attentive players notice something change.

**The Builder Signal**: When Klaus holds the gold crystal or reads Barry's frequency chart, a brief musical phrase — warm, major key, completely unlike anything else in the game. Builder resonance. Joy. It lasts a few seconds and then fades. The player should feel its absence.

### Sound Design

**Ambient layers**:
- Distant wind through gothic architecture
- Crystal chimes (emotional formations resonating with the Sorrowful Moon)
- Faint, distant weeping (Nocturne standard — this is not creepy, it is cultural)
- Footsteps on stone (Klaus's boots are distinctive — precise, measured)

**Interactive sounds**:
- Hotspot discovery: a soft crystalline ping
- Item acquired: a quiet click (Klaus pocketing something in his coat)
- Evidence connected: a resonant chord — a small version of Nocturne's cello frequency
- Wrong action: a muted thud (not punitive — Klaus sighing internally)
- Dialogue choice: the sound of a page turning

**The laughter**: Heard once, in Moth's memory crystal. Not a recording of laughter — a crystal's interpretation of laughter. Musical. Alien. Beautiful. It should make the player understand why forty-two people were horrified by their own joy.

## XI. TECHNICAL CONSTRAINTS

### 15MB Hard Cap — Size Budget

The 15MB limit is absolute. Browser-playable. No exceptions.

| Category | Budget | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Engine (Godot 4 HTML5) | ~3.5 MB | Compressed WASM + JS loader |
| Background Art (10 rooms) | ~4 MB | 320×180 pixel art, PNG compressed, ~400KB each |
| Character Sprites | ~1.5 MB | Klaus walk cycle + 6 NPC portraits with expressions |
| UI Elements | ~0.5 MB | Inventory bar, dialogue box, evidence board, cursors |
| Audio — Music | ~2 MB | Cello theme + archive drone + Builder signal. OGG Vorbis, low bitrate |
| Audio — SFX | ~1 MB | Ambient loops + interaction sounds. Short, compressed |
| Script / Dialogue | ~0.3 MB | All text, dialogue trees, puzzle logic |
| Scene Data | ~0.2 MB | Hotspot definitions, room layouts, state management |
| **Contingency** | **~1.5 MB** | Buffer for optimization or additional content |
| **Total** | **~15 MB** | |

### Optimization Strategies

- **Pixel art at native 320×180**: Dramatically smaller than HD pre-renders. Scaled via GPU. Crisp at any display size.
- **Limited color palette**: Nocturne's narrow palette (indigo/violet/blue/amber/gold) allows aggressive PNG compression. 16–32 color indexed palettes per background.
- **Audio compression**: OGG Vorbis at 64kbps for music, 48kbps for ambient loops. Short SFX clips. Music loops cleanly to minimize file count.
- **Sprite sheets**: All character animations on optimized sprite sheets. Limited frames (4-frame walk cycles, 2-3 dialogue expressions).
- **Text-driven narrative**: Klaus's internal monologue and dialogue carry the narrative weight. This is cheaper than animation. The game's richness is in the writing, not the spectacle.
- **No video. No voice acting.** Text and music only. Voice acting would consume the entire budget alone.

### Godot 4 HTML5 Implementation Notes

- **Export template**: Web export with SharedArrayBuffer support. Test cross-browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari).
- **Scene structure**: One scene per location. Room transitions via scene change — not continuous world.
- **State management**: Autoload singleton tracking puzzle progress, inventory, dialogue flags, evidence connections.
- **Save system**: LocalStorage-based. Single save slot. The game is short enough that save-scumming is unnecessary, but mid-session saves prevent frustration.
- **Input**: Mouse only. Click to move, click to interact. No keyboard shortcuts required (but escape for menu).

## XII. PUZZLE DESIGN

### Design Philosophy

Every puzzle in The Laughing Funeral is an act of investigation. There are no sliding tile puzzles, no arbitrary lock combinations, no "use rubber chicken with pulley" logic. Every challenge should feel like something an investigator would actually do: examine evidence, interview witnesses, cross-reference information, make deductions.

**The rule**: If the player cannot explain *why* the solution works in terms of the investigation, the puzzle is wrong.

### Puzzle Inventory

#### Puzzle 1: The Vestibule — Establishing the Case
**Type**: Observation + Dialogue
**Goal**: Learn the basics of the case from Director Voss
**Mechanic**: Examine the Vestibule's crystal displays and sign the Ledger. This unlocks dialogue options with Voss. The player must ask about specific crystals they've examined to demonstrate familiarity with Nocturne mourning customs — Voss won't discuss the case with someone who hasn't shown respect for the institution.
**Solution**: Examine three specific crystal displays (grief, longing, regret), then engage Voss with informed observations. Voss provides the guest list and the basic timeline.
**Difficulty**: Tutorial-level. Teaches examine-then-dialogue loop.

#### Puzzle 2: The Mourning Hall — Reading the Room
**Type**: Observation + Deduction
**Goal**: Identify the three anomalous mourners
**Mechanic**: Examine the chair-back crystals in the Mourning Hall. Most chairs have the same type of jagged, bright crystal. Three chairs are different — their crystal formations are subtler, more complex. The player must examine enough chairs to identify the pattern and locate the three outliers. Cross-reference with the seating chart (found on the lectern) to identify the names.
**Solution**: Examine at least six chairs (three normal, three anomalous), then examine the seating chart. The names auto-populate in Klaus's evidence board: Sable Thorn, Vesper Venn, "M.K."
**Difficulty**: Low. Teaches environmental observation.

#### Puzzle 3: The Guest Register — Identifying M.K.
**Type**: Cross-reference
**Goal**: Determine what "M.K." means
**Mechanic**: The guest register shows "M.K." signed with unusual ink. The player examines the register, then examines the ink samples in the Vestibule's writing desk (Nocturne funeral houses provide formal pens). By comparing ink composition (hover to see descriptions), the player identifies that M.K.'s ink does not match any Nocturne-sourced pen. Klaus's monologue notes it's institutional ink — DRM standard issue.
**Solution**: Examine register, examine ink samples at writing desk, combine the two observations. Klaus deduces "M.K." is not a name but a filing designation.
**Difficulty**: Low-medium. First evidence-combination puzzle.

#### Puzzle 4: Sable Thorn Interview
**Type**: Dialogue + Evidence Presentation
**Goal**: Obtain Aldric's research journal
**Mechanic**: Sable is protective and guarded. The player chooses dialogue approaches (direct/indirect/silence). Sable responds best to indirect questions — direct questions make her defensive, silence makes her anxious. The player must present the anomalous chair crystal as evidence to demonstrate they're investigating seriously. Once trust is established, Sable provides the journal — with missing pages.
**Solution**: Choose indirect dialogue options, present the chair crystal, ask about Aldric's final research. Sable gives the journal and mentions the missing pages.
**Difficulty**: Medium. First NPC with variable responses.

#### Puzzle 5: Vesper Venn Interview
**Type**: Dialogue + Emotional Observation
**Goal**: Obtain the key to Aldric's locked study
**Mechanic**: Vesper is in the Crystalline Garden. She is grieving properly — the player can observe that her mourning crystals are genuine and deep. The key unlocking moment is when Klaus notices that Vesper's crystals contain traces of guilt (a specific color shift visible in the crystal). Asking about the guilt — indirectly — unlocks Vesper's confession about the estrangement. She gives the key because Klaus understands.
**Solution**: Examine Vesper's mourning crystals (hotspot on her lapel), identify the guilt trace, use indirect dialogue to ask about the estrangement. Vesper provides the key and context about Aldric's final weeks.
**Difficulty**: Medium. Requires observation before dialogue.

#### Puzzle 6: The Locked Study — Aldric's Discovery
**Type**: Environmental Search + Item Collection
**Goal**: Find and understand Aldric's research
**Mechanic**: Use Vesper's key to enter. The study contains multiple hotspots: equipment, notebooks, crystal samples. The critical item is the gold crystal sample and Aldric's note. The player must examine the resonance amplifier (which Klaus recognizes as modified) and the crystal samples (most normal, one gold and warm) to piece together what Aldric was doing.
**Solution**: Use key on door, examine amplifier, examine crystal samples, read Aldric's note. Items collected: gold crystal sample, Aldric's note.
**Difficulty**: Low-medium. Exploration-driven, no complex logic.

#### Puzzle 7: Tracing the Ink — The DRM Connection
**Type**: Logic Chain
**Goal**: Connect "M.K." to Barry Kowalski's DRM personnel code
**Mechanic**: The player has identified the ink as DRM institutional. Now they need to determine what "M.K." stands for. In the Locked Study, Aldric's files include a cross-reference document — a list of DRM archival codes used in Nocturne's overflow storage system. The codes follow a pattern: Department, Personnel Initial. "M.K." = "Maintenance, Kowalski." The player must examine the code list, identify the pattern, and select the correct interpretation from three options on the evidence board.
**Solution**: Find the DRM code list in the Locked Study, examine it, go to the evidence board, connect "M.K." with the DRM code format. Select "Maintenance, Kowalski" from the options.
**Difficulty**: Medium. Requires connecting information from multiple sources.

#### Puzzle 8: The Archive Filing System
**Type**: Logic / Pattern Recognition
**Goal**: Navigate the archive to find relevant records
**Mechanic**: The Nocturne archive is not organized alphabetically. It is organized by emotional frequency — each funeral's records are filed under the dominant crystal type produced at the ceremony. The player must examine the index (a large reference chart on the archive wall) to determine the filing categories, then query the Archive Automaton with the correct category for Aldric Venn's funeral. Since the funeral produced gold crystal — a category that doesn't exist in the system — the Automaton redirects to "Anomalous" filings, which leads to the DRM overflow shelf.
**Solution**: Examine the index chart, note the absence of "gold" in the filing categories, query the Automaton with "anomalous" or "unclassified." The Automaton directs Klaus to the overflow shelf.
**Difficulty**: Medium. Requires understanding the system's logic and its limits.

#### Puzzle 9: Finding Form 12-C
**Type**: Search + Discovery
**Goal**: Locate Barry's ledger among the DRM files
**Mechanic**: The DRM shelf contains seven ledgers. The player examines each (brief descriptions from Klaus for most). The sixth ledger's spine reads *Anomalous Resonance — Unclosed Cases.* Inside: multiple case files. The player scrolls through them (a document interface) until reaching Form 12-C. This is not a difficult puzzle — it is a discovery moment. The difficulty is emotional, not mechanical.
**Solution**: Examine each DRM ledger on the shelf, open the sixth, scroll through case files to Form 12-C.
**Difficulty**: Low. This is a narrative beat, not a logic gate.

#### Puzzle 10: Reading Barry's Entries
**Type**: Document Examination
**Goal**: Read the full Form 12-C and find the hidden frequency chart
**Mechanic**: The player reads through Barry's entries (presented as a scrollable document with Klaus's internal monologue providing context). After reading Entry 14, the player must interact with the back cover — a hotspot that only becomes active after reading all entries. The folded frequency chart is hidden there.
**Solution**: Read all entries, interact with the back cover, unfold the frequency chart.
**Difficulty**: Low. Discovery-driven.

#### Puzzle 11: The Evidence Board — Making the Connection
**Type**: Deduction / Assembly
**Goal**: Connect all evidence to form the complete picture
**Mechanic**: The evidence board presents all collected clues as movable elements. The player must draw connections between related evidence. Required connections:
- Aldric's gold crystal → Builder signal (from Barry's frequency chart)
- Barry's frequency chart → Eidolon Manse location (the signal runs through the building)
- Form 12-C → Clive (the "object" Barry was investigating)
- Aldric's research → Signal activation (his analysis pinged the dormant network)
**Solution**: Draw four correct connections on the evidence board. Each correct connection triggers a line of Klaus's monologue explaining the logic.
**Difficulty**: Medium-high. Requires synthesizing all gathered evidence.

#### Puzzle 12: The Presentation
**Type**: Dialogue — Final Scene
**Goal**: Present findings to Director Voss and witnesses
**Mechanic**: Klaus stands before the assembled NPCs. The player selects evidence to present in order. The correct order follows Klaus's investigative logic: start with the funeral (what happened), move to the research (why it happened), end with the Builder signal (what it means). Presenting evidence in the wrong order doesn't fail — Klaus adjusts, but the NPCs respond less completely. Presenting in the correct order earns full responses and the complete ending.
**Solution**: Present evidence in order: gold crystal → Aldric's research → Barry's frequency chart → Form 12-C. Each presentation triggers NPC reactions and Klaus's explanatory monologue.
**Difficulty**: Medium. Order matters for completeness, not for completion.

### Post-Solution: The Barry Beat

After the presentation, the player returns to the archive room one final time. A quiet scene. Klaus stands before the DRM shelf. The player examines the ledger once more. Klaus's final monologue:

> "Barry Kowalski was a night guard. He worked shifts in a building full of equipment he wasn't qualified to operate, and he changed everything by sitting in the dark and paying attention. He followed a trail through every frequency in the spectrum, and his trail ran through this room. Through the bones of my world."

> "I left Nocturne because I found files that shouldn't have existed. Now I know why they existed. Barry was here first."

> "The Ephergent Frequency sounds, to me, like a case that has been solved but not yet presented. All the evidence assembled. All the connections made. Waiting for someone to stand up and say: here is what happened. Here is what it means."

Klaus closes the ledger. Opens his coat. Places it in an interior pocket.

> "Here is what we do about it."

Fade to black. End card: **Klaus returns to the Ephergent.**
