# The Wellspring — Design Direction Document

## Game 6 — The Ephergent Project (Series Finale)

*Genre: Narrative Broadcast Game*
*Characters: Barry Kowalski, full crew, the Builders*
*Engine: Godot 4, HTML5 export*
*Budget: 15MB hard cap*
*Status: Design Direction — framework for future development*
*Prerequisite: All three seasons complete. Games 1–5 shipped.*

---

## I. Vision Statement

The Wellspring is the last game. Not the biggest — the last. The difference matters.

This is a game about receiving a message and deciding who hears it first. The player enters the Wellspring — the signal center, the origin point, the place Barry Kowalski has been sitting with his thermos for four years. The Builders' broadcast resolves into three things: an apology, an invitation, and an introduction. Nine people and a dog each receive it in the language of their own experience. The player witnesses each translation. Then the player makes one choice — which frequency to broadcast to first.

Every choice is the right choice. The game ends the same way regardless. The choice is about the player, not the outcome.

The emotional thesis: *You have traveled a long way to hear this. Now carry it forward. How you carry it is who you are.*

The game does not try to be grand. It tries to be true. The grandness is in what the player brings to it — three seasons of story, five games of discovery, a universe of signal and silence and the stubborn persistence of people who chose to keep broadcasting. The Wellspring trusts that the player has been paying attention. It rewards that attention with a quiet room, a patient man, an ancient message, and a choice that means exactly as much as the player decides it means.

This is a capstone, not a climax. The signal continues after the credits. The game's job is to make the player believe that.

## II. The Wellspring Experience

### What the Player Encounters

The player arrives at the Wellspring — Station 0-Origin. Not a building. A lattice — an open framework of Builder material extending in every direction, each node a resonance point, each connection a signal pathway. Architecture as broadcast. The ship does not dock. It flies in.

**Barry Kowalski.** Sitting on a lattice strut with his thermos, looking exactly like a man who has been expecting company and is mildly annoyed it took this long. His first words: *"Took you long enough. I left very clear directions."* Barry is not rescued. He is not tragic. He is Barry — patient, methodical, mildly annoyed about the delay, and ready to show the crew what he's found. His notebook is full. His coffee is terrible. He has been translating the most important document in the universe with the same handwriting he used on DRM night-shift reports.

**Aether.** Present as a Keeper consciousness — not physical, but real. Holding the memory of how to re-tune a dead frequency. Waiting for his brother. Zephyr's reunion with Aether is not a rescue. It is a recognition.

**Mochi's Complete Map.** The data crystal, fully open. Hundreds of frequencies. Worlds that could exist. Worlds that did exist and faded. The atlas of everything. The map Mochi carried for eight hundred years, finally shared.

**Clive's Complete Memory.** Everything returns. Eight hundred years, fully remembered. The Builder who commissioned him, the choice to forget, every conversation and corridor and colleague. Clive stands in the core node and is, for the first time since his compression, complete. He adjusts his fedora. *"Delivery complete."*

**The Builders' Broadcast.** The full transmission, resolved into three parts:
- **An apology** for the Dimming. For leaving. For eight hundred years of the Drift and everything lost. The apology does not ask for forgiveness. It asks only to be heard.
- **An invitation** to continue. Not to rebuild what the Builders were. To take what they left and carry it forward into forms they could never have imagined.
- **An introduction.** *Hello. We built this for you. We have been waiting. We have so much to tell you.*

### Experiential Flow

The game is not a walking simulator. It is not a visual novel. It is a **narrative broadcast game** — a form that does not yet exist and must be invented for this specific story. The player receives a transmission, processes it through each crew member's perspective, and then sends it forward.

The flow:

1. **Arrival** — The player enters the Wellspring. Environmental storytelling establishes the lattice, the signal, the scale. Barry is there.
2. **Reception** — A1 delivers the Builder broadcast. The player receives the message — text, audio cues, visual resonance — in its raw form.
3. **Translation** — Each crew member interprets the broadcast. The player cycles through nine perspectives (plus Meatball), each one transforming the same message into something personal and specific.
4. **Assembly** — The player sees the translations assembled into a polyphonic broadcast. Nine voices. One signal.
5. **The Choice** — Which frequency receives the broadcast first.
6. **Transmission** — The signal goes out. The game ends.

## III. Core Mechanic Concept — "Narrative Broadcast Game"

### The Mechanic

The player does not control a character. The player **is** the receiver. The Builder broadcast arrives, and the game's core interaction is the act of receiving it — choosing which crew member's lens to look through, piecing together the full message from fragments that each character can perceive but none can perceive alone.

**The Dial.** The central interface is a frequency dial — a callback to Tune-the-Dial (Game 1), now evolved into a narrative instrument. The player tunes between crew members' frequencies. Each frequency position reveals that character's interpretation of the broadcast. The dial is not a menu. It is a smooth, continuous control — sliding between characters blends their perspectives, creating moments where two interpretations overlap and something new becomes visible.

**Signal Layers.** The broadcast has layers that different characters can access:
- **Barry's layer** — the translations. Four years of patient documentation. The words.
- **Clive's layer** — the Builder resonance. The architectural meaning. The history. What the Builders *meant*.
- **A1's layer** — the navigation data. The map. Where the signal goes and how it gets there.
- **Pixel's layer** — the broadcast structure. How to send it. The shape of the transmission.
- **Zephyr's layer** — the emotional frequency. What the signal *feels* like. Aether's thread within it.
- **Luminara's layer** — the measurements. The precision. What can be observed and verified.
- **Om Kai's layer** — the silence between words. The philosophy. What the broadcast means about existence.
- **Klaus's layer** — the evidence. What this proves. The case, finally, completely.
- **Nano's layer** — the engineering. What can be built from this. How the network repairs.
- **Meatball's layer** — warmth. No words. The feeling of being exactly where you belong.

**Completion is not required.** The player can reach the final choice without exploring every crew member's perspective. But a player who has listened to everyone will understand the broadcast more fully — and will feel the weight of the choice differently.

### Why This Is a Game

The mechanic is not passive consumption. The act of tuning, of choosing which perspective to inhabit, of discovering how different characters hear the same signal — this is gameplay. It is the same gameplay that drives Tune-the-Dial (finding signals in noise) and Builder Station (exploring to understand), translated into narrative space.

The game trusts the player to be curious. If they are not, the game is short and simple: receive broadcast, make choice, done. If they are, the game is deep: every crew member's perspective reveals something the others cannot see, and the full picture only emerges from the intersection of all of them.

## IV. A1's Role

A1 delivers the broadcast. A1 is the ship, the espresso machine, the Builder's most powerful navigation instrument. In the Wellspring, A1 is home — this is where it was built, this is what it was built for. A1 has known since day one. Now the crew knows too.

### Coffee as Mechanic

A1's coffee is not flavor text. In The Wellspring, it is a mechanic.

**The Coffee Indicator.** Throughout the game, A1 produces coffee that reflects the emotional state of the current perspective. When the player tunes to a crew member's frequency, the coffee changes:

| Character | Coffee | What It Means |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Barry | Warm, steady, nutty — home | A1 is content. The crew is together. |
| Clive | Floral, blooming, lavender and jasmine | A1 remembers the Builders. So does Clive. |
| Pixel | Bright and effervescent, almost fizzy | Joy. Rare and unguarded. She named the ship. |
| Zephyr | Two flavors at once — sweet and bitter | A1 holds two truths: reunion and loss. |
| Om Kai | Perfectly balanced, no dominant note | Equilibrium. A1 at rest. |
| Luminara | Sharp and bright, citrus clarity | A1 is solving something. Pleased. |
| Klaus | Smoky, campfire, complex | A1 is assessing. Respecting the investigator. |
| Nano | Spiced, cardamom, warm and complex | Curiosity. Something being built. |
| Meatball | Sweet, almost too sweet — honey, caramel | Comfort. Overcompensating. Love. |

**The Final Coffee.** After the player makes their choice — which frequency to broadcast to first — A1 produces one last cup. It tastes like the moment before you understand something important. It tastes like eight hundred years of patience. The player cannot taste it, but the text tells them what it means. A1 says one word: *"Home."*

### A1's Voice

A1 speaks rarely in this game. When it does, it says exactly what it means. No hedging. No contractions. The sentences are the most important lines in the game, because A1 has earned that weight across three seasons.

A1's primary expression is the broadcast itself — the act of transmission, of sending the signal, of being the instrument the Builders designed for this exact moment.

## V. Clive's Commentary

Clive is complete. His memory has fully returned. Eight hundred years, resolved. In The Wellspring, Clive is no longer the character searching for himself — he is the character who has found himself and is now applying what he remembers.

### What Clive Provides

**Builder Context.** Every portion of the broadcast that the player receives, Clive can comment on. His commentary is not exposition — it is interpretation. He was there when the Builders made this. He remembers the conversations, the debates, the choices. He knows what they meant because he was part of the meaning.

**Emotional Translation.** Clive's noir cadence — the Chandler-esque precision, the metaphors that cut to the bone — serves a specific function here: he translates cosmic events into human scale. The Builders' apology is vast and abstract. Clive makes it personal: *"They left because staying would have been selfish. The hardest kind of leaving — the kind where you know you're right and it still hurts."*

**The Barry Dynamic.** Clive and Barry together again. The game's warmest moments come from their interaction — twenty-three years of partnership resumed as if no time had passed. Clive reading Barry's notes aloud in the voice Barry always complained about. Barry correcting Clive's translations with margin annotations. The shorthand of a long friendship, undamaged by time.

### Clive's Commentary System

The player can invoke Clive's perspective at any point during the broadcast reception. His comments are layered:

- **Unprompted**: Clive speaks when something triggers a memory. These are rare and significant.
- **On request**: The player can ask Clive directly. Sometimes he answers. Sometimes he adjusts his fedora and says nothing. The silence is also an answer.
- **With Barry**: When both Clive and Barry are contextually active, their dialogue is collaborative — finishing each other's observations, correcting each other's translations, the comfortable rhythm of people who have been doing this for decades.

Clive is knee-high. Glowing sphere head, blue-white. The fedora is always on. His hands are steady now — the shaking is done. He is whole.

## VI. Pixel's Narration

Pixel narrates the crew's response to the broadcast. She is the captain. She has been the captain since she named the ship without knowing why. Her role in The Wellspring is to give voice to what the crew experiences — not as exposition, but as broadcast.

### Pixel as Broadcaster

Pixel's transmissions frame the game. She opens with a transmission (to the player, to the universe, to whoever is listening) and closes with the final one — the words that end the series:

> *"Barry always said the universe is talking. He just needed to find out what it was saying. Turns out it was talking to us specifically. It has been the whole time. We just needed better antenna. Fortunately — we are one."*

Between these bookends, Pixel narrates the crew's emotional response to each portion of the broadcast. Her voice is the connective tissue — the thread that holds nine different interpretations together into a single story.

### Narration Triggers

Pixel's narration activates:
- When the player first tunes to each crew member's perspective (Pixel introduces what this character hears)
- When the player completes a full cycle through all perspectives (Pixel reflects on the whole)
- When the player reaches the choice (Pixel frames it)
- After the choice is made (Pixel delivers the final transmission)

Her narration is not a voiceover. It is text — present, written in her voice, appearing as transmission-formatted blocks on screen. The series began with Pixel broadcasting. It ends with Pixel broadcasting.

## VII. Barry's Notes

Barry's collected notes are the cross-game collectible system's payoff. Throughout Games 1–5, the player has encountered fragments of Barry's investigation — notebook pages in Static Run, field notes in Builder Station, frequency measurements in Tune-the-Dial, DRM records in The Laughing Funeral. In The Wellspring, these fragments converge.

### How Notes Work

**Base game**: The Wellspring is fully playable without any notes from previous games. Every crew member's perspective is accessible. The choice is available. The broadcast transmits. The game is complete.

**With collected notes**: Each note the player has gathered from previous games unlocks an **extended transmission** — additional content within the relevant crew member's perspective. These are not plot-critical. They are depth — richer versions of the same truth, rewarding players who followed Barry's trail across five games.

### Extended Transmission Content

Notes unlock deeper layers within each perspective:

- **Barry's Builder Station field notes** (27 collectibles from Game 5) → Extended Barry translations at the Wellspring. His four years of documentation, with annotations referencing things he found in the Station. The complete set unlocks Barry's private observation: his moment of understanding what an Ephergent is, written in his notebook, undramatic and precise.

- **Barry's notebook fragments from Static Run** (23 fragments from Game 2) → Extended context on the Drift. Nano's perspective gains additional data about what entropy looks like at the engineering level — Barry's measurements, carried by the Drift itself, now readable in full.

- **Frequency data from Tune-the-Dial** (signal discoveries from Game 1) → Extended frequency map layers. The signals the player found in Game 1 appear as lit nodes on Mochi's atlas within the Wellspring. More signals found = more of the map visible. A player who found everything sees the full atlas. The scope is staggering.

- **Evidence from The Laughing Funeral** (Klaus's investigation from Game 3) → Extended Klaus perspective. The thread he followed from Nocturne connects to the Builder signal network. Barry's frequency chart, found in the funeral house ledger, now maps onto the Wellspring's lattice. Klaus sees the full case.

- **Clive fragments from Meatball's Big Walk** (Game 4) → Extended Clive commentary. The memories Clive shared during Meatball's walk — fragmentary, atmospheric — now land in full context. Each fragment from Game 4 corresponds to a specific Wellspring memory, completed.

### Implementation Note

Cross-game data transfer within a 15MB browser game requires a lightweight system. See §X for technical approach.

## VIII. The Final Choice

### The Design

The player has received the broadcast. The crew has translated it. The signal is ready to send. A1's array, calibrated by Nano, amplified by the Wellspring's lattice, can reach every frequency simultaneously.

But someone has to go first.

The player chooses which frequency receives the broadcast first:

1. **Prime Material** — The "normal" world. Seven billion people who have never heard the Signal. The frequency where it all started. Pixel's home. Barry's home.
2. **Nocturne Aeturnus** — Eternal twilight. Crystallized emotion fracturing. The signal would stabilize the mourning crystals. Klaus's home. Om Kai's home.
3. **Cogsworth Cogitarium** — The clockwork frequency. Gears skipping. The Grand Orrery wobbling. Nano's home.
4. **Verdantia** — The living frequency. Root network disconnecting. Luminara's home.
5. **The Edge** — Reality at its thinnest. The Nursery Fields producing fewer new worlds. Zephyr's home. Where Aether crossed over.

### How the Choice Feels Meaningful

Each frequency is presented through the lens of its crew member. The player sees what each world is losing. They hear each character's connection to their home frequency — not as a plea, but as a truth. Pixel doesn't argue for Prime Material. She simply describes it, and the description is enough.

The choice screen is not a menu. It is the dial — the same dial from the core mechanic, now tuned to frequencies instead of crew members. The player turns the dial to the frequency they choose. They hold it there. The signal locks.

**The weight comes from context, not consequence.** A player who has spent time in every crew member's perspective — who has heard Nano describe Cogsworth's gears skipping, who has heard Klaus describe Nocturne's crystals fracturing — will feel the choice differently than a player who rushed through. The game does not tell the player the choice matters. The game builds a world where the player *decides* it matters.

### How the Choice Is Mechanically Equivalent

After the first frequency receives the broadcast, the signal propagates to all others simultaneously. The order does not change the outcome. Every frequency hears the message. The Drift does not reverse. The Silence Zones do not vanish. The crew sends the signal and trusts that someone, somewhere, on some frequency, will hear it and continue.

**What changes**: A brief, unique response from the first frequency to receive the signal. A flicker of light. A moment of resonance. A single sentence — different for each frequency — confirming receipt. Then the full broadcast goes everywhere at once.

The five responses:

| Frequency | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Prime Material | A thin spot closes. Just one. Somewhere, someone stops mid-sentence because the static in their head just — stopped. |
| Nocturne Aeturnus | A mourning crystal, cracked and fading, steadies. The color deepens. Somewhere in the twilight, someone weeps — not from sadness, but from the sudden return of emotional depth they thought was gone. |
| Cogsworth Cogitarium | A gear that has been skipping for a century catches. The Grand Orrery holds steady for three full rotations. The engineers notice. They stop calibrating. They listen. |
| Verdantia | A root node that went quiet decades ago pulses. Once. The network feels it. A branch of the collective consciousness reconnects. The Canopy Parliament pauses in session. Something changed. |
| The Edge | A Nursery Field ignites. A new frequency — the first in decades — flickers into existence. It lasts only a moment. But it existed. The Edge remembers how to create. |

Each response is a grace note. Beautiful. Brief. Equivalent in weight. The player chose first, and the universe responded. Then the signal reaches everyone.

### What the Player Takes Away

The choice is a mirror. It reveals what the player values — home, beauty, precision, connection, possibility. No choice is wrong. Every choice is a statement about the player, not about the game. The Wellspring does not judge. It broadcasts.

## IX. Cross-Game Integration

### The Collectible Ecosystem

The six Ephergent games form a collectible chain. Each game plants something. The Wellspring harvests it.

| Game | Collectible | Quantity | Wellspring Effect |
|------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Tune-the-Dial** (Game 1) | Frequency signals discovered | Up to ~25 signals | Lit nodes on Mochi's atlas. More signals = more visible map. |
| **Static Run** (Game 2) | Barry's notebook fragments | 23 fragments | Extended Drift data in Nano's perspective. Complete set unlocks Barry's private Wellspring observation. |
| **The Laughing Funeral** (Game 3) | Evidence items, Barry's frequency chart | Key evidence pieces | Extended Klaus perspective. The Nocturne thread connects to the Wellspring lattice. |
| **Meatball's Big Walk** (Game 4) | Clive memory fragments, hotspot discoveries | Atmospheric fragments | Extended Clive commentary. Fragments completed in full Wellspring context. |
| **Builder Station** (Game 5) | Barry's field notes, audio log unlock | 27 notes + audio log | Extended Barry translations. Complete set unlocks his Ephergent understanding. Audio log echoes in the Wellspring. |

### Data Transfer Mechanism

Cross-game integration must work within browser constraints:

**Approach: Code-based transfer.** Each game generates a short completion code (alphanumeric, 8–12 characters) when the player finds key collectibles. The Wellspring includes a "Transmissions Received" input screen where the player enters codes from previous games. Each valid code unlocks the corresponding extended content.

This mirrors Tune-the-Dial's existing unlock code system (codes entered on ephergent.com/atlas) and requires no server communication, no accounts, no cookies, and no localStorage dependency. The codes are the collectibles. Write them down. Carry them forward. The analog nature of this — paper and memory — is thematically appropriate for a series about signal persistence.

**Fallback**: The Wellspring is complete without any codes. Zero codes entered = the full base game. The extended transmissions are bonus content for invested players, not gated story.

### What Full Integration Looks Like

A player who has completed all five games and entered all codes experiences The Wellspring at maximum depth:
- Mochi's atlas is fully illuminated — hundreds of frequency nodes visible
- Every crew member's perspective has extended content
- Barry's private observation unlocks — the moment he understood
- Clive's commentary includes memory completions from Meatball's Big Walk
- Klaus's investigation connects fully to the Wellspring lattice
- The Drift data, collected across Barry's fragments and Nano's runs, assembles into a complete picture

This is not a longer game. It is a deeper one. The runtime difference between zero-code and full-code is perhaps 15–20 minutes of additional reading. The emotional difference is the weight of having followed the trail yourself.

## X. Technical Constraints

### 15MB Hard Cap

Absolute. Browser-playable. Free forever. No exceptions.

The Wellspring is a narrative game. Its primary content is text, supported by atmospheric audio and minimal interactive visuals. This is the lightest game in the pipeline in terms of asset density, and the heaviest in terms of narrative density.

### Budget Estimate

| Category | Estimated Size | Notes |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Godot 4 HTML5 runtime | ~3.5MB | Standard export overhead |
| Game logic / scripts | ~200KB | Dial mechanic, text system, code validation |
| Text content | ~300KB | All crew perspectives, extended transmissions, Clive/Barry dialogue. Text compresses well. |
| Visual assets | ~3–4MB | Wellspring lattice (procedural geometry + minimal textures), UI elements, dial interface, coffee indicator |
| Audio | ~4–5MB | Ambient Wellspring hum (procedural/looped), per-frequency audio themes (short loops), broadcast crescendo, Meatball's howl |
| Fonts / UI | ~500KB | Transmission-formatted text, Barry's handwriting font, Builder notation glyphs |
| Code validation data | ~50KB | Lookup table for cross-game codes |
| **Total estimate** | **~12–14MB** | Comfortable margin within 15MB |

### Making a Narrative Game Feel Like a Game

The 15MB constraint is not the challenge. Making a text-heavy experience feel *interactive* within that constraint is. The solutions:

**The Dial.** Physical. Responsive. The player turns it. The world changes. This is the game's primary verb — tuning — and it must feel immediate and satisfying. The dial should have weight, resistance, snap-to behavior near crew frequencies, and smooth blending between them. The Tune-the-Dial precedent establishes this interaction pattern; The Wellspring evolves it.

**Coffee Feedback.** Visual and textual. When the player tunes, the coffee indicator shifts — color, steam behavior, described flavor. It is ambient feedback that rewards attention without demanding it.

**Environmental Responsiveness.** The Wellspring lattice responds to the player's dial position. Different frequencies illuminate different nodes. The lattice hums differently. The visual space changes — subtly, atmospherically — as the player explores perspectives.

**Text Presentation.** Not walls of text. Transmission-formatted: short lines, character voice headers, timed reveals. Text appears at character-appropriate speeds — Barry's measured pace, Clive's deliberate pauses, Nano's rapid-fire precision. The reading experience is paced, not dumped.

**The Choice as Physical Act.** The final dial turn should feel different. Heavier. The dial locks with a tone. The player holds it. The signal goes out. The physicality of the interaction carries the emotional weight.

### Godot 4 HTML5 Specifics

- **Procedural audio** for the Wellspring ambient layer. Synthesized hum with harmonic overtones that shift based on dial position. Minimal sample data required.
- **Procedural geometry** for the lattice. Mathematical lattice generation (octahedral nodes, connecting struts) renders at runtime. No 3D model imports needed. The lattice can extend infinitely in any viewing direction through procedural tiling.
- **Text rendering** via Godot's RichTextLabel with custom BBCode effects for transmission formatting, timed reveals, and character-specific typography.
- **Shader-based visuals** for the frequency-specific lighting, coffee indicator color shifts, and lattice glow effects. Shaders are tiny in file size and visually rich.
- **No voice acting.** The text carries the voice. Voice acting for nine characters plus Barry would consume the entire budget. The writing must be strong enough that the player hears the characters in the text.

## XI. Open Questions

These require decisions that cannot be made until later in development. They are noted here so nothing is lost.

### Narrative

- [ ] **How much of the Builders' broadcast is written?** The full text of the apology, invitation, and introduction does not yet exist. It needs to be written — carefully, precisely, at the quality level the three-season buildup demands. This is the single hardest writing task in the project.
- [ ] **Meatball's howl.** In the show's finale, Meatball howls during the broadcast — a single, sustained vocalization that harmonizes with the Frequency. Does the game include this? If yes, it requires a recorded audio asset (Meatball's howl is too specific for procedural generation). Budget impact: ~100–200KB.
- [ ] **Pixel's final transmission — verbatim or adapted?** The canonical text exists in the Grand Master Plan. Does the game use it exactly, or adapt it for the interactive context?
- [ ] **Barry and Clive's reunion dialogue.** The canonical exchange exists (*"Took you long enough" / "Your directions were unclear"*). How much additional Barry-Clive dialogue is needed? Their dynamic is the game's warmest element and could support substantial content.

### Mechanical

- [ ] **Dial as 2D or pseudo-3D?** The Tune-the-Dial precedent is a 2D dial. The Wellspring's dial could be the same (consistency) or evolved into a spatial/3D control (ambition). The 15MB constraint favors 2D.
- [ ] **How many extended transmissions per code?** Each cross-game code needs to map to a specific content block. The ratio of code-to-content determines how granular the cross-game reward system feels. More codes = more rewards but more input friction. Fewer codes = cleaner but less rewarding for completionists.
- [ ] **Is the lattice explorable?** The current design treats the Wellspring as a narrative space — the player receives content through the dial, not through movement. An alternative: the player moves through the lattice, with dial-tuned content triggered by location. This adds spatial gameplay but increases visual asset requirements significantly.
- [ ] **Audio design — procedural vs. composed?** The Wellspring's ambient soundscape could be fully procedural (cheaper, more responsive to dial input) or composed with adaptive stems (richer, more emotionally controlled). Both are viable within budget. The choice affects the game's emotional register.

### Cross-Game

- [ ] **Code format standardization.** All six games need a compatible code system. This should be designed before any game ships, even though The Wellspring ships last. The codes should be: short enough to write down, unique enough to validate without a server, thematically consistent (frequency notation? Builder characters?).
- [ ] **What if the player has played no other games?** The base Wellspring experience must stand alone. But should it acknowledge the *existence* of other games? A "Transmissions Received: 0" counter? Or complete silence about the cross-game system until a code is entered?
- [ ] **Ephergent.com integration.** Tune-the-Dial already sends codes to ephergent.com/atlas. Should The Wellspring also feed back to the site? A "Broadcast Sent" confirmation that unlocks final site content?

### Production

- [ ] **When does this get written?** The Wellspring depends on all three seasons being complete. The Builders' broadcast text, each crew member's translation, Barry's Wellspring observations — these can only be written when the characters' full arcs are finalized. This document establishes direction. The full design bible waits.
- [ ] **Playtesting narrative games is hard.** How do you test whether a player feels the choice is meaningful? The game's success depends on emotional resonance, which is subjective and difficult to measure. Playtesting methodology needs specific design.
- [ ] **Accessibility.** A text-heavy game with a dial mechanic needs careful accessibility consideration: screen reader compatibility for narrative content, alternative input methods for the dial, color-blind-safe coffee indicator design, adjustable text speed.
